## 1. Core Sales Philosophy

### The Schiffman Sales Model

Sales is not about convincing, manipulating, or tricking people into buying. It is about understanding what the prospect is trying to accomplish and helping them do it better. The fundamental process:

```
1. Find out what the prospect DOES (not what they "need")
2. Understand HOW they do it, WHEN, WHERE, with WHOM, and WHY that way
3. Help them do it BETTER using your product or service
4. Build a plan that MAKES SENSE from their point of view
```

### Three Pillars of Sales Success

| Pillar | Description | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Obsession** | Absolute daily commitment to selling activity | Follow a disciplined routine every working day without exception |
| **Utilisation** | Exploit every resource, tool, and opportunity available | Company materials, past successes, social events, industry knowledge |
| **Implementation** | Execute rather than over-analyse | Don't fall prey to "paralysis of analysis" — make the calls, do the work |

### Your Real Competition

The number-one competitor every salesperson faces is NOT a competing company. It is the **status quo** — what the prospect is already doing. If the prospect actually needed to change, they would have done so already. Your job is to become a **messenger of positive change** by demonstrating how they could do what they do even better.

### Convey, Don't Convince

Sales is not about getting other people to see your point of view. It is about getting you to see things from other people's points of view:

- Build trust and rapport
- Find out what they do
- Emphasise relevant past successes
- Highlight credible solutions to important problems

Being too anxious to close, and working to get the prospect to see your point of view, will only decrease your odds. When people feel good about a purchase, they say "I bought this." When they feel pressured, they say "Some salesperson sold me this." The first produces a customer; the second produces a complaint.

## 2. The Four-Step Sales Process

Schiffman's sales cycle has four distinct steps. The objective of each step is always to advance to the next step.

```
Step 1: OPENING / QUALIFYING (Prospecting, cold calls, initial contact)
    Objective: Get agreement to move into a meaningful interview

Step 2: INTERVIEWING / INFORMATION GATHERING (Discovery)
    Objective: Gather enough information to build a tailored presentation
    This is the LONGEST phase — approximately 75% of total effort

Step 3: PRESENTATION (Proposal delivery)
    Objective: Present a plan that makes sense based on verified information
    Only present when you have sufficient information

Step 4: CLOSING (Decision)
    Objective: Get the prospect to agree to become a customer
    "It makes sense to me — what do you think?"
```

### Critical Ratio: 75/25

In the ideal sale, approximately 75% of work happens BEFORE the presentation. Most salespeople invert this by rushing to present and close with insufficient information. The result is low close rates and wasted effort.

```
WRONG approach (most salespeople):
[===BIG OPENING/DUMP===][few questions][=PRESENT=][===BIG CLOSE===]

RIGHT approach:
[Opening][=======INFORMATION GATHERING=======][Present][Close]
```

### The "Playing Ball" Test

At each step, the prospect must be "playing ball" — actively engaging, returning calls, scheduling next meetings, sharing information. If they are not playing ball, they are not a real prospect. Do not waste time on prospects who won't engage.

## 3. The PIPA Sequence (First Meeting Framework)

The PIPA sequence is Schiffman's structured framework for conducting effective first meetings with prospects. It prevents "product dumping" and "slapshot selling."

### P — Present (Brief Introduction)

After brief rapport-building small talk, transition with:

> "Mr. Prospect, would it help if I told you a little bit about our company and what we do?"

This almost always gets a "yes." Deliver a **two-to-three sentence** company introduction. NOT a product dump. Example:

> "ABC Widget Development is the largest specialised widget manufacturing company in the country. We've been in business since 1923, and I've been working for the company as a senior account representative since 2018."

### I — Interview (Information Gathering)

Immediately after the brief introduction, transition to a **do-based question** that meets three criteria:

1. Focuses on what the **prospect does**
2. Addresses an area where you have **added value** for other customers
3. Is **easy for the prospect to answer**

Example: "I'm just curious, have you ever worked with a specialised widget manufacturer before?"

Useful transition phrases: **"I'm just curious..."** and **"By the way..."**

#### Do-Based Questions by Timeframe

| Timeframe | Question Type | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Past** | What they have done | "What results did you expect from that approach?" / "How did you decide whether to continue?" |
| **Present** | What they are doing now | "What are you doing now in this area?" / "Why did you choose to work with XYZ?" |
| **Future** | What they want to achieve | "What would you personally like to accomplish?" / "How will you measure progress?" |

#### If the Prospect Contacted You

> "I'm just curious — what made you decide to call us about [topic]?"

#### If You Contacted the Prospect

> "I'm just curious — what were you going to do if I hadn't gotten in touch with you?"

#### Essential Information to Capture in First Meeting

Do not consider a first meeting successful unless you have documented:

- The prospect's work history and background
- The kinds of internal and external customers the prospect must serve
- What the prospect is trying to accomplish
- The standards used for previous purchase decisions in your area

Key question for understanding past decisions: **"Why did you decide to do it that way?"** or **"How did you choose them?"**

### P — Present a Next Step Option

Once you have gathered enough information, present a specific next step:

> "Based on what you've told me today — specifically X, Y, and Z — I think I should put together a preliminary proposal to give you an idea of what we might be able to do for you in this area. Why don't I come back here next Tuesday at two o'clock?"

Rules for the next step:
- Make it **easy, logical, and helpful**
- Connect it to a **specific date and time**
- Make the conversation about **when** you'll meet next, not **whether**

#### Parable Selling

Use success stories to position the next step:

> "It's interesting that you mention XYZ challenge — that's very similar to the situation I faced with my last customer, Tommy Bigshot. What we found was... Why don't I come back next Tuesday at two o'clock and show you the plan I put together for Bigshot?"

### A — Agreement (Confirmation)

Ensure the prospect specifically agrees to the proposed next step:

> "So — do you think it makes sense for us to get together again at this time next week?"

If the prospect gives a confusing or negative response, use the **"take responsibility" technique**:

> You: "So — do you think it makes sense for us to get together next Tuesday?"
> Prospect: "I'd really rather not."
> You: "I'll be honest — that's a surprise to me. Did I do something wrong?"
> Prospect: "Oh, no — it's nothing you did. The thing is..."

**"The thing is..."** are magic words. Everything that follows them contains critical information about the real obstacle.

## 4. Prospecting and Lead Generation

### The Non-Negotiable Prospecting Commitment

Prospecting must happen every single working day. It is the activity that drives the entire sales pipeline. Schiffman's personal daily routine:

```
15 dials per day
  → 7 conversations
    → 1 new appointment
      → 5 appointments per week
        → 1 sale per 5-8 appointments
          → ~50 new customers per year
```

### Prospecting Probability Tiers

| Prospect Type | Close Probability | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing customer** | 1 in 2 (50%) | Highest priority for repeat/upsell |
| **Past customer (inactive)** | 1 in 4 (25%) | Maintain regular contact |
| **New prospect (cold)** | 1 in 20 (5%) | Requires high volume of activity |

### The Four-Step Cold Calling Process

```
Step 1: OPENING STATEMENT
  Reference a specific success with another client. Never make ludicrous claims.

  WRONG: "I can save you a million dollars by Monday morning"
  RIGHT: "A couple of months ago, I finished working with XYZ company,
         and put together a programme that increased their sales by 42%
         this quarter over last year. What I'd like to do is stop by
         next Tuesday at 3:00 and tell you about the success I had."

Step 2: PROSPECT'S RESPONSE
  Expect pushback related to what they do, not what you do:
  "We handle that in-house" / "We don't use outside providers" /
  "We just did that" / "Not interested"

Step 3: TURNAROUND
  Acknowledge and redirect to what they do:
  "That's exactly what a lot of my customers said before they saw how
  our programmes could complement their existing approach. What kind
  of in-house programmes are you running now?"

Step 4: ASK FOR THE APPOINTMENT
  "Based on what you've told me, I really think we ought to get together.
  How's Tuesday at 3:00?"
```

### Nine Principles of Cold Calling

1. Set your goal for the **number of first appointments** you want to maintain AT ALL TIMES, not just daily dial counts
2. **Make cold calls daily** with the objective of setting at least one new appointment per day — block uninterrupted time for calling
3. **Track dials, completed calls, and appointments set** on a daily basis — compile results and benchmark your activity
4. **Do not stop dialing** if results are poor — take a break, practise, adjust your approach, diversify your leads, but keep calling
5. **Always be prepared to call** — have a lead list ready at all times, use unexpected free time for prospecting, do research "off-peak"
6. **Use third-party references** — briefly reference past success stories, but never promise identical results; ask for a meeting instead
7. **Practise each aspect** until comfortable — prepare for specific objections and be more ready to turn them around than the prospect is to brush you off
8. **Ask directly for the appointment** — if you haven't asked for one meeting at one specific date and time during the call, you're not doing it right
9. **Never forget:** Sales come from prospects, and prospects come from appointments

### Getting Return Phone Calls

When leaving voicemail, create a reason for the prospect to call back:

> "Would you please call me back? I just want to apologise for what I've done."

This take-responsibility approach generates curiosity and callbacks.

### Handling "Just Send Me Information"

> Prospect: "Just send me some information."
> You: "I prefer not to send information — let's just get together instead. How's Tuesday at two o'clock?"

This approach forces a decision: either the prospect agrees to meet (a real prospect) or reveals they were never serious (saving you time).

### Fallback Prospects (Recycling Old Leads)

"No" usually means "no for now," not "no forever." Revisit rejected prospects on a regular schedule (approximately every 3 months):

> "I understand you didn't buy from us six months ago, but I'm just calling today to find out how things are going in your [area], and to see if you have any new projects up and running."

- Fallbacks can increase revenue by **5-10%**
- People leave jobs, get promoted, competitive situations shift
- Previous "no interest" lists are a highly promising source of appointments
- Creative re-engagement: "We were having a sales meeting and your name came up. I was thinking we haven't talked in a while."

## 5. Qualification and Discovery

### The Listening Imperative

The most reliable way to distinguish successful from unsuccessful salespeople is to watch how they interact with a prospect. Failures do all the talking. Successes listen.

- A good salesperson doesn't so much sell as **help**
- Very little communication is verbal — most is nonverbal; "listen" for body language, tone, and hesitation
- Ideally, you must know what it will take for the prospect to do the selling himself

### Empathy as a Sales Tool

Empathy is not sympathy. It is the ability to understand and share the prospect's perspective without losing objectivity:

- Understand their situation from their point of view
- Acknowledge their concerns before proposing solutions
- Show that you have grasped the implications of their challenges

### Don't See the Prospect as an Adversary

The prospect is not someone to be conquered or overcome. They are a potential partner with problems you may be able to help solve. The adversarial mindset leads to:

- Arguments instead of discussions
- Winning debates but losing sales
- Short-term transactions instead of long-term relationships

### Don't "Product Dump"

The most common reason first meetings fail is the "product dump" — reciting a lengthy monologue about your company, products, and services. Product dumping happens when:

- Salespeople are nervous and fall back on what's familiar
- They assume they know what the prospect needs
- They try to "cover everything" in the first meeting

The copier salesman cautionary tale: A salesperson delivered a 20-minute uninterrupted product monologue, then tried to close — without ever asking what business the prospect was in. He was trying to sell a UGX 55M copier without knowing what it would be used for.

**Rule:** The amount of information you take IN during the first meeting should always exceed the amount you send OUT.

### Avoid "Slapshot Selling"

Slapshot selling is argumentative, knee-jerk selling that spends more time batting away objections than finding out what the prospect actually does. Characteristics:

- Firing off memorised questions
- Reciting product information when challenged
- "Please keep your comments to yourself and let me finish my PowerPoint"
- Responding to every objection with "Yes, but we also offer..."

### Ask "Does This Make Sense?"

Throughout the sales process, proactively check alignment:

> "So, Mr. Smith, does what I'm talking about make sense?"

Do not save this question for closing. Ask it throughout. If the answer is no:

> "Okay, why not? Where did I take a wrong turn?"

Nine times out of ten, the prospect will say: "It's nothing you did. Here's what the problem is..."

### Present Hypothetical Options to Get Corrected

Prospects love to correct salespeople. Use this:

> "So, can I assume your customers use standard-sized widgets, or do they prefer the extra-large variety?"
> "Actually, most of our customers use very small widgets."

Write it down. Taking notes encourages the prospect to open up and raises their status in the conversation.

### Key Questions for Best Accounts

Study your best existing customers to understand why they bought:

- Why did your number-one account choose you?
- What specific problem did you solve for them?
- How did they describe the value you provide?
- What would they say if asked why they stay with you?

Use these answers to frame discussions with new prospects.

### Find Out What's Changed

For existing relationships and returning prospects, the critical question is always: **"What's changed?"**

- Changes in personnel, strategy, budget, competitive landscape
- New projects, new challenges, new priorities
- Shifts in how they do what they do

## 6. Presentation and Proposal

### Don't Present Too Early

A low closing ratio almost always means presenting before gathering enough information. Before making a formal presentation, confirm:

| Checkpoint | Question to Ask Yourself |
|---|---|
| **Right person** | Am I talking to the decision-maker or someone who can get the decision made? |
| **Right plan** | Does this plan make sense based on what I know they're actually trying to do? |
| **Budget discussed** | Have I raised pricing and budget issues myself? Does the pricing make sense? |
| **Realistic timetable** | Is the implementation or delivery schedule concrete, not theoretical? |
| **Close expectation set** | Does my contact know I expect to close this sale? |

If any answer is "no," you are not ready to present.

### Setting Close Expectations

Before the formal presentation, explicitly state your intention:

> "I'm going to gather everything we've done into a formal proposal for our meeting next Tuesday, and at that point, I don't see any reason why we wouldn't be able to finalise this."

If the contact reacts negatively, you have discovered a hidden objection BEFORE wasting time on a doomed presentation.

### Verify Information with a Preliminary Proposal

Between information gathering and formal presentation, insert a **verification step**:

```
Open → Gather Information → VERIFY → Present → Close
```

The preliminary proposal (or outline) is a 2-3 page summary that says "I am not a formal proposal." It allows the prospect to:

- Correct misunderstandings before you invest in a full proposal
- Write all over it and make changes
- Reveal new information and concerns
- Signal whether they are truly "playing ball"

After review: "I think we've got everything in place. Let me make these changes and come back Friday at three o'clock. At that point, we should be able to finalise this and set your delivery dates. Does that make sense?"

### Quality Over Quantity

What makes more sense:

- 5 customised proposals to 5 prospects who are "playing ball" with you, OR
- 25 uncustomised proposals that have little to do with what people are trying to accomplish?

### Never Make a Presentation You Don't Think Will Close

If you don't believe your presentation will result in a closed sale, something is wrong with your preparation. Go back and gather more information.

### Take Responsibility When Presentations Go Wrong

When you hit a brick wall after what should have been a successful presentation:

> "Mr. Jones, I'm really not sure what to say. I'm quite confident that we have the best service, the best pricing, the best customisation. In fact, I can only think of one reason you might not decide to sign on with us today, and that is that I must have done something terribly wrong in giving my presentation. So I'm going to ask you to give me a hand and tell me where I went off course."

The common response: **"No-no-no, it has nothing to do with you. The thing is..."** Now you get the real obstacles.

This technique requires absolute faith that you can deliver on your promises.

### Use "People Proof" (Social Proof)

Reference specific success stories with other clients:

- Name the company (with permission)
- State the specific result achieved
- Draw parallels to the prospect's situation
- Offer to show the detailed case

### Prepare for Objections

Anticipate the specific objections you will hear and prepare responses in advance. Read industry publications (both yours and your clients') to stay current on trends and challenges. Successful salespeople:

- Anticipate objections before they arise
- Prepare their organisations for likely prospect responses
- Regularly update their sales materials and question sequences based on new industry information

## 7. Closing Strategy

### The Anti-Close: "It Makes Sense to Me — What Do You Think?"

Schiffman's closing technique is deliberately simple. After thorough information gathering, verification, and a tailored presentation:

> "So, Mr. Smith, that's our proposal. I have to tell you, we've spent a lot of time putting this together, and it really makes sense to me. Does it make sense to you?"

Two possible outcomes:

1. **"Yes, it makes sense to me too"** — You have a sale
2. **"No, it doesn't make sense"** — Pull back and ask: "I must have taken a wrong turn somewhere. Where did I go wrong? What doesn't make sense?" Then take detailed notes

### Why Traditional "Closing Tricks" Fail

Manipulative closing techniques are unnecessary and counterproductive:

- "My boss will fire me if you don't sign"
- "Here's a pen, press hard, you're making three copies"
- "Did you want green widgets or blue widgets?"
- "If you buy from me today, I'll win a trip to Hawaii"

These may produce occasional one-time sales, but they destroy trust and prevent long-term partnerships.

### The One-Third Rule

| Segment | Outcome | Your Influence |
|---|---|---|
| **Bottom third** | Will buy from you no matter what you do | Just show up at the right time |
| **Middle third** | Could go either way | **THIS is where your skills matter** |
| **Top third** | Will not buy from you no matter what you do | Competition got there first, or insurmountable obstacles |

Focus your energy on winning the middle third through superior information gathering and relationship building.

### Never Walk Away Without Asking for Action

Always ask the prospect to DO something — schedule a meeting, take a call, meet your technical team, review a proposal. This is not being bossy; it is being professional.

When the prospect says "Just send me some information," respond:

> "I prefer not to send information. Let's just get together. How's Tuesday at two o'clock?"

### Always Move the Sale to the Next Step

The most difficult transition is getting the second or third meeting, not the first. At the end of every meeting:

> "What I'd like to do, instead of ending right now, is think about everything you've told me and look over all my notes. Over the next week, I'll put together an outline of what we might be able to do for you, and I'd like to come back and show you what our thinking is."

If the prospect catches the ball — they're playing ball. If they let it drop — you know where you stand. The real sin is not getting rejected; it is **not knowing where you stand**.

## 8. Pipeline Management and Ratios

### Know Your Ratios

Sales is not a "numbers game" — it is a **ratios game**. Track these obsessively:

| Ratio | What to Track |
|---|---|
| **Dials to discussions** | How many calls to reach one live person? |
| **Discussions to appointments** | How many conversations to get one meeting? |
| **First to second appointments** | How many first meetings advance? |
| **Visits to sales** | How many total meetings per closed deal? |
| **Average deal value** | What is each sale worth? |

### Pipeline Attrition Reality

Salespeople consistently overestimate their pipeline. Example:

```
Start with 20 prospects, closing ratio 1 in 5:
  Make 1 sale → Actually lose 5 prospects (1 became customer, 4 fell out)
  Remaining: 15 (NOT 19 as most salespeople think)

  Make sale #2 → Lose 5 more
  Remaining: 10 (NOT 18 as most think)
```

**You cannot ease up on prospecting after making a sale.** After closing, it is MORE essential than ever to replenish your prospect supply.

Daily activity must be driven by prospecting. Post this where visible: **"I need to prospect on a regular basis, and that should be the key to my plan."**

### Pipeline Activity Allocation

Spend proportionally more time on **early-stage prospects** (building new relationships) than on late-stage prospects (almost ready to close). The near-close deals need only one or two more touches; the early relationships need the investment.

### Don't Get Fooled by "Sure Things"

Beware of prospects who:

- Say all the right things but never commit to next steps
- Are enthusiastic in meetings but unresponsive between meetings
- Promise decisions that keep getting postponed
- Cannot or will not introduce you to the actual decision-maker

If a prospect is not playing ball, retreat and invest time elsewhere.

### Strategise Two Weeks in Advance

Plan your sales activities at least two weeks ahead:

- Which prospects need follow-up?
- Which need next meetings?
- Which are ready for proposals?
- Where are new appointments needed?

### Look Two Cars Ahead

Like driving, successful salespeople anticipate what's coming:

- Read industry journals that affect key industries in your prospect base
- Develop networks that keep you informed of emerging trends
- Anticipate objections and prepare responses before meetings
- Update sales materials and question sequences based on new information

## 9. Relationship Selling and Account Development

### The Four Levels of Sales Relationships

| Level | Role | Characteristics | Vulnerability |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Seller** | Peddler | Close on one factor (price, speed); no real relationship; everything short-term | Extremely high — lose customer the moment a competitor offers a better price |
| **2. Supplier** | Regular vendor | Ongoing purchases; slightly more information; still largely transactional | High — vulnerable to competitive shifts |
| **3. Vendor** | Trusted provider | Loyalty, trust, deeper relationship; advance warning of changes; chance to adjust | Moderate — have a relationship buffer |
| **4. Partner** | Strategic ally | Interdependent, mutually beneficial; shared planning process; input on strategic decisions; customer needs you as much as you need them | Low — deeply embedded in customer's operations |

**Goal:** Progress every significant account from seller to partner over time.

### How to Reach Partner Level

- Track down as much information as possible about the customer's company
- Learn about their challenges and goals at the level of their senior people
- Consistently develop ways to help them do what they do better
- Have contacts routinely request your input before making strategic decisions
- Participate in their annual planning process

### Act Like a Consultant (Because You Are One)

Position yourself as someone who solves problems, not someone who sells products:

- Diagnose before prescribing
- Ask questions before making recommendations
- Provide value even when not directly selling
- Share industry insights and relevant information freely

### Communicate That You Are Trustworthy

Trust is the foundation of all effective selling. Never use "tricks" that undermine credibility:

- If you sell cars, talk about cars — don't lure people with fake contests
- Never start a business relationship with deception
- Word gets around about dishonest tactics
- Credibility is too expensive to sacrifice for a quick sale

### Build Relationships Through Company Events

Use company events, social outings, and industry functions to move relationships forward. These are underutilised tools that create personal connections beyond the transactional.

### Ask for Referrals

Systematically ask satisfied customers for referrals:

- Ask after successful delivery or positive feedback
- Make it easy — ask for specific names and introductions
- Follow up on every referral promptly
- Report back to the referrer on the outcome

### Know When to Retreat

Not every prospect will become a customer. When someone drops out of the relationship:

- Don't play ego games — let it go
- Sometimes chemistry doesn't click; sometimes reasons are beyond your control
- Avoid "gerbil salesmanship" — going around and around without progress
- The objective is not to avoid losing a single battle, but to win the war
- When it's time to retreat, pick up the phone and start prospecting for new relationships

## 10. Follow-Up and Retention

### Follow-Up Letters and Notes

Write a thank-you note after every first meeting. Most salespeople ignore this. A brief, professional note:

- Keeps your "line taut" — the prospect remembers you
- Reinforces positive impressions from the meeting
- Differentiates you from competitors who don't bother

Example:

> "Dear Jill: Just wanted you to know what a pleasure it was to get to see your plant firsthand. I'll be dropping by with that proposal we discussed on Wednesday as scheduled. I'm quite certain we can work together to increase your widget production. Sincerely, Peter"

After the first meeting, even a spectacular one, the prospect will stop thinking about you within days. Without follow-up, you're relying on luck.

### Follow-Up Timing

| Stage | Follow-Up Action |
|---|---|
| After first meeting | Professional thank-you letter on company stationery |
| After major proposal | Thank-you note + summary of next steps |
| After decision to buy | Welcome and confirmation of onboarding plan |
| Ongoing customer | Periodic notes, relevant industry clippings, check-ins |

### Keep in Contact with Past Clients

Past clients are highly qualified leads. Maintain an organised file of inactive accounts and contact them periodically:

- Don't use hard-sell tactics — stay calm, friendly, and professional
- Remind them you're still delivering excellent results
- Check if they need your product or service again
- If they're not ready now, check back in a month or two

### Return Calls Within Twenty-Four Hours

Professional responsiveness builds trust. Never let a prospect or customer's call go unanswered for more than 24 hours.

### Follow the "Yes"

When a prospect says yes to something — a meeting, a concept, a proposal element — follow that momentum. Build on agreements rather than introducing new uncertainties.

### Raise Tough Issues Yourself

Don't wait for the prospect to bring up pricing, competitive concerns, or implementation challenges. By raising tough issues yourself:

- You demonstrate confidence and honesty
- You control the framing of the discussion
- You prevent surprises during the close
- You build trust by showing you have nothing to hide

## 11. Sales Mindset and Discipline

### Daily Discipline

- **Make a to-do list** — identify important objectives before the day starts
- **Start early** — arrive 45 minutes before everyone else for uninterrupted planning time
- **Block prospecting time** — protect it from interruptions
- **Plan the day efficiently** — allocate time to prospecting, meetings, follow-up, and preparation

### Maintain Motivation

- Find one new idea per month and implement it
- Use books, training materials, and seminars
- Keep your motivation fuelled — sales requires constant renewal of energy and ideas

### Don't Take Rejection Personally

Rejection is a normal, inevitable part of sales. It is not a reflection of your personal worth:

- Some prospects won't buy regardless of how good you are
- Focus on improving ratios, not eliminating rejection
- Every "no" brings you statistically closer to a "yes"

### Take Notes

Always take written notes during prospect meetings:

- It gives you the information you need for follow-up
- It encourages the prospect to open up (people share more when they see you writing)
- It raises the status of the prospect (they feel important)
- It prevents you from forgetting critical details

### Control the Flow

People respond in kind to what they hear. When you initiate a conversation, you control its direction:

- **Positive opening:** "Do you know what I love about your company?" — opens the prospect up
- **Negative opening:** "Let me tell you what needs changing" — makes them defensive

Plan your opening questions carefully. Anticipate the response and prepare your follow-up.

### Act Like an Equal

You are a professional providing a valuable service. Do not grovel, apologise for taking the prospect's time, or act subordinate. Equally, do not be arrogant or dismissive. Treat every interaction as a conversation between two professionals who may benefit from working together.

### Show Enthusiasm

Genuine enthusiasm for your product and your work is contagious. If you don't believe in what you sell, the prospect will sense it immediately.

### Tell the Truth

Honesty is not just ethical — it is practical. The truth is easier to remember than fabrications. Lies compound and eventually destroy credibility.

### Give Speeches to Business and Civic Groups

Public speaking positions you as an expert, creates brand awareness, and generates warm leads. This is one of the most underutilised sales tools available.

### Use E-mail Intelligently

Email is a supplement to personal contact, not a replacement. Use it for:

- Confirming meeting times and details
- Sharing brief, relevant information
- Following up after conversations
- Never as a substitute for a phone call or face-to-face meeting

- [ ] Deliver brief company introduction (PIPA: Present)
- [ ] Transition immediately to do-based question (PIPA: Interview)
- [ ] Listen more than you talk (aim for 70/30 prospect/you ratio)
- [ ] Take detailed written notes
- [ ] Explore past, present, and future
- [ ] Do NOT product dump — gather information
- [ ] Propose a specific next step with date and time (PIPA: Present option)
- [ ] Confirm agreement on next step (PIPA: Agreement)

### After the First Meeting
- [ ] Send a thank-you note within 24 hours
- [ ] Review and organise your notes
- [ ] Assess: Is this prospect "playing ball"?
- [ ] Begin preparing preliminary proposal or outline
- [ ] Schedule the next meeting in your calendar

### Before Presenting
- [ ] Verify all information via preliminary proposal
- [ ] Confirm you're talking to the decision-maker
- [ ] Confirm budget and pricing have been discussed
- [ ] Confirm implementation timetable is realistic
- [ ] Ensure prospect knows you expect to close
- [ ] Customise the presentation to their specific situation

### Closing
- [ ] Deliver presentation based on verified, customised information
- [ ] Ask: "It makes sense to me — does it make sense to you?"
- [ ] If "no" — take responsibility and ask where you went wrong
- [ ] Listen for "The thing is..." — capture the real obstacle
- [ ] Propose revised next steps based on new information

### Ongoing Pipeline Management
- [ ] Prospect every day without exception
- [ ] Track all ratios (dials, conversations, appointments, sales)
- [ ] Review and replenish pipeline weekly
- [ ] Revisit fallback prospects on a 3-month cycle
- [ ] Keep past clients in regular contact
- [ ] Ask for referrals from satisfied customers
- [ ] Plan activities at least two weeks in advance
