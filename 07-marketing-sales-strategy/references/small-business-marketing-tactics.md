## 1. Marketing Planning Framework (9-Step Plan)

Jarvis's 9-step marketing plan provides a practical framework for small businesses that lack formal marketing training. Each step maps to a section of the final written plan.

### The 9 Steps

```
Step 1: Setting Your Direction
  - Define what you are selling and where revenue streams come from
  - Identify what is most profitable and what your capacity is
  - Assess existing customer base: who, how many, best vs worst
  - Define longer-term vision and specific business goals

Step 2: Understanding Your Market and Competition
  - Market size, potential demand, trends, hot issues, gaps
  - Competitor strengths, weaknesses, and gaps in their offering
  - What benefits you offer that competitors do not

Step 3: Understanding Your Customer
  - Define ideal client criteria and target customer profiles
  - Identify main problems and needs of target customers
  - Determine where to find these customers and how they buy

Step 4: Creating Your Solution
  - Define the value proposition and key service elements
  - Articulate how the solution solves clients' problems
  - Define packaging, pricing, and guarantees

Step 5: Creating a Path to Your Business
  - Design lead generation and list-building systems
  - Define the steps up the sales ladder from first contact to long-term customer
  - Set up systems to capture and record prospect details

Step 6: Creating Your Marketing Message and Materials
  - Define brand image, name, strap-line, and key messages
  - Design marketing materials aligned to target market

Step 7: Choosing and Planning Marketing Methods
  - Evaluate past performance (what worked, what did not)
  - Select methods for profile building, new customer acquisition, and retention
  - Assess potential value of each channel for the specific business

Step 8: Developing Your Budget and ROI
  - Cost out each chosen marketing method
  - Define resources required and expected return per channel
  - Set measurement criteria for ROI

Step 9: Planning Your Strategy and Actions
  - Create critical path plan for each marketing goal
  - Set priorities, milestones, deadlines, and responsibilities
  - Define monitoring, evaluation, and review processes
```

### Marketing Plan Template Headings

```
1. Business description - who you are and what you do
2. Business direction - ambitions for the business
3. Sales targets - the numbers you want to reach
4. The marketplace - research, needs, trends, gaps, market growth
5. The competition - who they are, offerings, strengths, weaknesses
6. Target customer group(s) - who you are aiming at
7. Target area(s) - where you want business to come from
8. Niche - target specialist areas and business positioning
9. Key marketing messages - brand image, strap-line, differentiation
10. Marketing goals and objectives - what you want to achieve
11. Marketing methods - chosen methods to utilise
12. The budget - amount committed and spending plan
13. The strategy - how you are going to do it and calendar of actions
```

## 2. Marketing Foundations and Positioning

### Three Ways to Grow Any Business

Every marketing plan should address all three growth levers:

```
1. Get new customers
2. Increase the amount existing customers spend with you
3. Increase the frequency with which they do business with you
```

The existing customer base typically holds the most untapped potential. It is easier and less expensive to build on existing relationships than to develop new ones.

### Customer Lifetime Value Calculation

```
Number of customers x Average spend x Frequency per year = Annual value
Annual value x Average years as customer = Customer lifetime value

Example: 100 customers x GBP 1,000 x 3 times/year = GBP 300,000/year
GBP 300,000 x 2 years = GBP 600,000 total base value
Each new customer = GBP 6,000 potential lifetime value
```

### Niche Selection Criteria

A successful niche requires:
- A group of people with a common problem
- Demand for a solution coupled with ability and willingness to pay
- A large enough number of these people to support the business
- An easy-to-track-down target customer you can afford to contact
- Alignment with your own personal skills and strengths
- Experience within the niche and understanding of the market
- Others operating within the niche (indicating demand)
- Ability to offer something better, different, or more appealing

### Unique Selling Proposition (USP) Development

**Process for developing a USP:**
1. Find out the main problems or frustrations customers have with similar products/services
2. Identify what is special and unique about your business
3. Review testimonials for what satisfied customers repeatedly praise
4. Study competitor advertising and note the promises they make
5. Match what you do particularly well with problems people want solved
6. Choose benefits that make you stand out from the crowd
7. Pick elements that are specific and measurable (speed, quality, results)
8. Test USPs in advertising and monitor response

**USP SMART test:** The USP must be Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound.

**Examples:**
- Website design company: "A quick, easy, attractive website in a week"
- Building company: "The price quoted is the price you pay"
- Delivery service: "Fresh hot pizza in 30 minutes or less" (Domino's)

### Brand Identity Essentials

A brand is the memorable message created when a company successfully promotes the emotional connection it wants customers to have with the business. Even small businesses need a clear identity and memorability in their marketplace.

**Brand development checklist:**
- Define what emotions you want customers to associate with your business
- Ensure brand identity is a true reflection of your essence
- Communicate instantly with your ideal target audience
- Reflect the business you are in and be easy to understand
- Use consistently across all marketing and delivery materials

### Strap-Line Development

A strap-line communicates in a few words the essential value you offer.

**Effective strap-lines address customer values and promise outcomes:**
- Sainsbury's: "Making life taste better"
- Home Base: "Making a house a home"
- Norwich Union Direct: "Let us quote you happy"

**Key principle:** People buy outcomes, not products. Attach the fundamental outcomes customers seek to every marketing message:
- "Come and get your hair cut" vs "Come and transform your looks"
- "Open a savings account" vs "Plan a secure future"

### Elevator Speech Framework

A 60-second elevator speech structure:
1. Who you are and who you work with
2. What specific problems you solve
3. What results your customers get
4. What makes you different
5. A call to action or conversation opener

## 3. Customer Retention and Existing Customer Growth

### Communication System

A planned series of activities to maintain constant communication with existing customers:

**Elements of a communication system:**
- Monthly e-newsletter
- New product or service alert emails
- Annual or biannual customer review meeting
- Invitations to special events or hospitality days
- Christmas cards and gifts
- Free tickets to events
- Free useful information or learning opportunities

### Building Trusting Relationships

Trust is the foundation of long-term customer relationships. Trust-building actions:
- Be consistent and reliable in delivery
- Follow through on commitments
- Be transparent about challenges
- Communicate proactively, especially when there are problems
- Show genuine interest in customers as people, not just revenue sources
- Remember personal details and important dates

### Customer Needs Review Process

Regular customer reviews uncover upsell and cross-sell opportunities:

**Review meeting structure:**
1. Discuss how well current services are meeting their needs
2. Ask about changes in their business or life situation
3. Explore emerging problems or challenges
4. Present relevant new products or services
5. Agree on next steps and follow-up

### Encouraging Referrals and Recommendations

Referrals are the most cost-effective source of new business.

**How to generate more referrals:**
- Ask satisfied customers directly: "Who do you know who might benefit from what we do?"
- Time the ask to moments of peak satisfaction (after a successful delivery or positive feedback)
- Make it easy to refer (provide cards, links, or simple instructions)
- Thank and reward referrers promptly
- Keep referrers informed about outcomes
- Create a structured referral programme with clear incentives

**Testimonial collection process:**
- Ask for specific feedback after successful project completion
- During telephone surveys, ask if you can quote positive statements
- Use exact customer language in marketing materials
- Publish case studies showing how customers achieved goals through your product or service

### Upselling and Cross-Selling

**Selling up:** Encouraging customers to upgrade to higher-value products or services.
**Selling more:** Offering complementary products or additional services.

**How to sell up and sell more:**
- Review what each customer currently buys and identify gaps
- Bundle products into packages at attractive price points
- Offer tiered service levels (basic, standard, premium)
- Use customer review meetings to present additional offerings
- Train all customer-facing staff to identify and suggest relevant additions

### Product Packaging Strategies

Packaging products and services into clear bundles simplifies buying decisions:
- Create packages at different price points (good, better, best)
- Bundle complementary services together
- Offer starter packages for new customers
- Design annual or subscription packages for recurring revenue
- Name packages memorably and communicate clear value at each level

### Newsletter Best Practices

**Content mix for effective newsletters:**
- Useful tips and advice relevant to your audience
- Industry news and trends
- Customer success stories and case studies
- New product or service announcements
- Special offers exclusive to subscribers
- Upcoming events and opportunities

**Distribution:** Monthly is the most common frequency. Keep content concise, valuable, and consistent with brand messaging. Always include a call to action.

### Email Marketing Tactics

- Build an opt-in list (never buy lists)
- Segment your list by customer type, interest, or purchase history
- Personalise subject lines and content
- Test different subject lines and measure open rates
- Include a clear call to action in every email
- Track opens, clicks, and conversions
- Follow up email campaigns with telephone calls for maximum impact

### Hospitality and Special Events

Using events and hospitality to strengthen customer relationships:
- Host annual customer appreciation events
- Invite top customers to industry events or conferences
- Organise informal social gatherings (lunches, golf days, outings)
- Create exclusive learning events for customers (workshops, masterclasses)
- Use events as opportunities to introduce new products or services

## 4. Networking, Alliances, and Referrals

### Alliance Relationships

An alliance is a mutually beneficial relationship with a non-competing business that serves the same target customer.

**How to set up alliance relationships:**
1. List all things your customers might buy that are associated with your services
2. Identify contacts who provide complementary services
3. Offer a free experience of your service so alliance partners can evaluate quality
4. Set up meetings to explore the best way of working together
5. Formalise the arrangement with clear expectations and reciprocal commitments
6. Monitor and review the relationship regularly

**What makes alliances successful:**
- No competition between the businesses
- Services naturally complement each other
- Recommending your service adds value to the alliance's customer relationship
- Built on trust and rapport
- Both parties actively promote each other

### Joint Ventures

A joint venture is a collaborative marketing project between two or more businesses for mutual benefit, such as:
- Co-hosting events or workshops
- Creating joint products or service packages
- Sharing marketing costs for a campaign
- Cross-promoting to each other's customer bases
- Sharing exhibition or conference costs

### Word of Mouth Marketing

Word of mouth is the most powerful marketing tool, but it must be actively cultivated:

**How to stimulate word of mouth:**
- Deliver exceptional, talk-worthy service
- Create memorable customer experiences
- Ask satisfied customers to spread the word
- Make your business easy to talk about (clear USP, memorable name)
- Give customers stories to tell (remarkable events, generous gestures)
- Provide shareable content, offers, or tools
- Thank people who refer others to you

### Networking Strategy

**Networking for the right reasons:**
- Have a clear purpose for attending each event
- Prepare a concise, compelling elevator speech
- Focus on building relationships, not making immediate sales
- Listen more than you talk
- Follow up within 48 hours with new contacts
- Track contacts and outcomes in a CRM system
- Join 2-3 groups and attend consistently rather than many groups sporadically
- Offer to speak at networking events to position as an expert
- Always carry business cards with a compelling offer or message

**Networking follow-up process:**
1. Within 48 hours: Send a personal email or note referencing the conversation
2. Within 1 week: Connect on LinkedIn or appropriate social platform
3. Within 2 weeks: Offer something of value (article, introduction, resource)
4. Monthly: Stay in touch through newsletter or periodic check-in
5. Ongoing: Look for opportunities to refer business to them

### Education-Based Customer Acquisition

**Using education to attract customers:**
- Run free workshops or seminars on topics your target audience cares about
- Offer free consultations, health checks, or assessments as entry points
- Create free reports, guides, or tools that address common customer problems
- Speak at conferences and events in your industry
- Write articles for industry publications
- Position every educational interaction as a demonstration of expertise

**Examples of educational lead magnets:**
- Nutritionist: Free report "How to feel super charged" plus free health assessment
- Financial consultant: Free report "How to raise finance for your business venture"
- Property company: Free monthly property industry update with buying and selling tips
- Marketing consultant: "Nine steps to create a successful sales and marketing system"

## 5. PR and Profile Building

### Getting Free PR

PR provides natural third-party endorsement and is one of the most cost-effective marketing tools.

**PR channels:**
- Articles in industry press and websites (on and offline)
- TV and radio interviews
- Press releases to newspapers and magazines
- Speaking at events and conferences
- Charity event participation
- Sponsorship activities

**How to build a PR machine:**
1. Identify publications and websites your target audience reads
2. Find out what editors and feature lists are looking for
3. Offer article series to build continuous relationship with editors and readers
4. Research hot topics and offer unique angles backed by data
5. Contact programme researchers for TV and radio opportunities
6. Build relationships with editors and journalists before pitching
7. Measure results and notice what is getting published where

**Article writing tips:**
- "How to" titles and lists of tips are popular for gaining reader attention
- Back up articles with solid research data where possible
- Mention your position, qualifications, and expertise in author bio
- Offer a free report that requires readers to make contact (tracks response)
- Propose article series rather than one-off pieces for sustained visibility

### Press Release Formula

**What makes a press release succeed (the 1% that gets published):**
- Written from the journalist's perspective (newsworthy and of reader interest)
- Targeted towards audience needs or interests
- Creative, offering something different and original
- Properly written in journalistic style (short sentences, short paragraphs)
- Sent to the correct named journalist or editor
- Matched to the right publication

**Newsworthy press release categories:**
- Announcements: New products, management changes, mergers, partnerships
- Statements: Positions on industry issues, new legislation
- Events: Company-hosted events, trade shows, speaking engagements
- Customer stories: Case studies showing customer success
- Accomplishments: Milestones, awards, new sales levels, expansions
- Employees: Achievements, community service, good deeds
- Charity: Involvement in charitable events and causes

**Press release structure:**
```
PRESS RELEASE (bold, top of page)
To: Editor's name, title of publication
From: Your name, position, company
For immediate release

Subject: [Attention-grabbing one-line headline]
Summary: [One or two sentences explaining what the release is about]
[Body: Most important information first - not the complete story, just the story idea]
[Organisation background paragraph]
Attachments: [Mention relevant pictures]
Contact: [Full contact details]

Keep to one page maximum.
```

### Becoming a Well-Known Expert

**How to build expert status:**
- Describe expertise in terms of customer problems solved
- Build a portfolio of testimonials and references
- Work in a targeted, narrow field solving specific problems for specific people
- Get a book published sharing your expertise
- Write articles for business publications and websites
- Get on TV or radio shows as the resident expert
- Run open events and seminars in your area of expertise
- Join associations and professional institutes
- Speak at conferences
- Build word of mouth and referral marketing
- Give ideas away through free newsletters or information sheets

### Sponsorship Strategy

**Sponsorship planning for maximum return:**
1. Define what you want to achieve (profile, education, leads, loyalty)
2. Identify events that match your target audience's interests and values
3. Know your brand values and choose events that enhance them
4. Research sponsorship opportunities and audience profiles
5. Plan how to integrate sponsorship into current marketing activities
6. Decide on budget and measurement criteria
7. Get team buy-in and involvement

### Banners, Posters, and Signs

Effective signage is about location and clear communication:
- Vehicle graphics maximise advertising during daily business travel
- Builders' boards and site banners capture local attention during projects
- Exhibition banners and posters communicate at indoor and outdoor events
- Bus stop posters, roundabout sponsorship, and billboard advertising target commuters
- Clear, well-designed, brand-representative signage for business premises

## 6. Direct Sales and Selling Services

### Cold Call Opening Framework

**Warm the cold call:**
- Send a letter or email of introduction first, then follow up by phone
- Think of calls as research, not selling
- Find the decision maker's name in advance with a separate call

**Call structure:**
```
1. Introduction: "Good morning [name], my name is [you] from [company].
   I understand you are the person who manages [relevant area].
   Have I called at a convenient time?"

2. Purpose: "I am calling to follow up a letter I sent / We work with
   [target market] helping them [solve problem]. I don't know if this
   is something you are exploring, but I wanted to find out."

3. Qualify: Ask 2-3 questions to establish if they are a prospect
   - Do they currently use similar products/services?
   - Are they experiencing the challenges you address?
   - Are they open to meeting?

4. Close: Arrange appropriate follow-up
   - An appointment
   - A longer telephone consultation
   - A CRM note to call back at a future date
   - Confirm follow-up by email
```

### Appointment-Making Strategies

- Use telephone prospecting with a warm introduction
- Leverage networking events for face-to-face appointment setting
- Offer free consultations, demonstrations, or health checks as entry points
- Make the appointment recommendation sound like an opportunity for the prospect

**Examples of free entry-point offers:**
- Marketing consultant: Free one-hour marketing consultation
- Financial consultant: Free personal finance assessment with report
- Health and safety consultant: Free health and safety check with report
- Website company: Free "How well marketed is your site?" report

### Building Rapport in Sales

- Start conversations by finding common ground
- Share something appropriate about yourself as a person
- Put yourself in their shoes and consider their outcomes
- Get genuinely interested in their life, challenges, and goals
- Be open-minded and find something to like about each person
- Care genuinely about helping them succeed
- Be aware of how they respond to you
- Match body language and voice tone

### The Art of Listening in Sales

- Give the speaker full and undivided attention
- Prepare yourself mentally before the meeting (clear your head)
- Arrange a place free from distractions
- Ask relevant questions and summarise regularly to check understanding
- Notice and feed back the speaker's language
- Match the energy, tone, and pace of the speaker's voice
- Hear beyond words: reality, concerns, opinions, beliefs, feelings, desires, needs

### Finding the Prospect's Pain

**Key discovery questions:**
- "What is most important that you find out about this?"
- "Why is that most important to you?"
- "When choosing [service] what matters most?"
- "What problems have you been experiencing?"
- "What has motivated your need to find a solution?"
- "What has been most challenging for you?"
- "How do you hope we can help you?"
- "What are your timescales?"

### Tailoring Sales Propositions

1. Summarise the prospect's present problems and desired outcomes
2. Use their language, not yours
3. Get full agreement on their issues before presenting solutions
4. Connect each need with a specific solution description
5. Project into the future by relating the solution to the bigger picture
6. Share relevant examples of similar customers achieving good results
7. Make recommendations confidently

### Winning Proposal Structure

```
1. Introduction - Reference last meeting, overview of proposal content
2. Your Situation - Summary of what you established was important (their language)
3. Your Objectives - Bullet point exactly what they told you
4. Your Solution - Solution broken down with clear key elements
5. Value - What is in it for them (results, not processes)
6. Your Price - Charges and price assumptions, include guarantees
7. Your Plan - Suggested targets, timescales, calendar of actions
8. Your Next Step - Agreed follow-up
9. Sign Off - Positive statement about working together

Keep to 2-3 pages. Follow up within one week. Never do proposals on speculation.
```

### Follow-Up System

Following up sales letters or direct mail increases response by 20-50%.

**Follow-up principles:**
- Always ask if it is convenient to talk
- Remind of the last contact
- Have full details of previous contact in front of you
- Connect with the rapport from the last meeting
- Be disciplined and carry on until there is a conclusion
- Do not pester (do not call and leave messages every day)
- Maintain control by calling back rather than asking them to call you
- Keep an up-to-date customer management system

## 7. Digital and Online Marketing

### Driving Business to Your Website

**Website marketing essentials:**
- Ensure the website is easy to navigate and clearly communicates what you sell
- Include strong calls to action on every page
- Feature your USP prominently on the home page
- Provide valuable content that attracts and retains visitors
- Capture visitor details through opt-in offers (free reports, newsletters)
- Optimise for search engines (SEO)
- Submit to Google and set up Google Ads campaigns
- Build quality inbound links from relevant websites
- Track visitor behaviour and conversion rates

### Building an Opt-In Mailing List

**The opt-in list is the most valuable marketing asset a small business can build.**

**List building tactics:**
1. Create a compelling free offer (report, newsletter, consultation, sample)
2. Develop a title that promises a benefit and suggests important learning
3. Place lead generation messages where your target customers are:
   - Professional association newsletters and magazines
   - Networking group websites
   - Business club forums and online discussion groups
   - Google adverts and classified advertising
   - Targeted complementary business e-zines
   - Your own website prominently
4. Offer free tips to e-zines in return for promoting your offer
5. Provide free ideas and advice on online discussion forums in your area of expertise

**Lead generation message formula:**
```
[Question that identifies the prospect's problem]
[Promise of a free valuable resource]
[Simple action to take]

Example:
"Do you need a more consistent flow of new customers?
Get your free report: Nine steps to create a successful sales and
marketing system for your business.
Download your copy from [website]"
```

### Email Marketing Best Practices

- Send only to opt-in subscribers
- Deliver genuine value in every communication
- Keep content concise and actionable
- Include clear calls to action
- Test subject lines and content formats
- Track opens, clicks, and conversions
- Combine email with telephone follow-up for maximum impact
- Segment lists by customer type and interest

### Typical Small Business Marketing Mistakes to Avoid

From Jarvis's common-mistakes checklist:
- No plan: haphazard activities
- Too busy doing business to develop business
- Jumping from one failed idea to another without analysis
- Marketing plan on a post-it note or only in your head
- Untargeted attempts to generate sales
- Not testing and measuring to know what works
- Trying to do too much too quickly
- Wasting money repeating what does not work
- Poor decisions based on sales pitches rather than evidence
- Letting fear paralyse positive action
- Mixed messages with no coherent theme
- Over-complicating marketing activities and messages
- Working very hard without matching results
- Relying on only one or two methods of generating business
- Suffering feast and famine revenue cycles
- Keeping poor records of prospects and customers
- Not being able to explain why someone should buy from you

### Special Offers and Incentives

- Time-limited offers create urgency
- Introductory offers lower the barrier for first-time customers
- Bundle offers increase average transaction value
- Loyalty rewards encourage repeat purchase
- Referral incentives reward existing customers for bringing new ones
- Seasonal promotions align with customer buying patterns

### Window Displays and Physical Presence

For retail and premises-based businesses:
- Change displays regularly to attract repeat attention
- Feature specific products or promotions prominently
- Use clear pricing and benefit-focused messaging
- Ensure the display reflects brand identity
- Make it obvious what you are selling from the street

## 9. Marketing Systems and Processes

### Customer Record Keeping (CRM)

**Essential customer record fields:**
- Full name, company name, address
- Telephone, mobile, fax, email, website
- Birthday and personal information (as appropriate)
- Business information and work/client history
- Samples of work
- Spending patterns and purchase history

**CRM discipline:**
- Ask for full customer details at point of sale
- Update records at least once a year (use as communication opportunity)
- Enter data daily rather than letting it build up
- Ensure correct spelling and complete details
- Give customers a good reason for collecting their details

### Communication Calendar Template

A 12-month schedule of all marketing touches to customers:

```
January:   Newsletter - New Year sale - website promotion
February:  Newsletter - new products update
March:     Newsletter - seasonal promotion - website promotion
April:     Newsletter - telecommunication customer record update
May:       Newsletter - annual customer review - website promotion
June:      Newsletter - conference email/direct mail
July:      Newsletter - special hospitality event - website promotion
August:    Newsletter - conference email/direct mail
September: Newsletter - telecommunication - website promotion
October:   Newsletter - conference - customer satisfaction survey
November:  Newsletter - Christmas gift ideas email - website promotion
December:  Newsletter - Christmas card - Christmas party
```

**Calendar design principles:**
- Plan each touch to support and link with others
- Consider separate calendars for existing customers vs prospects
- Design individual nurturing calendars for high-value prospects
- Review from the customer's perspective: not too much, not too little
- Ensure each touch delivers value, not just promotion

### Monthly Marketing Meeting Structure

```
1. Highlights of the month
2. What marketing activities have we implemented this month?
3. How well have they worked - what is our evidence?
4. What could we fine tune or improve?
5. What have we got planned for next month?
6. Allocation of tasks and responsibilities
7. Marketing goals review
8. Action plan for the month
```

### Marketing Evaluation System

**Test and measure every marketing activity:**
- Ask enquirers where they heard about the business
- Keep records of response data over defined periods
- Run specific offers in selected channels and track responses
- Test headlines in smaller batches before scaling
- Track contacts and business from networking events
- Offer free reports in articles to track reader response
- Use before-and-after sales data for brand awareness campaigns
- Calculate cost per lead and cost per acquisition per channel
- Stop any activity costing more than the return it generates
- Repeat all successful activities

### Complaint Handling System

**Complaints are gifts of learning. A system for handling them:**
1. Listen, understand exactly what the customer is dissatisfied with, and apologise
2. Offer a solution that exceeds expectations and puts a smile on their face
3. Give the customer a choice of solutions (restores their sense of control)
4. Write a letter of apology or make a personal director-level phone call
5. Record in a complaints log: date, customer details, complaint, cause, solution, and system changes
6. Learn from complaints and make changes to prevent recurrence

**Key principle:** One complaint shared with 20 other people becomes negative marketing very quickly. A complaint handled well can strengthen the customer relationship.

```
  - Achieve [number] quality website links
```

### Budget Estimation Method

```
If you have been in business for over a year:
1. Total up all marketing-related expenditure
2. Count new customers acquired (or units sold)
3. Divide expenditure by customers (or units) = cost per acquisition
4. Multiply acquisition cost by next year's customer goal = estimated budget needed
5. Compare budget to projected revenue to check percentage is realistic
```

### Strategy Critical Path Template

```
For each marketing goal:
1. Draw a timeline from now to goal completion
2. Break time period into sections (e.g., quarters or months)
3. Create a specific sub-goal for each time section
4. For each sub-goal, answer:
   - What needs to happen to achieve this?
   - What must be achieved before moving on?
   - What needs to happen by when?
   - How long will things take?
   - What resources are needed vs available?
5. Plan steps in critical order
6. Assign responsibilities and deadlines
```

## Key Principles Summary

1. **Plan before acting.** A simple marketing plan beats haphazard activity every time.
2. **Know your numbers.** Test, measure, and track everything.
3. **Existing customers are gold.** Retention and growth from existing customers is cheaper and faster than acquisition.
4. **Niche down.** Specialists win over generalists in trust, pricing power, and referrals.
5. **Communicate value, not features.** People buy outcomes and solutions to problems.
6. **Build systems, not campaigns.** Communication calendars, CRM discipline, and follow-up processes compound over time.
7. **Referrals and alliances are the highest-ROI channels.** Invest time in building these relationships.
8. **PR is free advertising.** Articles, press releases, and expert positioning generate credibility that money cannot buy.
9. **Make it easy to buy.** Simplify every step from first contact to purchase and beyond.
10. **Stay consistent.** Marketing works if you work at it. Consistent mediocre marketing beats sporadic brilliant marketing.
