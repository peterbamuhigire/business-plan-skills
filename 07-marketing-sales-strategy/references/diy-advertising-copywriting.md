# DIY Advertising and Copywriting: Production Handbook

**Source:** *Do It Yourself Advertising and Promotion: How to Produce Great Ads, Brochures, Catalogs, Direct Mail, Web Sites, and More!* — Fred E. Hahn with Kenneth G. Mangun (3rd ed., 2003, John Wiley & Sons).

**Key theme:** A campaign-level production handbook for small business owners and marketing managers who must create professional advertising without an agency. Every chapter covers both the strategy ("why") and the craft ("how to actually make it"). Unlike strategic frameworks covered elsewhere in this suite, this reference focuses on the mechanics of writing and producing individual advertising pieces — from headline construction to radio script timing to direct mail package assembly.

## 1. The Advertising Contract

Hahn frames every advertisement as a legally and morally binding promise to the prospect. Before producing any creative work, the advertiser must define three contract elements:

| Element | Question to answer | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **What you promise** | What is the specific benefit being offered? | "You will harvest 40% more water per acre." |
| **To whom** | Who is the clearly identified prospect? | "Smallholder farmers in Wakiso and Mukono districts." |
| **Under what conditions** | What are the offer terms, proof, and call to action? | "Free installation assessment this month — call 0700 123 456." |

An ad that breaks any element of the contract will underperform regardless of creative quality. The most common violations in SME advertising are: (a) a "what" that is vague or feature-led rather than benefit-led; (b) a "to whom" that is too broad ("everyone"); and (c) conditions that are either absent (no call to action) or too complex (multiple steps, unclear expiry).

## 2. The Five-Step Message Hierarchy (AIDA + Conviction)

Hahn extends the classic AIDA model with a fifth step — **Conviction** — that most SME advertisers omit entirely. Every piece of copy should carry the prospect through all five steps in order.

| Step | Function | Common failure mode |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Attention** | Stop the prospect — headline, visual, opening line | Weak or generic headline; company name leads instead of benefit |
| **2. Interest** | Make them want to read on — frame a relevant problem or desire | Opening talks about the company, not the reader's situation |
| **3. Desire** | Build the case that your product solves the problem | Lists features without translating them to benefits |
| **4. Conviction** | Proof that the benefit claim is true — testimonials, credentials, guarantees, data | Step skipped entirely; assertions left unproven |
| **5. Action** | A specific, low-friction next step | Vague close ("contact us"); no deadline; phone number buried |

**Diagnosis table — where SME ads most commonly fail:**

| Symptom | Likely failure step | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Ad is read but generates no enquiries | Step 5 — weak or absent CTA | Add a specific, time-bound action with one contact method |
| Prospects say "sounds good" but don't buy | Step 4 — no conviction/proof | Add a testimonial, guarantee, or verifiable statistic |
| Ad is ignored | Step 1 — headline fails to stop | Rewrite headline using benefit, number, or "you" (see Section 4) |
| Prospects engage but lose interest quickly | Step 2 — interest not established | Open with reader's problem, not your company description |
| Prospects understand features but not relevance | Step 3 — desire not built | Apply Benefit-Feature-Proof structure (Section 3) |

## 3. Benefit-Feature-Proof Structure

For every product or service attribute, copy should be constructed in three layers:

- **Feature** — what it *is* (the ingredient, specification, or mechanism)
- **Benefit** — what the feature *does for the customer* (the outcome they actually want)
- **Proof** — why the customer should believe the benefit claim (evidence, test, credential, guarantee)

**Hahn's rule:** never lead with a feature. Always lead with the benefit. Reserve features for the supporting body copy — they provide credibility, not motivation.

### Worked Uganda Example — Solar Water Pump

| Layer | Statement |
|---|---|
| **Feature** | "Solar-powered water pump with 200-litre holding tank and food-grade hosing." |
| **Benefit** | "Clean water in your home every day — even during grid outages and dry-season water shortages." |
| **Proof** | "Installed in 340 households in Wakiso district. Zero maintenance call-backs in 18 months. Certificate of compliance from National Water and Sewerage Corporation." |

**Application in a business plan:** When writing the Products and Services section (Skill 03) or the marketing copy within the Marketing Strategy section (Skill 07), structure each product description using Benefit → Feature → Proof rather than a product specification sheet. Lenders and investors respond to benefit-led language; customers buy for the same reason.

## 4. Copywriting Rules and Headline Tactics

### Headlines

- **Use numbers:** "7 ways to cut your fuel bill by 30%" outperforms generic claims by 40–60% in direct response split tests. Numbers signal specificity and create expectation.
- **Use "you" or "your":** The word *you* is the most powerful word in advertising copy. "You could save UGX 200,000 a month on fuel" outperforms "Our system saves fuel."
- **Make a specific promise, not a vague boast:** "Grows your hair in 30 days or money back" beats "The leading hair solution." Specificity implies testability; vague boasts imply nothing can be verified.
- **News-format headlines consistently win in direct response:** "Introducing...", "At last...", "Now..." signal new information and outperform clever or witty headlines in measured tests.

### Body Copy

- **Write the way your best salesperson talks.** Read every sentence aloud. If you would not say it in conversation, cut it.
- **Short paragraphs:** 3–5 lines maximum. One idea per paragraph. White space is a design element, not wasted space.
- **Use the "so what?" test:** After every feature claim, ask: *"So what does this mean for the reader?"* Then answer it in the next sentence. If you cannot answer it, cut the claim.
- **Subheads every 4–6 paragraphs:** Skimmers (the majority) read the headline, subheads, captions, and the PS. These elements must tell the whole story independently of the body copy.
- **Bold and underline sparingly:** Mark only the single most important phrase per section — the more you emphasise, the less anything stands out.

## 5. Print Ad Production Checklist

Use this checklist before submitting any print advertisement (newspaper, magazine, flyer, billboard) for production.

| # | Check |
|---|---|
| 1 | Headline states the benefit, not the product category or company name |
| 2 | Subhead expands the headline promise with a secondary reason to read on |
| 3 | Body copy written in second person ("you", "your") throughout |
| 4 | One central visual that reinforces the headline — not a collage |
| 5 | Logo and address are small and positioned at the bottom |
| 6 | White space is used deliberately — the ad does not feel cluttered |
| 7 | Call to action is specific: one phone number, URL, or location with hours |
| 8 | Ad tested at thumbnail size (25mm × 35mm) — is the headline still readable? |

## 6. Radio Script Checklist

### Word Count Rule

| Spot length | Maximum word count |
|---|---|
| 30 seconds | 75 words |
| 60 seconds | 150 words |

Read the script aloud with a stopwatch. Do not rely on word count alone — pacing varies. The 75/150 word rule is a guide; the stopwatch test is the final authority.

### Checklist

| # | Check |
|---|---|
| 1 | Business name mentioned at least three times — beginning, middle, and end |
| 2 | Single central message only — no lists of benefits or feature catalogues |
| 3 | Local references included where possible (street names, cultural touchpoints) |
| 4 | Call to action repeated at least twice |
| 5 | Script read aloud and timed against the word count rule above |
| 6 | Humour avoided unless the product category specifically calls for it |
| 7 | Spot ends with phone number or URL stated slowly and repeated |

### Uganda FM Station Reference

| Station | Signal area | Primary audience | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| KFM 93.3 | Greater Kampala | English-speaking urban adults | Premium rates; high SME and professional audience |
| Capital FM 91.3 | Kampala / Entebbe corridor | English / upmarket | Strong on financial and lifestyle programming |
| CBS FM 88.8 | Central Uganda | Luganda-speaking urban and peri-urban | Largest single-language listenership in Buganda region |
| Radio One 90.0 | Nationwide | Mixed English/vernacular | Strong news credibility; government and NGO audiences |
| Bukedde FM | Central / Eastern | Luganda mass market | Tabloid positioning; high reach for FMCG and agri products |
| Radio Simba 97.3 | Kampala and surrounds | Luganda urban | Strong entertainment and lifestyle; youth-leaning |

**Note:** Radio reaches approximately 73% of Ugandan adults versus approximately 30% for television — the most efficient broadcast medium for SME advertising budgets. Regional vernacular stations serve upcountry districts at significantly lower rates than Kampala stations.

## 7. Direct Mail Package Architecture

A full direct mail package has five components. Each must earn the reader's attention before the next is opened or read.

| Component | Function | Uganda / Digital Equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| **Outer envelope** | The first sale — must earn the open. Teaser copy or a personalised name increases open rate. | SMS preview line (first 160 characters); WhatsApp message subject line |
| **Sales letter** | The primary selling document; follows the full five-step hierarchy. This is the most important component. | WhatsApp broadcast message body; bulk SMS body text |
| **Brochure** | Visual proof — features, specifications, photographs, testimonials. Supplements but never replaces the letter. | WhatsApp image/PDF attachment; link to product page or Facebook catalogue |
| **Order form / reply card** | The action device. Must restate the full offer independently — do not assume the reader has read the letter. | Reply "YES" to this number; click link; M-Pesa to till 123456 |
| **Lift note** | A short message from a second authority (a satisfied customer, a partner, a credentialled expert). Optional but increases response. | A forwarded WhatsApp message from a known referrer; a screenshot testimonial |

**The letter-first rule:** When testing or improving a direct mail campaign, change the letter before changing any other component. The letter drives more of the response than any other element. Improving the outer envelope, brochure, or lift note with a weak letter will not meaningfully improve response rates.

## 8. Direct Mail Letter Checklist

| # | Check |
|---|---|
| 1 | Opens with the reader's problem or desire — not the company name or history |
| 2 | Letter is personalised ("Dear [First Name]" or "Dear Fellow Business Owner") |
| 3 | First paragraph contains the core offer or a compelling teaser |
| 4 | Guarantee is prominently stated — not buried in terms and conditions |
| 5 | Bold and underlines used sparingly to guide skimmers to key phrases |
| 6 | Reply device (order form, CTA) is self-contained — all offer details repeated |
| 7 | PS is used as a second headline — it is the second most-read element after the opening |
| 8 | PS is set in a different font or handwritten style to signal it was added personally |

**PS rule:** Never omit the PS in a sales letter. Research consistently shows the PS is read immediately after the salutation by a large proportion of readers who skip the body copy. It should restate the strongest single benefit or the deadline, not add new information.

## 9. Trade Show Booth Checklist

| # | Check |
|---|---|
| 1 | Company name is clearly visible from 10 metres — in the largest type on the booth |
| 2 | A single tagline or message — not a product list or features catalogue |
| 3 | All booth staff briefed on three opening questions to ask every visitor (qualify before pitching) |
| 4 | Lead capture system is in place and tested before the show opens (card bowl, tablet form, or printed capture sheet) |
| 5 | Literature rack is physically separated from the conversation zone — do not let brochures substitute for dialogue |
| 6 | A show-exclusive offer or incentive is clearly posted (discounts, free consultations, prize draw) |
| 7 | Pre-show invitations have been sent to existing clients with your booth number and a reason to visit |
| 8 | Post-show follow-up plan is fully prepared before the show opens — scripts, email sequences, call schedule |

### Uganda Trade Fair Examples

| Event | Organiser | Timing | Audience |
|---|---|---|---|
| Uganda International Trade Fair | UEPB / UMA | October annually (Lugogo, Kampala) | Buyers, importers, distributors, government procurement |
| Kampala City Festival | KCCA | October annually | Consumer audience; SME stalls; high footfall |
| Agribusiness Forum | MAAIF / NARO | Variable | Agri input suppliers, farmers, cooperatives |
| East Africa Trade and Investment Hub expos | USAID EATIH | Variable | Export-ready SMEs; international buyers |

Apply the pre/during/post three-phase system: send invitations with booth number at least two weeks before; operate the booth with a qualified lead capture system; follow up within 48 hours of the show closing.

## 10. Testing Hierarchy

### Hahn's Five-Level Testing Hierarchy

Test variables in this order — higher levels produce larger swings in response rate and should therefore be tested before lower levels.

| Level | Variable | Typical response swing | Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | The **list** (who you contact) | 300–500% | Mailing the wrong audience cannot be fixed by better copy |
| **2** | The **offer** (what you promise and at what price/terms) | 100–300% | A stronger offer beats better creative every time |
| **3** | The **headline** / outer envelope copy | 50–150% | The first three seconds determine whether anything else is read |
| **4** | The **format** (self-mailer vs. package; postcard vs. letter; SMS vs. WhatsApp) | 20–80% | Format affects attention and perceived value |
| **5** | The **body copy** and creative | 10–30% | Copy refinement matters least until levels 1–4 are optimised |

### One-Variable Rule

Never change two variables simultaneously in a test. If you change the offer and the headline at the same time and response rate improves, you will not know which change caused the improvement — and you cannot replicate or scale it.

### Break-Even and CPO Formulas

```
Break-even response rate = Cost per piece mailed ÷ Revenue per response

Example (Uganda SMS campaign):
  Cost per SMS: UGX 50
  Revenue per conversion: UGX 80,000
  Break-even rate: 50 ÷ 80,000 = 0.0625% (625 responses per 1,000,000 sent)

Target CPO (cost per order) rule:
  CPO should not exceed 30–40% of customer lifetime value (CLV)

  Example: If a customer is worth UGX 500,000 over their lifetime,
  your acceptable cost per acquired customer = UGX 150,000–200,000
```

## 11. Direct Mail Lists

### House List vs. Rented List

| List type | Expected response rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **House list** (your own customers and enquirers) | 3–10× higher than rented | Your most valuable marketing asset. Must be maintained, segmented, and protected. |
| **Rented list** (third-party names) | 0.5–2% typical | Quality varies enormously. Always test before full rollout. |

### RFM Segmentation

Segment the house list by three dimensions to allocate offers and budget:

- **Recency** — how recently did they buy or enquire? (most predictive)
- **Frequency** — how many times have they bought?
- **Monetary value** — what is their average transaction or total spend?

Best customers (high R, high F, high M) should receive your strongest offers and personalised communications. Do not send the same message to lapsed customers and active high-value customers.

### Hotline Names

When renting a list, always request **hotline names** — prospects who have responded to a similar offer within the past 90 days. Hotline names are significantly more responsive than the overall list average because they have demonstrated recent purchase intent.

### Test Before Rollout

Test a minimum of 1,000 names from any new list before committing to a full rollout. At a 1% response rate, 1,000 names will yield approximately 10 responses — sufficient to calculate a break-even and estimate rollout viability.

## 12. Yellow Pages and Google My Business

The principles Hahn established for Yellow Pages display advertising apply directly to Google My Business (GMB) and Uganda Yellow Pages online listings. The medium has changed; the psychology of the directory-searcher has not.

### Display Ad and GMB Profile Rules

| Rule | Yellow Pages original | GMB / Online equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| **Lead with a person, not a product** | Use a photo of a real person (staff or owner) — increases readership and trust | Profile photo should show the owner or staff, not just a logo |
| **USP in the first headline** | Your competitive advantage in the first line of the display ad | First line of business description; keyword-rich and benefit-led |
| **Hours of operation** | List all trading hours prominently | Verified opening hours — update for public holidays |
| **Colour discipline** | Use a second colour only for the border and the headline — colour in body copy reduces readability | Use cover photo and post images consistently; avoid visual clutter |
| **Test and revise** | Biggest mistake: running the same ad 12 months without testing any element | Update GMB posts, photos, and offers regularly; respond to all reviews |

### The 10 Deadliest Yellow Pages Mistakes (Barry Maher, cited in Hahn)

1. Using your company name as the headline
2. Listing features instead of benefits
3. Omitting the call to action entirely
4. Making the ad too busy — trying to say everything
5. Using a logo as the dominant visual
6. Listing a phone number only — no address or hours
7. Assuming all prospects know what you do
8. Using industry jargon
9. Failing to match the ad to the category the customer searches under
10. Renewing the same ad year after year without measurement

### Uganda GMB Equivalents

For Uganda SMEs, the GMB profile combined with Facebook Page and WhatsApp Business forms the functional equivalent of the Yellow Pages display ad ecosystem. The same rules apply: a clear benefit headline, a person in the profile photo, hours of operation, a specific offer or CTA, and regular updating.

## 13. Website Copy Principles

These principles were written for the 2003 web era but remain accurate for any digital presence where text is the primary selling medium.

| Principle | Application |
|---|---|
| **Above-the-fold benefit + CTA** | The benefit statement and the primary call to action must be visible without scrolling. Do not make the visitor hunt for what you offer or what to do next. |
| **Benefit-centred navigation** | Label navigation by what the visitor gets ("Save on fuel costs", "Book a free assessment") rather than by company structure ("Products", "About Us", "Services"). |
| **One clear next step per page** | Each page should have exactly one primary CTA. Multiple competing CTAs (call us / email us / download this / follow us) divide attention and reduce total conversion. |
| **Email capture with value exchange** | If collecting email addresses or WhatsApp numbers, offer something of genuine value in return — a price guide, a calculation tool, a free consultation. "Subscribe to our newsletter" is not a value exchange. |

## 14. Uganda / East Africa Media Mapping Table

| Hahn Principle | Uganda / East Africa Application |
|---|---|
| **House list beats rented lists** | A mobile number list built from M-Pesa transactions, MTN/Airtel loyalty programmes, or WhatsApp opt-ins is your most valuable marketing asset. Protect and segment it using RFM logic. |
| **Radio is the most cost-efficient broadcast medium for SMEs** | FM radio reaches approximately 73% of Ugandan adults versus approximately 30% for television. Apply the radio script checklist to Kampala stations (KFM, CBS, Capital FM) and regional vernacular stations for upcountry audiences. |
| **Trade show pre/during/post system** | Kampala trade fairs (UEPB/UMA annual fair at Lugogo, KCCA City Festival, agribusiness forums) follow the same three-phase system Hahn recommends. Send personalised invitations with booth numbers; capture leads on the day; follow up within 48 hours. |
| **Direct mail package** | Physical mail in Uganda is slow and unreliable for SME use. Substitute WhatsApp broadcast lists and bulk SMS — the same five-component AIDA architecture applies. The "letter" becomes the WhatsApp message body; the "brochure" becomes a PDF or image attachment; the "order form" becomes a reply keyword or M-Pesa till number. |
| **Yellow Pages equivalent** | Google My Business listing plus Facebook Page plus Uganda Yellow Pages online. Apply the same display-ad rules: benefit headline, person photo, hours of operation, specific CTA, and regular updates. |
| **Testing before full rollout** | Uganda's digital advertising environment allows micro-testing via Facebook Ads with budgets of UGX 20,000–50,000 (approximately USD 5–14) before committing to print, radio, or bulk SMS spend. Test the offer and the headline before scaling. |
| **Guarantee as a differentiator** | An explicit money-back or free-replacement guarantee is rare in the Uganda SME market. A clearly and prominently stated guarantee — supported by the Conviction step of the five-step hierarchy — is a genuine competitive differentiator in most product and service categories. |
