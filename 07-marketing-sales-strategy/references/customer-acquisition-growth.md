# Customer Acquisition & Growth Frameworks

> Based on Blank, S. & Dorf, B. (2012). *The Startup Owner's Manual*; Kagan, N. (2024). *Million Dollar Weekend*.

## Get/Keep/Grow Customer Relationships Framework

Three phases of customer relationship management (Blank & Dorf, 2012):

### Getting Customers (Acquisition + Activation)

**Physical channel funnel:**
Awareness → Interest → Consideration → Purchase

**Web/mobile funnel:**
Acquire → Activate → Convert

**Acquisition channels (earned vs. paid):**

| Channel Type | Examples | Cost | Speed |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Earned media** | Word of mouth, press, social sharing, SEO | Low/free | Slow to build |
| **Paid media** | Facebook/Google ads, radio, print, billboards | Variable | Fast but stops when you stop paying |
| **Viral** | Referral programmes, sharing features, network effects | Low | Exponential if product fits |

**Three types of viral marketing:**
1. **Word of mouth** — customers tell others because the product is remarkable
2. **Sharing** — the product has built-in sharing (e.g., "Sent from my iPhone")
3. **Direct network effects** — the product becomes more valuable as more people use it (e.g., WhatsApp)

**Activation is the choke point** (Blank & Dorf, 2012). Many businesses acquire visitors but fail to activate them. For landing pages, test these five questions:
1. Why am I here? (clear headline)
2. What makes it special? (value proposition)
3. How do I know it's good? (social proof)
4. Where do I get more info? (details without overwhelming)
5. What next? (clear call to action)

### Keeping Customers (Retention)

Track individual customer behaviour over time:
- Dwindling visits, page views, or time spent = churn warning
- Use cohort analysis by customer tenure (month 1 vs. month 6 behaviour)
- Retention programmes: loyalty rewards, regular communication, personalised outreach
- Blogs, newsletters, WhatsApp groups for ongoing engagement

### Growing Customers (Upsell + Referral)

- **Cross-sell** — complementary products ("customers who bought X also bought Y")
- **Up-sell** — premium versions, larger quantities, longer contracts
- **Next-sell** — the natural next purchase in the customer lifecycle
- **Unbundling** — selling components separately that were previously bundled
- **Referral programmes** — structured incentives for customer-to-customer referrals
- **Viral loops** — product features that naturally generate referrals

## Sales Roadmap Framework

Build a repeatable sales process by answering these questions (Blank & Dorf, 2012):

1. **Who influences** the purchase decision?
2. **Who recommends** solutions in this space?
3. **Who decides** (has authority to say yes)?
4. **Who sabotages** (actively or passively resists)?
5. **Where is the budget?** (who controls funds, what budget category?)
6. **How many sales calls per sale?** (average touches to close)
7. **Average sales cycle length?** (days/weeks/months from first contact to payment)
8. **What is the selling strategy?** (solution selling, relationship selling, transactional)

### Organisation and Influence Maps (B2B)

For each target organisation, map:
- Formal hierarchy (who reports to whom)
- Informal influence (who actually shapes decisions)
- Champions (internal advocates for your solution)
- Blockers (people who resist change)

### Support/Approval Matrix

| | Needs Your Support | Doesn't Need Your Support |
|---|---|---|
| **Must Approve** | Priority engagement: educate and gain buy-in | Formal alignment: ensure no objections |
| **No Approval Needed** | Nurture as champion: keep them enthusiastic | Monitor: don't ignore, but low priority |

### Four Types of Customers for Sales Prioritisation

| Type | Characteristics | Sales Approach |
|---|---|---|
| **Earlyvangelists** | Active problem, budget, willing to take risk | Sell the vision; close fast |
| **Early Evaluators** | Interested but cautious, need proof | Demos, pilots, references |
| **Scalable Customers** | Will buy when product is proven | Standard sales process |
| **Mainstream Customers** | Need category maturity and social proof | Marketing-driven, not founder-sold |

### Product Positioning Statement (Geoffrey Moore template)

> "For [target customer] who [statement of need], the [product name] is a [product category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [competitive alternative], our product [primary differentiation]."

**Key insight:** The differentiator is NOT the compelling reason to buy — the *benefit* of the differentiator is. "Faster processing" is a feature; "save 2 hours per day" is a benefit.

### Test-Selling Methodology

During Customer Validation (Blank & Dorf, 2012):
- Sell at **near full price** to earlyvangelists
- If you can't sell at full price, the value proposition needs work (don't discount your way to validation)
- Track everything: objections, questions, time to close, decision process
- Build a probability-adjusted sales forecast from actual sales data

## Content Circle Framework

Three concentric circles for building an audience post-validation (Kagan, 2024):

| Circle | Description | Uganda Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Core** | Very narrow niche within a niche | "Kampala restaurant inventory management tips" |
| **Medium** | Broader overlapping topic | "Restaurant business management in East Africa" |
| **Large** | Largest related audience | "Small business management, entrepreneurship" |

Start with Core content. Expand outward as audience grows.

## Five-Question Marketing Plan

Once the business model is validated, answer these five questions (Kagan, 2024):

1. **What is your one goal for this year?** (Revenue target, customer count, or market share)
2. **Who exactly is your customer and where can you find them?** (Specific locations, groups, platforms)
3. **What is one marketing activity you can double down on?** (The one channel that works best)
4. **How can you delight your first 100 customers?** (Word-of-mouth generation)
5. **If you HAD to double your business with no money in 30 days, what would you do?** (Forces creative, low-cost strategies)

## Experiment-Based Marketing

Create and track marketing experiments systematically (Kagan, 2024):

| Channel | Expected Sales | Actual Sales | Cost | ROI | Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| WhatsApp broadcast | 10 | 8 | Free | ∞ | Double down |
| Facebook ad | 5 | 1 | UGX 200K | Negative | Kill or redesign |
| Market sampling | 3 | 5 | UGX 100K | Positive | Expand to more markets |
| Referral programme | 7 | 12 | UGX 50K/referral | High | Scale up incentive |

**Rule:** Run each experiment for 30 days. Then double down on what works, kill what doesn't. Don't spread thin across many channels.

## Email List Building Progression

Staged growth approach for email/WhatsApp list building (Kagan, 2024):

| Stage | Size | Tactic |
|---|---|---|
| 0-10 | Dream Ten | Personal outreach to 10 ideal contacts |
| 11-50 | Lazy Marketing | Add link to email signature, social bios, business cards |
| 51-100 | Community Posts | Share in existing WhatsApp groups, Facebook groups, market associations |
| 100+ | Lead Magnets | Offer checklists, templates, guides in exchange for contact |

## The Law of 100

Commit to 100 repetitions of any marketing activity before evaluating whether it works (Kagan, 2024):
- 100 customer conversations
- 100 WhatsApp messages
- 100 social media posts
- 100 market visits

This combats premature abandonment during the inevitable "dip" where results feel slow.
