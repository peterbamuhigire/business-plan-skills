# Growth Strategy Playbook
*Source: Umbrex Consulting LLC (2025), ISBN 978-1-968040-XX-X, 203 pages*

## 1. Framing the Growth Mandate

### Growth Mandate Archetypes
Before generating options, define the mandate. Four archetypes:

| Archetype | Typical Horizon | Primary Metric |
|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| **Protect and optimise** | 1–2 years | Margin and cash; hold share |
| **Accelerate the core** | 2–3 years | Revenue growth in existing markets |
| **Expand adjacencies** | 2–4 years | Share in new segments/channels/geographies |
| **Transform** | 3–5+ years | New business model or market position |

One strategy can combine archetypes, but leadership must be explicit about the primary mandate. Mixing mandates without sequencing creates confusion about priorities.

### Time Horizons
- **Now (0–12 months)**: core improvement, hygiene, quick wins
- **Next (12–36 months)**: adjacent moves requiring new capability or access
- **Later (3+ years)**: transformational or speculative options

Resources, risk tolerance, and governance norms differ by horizon. Stage investments accordingly.

### Setting Quantified Growth Targets
Targets must be measurable, time-bound, and decomposable into drivers. "Grow faster" is not a target. "Grow net new ARR by $40M over two years while improving gross margin by 300 bps" is a target.

**Reconciliation process:**
1. Top-down target: creates urgency, prevents sandbagging
2. Bottom-up feasibility: surfaces constraints and required capability changes
3. Reconciliation: if gap remains, either add bigger moves (new channels, acquisitions) or change constraints (funding, talent, operating model)

**Driver decomposition** (two-layer minimum):
- Layer 1: by business unit, segment, region, or channel (resource allocation)
- Layer 2: by economic drivers — volume, price, mix, retention, expansion, cost-to-serve

Use ranges, not single numbers, when uncertainty is high. Commit to a floor and upside; state what must be true to achieve upside.

### Non-Negotiables (Guardrails)
3–7 measurable constraints that the strategy will not violate, even if an option looks attractive:
- Financial health: cash flow requirements, margin floors, capex limits, leverage constraints
- Customer promise: service levels, quality thresholds, data privacy and trust
- Risk and compliance: regulatory rules, safety standards, cybersecurity
- Capacity reality: hard limits in operations, supply chain, service delivery, hiring pace
- Identity and values: ethical standards, mission integrity, brand positioning boundaries

Make guardrails measurable: "protect the brand" → "do not enter segments requiring discounting below X margin or violating safety commitments."

### Growth Ambition Scorecard
The North Star metric + KPI tree:
- **Outcomes**: North Star metric + 2–4 supporting outcomes (margin, cash, share, impact)
- **Drivers**: 3–5 leading indicators tied to the biggest growth levers (pipeline, conversion, adoption, retention, price realization, cost-to-serve)
- **Enablers**: 2–3 capability/execution milestones that must be delivered for drivers to move

Enterprise scorecard = 8–12 metrics maximum. Each metric must be decision-linked: who owns it, how often reviewed, and what decision changes if it moves up or down.

**Guardrail metrics** appear on the scorecard with thresholds (not just targets) that trigger leadership intervention.

## 2. Diagnosing the Growth Engine

Before designing options, diagnose what the business actually does today. Most growth strategies fail because they over-invest in the wrong levers, under-estimate execution difficulty, or misread what customers value.

### Baseline Performance
A baseline separates **momentum** (what would happen anyway) from **management** (what you deliberately create).

**Steps:**
1. Use 24–36 months of actual performance data; label anomalies explicitly
2. Build a **waterfall by business model**:
   - Recurring revenue: starting base → new adds → expansion → contraction → churn → price/packaging effects
   - Transactional: customer count → conversion rate → average order value → purchase frequency → mix → price realization
   - Sales-led B2B: pipeline creation → stage conversion → win rate → average contract value → sales cycle → renewals → expansion
3. Identify which 2–3 drivers explain most variance
4. Distinguish repeatable internal improvement from external tailwinds
5. Separate baseline from budget — the gap is where the organisation is relying on hope

**Baseline brief** (one page): baseline trajectory + driver waterfall + top variance drivers + growth/profit concentration by unit + 3–5 implications for leverage.

### Unit Economics and Value Drivers
Growth without value creation is a trap. Diagnose: which customers and motions create value, and which destroy it?

**Economics by business model:**
- Subscription: CAC by channel/segment, gross/contribution margin, NRR and GRR, payback period, servicing cost per customer, LTV assumptions
- Transactional: contribution margin per order and per customer, incremental marketing efficiency, repeat rate and cohort repurchase curves, returns/refunds/fraud leakage, fulfilment/support costs
- Platform: unit economics for each side, take rate, subsidy levels, cross-side elasticity, network effect evidence

**Economics heatmap**: segment on one axis, channel/product on the other, showing growth rate AND contribution margin. Identify zones where growth improves value vs. destroys it.

**Cohort analysis as truth serum**: do later cohorts retain worse than earlier cohorts? Does cost-to-serve decline with tenure? Cohort evidence is a gating input for any major growth plan.

Key value levers and second-order effects:
| Lever | Second-order effect to manage |
|-------|-------------------------------|
| Pricing and discount discipline | May reduce volume without improved value communication |
| Retention and customer success | Requires investment in onboarding, tooling, product quality |
| Channel mix shifts | May introduce channel conflict or reduce customer experience control |
| Service model redesign | Must protect moments that drive retention |

### Customer and Channel Reality
**Actionable segmentation**: separates customers with different needs, buying processes, willingness to pay, and cost-to-serve. If segments don't imply different offers, messages, or motions, they are not strategic.

**Customer journey mapping** (evidence-based, not anecdotes):
- Discovery and shortlist: how customers find options, what triggers consideration
- Evaluation: what customers test and compare, who influences the decision
- Purchase and onboarding: what delays conversion and time-to-value
- Usage and value realization: behaviours that correlate with retention and expansion
- Renewal, repeat, and advocacy: what drives loyalty, referrals, and share of wallet

**Channel diagnostic** (four views):
1. **Channel contribution**: share of revenue, growth, and profit by channel
2. **Channel efficiency**: cost of sale (CAC), productivity, and payback by channel
3. **Channel capacity**: realistic volume the channel can absorb
4. **Channel fit**: which segments each channel serves well vs. fails

Look for **hidden channels**: integrators, OEM embedding, marketplaces, procurement frameworks, referral loops. These often offer disproportionate leverage.

**Churn diagnosis**: separate voluntary from forced churn; identify "moments that matter" that most predict retention; internal teams often blame price when the real cause is poor onboarding, unclear ROI, or unreliable service.

### Competitive Context
Goal: understand where you have advantage, where you have parity, where you are structurally disadvantaged.

**Competitive set definition**: include substitutes (what would customer do if you didn't exist?), bundled incumbents, platform players, in-house builds.

**Basis of competition diagnosis**: "We win when X matters, and we lose when Y matters."
Common bases: price and TCO, performance and reliability, time-to-value, integration and ecosystem fit, brand trust and risk reduction, distribution reach, service outcomes.

**Win/loss analysis**: even imperfect CRM data shows patterns. Pair with competitor offer structure analysis (packaging, bundling, discount practice, switching costs).

**Competitor economics and intent hypotheses**: where do they likely make money? What are they investing in? What trade-offs are they willing to make?

### Growth Constraints Assessment
Constraints are not reasons to be cautious. They are inputs to design.

| Constraint | Diagnostic Question | Relief Mechanisms |
|------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Capacity** | If demand doubled in 6 months, what breaks first? | Map the "drumbeat" (bottleneck step); estimate expansion investment and time |
| **Talent** | Do we have people to execute likely options? | Identify "gating roles" and realistic timelines to fill/develop |
| **Capital** | If we invest here, what do we stop/delay/shrink? | Make trade-offs explicit; avoid "growth on top of the day job" |
| **Brand and trust** | What options are incompatible with the brand promise? | Specify what the strategy will not do even if tempting short-term |
| **Regulatory/risk** | What rules, approvals, or privacy constraints apply? | Early engagement with compliance/regulators; identify blockers before scaling |

**Constraint heatmap**: for each major growth vector, which constraints bind first, what relieves them, and who owns each.

## 3. The Seven Degrees of Freedom for Growth

A portfolio framework from McKinsey that prevents narrow thinking without encouraging sprawl.

### How to Use the Framework
**Purpose**: expand the option set + make trade-offs explicit. It is NOT a theory of competitive advantage.

**The cardinal rule**: do NOT pursue all seven degrees simultaneously. Each requires different data, talent, and decision cadence. Most coherent strategies concentrate on 2–4 degrees.

**Three moments of use:**
1. **Framing**: which degrees are in scope vs. explore only vs. out of scope?
2. **Option generation**: generate options degree-by-degree; convert ideas into comparable one-page memos
3. **Portfolio design**: select coherent mix, sequence over time, resource with explicit owners and budgets

**Degree brief** (one page before generating options for each degree):
- Growth hypothesis: where, specifically, could growth come from?
- Value mechanism: how it would create value (volume, price, retention, margin, cash)
- Assets to leverage: advantage already in hand that makes this degree plausible
- Capabilities required: new skills, systems, or partnerships needed to scale
- Fast proof: smallest test that would increase/decrease confidence quickly

### The Seven Degrees

**Degree 1 — Deepen Penetration with Current Customers and Markets**
Growth from the inside out. Fastest path to impact because it leverages existing distribution.
- Mechanisms: higher usage, higher frequency, increased share of wallet, improved retention, account expansion, improved pricing/mix
- Plays: tighten discount governance; redesign packaging to match willingness to pay; improve onboarding and activation; build customer success plays; cross-sell and upsell with clear eligibility rules
- Decision metrics: NRR, GRR, churn by cohort, adoption intensity, price realization, attach rates
- Common traps: using discounting as primary lever; pushing cross-sell before core job is solved; scaling acquisition while churn is high
- Foundation for riskier bets: produces cash, improves predictability, builds execution muscle

**Degree 2 — Access New Customer Segments and Channels**
Expand access to new buyers for your current offer. Often a go-to-market redesign.
- Segment expansion: vertical (new industries), size-based (enterprise/midmarket/SMB), use-case based
- Channel expansion: inside sales, self-serve, resellers, systems integrators, marketplaces, OEM embedding, platform partnerships
- Proof required before scaling: partner channel can create pipeline AND renew customers profitably
- Economics differ by segment and channel — model explicitly before committing resources

**Degree 3 — Expand Geographically**
Enter new countries or regions with your current offer.
- Requires: beachhead segment where advantage transfers + clear localisation plan
- Entry modes: direct, distributor, partnership, acquisition
- Proof required: beachhead segment achieved target unit economics before broad expansion
- Uganda/EA opportunity: AfCFTA, EAC Common Market (seven freedoms, no work permit requirement for EAC nationals) create regional expansion pathways

**Degree 4 — Extend the Offer for Current Customers**
Add adjacent products/services to existing customers.
- Adjacent job must be one current customers already pay to solve
- High-margin, low-complexity attachment is the ideal starting point
- Proof required: new offering attaches at meaningful rates AND does not increase churn in core
- Mechanisms: bundling, services attachment, premium tier entry, adjacent module development

**Degree 5 — Change the Profit Model / Monetisation**
Redesign how you deliver and charge for value.
- Pricing model shifts: one-time → subscription, project-based → retainer, feature-based → outcome-based, flat fee → usage-based
- Value to unlock: adoption friction reduction, natural expansion, alignment of value capture to outcomes
- Warning: not a pricing exercise — it is a system change (metering, entitlement, sales behaviour, finance processes, customer success)
- Proof required: migration pilot with stable churn AND improved net retention before scaling

**Degree 6 — Change Your Role in the Value Chain**
Move upstream or downstream; integrate, platform-play, or restructure distribution relationships.
- Examples: become the platform players use, acquire distribution, eliminate intermediaries, create a marketplace
- High execution risk; requires strong existing position and clear mechanism to capture margin
- Partner reaction must be modelled: will key partners cooperate or retaliate?

**Degree 7 — Build New Businesses**
Create genuinely new business lines leveraging corporate assets.
- Requires: repeatable acquisition AND improving cohort economics as the proof standard
- Wedge use case must validate demand and economics within 90–180 days
- Highest uncertainty; should be staged with explicit kill criteria

## 4. Option Generation

### Structured Ideation Sessions
**Prework (48 hours before):** share four pages — ambition and guardrails, growth-engine baseline, market and profit pool map, Jobs and Journey Brief. Ask each participant to submit 3 option starters (verb + customer + mechanism, tagged to a degree).

**Participant design:** 8–12 people; include commercial, product, customer success, operations, finance, technology. Keep core group small; broaden asynchronously.

**Roles:**
- Facilitator: protects time boxes, enforces option format
- Scribe: most important role — captures options live in shared format
- Fact-base keeper: answers data questions; flags assumptions
- Red team (rotate): challenges feasibility
- Executive sponsor: opens with mandate/guardrails; closes with what happens next

**Behavioural rules:**
- Options, not opinions: disagreement → alternative option, not debate
- One primary mechanism: split "and" ideas into separate bets
- Specificity required: every option names target segment and first proof point
- Evidence tag: attach evidence or label as hypothesis to test

**3-hour agenda:**
- 0:00–0:15 Context: confirm mandate, horizon, non-negotiables, growth gap
- 0:15–0:35 Insight recap: share profit pools, moments that matter, constraints
- 0:35–1:35 Degree sprints: generate options one degree at a time
- 1:35–2:10 Cluster and sharpen: merge duplicates, split multi-part ideas
- 2:10–2:40 Reality pass: constraint breakers, capability-heavy options
- 2:40–3:00 Commit: assign owners for one-page memos

**Mini-option format** (during session): Verb + target / mechanism / first proof point.
Example: "Reduce churn in Segment A by redesigning onboarding for the first success milestone; mechanism: time-to-value; proof: activation rate improves from X to Y within 8 weeks."

Cleanup within 48 hours: rewrite options to remove ambiguity, map to primary degree, remove duplicates, publish inventory.

### One-Page Option Memo (Standard Format)
Every option must have the same structure for comparison:
1. **Option statement**: one sentence — verb + target + mechanism
2. **Degree and horizon**: primary degree + now/next/later
3. **Customer and job**: who, what job, what success looks like
4. **Value mechanism**: primary driver and how it moves
5. **Economics**: impact range, timing, 3 key assumptions
6. **Investment**: people, budget, tooling, one-time vs. recurring
7. **Capabilities and dependencies**: build/buy/partner; critical dependencies and owners
8. **Risks and mitigations**: top 3 risks + concrete mitigations
9. **First 90 days**: actions and proof points; leading indicator to monitor
10. **Owner**: executive sponsor + initiative lead role

**Good-enough sizing** (long-list stage): avoid heavy models. Use simple driver math with ranges.
- New segment: reachable accounts × plausible win rate × average contract value
- Retention recovery: churn reduction in a cohort × revenue per customer, adjusted for service cost changes
- Pricing/packaging: expected price realization uplift × affected revenue base + conservative volume sensitivity

**Confidence labels:**
- High: internal data or proven execution; limited new capability required
- Medium: supported by evidence and analogues; some new capability or behaviour change required
- Low: hypothesis-driven; material uncertainty; requires testing or different governance

**Quality gate**: specific target + one dominant mechanism + explicit assumptions + 90-day proof point + realistic resource envelope with named owner.

## 5. Core Growth Options

### Pricing, Packaging, and Revenue Leakage Removal (Degree 1)
Pricing and packaging touch nearly every transaction and renewal. The objective: increase net price realization while reducing buying and renewal friction.

**Three leakage patterns:**
1. **Discount drift**: sellers discount preemptively; company stops knowing true willingness to pay
2. **Concession creep**: non-price concessions (extra support, custom work, extended terms) normalised and quietly increase cost-to-serve
3. **Renewal reset**: renewals become renegotiations because original deal was over-customised or value was not made visible

**Hygiene moves (payback 1–2 quarters):**
- Tighten discount guardrails: define approval ladder, required trade-offs for discounts, reason capture
- Standardise concessions: price them or bound them with clear rationale
- Fix quote-to-cash reliability: eliminate billing errors, credits, entitlement disputes

**Pricing as operating system:** lightweight weekly mechanism (deal desk or pricing council) that approves exceptions quickly AND harvests learning from them.

**Packaging principles:**
- Value-based fences: tiers differ on outcomes customers care about, not arbitrary feature gates
- Operational feasibility: must be sellable, implementable, and supportable without heroics
- Renewal simplicity: the renewal should feel like "continue," not re-specification

**Rollout as change programme:** internal adoption (train + instrument compliance) + customer communication (value language, fair transition) + operational execution (test end-to-end before launch; define pause conditions).

**Leakage-removal checklist:**
- Discounting: documented discount ladder with exception capture?
- Concessions: standardised with internal cost visibility?
- Renewals: explicit rules (uplift, true-ups, tier migration) with value proof motion?
- Entitlements: know what customer bought, what they use, what they are entitled to?
- Billing: % invoices requiring credits, and root causes identified?
- Exceptions: trigger a learning loop that updates defaults?

### Retention and Expansion — Lifecycle Growth and NRR Levers (Degree 1)
Retention is the quiet multiplier of growth. A small improvement in churn is often more durable than a large increase in acquisition.

**Lifecycle stages and milestones:** acquisition → activation → adoption → value realization → renewal → expansion. For each stage: define the customer milestone + internal owner.

**Time-to-value as strategic metric:** treat it as a metric in recurring revenue models; reduce it by standardising onboarding, clarifying responsibilities, removing product friction.

**Churn segmentation:**
- Early churn: fix through onboarding and expectation setting
- Late churn: fix through reliability, roadmap confidence, workflow embedding
- Commercial churn: fix through renewal packaging and fair migration rules
- Voluntary vs. forced (customer closures)

**Retention driver tree:** identify 2–3 leading indicators that predict renewal (adoption depth, usage frequency, time since last meaningful outcome, support issue severity/recurrence, stakeholder engagement). Build an early-warning system, not an elegant score.

**Four repeatable plays:** onboarding + adoption + renewal + save. Each: target cohort + trigger + steps + assets required + KPI.

**Expansion pathways** (each needs eligibility rule + value story + mechanism):
- Additional users / higher usage / more sites
- Adjacent modules / workflows
- Services attached to core product
- True-ups / usage-based billing

**NRR lever trade-offs:** price uplifts improve NRR but can increase churn if value not visible; usage expansion improves NRR but requires transparent metering; cross-sell improves NRR but requires product fit and enablement; true-ups improve NRR but can erode trust if surprising.

**Success plan template** (create at onboarding):
- Job and outcomes: desired outcomes in customer language with 2–3 measurable indicators
- Milestones: first success, steady state, renewal proof milestone
- Roles: customer owner, executive sponsor, your owner, escalation path
- Value proof: how ROI or risk reduction will be quantified and when
- Expansion hypothesis: next logical expansion if outcomes achieved

### Channel Performance and Coverage Improvement (Degree 1/2)
Highest-return channel work is usually improving routing, coverage, partner productivity, and measurement — not launching something new.

**Channel scorecard per channel:**
- Contribution: revenue and profit
- Efficiency: CAC or cost of sale + payback
- Conversion: funnel or pipeline metrics
- Capacity: how much volume the channel can scale to

**Three coverage moves:** rebalancing (align territories to where profit pools shift), specialisation (focused motions for segments requiring different service), routing and follow-up discipline (handoff quality between marketing/SDR/AE/customer success).

**Partner channel success requirements:** reason to sell you (margin, services revenue, attach opportunities), deal registration, co-selling rules, clear handoffs. Measure partner-sourced pipeline, win rates, retention, and cohort renewal quality — not just volume.

**Attribution reality:** attribution systems over-credit last touch. Use practical tests + triangulation of spend with pipeline and cohort outcomes. Shift budget toward channels that create incremental pipeline and retain well.

### Commercial Productivity — Sales Motions, Incentives, Enablement (Degree 1)
**Sales motion definition:** self-serve, inside sales, field sales, partner-led, or hybrid. Each has different economics and requires different enablement, tools, and incentives.

**Five productivity drivers:** pipeline created per rep, stage conversion, win rate, average deal size, cycle length. Find the binding constraint and focus there.

**Sales management systems over individual seller talent:** weekly leading-indicator reviews + monthly outcome reviews + clear coaching loops. **Mutual action plan** for complex deals: agreed steps, owners, dates across buyer and seller.

**Incentive alignment rule:** if a seller can maximise pay by doing the opposite of the strategy, the comp plan is broken. Practical fixes: segment-based accelerators, guardrails on discounting, quality component tied to renewals for motions where long-term value matters.

**Enablement quality over volume:** prioritise assets that move deals — segment-specific value narrative, proof library (case studies, ROI tools, security/compliance packets), discovery guides, mutual action plan templates. Measure asset usage and whether assets improve conversion.

**Sales motion canvas template:**
- Target: segments, buyer roles, qualification thresholds
- Value story: job-to-be-done, quantified outcomes, proof required for each buying role
- Stages: standard stages with exit criteria and required artefacts
- Friction removers: security, legal, procurement, implementation accelerators
- Pricing and packaging: default offer, discount rules, expansion path
- KPIs: pipeline, conversion, cycle time, win rate, deal size, margin

## 6. Adjacent Growth Options (Degrees 2–4)

### Segment and Channel Adjacencies (Degree 2)
Not "sell the same thing to more people" — it is a go-to-market redesign.

**Adjacency logic test** (three questions):
1. Do we have a transferable right to win (proof of solving the adjacent segment's core job)?
2. Can we reach these customers through a channel we either have or can build affordably?
3. Do the economics work — unit economics similar to or better than the core?

**Beachhead principle:** enter with a specific segment, geography, or use case rather than a broad launch. Prove before scaling.

**When to avoid Degree 2:** when Degree 1 churn is still high (new customers churn faster than you add them), when product-market fit is unproven, or when channel economics for new segments are structurally worse.

### Geographic Expansion (Degree 3)
**Entry mode selection:**
- **Direct**: highest control, highest cost, requires on-the-ground capability
- **Distributor/reseller**: lower cost, lower control, depends on partner quality
- **Partnership**: shared investment, requires alignment of incentives
- **Acquisition**: fastest market access, highest risk, requires integration capability

**East Africa specific:** EAC Common Market Protocol provides right of establishment and free movement of services for EAC-registered companies. No work permit requirement for EAC nationals. Rules of Origin (35% local value-added for AfCFTA zero tariff) apply to goods.

**Geographic expansion checklist:**
- Is there a beachhead segment where the offer transfers without major modification?
- What must be localised (pricing, language, distribution, regulatory compliance)?
- Do we have or can we acquire on-the-ground capability within affordable lead time?
- What entry mode matches constraints (capital, management bandwidth)?

### Offer Extension for Current Customers (Degree 4)
**Adjacent job identification:** what jobs are current best customers paying to solve today that you could credibly solve better together?

**High-yield adjacency criteria:**
- Customer already has the pain (not latent)
- You have data/access/trust advantage
- Low implementation complexity (can attach without major product/service rebuild)
- High gross margin (service-led extensions often qualify)

**Common trap:** moving to Degree 4 before the core (Degree 1) is stable. Extensions amplify churn if core reliability is not established first.

## 7. Option Evaluation and Portfolio Design

### Evaluation Framework
Evaluate each option on four dimensions:

| Dimension | Key Questions |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Strategic fit** | Does it reinforce or contradict the equity story and non-negotiables? |
| **Economic impact** | Revenue and margin contribution, timing, and quality (recurring vs. one-time)? |
| **Execution feasibility** | Do we have the capabilities, data, and talent to execute within the mandate horizon? |
| **Risk profile** | What are the top 3 failure modes and are mitigations credible? |

**Five shortlisting screens:**
1. Is there a specific target segment or customer cohort?
2. Is the dominant value mechanism clear (conversion, price, retention, expansion, cost-to-serve)?
3. Are assumptions explicit (not hidden in averages or narratives)?
4. Is there a 90-day proof point that creates genuine learning (not a Gantt milestone)?
5. Is there a realistic resource envelope with a named owner?

### Portfolio Design Principles
A growth strategy portfolio should:
- Cover a **time horizon** (now/next/later) — not be all short-term or all long-term
- Balance **risk** — some high-confidence core + some medium-confidence adjacent + one or two exploratory bets
- Be **coherent** — options should reinforce each other (Degree 1 funds Degree 2; Degree 2 proves before Degree 3 scales)
- Be **capacity-constrained** — teams can only execute 3–5 heavy initiatives simultaneously; sequencing is not optional

**Sequencing logic:**
- Lead with Degree 1 (highest confidence, produces cash, builds execution muscle)
- Stage Degrees 2–4 behind proof from Degree 1
- Fund Degrees 5–7 only when the core engine is stable and creates the cash headroom

**Resource allocation sanity check:** if you cannot name what will stop when you launch a new initiative, you are not ready to scale.

## 8. Growth Operating System

### Governance for Growth
Three-layer governance:
1. **Initiative layer**: weekly owner reviews — milestones, leading indicators, decisions needed
2. **Portfolio layer**: monthly growth review — reconcile initiative progress to scorecard; reallocate resources
3. **Leadership layer**: quarterly — capital allocation, major trade-offs, mandate refresh

**Non-negotiable forum rules:**
- Pre-read 24 hours in advance in stable format
- Meetings for discussion and decisions, not for reading updates
- Every meeting ends with documented decisions, owners, and deadlines
- Discussion centres on what changed, what is at risk, and what decision is needed

### Test-and-Learn Methodology
Do not design for scale; design to learn cheaply.

**Stage-gate model:**
1. **Hypothesis stage (0–30 days):** define the growth hypothesis, minimum viable test, kill criteria, and success metrics
2. **Learning stage (30–90 days):** run the test; collect evidence; make a go/no-go decision based on leading indicators
3. **Proof stage (90–180 days):** prove repeatable unit economics before broader rollout
4. **Scale stage (180+ days):** scale with confidence; apply learnings to refine the approach

**Kill criteria:** define in advance what evidence will cause you to stop. The discipline is: stopping is a victory, not a failure, if it frees capacity for what works.

**Fast proof examples by degree:**
- Degree 1 pricing: discount exception rate, net price realization change, churn threshold
- Degree 2 new segment: partner-sourced pipeline creation rate, first 90-day renewal rate
- Degree 3 geographic: beachhead segment conversion, cost-to-serve vs. core
- Degree 4 offer extension: attach rate, impact on core renewal
- Degree 5 model shift: migration rate, NRR post-migration vs. cohort baseline

### Stakeholder Alignment
**Internal narrative** (three questions all answers must address):
- Why: why change is necessary now, framed in customer and market reality — not blame
- What: what priorities will shift, what decisions will be made differently, what will stop
- How: how progress will be measured, what routines will change, what enablement will be provided

**Make "what will stop" explicit.** When employees hear only new initiatives, they infer leadership is adding work without understanding capacity limits.

**Audience tailoring:**
- Executives: portfolio logic, scorecard, decision cadence
- Middle managers: practical operating routines, clear decision rules, exception guidance
- Frontline teams: concrete changes in workflow, tools, escalation paths

**Communication channel = operating cadence**: when leaders consistently ask about leading indicators, enforce guardrails, and make decisions quickly, communication becomes behaviour, not slogans.

## 9. Uganda/East Africa Application

### Growth Mandate Adaptation
| Growth Challenge | Most Relevant Degrees | Uganda-Specific Notes |
|-----------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Grow revenue in existing market | Degree 1 | Mobile money penetration enables frictionless retention and expansion plays |
| Access new customer segments | Degree 2 | Regional growth within EAC; income-tier segmentation critical |
| East Africa regional expansion | Degree 3 | AfCFTA + EAC Common Market: rules of origin, no work permit for EAC nationals |
| Add adjacent services | Degree 4 | Health, financial services, agri-inputs are common high-value adjacencies |

### Growth Constraints Common in Uganda/EA
| Constraint | Impact | Mitigation |
|------------|--------|------------|
| Low smartphone/data penetration in rural areas | Limits digital-first go-to-market | USSD, WhatsApp, agent network, radio/community channels |
| Talent availability at mid-management level | Limits scale of multiple initiatives | Build → Buy; NEET youth pipeline (5.25M potential); EAC talent pool |
| Infrastructure reliability (power, roads) | Limits operational throughput and delivery SLAs | Backup power; hub-and-spoke logistics; last-mile partnerships |
| Working capital constraints (18+ month bank payment cycles) | Limits acquisition investment | Mobile money prepayment incentives; layaway/SACCO financing integration |
| Regulatory lag (new product/sector approvals) | Extends "learning" stage timeline | Early regulator engagement; sandbox participation (BOU FinTech sandbox) |

### Pricing and Packaging Norms for Uganda
- **Affordability architecture**: tiered pricing aligned with income quintiles Q1–Q5 is almost always necessary for consumer products
- **Mobile money as default payment**: M-Pesa/MTN MoMo/Airtel Money integration is a packaging decision, not a payments decision
- **Trust proxy signals**: community leader endorsement, visible local presence, and pay-after-value models reduce initial purchase risk for low-income segments
- **Informal procurement**: many SMBs buy through mobile phone conversations; formalised packaging and quotes must translate into informal channel equivalents
