# Building a StoryBrand — SB7 Framework
**Source:** Miller, Donald (2017). *Building a StoryBrand: Clarify Your Message So Customers Will Listen*. HarperCollins Leadership.

**Read when:** Writing marketing messaging, positioning statements, brand identity, website copy, the one-liner, email campaigns, or any section where the customer needs to be invited into a story. Also read when coaching clients on how to talk about their business.

---

## The Core Insight: Why Most Marketing Fails

Businesses make two fatal communication mistakes:

1. **They fail to focus on how their offer helps customers survive and thrive.** The brain is a survival organ — it filters everything through the question "does this help me eat, connect, find safety, gain status, or achieve meaning?" If your marketing doesn't answer that question, customers tune out.

2. **They cause customers to burn too many calories.** The brain conserves energy. Complex, cluttered, or self-focused messaging forces the brain to work too hard — so it switches off. The simpler and clearer the message, the more likely it is to be processed and remembered.

**The mantra:** *If you confuse, you lose.*

**The enemy is noise.** Most marketing collateral is noise — rambling backstories, internal goals, and irrelevant information that positions the brand as the chairs in the room, not the exits.

**Story is the solution.** Story is a sense-making device that organises information so the brain doesn't have to work. Good stories have a clear hero, a problem, a guide, a plan, a call to action, stakes, and a resolution. The SB7 Framework applies this structure to brand messaging.

## The SB7 Framework (BrandScript)

The StoryBrand BrandScript maps the customer's story in seven modules. Build one BrandScript for the overall brand, then sub-BrandScripts for each division or product line.

### Module 1 — A Character (The Customer is the Hero)

**Principle:** The customer is the hero of the story, not your brand.

When a brand positions itself as the hero, it unconsciously competes with the customer. Customers are already the hero of their own story — they are looking for a **guide**, not another hero.

**What to define:**
- What does this customer **want** as it relates to our brand? (One clear desire — not a list)
- The desire must connect to survival and thriving:
  - Conserving financial resources (saving money)
  - Conserving time
  - Building social networks / belonging to a tribe
  - Gaining status or identity
  - Accumulating resources / growing income
  - The desire to be generous / achieve meaning
  - The desire for self-realisation

**The story gap:** Once you name what the customer wants, you open a gap in their mind between where they are and where they could be. That gap is the engine of the story — it creates attention and desire.

**The one desire rule:** Diluting the hero's desire with too many ambitions collapses the story. Choose one primary desire for the overall BrandScript. Additional desires can be subplot BrandScripts for sub-products.

**BrandScript field:** *Character wants ________________.*

---

### Module 2 — Has a Problem (Three Levels of Conflict)

**Principle:** Companies sell solutions to external problems, but customers buy solutions to internal problems.

Problems deepen customer interest because every story is about conflict. The more you talk about the customer's problems, the more engaged they become.

**Three levels of conflict (and the villain behind them):**

**The Villain**
Every problem has a root cause — the villain. The villain gives conflict a focal point.
- Must be a **root source** (not a symptom)
- Must be **relatable** — customers immediately recognise it
- Must be **singular** — one villain
- Must be **real** — not manufactured fear
- Can be personalised/abstract: distractions, debt, inefficiency, bureaucracy, bad nutrition

**Level 1 — External Problem**
The tangible, physical obstacle: a leaky pipe, a bad website, a gap in the market. Most brands only address this level, which is why they struggle to connect emotionally.

**Level 2 — Internal Problem**
The frustration, doubt, or inadequacy the external problem causes. *This is the real reason customers buy.*
- "I feel embarrassed by my lawn" (not just "my lawn is overgrown")
- "I'm afraid I'll never retire comfortably" (not just "I need a financial plan")
- "I feel like I'm failing as a business owner" (not just "my revenue is flat")

Customers pay to resolve internal frustration. If your messaging speaks to the internal problem, you create a far deeper connection than competitors who only describe the external fix.

**Level 3 — Philosophical Problem**
Why does it matter at a deeper moral or social level? "It's simply wrong that ___."
- It's wrong that small businesses can't get a fair loan.
- It's wrong that families go hungry when food is wasted.
- It's wrong that hard-working entrepreneurs can't explain their own business clearly.

Philosophical problems connect a transaction to a cause or belief, turning customers into evangelists.

**BrandScript fields:** *Villain / External problem / Internal problem / Philosophical problem.*

### Module 3 — And Meets a Guide (The Brand as Guide)

**Principle:** Customers aren't looking for another hero — they're looking for a guide.

Position the brand as the guide, not the hero. The guide has already solved the problem the hero is facing. Think Yoda to Luke, Haymitch to Katniss, Lionel Logue to King George VI.

**Two characteristics every guide must demonstrate:**

**1. Empathy**
Express genuine understanding of the customer's frustration and pain. Customers trust brands that understand them.
- Empathetic phrases: "We understand how it feels to…", "Nobody should have to experience…", "Like you, we were frustrated by…"
- Empathy means: let customers know you see them as you see yourself. Find common ground.

**2. Authority**
Demonstrate competence without bragging. Four ways to show authority without positioning yourself as the hero:

| Method | Description |
|---|---|
| **Testimonials** | Let others speak — 3 focused testimonials is enough; avoid stacking 20+ which makes you look like you're competing with the customer |
| **Statistics** | Quantify impact: "We have helped 1,200 businesses in 12 countries" |
| **Awards** | Small logos at the bottom of the page; customers check the trust box without requiring more |
| **Logos** | For B2B: recognised client logos provide social proof of competence |

**The first impression test (Cuddy):** Customers ask two subconscious questions: *"Can I trust this person?"* (empathy answers this) and *"Can I respect this person?"* (authority answers this). Both must be answered quickly.

**BrandScript fields:** *Empathy statement / Authority proof.*

---

### Module 4 — Who Gives Them a Plan

**Principle:** Customers trust a guide who has a plan.

Even after the customer likes and trusts the guide, they won't buy without a clear path. A plan removes confusion and reduces the perceived risk of doing business with you.

**Two types of plan:**

**1. The Process Plan**
A step-by-step sequence that shows the customer how to engage the brand or use the product.
- Pre-purchase: "How to get started" (e.g., Schedule a call → We assess your needs → You get a tailored proposal)
- Post-purchase: "How to use the product" (e.g., Download the app → Enter your business details → Start managing your finances)
- Keep it to 3–4 steps maximum

**2. The Agreement Plan**
A set of agreements or commitments that reduce the customer's fear of buying.
- "Our price guarantee: we will match any verified competitor quote"
- "Our satisfaction commitment: if you're not completely happy, we refund in full"
- Used car dealer example: "We'll never use high-pressure tactics. We'll tell you exactly what we know about the car. We'll give you a fair price."

**Key insight:** The plan bridges the chasm between the customer's affection for the brand and their decision to purchase. Without a plan, customers remain stuck in indecision.

**BrandScript fields:** *Process plan (3–4 steps) / Agreement plan (optional commitments).*

---

### Module 5 — And Calls Them to Action

**Principle:** Customers do not take action unless they are challenged to take action.

People do not take initiative in a story — they act when challenged by an outside force. Brands must make explicit, confident calls to action.

**Two types of call to action:**

**1. Direct Call to Action (CTA)**
A clear invitation to buy, book, or commit.
- "Buy Now", "Schedule a Call", "Get a Quote", "Enrol Today"
- Must be direct and confident — being passive communicates weakness
- Place prominently: top right of website, above the fold, and repeated further down

**2. Transitional Call to Action**
A lower-commitment invitation for customers who aren't ready to buy yet — but that keeps them in your story.
- "Download our free guide", "Watch a 3-minute demo", "Get our free report"
- The transitional CTA collects email addresses and builds a nurture relationship
- Often leads to the direct CTA over time

**Website placement:**
- Direct CTA button: top right + centre above the fold. Use a contrasting colour. Repeat several times on the page.
- Transitional CTA: adjacent to the direct CTA (the "let's go on a second date" option next to "will you marry me?")

**BrandScript fields:** *Direct CTA / Transitional CTA.*

### Module 6 — That Helps Them Avoid Failure

**Principle:** Every human being is trying to avoid a tragic ending.

Stories require stakes. If nothing can be lost, no one pays attention. Showing customers what they risk by *not* engaging creates urgency.

**Loss aversion (Kahneman & Tversky):** The pain of losing is twice as powerful as the pleasure of gaining. Communicating what customers stand to lose is more motivating than only communicating what they stand to gain.

**How to define the stakes:**
- What health, financial, relationship, career, or opportunity costs does the customer face without your product?
- What tragic scene could befall them?
- What does inaction cost them over one year? Five years?

**Examples:**
- Financial advisor: "Don't spend your retirement years worried about money. You've worked too hard for too long."
- Education: "A long, boring summer — and regret that the time was wasted."
- Business planning: "Lose the bank's confidence before you even enter the room."

**Uganda/EA application:**
- Farming input: "Crops fail, debt accumulates, and children miss school — because you planted without a plan."
- Retail: "Your competitor opens next door while you're still thinking about it."
- Food processing: "UNBS shuts down your operation and your reputation doesn't recover."

**Warning:** Use failure sparingly. A few well-chosen consequences create urgency — too many create despair or distrust.

**BrandScript fields:** *3 consequences the customer faces by not buying.*

### Module 7 — And Ends in a Success

**Principle:** Never assume people understand how your brand can change their lives. Tell them.

Paint a clear, specific, aspirational vision of the customer's life after engaging your brand. Vague visions don't inspire — specific transformations do.

**Three ways stories resolve (and how to apply them):**

1. **Winning power and position** — Offer status, access, or prestige. E.g., loyalty tiers, "Preferred Member", limited editions, identity-associated products (Rolex, Gerber Knives).

2. **Union that makes the hero whole** — Help the customer achieve something they feel incomplete without: community, a relationship, a sense of completeness. Used for wellness, education, financial peace, relationships.

3. **Self-realisation** — Help the customer become a better version of themselves. Used for coaching, skills, personal development, faith-based brands.

**The Before and After grid (Ryan Deiss framework):**

| Dimension | Before | After |
|---|---|---|
| What do they have? | Problem / lack | Solution / possession |
| How do they feel? | Frustrated, anxious, stuck | Confident, proud, relieved |
| What is their average day? | Chaotic, inefficient, stressful | Ordered, productive, enjoyable |
| What is their status? | Overlooked, uncertain | Respected, competent |

**BrandScript fields:** *Success vision (specific and positive images of transformed life).*

### Foundation Module — Identity Transformation

**The deepest driver:** Every human being wants to transform — to become a better, more competent, more accepted version of themselves. Brands that participate in customer transformation create passionate advocates.

**Aspirational identity:**
- Who does the customer *want to become* as a result of engaging your brand?
- How do they want their friends to describe them?
- Define the arc: *From [current identity] → To [aspirational identity]*

**Examples:**

| Brand Type | From | To |
|---|---|---|
| Pet food | Passive dog owner | Every dog's hero |
| Financial advisor | Confused and ill-equipped | Competent and smart |
| Business planning service | Uncertain entrepreneur | Confident, fundable business owner |
| Skills training | Stuck job-seeker | Valued professional |
| Agriculture input | Subsistence farmer | Commercial farmer |

**Dave Ramsey model (best-practice guide):** Ramsey's brand addresses external problem (consumer debt), internal problem (confusion and hopelessness), and philosophical problem (accruing debt for things we don't need is morally questionable). He positions listeners as heroes on a financial freedom journey — and celebrates their transformation with the "Debt-Free Scream" climactic scene.

**BrandScript field:** *From ___ / To ___*

## The One-Liner

The one-liner is the single most important piece of copy any brand can create. It replaces a rambling pitch or tagline.

**One-liner formula:**
> "We help [CHARACTER] who [PROBLEM] [ACHIEVE RESULT / TRANSFORMATION]."

**Examples:**
- "We help small business owners who are frustrated with confusing marketing get a clear message that grows their revenue."
- "We help Ugandan entrepreneurs who can't get a bank loan create a bankable business plan that opens doors to finance."
- "We help farmers who are losing money to poor yields implement a simple farming system that doubles their harvest."

The one-liner replaces "What do you do?" It should be so clear that anyone who hears it immediately knows: (a) who you serve, (b) what problem you solve, (c) what transformation you offer.

## Implementing the BrandScript — The Marketing Roadmap

### Task 1: Build a Clear Website (5 Non-Negotiables)

1. **An offer above the fold** — The first thing visitors see must clearly state what you offer and what's in it for them. Options: promise aspirational identity, promise to solve a stated problem, or state exactly what you do. Must pass the "grunt test" (a distracted person understands the offer within 5 seconds).

2. **Obvious calls to action** — Direct CTA button (contrasting colour) at top right AND above the fold. Transitional CTA (lead generator) adjacent. Repeat the direct CTA further down. Never hide it.

3. **Images of success** — Show happy customers who have used your product and been transformed. Not your building, not your team selfie — images of the aspirational outcome.

4. **A bite-sized breakdown of revenue streams** — If you have multiple products/services, list them simply below the fold so customers can self-select without confusion.

5. **Very few words** — Cut half the words on your website. Replace paragraphs with bullet points. Replace text with images where possible. The fewer words, the more likely they will be read.

### Task 2: Create a Lead Generator

A transitional CTA that exchanges something of genuine value for a customer's email address.
- PDF guide, checklist, video series, tool, quiz
- Title formula: "How to [achieve outcome] without [feared cost]" or numbered tip list
- Must be immediately useful — establishes reciprocity and positions brand as the guide
- Place prominently as a transitional CTA on the website

### Task 3: Create an Automated Email Campaign

**Nurturing email formula (repeat 3× before each offer email):**
1. Talk about a problem the customer faces
2. Explain a plan to solve it
3. Describe how life looks once the problem is solved
- Include a P.S. — most read element in a mass email
- Purpose: remind customers you exist + build trust + establish reciprocity

**Offer email formula (every 4th email):**
1. Talk about a problem
2. Describe your product/service as the solution
3. Paint the transformation
4. Issue a direct, confident call to action (with limited-time offer if possible)

**Open rate reality:** 20% is industry standard — even emails that are deleted are "branding" your business into the customer's subconscious. Consistency beats perfection.

### Task 4: Collect and Tell Transformation Stories

Testimonials that follow the hero's transformation arc are far more powerful than generic praise ("Nancy is great!").

**The 5-question testimonial framework:**
1. What was the problem or challenge you faced before engaging us?
2. What did you find when you started working with us / using our product?
3. What specific result or transformation did you experience?
4. What would you tell someone who is hesitating to engage us?
5. How has your life/business changed as a result?

Use these questions as an interview script or written form. Edit responses into a case study or video testimonial.

## Using StoryBrand for Internal Culture

The BrandScript also fights the **Narrative Void** — the vacancy inside an organisation when there is no unifying story. Employees become disengaged not because they lack skill but because they don't understand the story they're in.

A StoryBrand-aligned organisation:
- Onboards new staff with the customer's story (not just company history)
- Frames job roles as plot roles in the customer's journey
- Shares customer transformation stories internally — celebrating customer wins, not just revenue targets
- Uses the BrandScript as the filter for all internal communication and decision-making

## Uganda / East Africa Application

**Customer-as-hero framing:**
In Uganda/EA markets, the hero narrative resonates especially strongly because customers often feel overlooked, underserved, or condescended to by institutions. Positioning the customer as the hero — and the brand as their guide — breaks the paternalistic pattern common in development-funded or large-institution messaging.

**Villain examples for common Uganda sectors:**

| Sector | External Problem | Internal Problem | Villain |
|---|---|---|---|
| Agricultural inputs | Low yields, crop failure | Fear of losing everything | Erratic weather + lack of knowledge |
| Financial services | No credit history | Shame of poverty | Predatory lenders / inaccessible banks |
| Education/skills | No certificate | "I'm not smart enough" | A system that ignores informal talent |
| Food processing | UNBS compliance gap | Fear of being shut down | Unclear regulatory requirements |
| Business planning | No bankable plan | "Banks never help people like me" | Complexity and financial jargon |

**Aspirational identities in Uganda context:**
- From: *struggling boda driver* → To: *asset-owning transport entrepreneur*
- From: *informal food seller* → To: *certified food brand owner*
- From: *first-generation business owner* → To: *job creator in my community*

**One-liner for Uganda planning service:**
> "We help Ugandan entrepreneurs who can't get a bank loan create a business plan that makes financiers say yes."

**Lead generator ideas for Uganda businesses:**
- "5 Reasons Banks Reject Loan Applications (and How to Avoid Each One)" — for any financial services business plan
- "How to Price Your Products So You Never Sell at a Loss" — for small manufacturers and traders
- "The Uganda Business Registration Checklist: Get Your TIN and Licence in 3 Steps" — for any startup

## Integration with Other Business Plan Sections

| Business Plan Section | Where StoryBrand Applies |
|---|---|
| **01 Executive Summary** | One-liner; brand story narrative (replace company-centric opener with hero framing) |
| **02 Company Overview** | Guide positioning (complement to Golden Circle / WHY); mission statement as guide's empathy statement |
| **03 Products & Services** | Frame products as the customer's plan — not features, but tools the hero needs to win |
| **05 Target Market** | Define the hero (character + aspirational identity) and the villain (external/internal/philosophical problems) |
| **06 Competitive Analysis** | Competitors who position themselves as heroes are vulnerable — position your client as the guide |
| **07 Marketing & Sales Strategy** | BrandScript → one-liner → website 5-step → lead generator → email nurture sequence → testimonials |
| **09 Management Team** | Founders are guides, not heroes — frame biographies around what they have conquered that the customer now faces |
| **Pitch Deck** | One-liner on slide 1; hero problem on slide 2; guide credentials slide 3; plan slide 4; success vision slide 5 |

```

            who want _________________

PROBLEM:    Villain: _________________
            External: ________________
            Internal: ________________
            Philosophical: ___________

GUIDE:      Empathy: "We understand ___"
            Authority: _______________

PLAN:       Step 1: _________________
            Step 2: _________________
            Step 3: _________________

CTA:        Direct: _________________
            Transitional: ___________

FAILURE:    If they don't act: _______

            To: _____________________
```
