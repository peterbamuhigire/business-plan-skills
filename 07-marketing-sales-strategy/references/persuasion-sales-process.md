## 1. Core Persuasion Principles

### The Persuasion Process

The process of influencing and persuading is about finding people's pain points, opening the pain tactfully, and then healing their pain points by providing new solutions through products or services.

1. **Find their deepest wants** — People will not buy something they do not feel they need. They purchase what they want.
2. **Find their pain points** — Your job is to use your products or services to heal them.
3. **Show them how to heal their pain points** — By getting clients to associate their most desired feelings to your products or services.

### Pleasure and Pain Principle

- People do things that give them enjoyment and avoid situations that cause pain.
- People are **far more motivated to avoid pain** than to gain pleasure.
- If someone does not buy, they associate more pain with buying than joy from having the product.
- If someone buys, they associate more happiness with the purchase than with not purchasing.
- **Key action**: Drive clients by consequences — negative consequences if they do not buy, positive outcomes if they do.

### Three Buying Drivers

- **Emotional reasons** people buy (desire, excitement, fear of missing out)
- **Logical reasons** people buy (facts, data, justification)
- **Major reasons** people do not buy (objections, fears, concerns)

**The goal**: Make emotional and logical reasons overwhelm the significant reasons they do not buy.

**Closing formula when sale is on the fence:**
1. Bring up their pain points to create more WANT
2. Add emotional reasons they need to buy
3. Add logical reasons to help them justify the purchase

### Four Pillars of Massive Influence

1. **Questions** — The most important persuasion tool. Questions build rapport, discover beliefs, reveal decision-making patterns, alleviate pressure, bring out objections upfront, demonstrate care, and enable test closing.
2. **Emotional discipline** — Control over your own emotions. Ability to take negative situations and turn them into positives.
3. **Rapport** — Long-term relationship building. People must feel you care, that you put them first, and that you are alike.
4. **Congruency** — What you say and who you are must match. Confidence that the value of your product far exceeds the cost. Refresh daily to avoid the Law of Familiarity (taking products for granted).

## 3. The 10-Step Sales Process

### Phase 1: Involvement

#### Step 1: Preparation

When we feel nervous and reluctant to engage clients, it stems from a lack of preparation.

**How to prepare:**
- **Know your clients** — Research needs, wants, and pain points. How do they spend money and time? Use internet, referrals, friends, co-workers.
- **Understand your competitors** — Know your advantages. Be tactful, never speak badly about competitors.
- **Create desire** — Create situations where clients convince themselves of the value, for their own reasons.
- **Have a positive expectation** — Be ready for the worst, but expect the best. Place yourself in a positive emotional frame while being prepared for changes.
- **Deep understanding of products/services** — Create a detailed list of benefits that matter to clients. So detailed you have an abundance of reasons they must buy.
- **Prepare for common objections** — Know precisely how to handle the most frequent objections before they come up.

#### Step 2: Peak Emotional State (Transferring Emotions)

Your peak performance comes from your peak emotional state. You are not selling products or services — you are selling your emotions.

- People directly associate your feelings and emotions to your products or services.
- If you are in a negative place, your negativity affects what you are selling.
- Any time you are in a powerful emotional state (positive or negative), those emotions become attached to anything happening around you.

**How to reach peak state:**
1. Pick an empowering word (e.g. "YES!") and a physical motion (clapping, jumping, snapping fingers)
2. Link your emotions to that word as an anchor
3. Change your body by getting in motion and using the empowering word
4. Use an internal 1-10 scale — goal is to feel far beyond a 10 before engaging clients

**Daily habits for emotional state:**
- Ask powerfully positive questions ("What can I be grateful for? What excites me today?")
- Move your body differently (clapping, snapping, physical motion)
- Make 12-15 Swish Patterns (visualise positive image bursting through negative image)

#### Step 3: Contact Your Clients

The Law of Averages: the more contacts you make, the more money you make.

**Making great connections:**
- **Have a plan for what you will say** — Use a script and know your focus. Practice so you are not focused on what to say next.
- **Make a ton of contacts** — Call and set appointments at your peak emotional state.
- **Get creative and have fun** — Find new and innovative ways to make contact.
- **Set specific, measurable goals** — Decide number of contacts weekly, monthly, yearly.
- **Always ask for referrals** — Whether the client purchases or not. Set up your next sale before your current deal is over.

**Why people fail to make contact:**
- They are unprepared
- They are not in peak emotional frame
- They believe they will be interrupting (you are only interrupting until you have their attention)

**8 ways to get client attention:**
1. **Compliment** — Tell them something genuine you like about them, with feeling
2. **Demonstration** — Show the client something or exhibit your product
3. **Intrigue** — Ask an engaging question or create suspense
4. **Additional information** — Offer information they are interested in but do not have
5. **Pain points** — Stir up emotion regarding their hurts
6. **Smile** — Already being in a positive state when you meet
7. **Referrals** — Use a reference to build instant rapport through third-party validation
8. **Questions** — Ask a question that grabs attention

#### Step 4: Build a Deep Relationship

The essential part of any sale is not the closing process — it is building rapport.

**Why clients choose you over competitors with the same offering:**
- They trust you
- They like you
- They have confidence in your ability to deliver
- They believe you have their best interests at heart
- They believe you will always put them first

**The power of compliments (3-step formula):**
1. Tell the person something you sincerely like or appreciate
2. Use the word "Because" to justify delivering the compliment
3. End the compliment by asking a question related to the praise

**How to use compliments:**
- Deliver compliments about other people (they will get back to the subject)
- Provide praise that shows you are paying attention to small details
- Write thank-you notes to clients
- Deliver 3rd-party compliments — let the client know what others like about them

**Developing massive rapport:**
1. **Mirroring** — Reflect the client's actions: rate of speech, way they speak, body language, words they use. Body language makes up more than half of all communication.
2. **Search for commonalities** — People like people who are like them. The more in common, the more rapport.
3. **Go at the client's speed** — Match their pace first, then gradually change it. The client will unconsciously follow.

**Qualities to mirror:**
- **Voice**: Keywords, volume, tonality, tempo, timbre
- **Body language**: Movements, facial expressions, gestures, eye contact, breathing patterns, physical proximity, touching, posture

#### Step 5: Captivate Them

Do not go into a presentation when the client is not yet interested. Get them excited to hear more first.

**How to captivate clients:**
- **Social proof** — Talk about something or someone they know
- **Ask questions** — Shows interest, gives client control, focuses attention on their wants
- **Demonstrate** — Show product/service and make clients part of the experience

**6-step benefit captivation formula:**
1. **Claim** something about your product or service that interests the client
2. **Follow up with a logical fact** beginning with "Because" to justify it
3. **Tell the client how it will benefit them** — "What's in it for me!"
4. **Follow up with an emotional benefit** — What will it do for them emotionally?
5. **Give evidence of your claim** — Something concrete to support your statements
6. **Ask an open-ended question** about their wants and needs

**Gaining trust and belief:**
- Deliver testimonials (letters, emails)
- Showcase product/service quality
- Provide evidence through stories and examples
- Show verifiable data
- Use analogies to relate to something the client understands
- Provide facts about products, services, or business
- Demonstrate what you are selling — when it works as claimed, there is no disbelief

### Phase 2: Qualify and Magnify

#### Step 6: Qualify Clients and Magnify Pain Points

Average salespeople try to find out a little, do a full presentation, and hard close. Professional persuaders discover what clients need, want, how they make decisions, and how they justify purchases.

**The goal**: Get people to associate their pains with not having something. Discover their pain points and magnify them to create desire for your solution.

##### WPNID Qualification Framework

Qualify every prospect on all five criteria before presenting:

| Criterion | Key Question | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Wants** | What does your client honestly want? | Discover true desires |
| **Pain** | What are your client's pain points? | Find problems to solve |
| **Needs** | What does your client need? | Identify requirements |
| **Income** | Does your client have the money? | Confirm budget/ability to pay |
| **Decision** | Can your client make the final purchasing decision? Do they have authority or must they decide with someone else? | Identify decision-maker |

**Critical rule**: Do not go through a full presentation just to find out they do not have the want, pain point, need, money, or decision-making power.

##### 11 Patterns of Client Thought

Understanding these patterns helps deliver information in a way that drives purchase decisions:

| Pattern | Discovery Question | Client Types | How to Sell |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Frame of reference** | "How do you know when you've done an outstanding job?" | **Internal**: Look within themselves. **External**: Need others' acknowledgement. | Internal: align with their values. External: use statistics, data, testimonials. |
| **2. Motivation patterns** | Observe their actions | Interested in effect on: other people, themselves, or the numbers | Match messaging to their focus |
| **3. Possible vs. needed** | "What made you choose your current career/product?" | **Possibility**: Inspired by what could be. **Necessity**: Inspired by what they need. | Possibility: paint the future. Necessity: show what they need to do. |
| **4. Polarity** | Observe responses | **Positive**: See how things are alike. **Opposite**: Focus on differences, do the opposite. | Positive: compare to past positive experiences. Opposite: use reverse psychology. |
| **5. Push or pull** | "What are your wants in a career/product?" | **Pull**: Motivated by pleasure and desire. **Push**: Motivated by fear and pain. | Pull: paint goals and rewards. Push: describe consequences and pain points. |
| **6. Value vs. price** | "What means more: quality or cost-effectiveness?" | **Value-oriented**: Pay more for convenience. **Price-oriented**: Cost over perceived quality. | Match emphasis to their priority |
| **7. Finisher vs. process** | "What gives more fulfilment: finishing or the process?" | **Finisher**: Must complete what they start. **Process**: Enjoy the journey. | Finisher: emphasise end result. Process: may need multiple meetings. |
| **8. Forest vs. trees** | "What do you value more: overview or details?" | **Forest**: Want the big picture. **Trees**: Want granular details. | Forest: summary only. Trees: detailed information. |
| **9. Past vs. future** | "What do you value more: track record or future possibilities?" | **Past**: Want proven longevity. **Future**: Want to know where it is going. | Past: show track record. Future: show possibilities ahead. |
| **10. Belief evidence** | "When do you believe someone is good — seeing, reading, doing, or hearing?" | **Sight, hearing, feeling, or data** — plus timeframe: automatic, few exposures, long time, or continuous re-proof | Match evidence type and frequency to their belief style |
| **11. Communication style** | Observe speech patterns and behaviour | **Feel**: Speak slowly and quietly. **Visual**: Speak louder/faster, use picture words. **Digital**: Logic, facts, data driven. **Hearing**: Articulate words carefully, rhythmic movements. | Enter the client's world using their preferred style |

#### Step 7: Cultivate Belief and Test Close

After qualifying, give clients logical reasons to justify buying and do temperature checks.

**Creating belief and justification:**
- Create enough compelling reasons (emotional and logical) that overpower reasons not to buy
- Ensure what you say matches your inner belief in the product
- Give clients enough compelling reasons to justify logically

**6+1 Step belief-building formula:**
1. Claim something about your products or services
2. Follow up with a logical fact
3. Tell the client how it will benefit them
4. Follow up with an emotional benefit
5. Give the client evidence of your claim
6. Ask open-ended questions about wants and needs
7. **Test close**: Start with a powerful phrase ("I'd like your opinion..." / "How do you feel...")

**Build at least 12 compelling reasons** using this formula to have an abundance of justifications ready at any time.

### Phase 3: Urgency

#### Step 8: Make it Tangible and Take an Assumptive Posture

Making it tangible means giving clients a sensory experience — less about data and logic, more about showing.

**The principle:**
- Clients who purchase believe their buying experience will be more pleasurable
- Clients who do not buy have linked more pain to the purchase than pleasure from obtaining it
- When you make their experience tangible (all senses), you are in position to be assumptive

**Direct, Exclaim, Visualise technique:**

| Technique | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct** | Rephrase what they want back to them. Direct their mind back to a fear or pain point. | "You said you wanted X which will provide Y. I remember you said [pain point]. Is this still correct?" |
| **Exclaim** | Tell them your product/service will do what they are looking for. Bring up biggest fear and how you solve it. | Assure the client and go into details about what they will receive that heals their fears and concerns. |
| **Visualise** | Paint what life will look like when they purchase. Tap into imagination. | "When you invest in our services, imagine a Saturday at 8am... you won't get those calls pulling you away from your family... Is this what you want to have?" |

**The power of questions in creating tangible experiences:**
- Questions induce reciprocity — whatever you ask, the client feels compelled to answer
- Questions change the focus of clients
- Questions change what people keep and what they remove from their minds

#### Step 9: Transform Client Objections into Client Commitments

Most salespeople see at least five objections before closing. Professional persuaders welcome objections as opportunities.

**What an objection really is:**
- Your opportunity to find out what the client is thinking
- Your best chance to close
- A question (the client is asking for more information on something that interests them)
- Your moment to discover what the client believes in and what they fear

##### 5-Step Objection Transformation Process

| Step | Action | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| **a. Ignore** | Ignore the objection the first time | It may be an unconscious reflex reaction. Use open-ended questions that do not end in yes or no. |
| **b. Listen** | If same objection comes up a second time, hear them out | Let the client vent. They will eventually run out of energy, often solving their own objection. |
| **c. Repeat** | Repeat the objection back as a question | "You don't have the money?" then stop and listen. Puts people in position to clarify their words. |
| **d. Dig deeper** | Find the real objection | "I'm sure you have a reason for saying that, what is it?" Listen carefully — this is a definite closing moment. |
| **e. Turn into commitment** | If you can solve the objection, would they move forward? | "If we could show you how to [solve their concern], in your opinion, do you feel you'd want to get started?" |

##### Additional Objection Handling Techniques

After the 5-step process, layer these techniques:

| Technique | How It Works | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Put client at ease** | Sit on the same side of the table. Be understanding, respectful, agreeable. | "I agree and..." / "I appreciate this about you and..." / "I respect X about you and..." |
| **Reframe as question** | "The question is how can we solve [objection] and give you [2-3 benefits]. That's the real question, right?" | Redirects focus to solution |
| **Answer with pain-point question** | "Isn't the real question, how can we get you [benefit] and if we don't, wouldn't it cost you [their pain points]?" | Leverages their own stated pain |
| **Outweigh with value** | "Can't we agree that receiving X, Y, Z is more important than this concern?" | Shifts weight of comparison |
| **Explain through** | Walk them through the solution logically | "You'll have a team dedicated to your needs, saving you those five days..." |
| **Tactful denial** | Challenge the objection with competitive evidence | "It doesn't cost too much when you compare our competition and the benefits you receive." |
| **Reduce to daily cost** | Break down the investment to its smallest unit | "$100/year = $8.33/month = $0.27/day. Are you going to let [pain] continue over $0.27 a day?" |
| **Gain agreement and test close** | "By doing X, that would solve the problem, wouldn't it?" | If yes, proceed to test close. If no, dig deeper (back to step d). |

##### Pre-framing Objections

Handle objections before they arise by addressing them proactively:
- "I realise the investment is challenging. We have this investment because our system consistently produces 6-figure opportunities. That's what sets us apart."

##### Minimise, Reduce, and Eliminate Objections

- Compare the objection to a benefit that overwhelms it
- Give to others and they will give back (reciprocity)
- Build massive rapport
- Test close often
- Pre-frame objections and handle them before they become big problems

#### Step 10: Create a Lasting Relationship and Make Buying Simple

**Make buying simple:**
- Pre-frame objections and handle them before reaching this step
- Serve clients and provide additional value like an unexpected gift
- Always stay aligned with the client — never get defensive or attack if they raise another objection

**Buyer's remorse prevention:**
- Create a long-term relationship between client and product/service
- Create a future so they understand what they will be doing with their purchase down the road
- Right after making the sale, engage the client's imagination about their future with the product
- Have them describe how their future looks with the product and how it benefits them in years ahead
- Example: "What will you be doing in X years, now that you have this? How do you see yourself?"
- This gives them ammunition to justify the purchase if anyone criticises their decision

## 4. Customer Psychology and Buying Triggers

### What the Prospect is Always Thinking

1. Will your products or services satisfy my wants and needs?
2. What is in this for me? Can you prove it? What is it?
3. Why do I need your product or service NOW?
4. Can you be trusted? Am I a transaction, or are you here to serve my interests?

### Why People Buy

- People buy on **emotion** and justify **logically**
- People need to feel motivation towards buying — they will not purchase unless they feel pressure
- **Outside influence**: External pressure from salesperson, advertising
- **Self-pressure**: Internal desire for something (much more potent — people always purchase for their own reasons)

### An Unmotivated Client Will Not Purchase

Professional persuaders get clients to:
- **Associate** their products/services with the client's feelings of desire
- **Link** not purchasing with the client's deepest pain points

### Types of Beliefs That Drive Decisions

1. **Blanket beliefs**: "You are..." / "I am..." / "The world is..." / "The rich are..." — These colour what things mean and how they are viewed. Never attack a person's belief system. Use questions to discover and align.
2. **Cause and effect beliefs**: "If I do X, then it is going to mean Y" — These give decisions a guiding light.

## 6. Test Closing Techniques

### Three Types of Test Closes

| Type | Purpose | Example | Benefit |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Opening test close** | Find where client is in the sales process | "Why are you seriously considering working from home?" | Reveals seriousness and emotional/logical buying reasons |
| **Trade-off test close** | Overwhelm reasons not to buy with benefits of buying | "Would it be worth X to have Y?" or "To complete your goal of X, would it be worth investing Y?" | Shifts the comparison in your favour |
| **Progressive test close** | Help client focus on purchasing | "If you felt this was a way to take control of your financial future, how would you find a way to make this happen?" | Client focuses on what they want instead of what they do not want |

### When to Test Close

- Start test closing from the very beginning of the interaction
- Do not wait until the end to discover the client is not with you
- Continuous test closing reveals: where the client is, what actions you need to take, potential outcome, and when to do a real close

### Client Buying Physiology (Signals to Close)

| Signal | What to Observe |
|---|---|
| **Body posture** | Relaxed and comfortable posture |
| **Demeanour** | Friendly, future-projecting the product as if they already own it |
| **Facial expressions** | More colour in face, smiling more |
| **Hands** | Open palms, touching their face, becoming more kinesthetic with products, wanting to feel and test |

## 7. Closing Strategies

- **Congratulate** them on the decision they have made (assumptive)
- **Tell a joke** and get them laughing before the close
- **Validate** their decision by assuring them it is the right time ("It's excellent you're getting this now. Soon this won't be accessible this way because of high demand.")
- **Open the application/order form** and begin filling it out with them
- **Give different choices** by asking a question ("Would you like this big or small? Today or tomorrow? Red or white?")
- **Give decision-making power** with a smaller decision ("How would you like this order shipped?")
- **Assumptive posture and validate**: "Congratulations! Could you spell your first and last name for me, please?"

## 8. Referral and Retention System

### Asking for Referrals (Script)

"My business can only grow through people who trust me and tell other people about the service I provided them. Those people refer me to people who can benefit the most from my services. How has my service been to you? If I offered you exceptional service, would you do me a favour? Who are five people you know that could also benefit from my services?"

### Referral Best Practices

- Encourage clients to personally call a couple of their referrals to provide positive feedback on you
- Provide incentives (gifts or compensation) for successful referrals
- Expect and believe you will receive referrals
- Research referrals before contacting them
- Ask for referrals whether or not you make the sale
- A reference can save many cold calls

### Referral Contact System

- **Keep a referral file** in alphabetical order with 13 sections
- Contact A-B in week 1, C-D in week 2, etc.
- Each client gets contacted at minimum 4 times per year

### Contacting Referrals (Script — Third-Party Compliment Method)

**Wrong way**: "Hi, my name is X from Y company, and your friend Z told me I should give you a call because you might like this."

**Right way**: "Hi X, you don't know me; however, we have a mutual friend whose name is Y. He/she shared something with me about you that I thought was incredible [share the compliment], and he/she thought you would benefit from what we have here. By the way, my name is A from B company."

## 11. Mastery and Implementation

### Five-Step Mastery Process

1. **Change** — Create an impact on yourself to change anything
2. **Repetition** — Doing something over and over makes you a master
3. **Execution** — Taking action and repeating in everyday life
4. **Habits** — Turn skills into daily habits; they become automatic and effortless
5. **Utilisation** — Continue utilising or you will lose it

### Four Steps to Learning (Maslow)

1. **Unconscious incompetence** — You do not know what you do not know
2. **Conscious incompetence** — You are aware you do not know something
3. **Conscious competence** — You learn it, but it takes full concentration
4. **Unconscious competence** — You can do it without thinking

### Implementation Approach

- Focus on improving one step at a time rather than memorising all ten and executing rigidly
- Any single step you improve will enhance all other steps
- Master one skill, move to the next, until all ten are learned
- At that point, the process becomes as effortless as breathing
