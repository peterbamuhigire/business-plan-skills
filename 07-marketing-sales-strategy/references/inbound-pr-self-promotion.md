# Inbound PR and Self-Promotion Reference

This file draws on two practitioner books published in the 1990s that remain foundational to low-cost, reputation-first marketing for service businesses and solo practitioners:

- **Edwards, P., Edwards, S. & Douglas, L.C. (1991).** *Getting Business to Come to You.* Jeremy P. Tarcher/Perigee. — A systems approach to pull marketing: building a reputation, referral machine, and expert profile so that clients arrive pre-sold.
- **Pinskey, R. (1997).** *101 Ways to Promote Yourself.* Avon Books. — A tactic-by-tactic playbook for professionals and entrepreneurs who must build their own public profile, structured across 101 discrete actions.

Together they argue that the most cost-efficient marketing for small service businesses is expert positioning rather than paid advertising, executed through writing, speaking, referrals, and systematic relationship maintenance. This file complements `guerrilla-tactics.md` (Levinson) and `small-business-marketing-tactics.md` (Jarvis) — cross-reference those files for advertising formats, customer retention, and digital tactics.

## 1. The Marketing Pyramid

Edwards & Douglas present a "pull" model structured as a six-level pyramid. The apex (highest trust, lowest cost per lead) is word-of-mouth referral; the base (lowest trust, highest cost per lead) is paid advertising.

```
               [Word-of-Mouth Referrals]        ← Most valuable; highest trust
              [Speaking / Articles / PR]
             [Networking / Associations]
            [Direct Mail / Newsletter]
           [Yellow Pages / Classified Ads]
          [Paid Advertising]                     ← Most expensive; lowest trust
```

**Strategic implication:** Invest from the top down. Every hour spent becoming known as an expert (writing, speaking, PR) returns more per pound of marketing investment than time spent buying ads. Paid advertising belongs at the base only — used to fill gaps in reach once the top layers are active.

**Why paid advertising is least efficient for service businesses:**
- It reaches strangers with no prior trust relationship.
- Service businesses are purchased primarily on trust — trust requires repeated exposure or a recommendation from someone known.
- Ads cease the moment you stop paying; expert reputation compounds indefinitely.

## 2. The Referral Machine

*(Edwards, Chapter 2)*

The referral machine is a four-step structured process for generating systematic word-of-mouth. It is not passive — it requires deliberate management.

### Step 1 — Identify Your Referral Sources
- Past and current clients (the most important category)
- Professional colleagues — accountants, lawyers, other service providers who see the same clients
- Complementary businesses — non-competing services serving the same client type
- Industry association contacts and committee members

### Step 2 — Make It Easy to Refer
- Give every referral source a single clear sentence describing your ideal prospect: "I'm looking for Kampala-based SME owners who need a bank loan and don't know how to prepare the plan."
- Leave surplus business cards and brochures with key referral sources; replenish when stocks run low.
- Create a memorable elevator pitch the referrer can use verbatim — do not make them improvise your description.

### Step 3 — Acknowledge and Reward Every Referral
- Thank every referrer within 48 hours — minimum a handwritten card; for high-value referrals, a small gift, a lunch, or a public acknowledgement in your newsletter.
- Never let a referral go unacknowledged. A single missed acknowledgement depresses future referrals from that source.

### Step 4 — Close the Loop
- Update the referrer on the outcome: "Your referral became a client — thank you." Or: "We didn't connect this time, but I appreciate you thinking of me."
- This keeps referrers engaged and demonstrates respect for their relationship capital.

### Uganda Trust Culture Note
Uganda's economy is relationship-driven. The dominant purchase trigger for professional services is "told by someone I know" — the equivalent of Karamoja-born trust. Referral marketing is disproportionately effective compared with markets where strangers routinely purchase online. Every shilling invested in the referral machine yields a higher return in Uganda than in more transactional markets.

## 3. Networking Follow-Up System

*(Edwards, Chapter 3 + Pinskey, Way 47)*

Networking is not collecting business cards. It is building relationships with people who can refer, hire, or champion you. The follow-up system is what converts an event into business.

### Pre-Event Preparation
- Research the attendee list if published; identify two or three people worth a meaningful conversation.
- Rehearse a 30-second introduction: name + what you do + who you serve + one current project or result.
- Carry business cards in your left pocket; received cards go in the right pocket — keep them separate.
- Set a goal: two or three meaningful conversations, not twenty card exchanges.

### At the Event
- Arrive early — easier to start conversations when the room is not yet crowded.
- Use open body language; avoid checking your phone.
- Ask questions and listen: "What are you working on that excites you right now?"
- End conversations graciously: "I don't want to monopolise your time — it was great meeting you."
- Listen for problems you can solve; do not pitch until asked.

### Post-Event: 48-Hour Follow-Up
- Write personal notes on received cards immediately after the event while memories are fresh.
- Send a brief personal message to every A-pile contact within 48 hours — reference the specific conversation.
- Add all contacts to a database with a note on where you met and what was discussed.

### Card-Sorting System
Sort received cards into three piles:
- **A — Promising:** Follow up within 48 hours with a personalised note or call.
- **B — Possible:** Add to mailing list; include in quarterly newsletter.
- **C — No fit:** File for reference or discard.

### 90-Day Keep-in-Touch Calendar
- For every A-pile contact, schedule a diary entry every 90 days.
- Touchpoint formats: article clipping relevant to their business, congratulatory note on a milestone, brief check-in call, or invitation to a free event.
- Consistency over 90-day cycles converts a cold contact into a warm referral source within 12 months.

## 4. The Article Marketing Loop

*(Edwards, Chapter 5 + Pinskey, Way 39)*

One article in the right publication does more for your reputation than six months of paid advertising. The loop multiplies its value across several channels.

### The 7-Step Cycle
1. **Write** — a how-to article on a topic your clients ask about frequently; 600–1,200 words.
2. **Publish** — in a trade journal, association newsletter, or local business paper.
3. **Reprint** — high-quality copies with the publication masthead clearly visible (proof of publication).
4. **Mail** — send reprints to all clients, referral sources, prospects, and media contacts.
5. **Speak** — each published article generates three or four speaking enquiries; accept them.
6. **Collect testimonials** — readers who contact you become case studies and testimonials for the next article.
7. **Repeat** — the loop compounds; the second article reaches a larger audience than the first.

### Uganda Publication Targets
| Publication | Format | Audience |
|---|---|---|
| Daily Monitor (Business section) | Contributed opinion / how-to | Kampala business professionals, SME owners |
| New Vision (Your Money) | Feature / expert column | Mass market; regional readers |
| CEO East Africa | Thought leadership | Senior managers, entrepreneurs, investors |
| Uganda Manufacturers Association Newsletter | Technical how-to | Manufacturing sector SMEs |
| Local chamber of commerce bulletins | Practical tip | Members of Kampala, Mbarara, Gulu chambers |

### Article-to-Speaking Pipeline
A published article establishes you as an authority before you speak. Reference the article when submitting a speaking proposal — it is your credentials in one page.

### Success Metric
Pinskey cites one contributor to a national US trade journal who received 756 enquiries from a single article — equivalent at USD 350 per client to approximately UGX 9.7M in business (at UGX 3,700/$1 planning rate). Scale down for Uganda's market, but the principle holds: one article in the right journal reaches thousands of prospects at zero media cost.

## 5. Speaking as a Marketing Strategy

*(Edwards, Chapter 5 + Pinskey, Ways 63–68)*

### The Free-to-Fee Sequence (7 Steps)
1. **Start free** — local business associations, chamber of commerce breakfast meetings, Rotary, Lions clubs.
2. **Submit conference proposals** — offer to speak for expenses only; industry events.
3. **Develop a signature presentation** — 45–60 minutes on your core expertise; rehearse it until it is fluent.
4. **Capture contact details** — use a sign-up sheet ("resources mentioned in today's talk") for every audience.
5. **Follow up every attendee within five days** — email, message, or brief note.
6. **Document speaking experience** — running list of events, audience sizes, and organiser testimonials.
7. **Transition to fees** — once you have five or more documented engagements with testimonials, approach corporate events, conferences, and government bodies with a fee.

### Uganda Venues
- Kampala Chamber of Commerce and Industry (KCCI) breakfast events
- Uganda Institute of Banking and Financial Services (UIBFS) seminars
- Uganda Manufacturers Association (UMA) trade fair sessions
- National Social Security Fund (NSSF) financial literacy programmes
- District-level business forums (Gulu, Mbarara, Jinja)
- Industry association annual conferences

### Radio and TV Interview Process
*(Pinskey, Ways 66–67)*
1. Identify talk-format shows; listen to several episodes to understand the host's style and audience.
2. Send a one-page pitch: your topic, why it matters to their listeners, three questions you can answer.
3. Offer to do a live call-in segment — the station gets interactivity; you get a longer segment.
4. Provide a resource (free report, WhatsApp number) listeners can use to contact you.
5. On TV: wear solid colours (not patterns); bring a prop or visual if the topic allows; prepare a 30-second "billboard" — what you want viewers to remember and how to reach you.

**Uganda radio targets:** KFM, Capital FM, CBS, Radio One, and vernacular stations (Bukedde Radio, Radio Simba) all carry business and lifestyle talk shows. A clear, practical topic with local examples — "Five mistakes Kampala SMEs make when applying for a bank loan" — will get you booked.

### Speaker Sheet Contents
A one-page document sent to event organisers:
- Headshot (professional photograph)
- Topic areas (two or three, expressed as benefit headlines)
- Audience feedback quotes (at least two named testimonials)
- Brief professional biography (four to six sentences)
- Contact information

## 6. Press Release Structure

*(Edwards, Chapter 4 + Pinskey, Way 87)*

### Inverted Pyramid Template
```
Paragraph 1: Who / What / Where / When / Why
             (the complete story in 35 words; if the editor reads nothing else, this is enough)

Paragraph 2: Most important supporting detail
             (the next most newsworthy fact)

Paragraph 3: Quote from the principal
             (owner, CEO, or named expert — in quotation marks)

Paragraph 4: Background and context
             (sector data, history of the business, additional facts)

Paragraph 5: Boilerplate company description
             (three sentences: what the company does, who it serves, when it was founded)

[Contact name, phone number, email — on every page]
```

### Formatting Rules
- Maximum 250 words / one page, double-spaced.
- Headline at the top that summarises the story in eight to ten words.
- Written in third person — not "we" or "our".
- Least important facts at the bottom; editors cut from the bottom upward.
- Send by email as plain text in the body, not as a PDF attachment.

### Newsworthiness Criteria (6 Types)
1. **Timeliness** — linked to a current event, season, or national policy change.
2. **Local angle** — "first in Uganda"; "serving Gulu's growing middle class."
3. **Human interest** — the individual story behind the business; the founder's journey.
4. **Counter-intuitive finding** — data or a result that challenges common assumptions.
5. **Controversy** — a position that invites debate (use carefully, but editors value it).
6. **Statistics** — a survey finding or market data point, especially if it contradicts received wisdom.

### When to Send a Release (7 Triggers)
1. New product or service launch
2. Company milestone (first anniversary, 1,000th customer, UGX 1 billion revenue)
3. Award received or granted
4. Appointment of key staff
5. Participation in a major event (trade fair, national conference)
6. Survey finding or research result you commissioned or conducted
7. New partnership or joint venture

### Uganda Local Angle Examples
- "First certified organic honey exporter in Lango sub-region awarded UNBS quality mark"
- "Kampala business planning firm reports 94% bank loan success rate for clients in FY 2025/26"
- "Women-owned logistics company creates 40 jobs in Wakiso district after UDB loan approval"

## 7. Feature Story Pitch

*(Pinskey, Way 88)*

A feature story is worth more than six months of advertising. The pitch letter to an editor must be brief, direct, and written from the editor's perspective — not yours.

### The 100-Word Rule
Maximum 100 words in the pitch letter. An editor's attention span is approximately four seconds. Lead with what makes the story different; attach a media kit but keep the letter minimal.

**Pitch structure:**
- Sentence 1: The story in one compelling sentence.
- Sentence 2–3: Why it matters to this publication's readers specifically.
- Sentence 4–5: The angle and your unique access or expertise.
- Sentence 6: What you are offering (interview, access, data, exclusive).
- Close: contact details.

### What Editors Want
- Stories that are interesting, valuable, or entertaining for their readership.
- Angles that are different, exciting, informative, important, or unique.
- NOT press releases dressed as stories.
- A pre-packaged story that requires minimal additional research from their staff.

### Media Kit Contents (6 Elements)
1. Professional photograph of you and/or the product or service in action.
2. Brief biography — professional credentials only (personal only if directly relevant).
3. Q&A — ten questions and answers about you, the company, and the service.
4. Reprints of any published articles (proof of subject-matter credibility).
5. Testimonials from named clients with titles and company names.
6. Contact information on every single page.

### Calendar Listings Tactic
Every newspaper, magazine, radio station, and website has a community calendar. Submit event listings three to four weeks before the event. Include: what, where, when, cost, contact. Send to every outlet in your city — event organisers read everything. This is free.

## 8. Niche Specialist Positioning

*(Edwards, Chapter 10)*

### The Generalist Trap
"I'll serve anyone" means you are no one's first choice. Specialists are perceived as more skilled, can charge higher fees, and receive more referrals because people know exactly who to send to them. A generalist who tries to appeal to everyone competes on price; a specialist competes on expertise.

### 4 Niche Selection Criteria
1. You have genuine expertise or passion in this area (clients will probe for this).
2. The niche is large enough to sustain your revenue target.
3. The niche has unmet needs — not already fully served by other specialists.
4. You can become known in the niche's community: its media, associations, events, and online forums.

### Positioning Statement Formula
> "I help [specific client type] who struggle with [specific problem] to [specific outcome] without [common fear or obstacle]."

### Uganda Examples
| Specialist Position | Statement |
|---|---|
| NGO accountant | "I help Kampala-based NGOs struggling with donor audit requirements to file compliant financial reports without risking deregistration." |
| Manufacturing lawyer | "I help Uganda manufacturing companies facing UNBS compliance issues to resolve regulatory disputes without shutting down production." |
| Business planning consultant | "I help Ugandan SME owners preparing for their first bank loan to build a bankable business plan without wasting months on formats the bank will reject." |
| Agri-export specialist | "I help Ugandan smallholder cooperatives seeking EU market access to meet EUDR traceability requirements without hiring a full-time compliance officer." |

Once the positioning statement is clear, every article, press release, and speaking topic should reinforce it. Consistent repetition of a specific claim creates a specialist reputation faster than breadth of service.

## 9. Special Reports

*(Pinskey, Way 42)*

A special report is a four to twelve page in-depth guide on a specific topic. It is a marketing document that provides genuine value — which is why it works. Recipients trust it enough to pass it to colleagues.

### What to Put Inside
- Solutions to a problem your clients frequently face.
- Survey findings or statistics (primary or secondary research; cite sources).
- A step-by-step "how-to" process relevant to your service.
- Trends affecting your client's business sector.

### 5 Headline Triggers
| Trigger | Example |
|---|---|
| How to make money from... | "How to Make Money from Your Existing Client Base Without Advertising" |
| How to save money on... | "How to Save UGX 2 Million on Your Annual Tax Bill" |
| How to choose the right... | "How to Choose the Right Accountant for Your Kampala SME" |
| How to avoid... | "How to Avoid the 5 Most Common Reasons Ugandan Banks Reject Business Plans" |
| How to improve your... | "How to Improve Your Cash Flow in 30 Days" |

### Distribution as List Builder and Media Hook
- Offer as a free download in exchange for an email address or WhatsApp number (list building).
- Send to your entire database quarterly as a keep-in-touch touchpoint.
- Include in every media kit sent to journalists — it positions you as a primary source.
- Send to journalists as a news hook: "I've just published a guide on X — would your readers find it useful?"

## 10. Newsletter System

*(Pinskey, Way 40)*

A newsletter is a systematic keep-in-touch tool. Its function is not to sell — it is to remain present in the minds of clients and prospects so that when they need your service, you are the first name they recall.

### The 60/20/20 Content Formula
- **60% educational content** — tips, case studies, sector trends, how-to guidance.
- **20% client spotlight or testimonial** — a client success story (with permission); a testimonial.
- **20% your news** — new service, upcoming event, special offer, company milestone.

If the ratio inverts (more than 50% self-promotion), subscribers disengage.

### Frequency Requirement
- Quarterly minimum; monthly preferred.
- Irregular frequency destroys the keep-in-touch function — subscribers forget you between issues.

### Design Rules (4 Rules)
1. Black ink only for body text — never colour text on a colour background.
2. Traditional typefaces (Helvetica, Times, Arial) — decorative fonts slow reading.
3. Do not make it look institutional — write conversationally, as if to one reader.
4. Your photograph on the masthead if it is a personal newsletter — it humanises the communication.

## 11. Joint Ventures and Strategic Alliances

*(Pinskey, Way 54)*

### The Logic
Two non-competing businesses serving the same client type create an arrangement in which each promotes the other to their respective contact lists. Both parties gain access to a warm audience at zero acquisition cost.

### Uganda Examples
| Partnership | Mechanics |
|---|---|
| Business planning consultancy + accounting firm | Monthly "SME toolkit" email co-branded by both; each refers clients needing the other's service |
| Photography studio + wedding caterer | Cross-referral card at booking; combined package discount |
| Printing company + branding designer | Joint quotation template; shared display at trade fairs |
| Legal firm + bank relationship manager | Referrals for new business registrations; compliance briefing evenings co-hosted |

### How to Find Partners
- Look for businesses your clients use immediately before and after they engage you.
- Ask existing clients: "Who else do you work with regularly?"
- Attend the same networking events your target clients attend — their other suppliers will be there.

### Cross-Promotion Mechanics
1. Agree on the format: co-branded email, referral card, joint event, guest column.
2. Exchange lists only if both parties have permission from subscribers; or co-host an event and invite both lists.
3. Track referrals by asking every new client "who referred you?"
4. Review the arrangement quarterly — retire partnerships that generate no referrals.

## 12. Relationship Maintenance Tactics

*(Pinskey, Ways 55–62)*

Systematic relationship maintenance is what converts a satisfied client into a reliable referral source.

### Formalised Referral Request Script
Ask every client at the close of an engagement:
> "My business grows primarily through referrals from people I've worked with. When you hear someone who needs [your service], I would be very grateful if you mentioned my name. Here are a couple of extra business cards to pass on."

This removes the social awkwardness of asking. Most clients are willing but never prompted.

### 48-Hour Handwritten Thank-You Note Rule
Send a handwritten note within 48 hours of:
- Any meeting, introductory call, or referral received.
- Any purchase or engagement signed.
- Any event you attended as a guest.

Email is not a substitute for paper when the relationship matters. In Uganda, where physical correspondence is less common in business, a handwritten note is highly memorable.

### Quarterly Touchpoint Calendar
Four planned contacts per year per A-list client or referral source:
- Q1: Article clipping or industry report relevant to their business.
- Q2: Brief check-in call or coffee meeting.
- Q3: Congratulatory note on a milestone (anniversary, expansion, award).
- Q4: Year-end greeting or invitation to a client event.

### Guest Columns in Partner Newsletters
Write a guest column for your joint venture partners' newsletters at no cost. You reach their list without paying for access; they receive free content. Arrange reciprocal columns — you publish their column in yours.

## 13. Self-Promotion Identity Tools

*(Pinskey, Part I — Ways 1–15)*

### Business Naming
- A descriptive name tells exactly what you do ("Kampala Business Plans") — immediately clear, low advertising burden, but hard to extend into new services.
- An evocative name creates an image or feeling ("Apex Consulting") — flexible, but requires more marketing to explain.
- For small businesses with limited marketing budgets, Pinskey recommends descriptive names.
- **Test:** Say the name to ten people who do not know you. Can they guess what you do? If fewer than seven can, reconsider.
- Avoid initials-only names — they communicate nothing and require expensive brand-building to attach meaning.

### USP Development Process (4 Steps)
1. List every benefit your service or product provides.
2. Identify which benefits are unique — competitors either cannot or do not claim them.
3. Select the one benefit most important to your target client.
4. Express it in a single sentence that can be said aloud in ten seconds.

**Uganda USP examples:**
- "The only business planning service in Uganda that guarantees your bank loan application is submitted correctly the first time."
- "Kampala's first zero-waste restaurant — every meal removes one plastic bottle from Lake Victoria."

### Logo Rules
- Design in black and white first — if it does not work in B&W, it will not work in colour.
- Test at 2.5 cm × 2.5 cm (business card size) — still legible?
- Consistency across all materials (letterhead, card, social media, signage) matters more than the logo's design quality.
- One-time professional design fee is a sound investment; avoid clip art and generic font treatments.

### Business Card and Picture Card
- Standard business card: name, title, company, phone, email, website; use the back for your top three services, a testimonial, or your USP statement.
- **Picture business card:** Adding a professional photo increases recall by 40–60% (Pinskey). Standard practice in real estate, consulting, and financial services — substantially underused in East Africa.
- Print on white or off-white — coloured cards are harder to write notes on after events.

## 14. Daily Marketing Rhythm

*(Edwards, Chapter 1)*

### The Marketing Hour
Edwards & Douglas recommend a structured daily marketing hour divided into four 15-minute blocks:

| Block | Activity |
|---|---|
| 15 minutes | Update contacts; respond to leads and enquiries |
| 15 minutes | Networking activity — call one person; follow up on a meeting |
| 15 minutes | Visibility activity — write a paragraph of an article; send a press release; post content |
| 15 minutes | Review pipeline and plan next steps |

### Why Daily Small Actions Compound
- A single press release sent once produces a small spike, then nothing.
- One article written monthly builds a body of published work within a year.
- One networking call per day produces 250 relationship-building touches per year.
- Compounding of expert reputation requires only consistency — not large budget.

### Why Sporadic Campaigns Fail
Sporadic marketing (busy → campaign → results → stop → cold → panic → campaign again) produces an unpredictable pipeline. The marketing hour breaks this cycle by making marketing a non-negotiable daily activity, not a reaction to a dry pipeline.

## 15. Creating and Starting Organisations

*(Pinskey, Way 52)*

### The Convener Positioning Advantage
Creating a new organisation — however small or informal — places you in the highest-visibility role of any group: the convener and host. You receive automatic authority without having to earn it within someone else's structure.

The organisation does not need to be formal or large:
- A monthly breakfast club for professionals in your niche.
- A quarterly learning forum for a specific industry.
- A women-in-business network.
- An annual award in your local industry.

### How It Functions as a Marketing Tool
- Media call you when they want a comment on industry issues — you are "the founder."
- Membership list = a qualified prospect database that grows itself.
- Events generate press releases, social media content, and speaking opportunities.
- Committee members become an inner circle of referral sources.

### Uganda Examples
- **Kampala Women Business Owners Breakfast Club** — monthly event; membership cap 40; media releases on each meeting generate consistent local press coverage.
- **Uganda SME Business Plan Competition** — annual award; sponsors pay for prize; entries are a database of 200+ SMEs who are warm prospects; winner announcement is a press event.
- **East Africa Agribusiness Forum** — quarterly roundtable; co-convened with a complementary business; combined mailing list reaches 500+ agri-sector contacts.

## 17. 90-Day Low-Budget Promotion Plan

A combined three-month action plan for a Ugandan SME launching or relaunching its marketing. Sources noted in parentheses.

### Month 1 — Foundation

- [ ] Write or review your USP: one sentence, ten seconds aloud *(Pinskey, Way 3)*
- [ ] Update your business card — add a professional photo and your USP on the reverse *(Pinskey, Ways 5–6)*
- [ ] Create or claim your Google My Business listing with photos and service description
- [ ] Write your first special report on the topic your clients ask about most frequently *(Pinskey, Way 42)*
- [ ] Identify five joint venture partners — businesses that serve your clients before or after you *(Pinskey, Way 54)*
- [ ] Start a contacts database — add every person you have ever done business with, noting where you met *(Edwards, Chapter 3)*
- [ ] Finalise your positioning statement using the "I help [type] who struggle with [problem]..." formula *(Edwards, Chapter 10)*

### Month 2 — Visibility

- [ ] Submit one article to a local business publication *(Pinskey, Way 39; Edwards, Chapter 5)*
- [ ] Send your special report to your entire database with a brief personalised message *(Pinskey, Way 42)*
- [ ] Send one press release on a genuinely newsworthy trigger — use the inverted pyramid template *(Edwards, Chapter 4; Pinskey, Way 87)*
- [ ] Attend one networking event; sort cards into A/B/C piles; follow up every A contact within 48 hours *(Edwards, Chapter 3; Pinskey, Way 47)*
- [ ] Design and post a four-postcard campaign for a specific current offer *(Pinskey, Way 75)*
- [ ] Place a tear-strip bulletin board notice in five locations your target clients visit *(Pinskey, Way 76)*
- [ ] Activate the daily marketing hour — four blocks of 15 minutes each morning *(Edwards, Chapter 1)*

### Month 3 — Momentum

- [ ] Speak at one free event — chamber of commerce breakfast, industry association, Rotary *(Pinskey, Way 63; Edwards, Chapter 5)*
- [ ] Write a second article; send high-quality reprints of the first article as direct mail to prospects *(Pinskey, Way 39)*
- [ ] Pitch two radio stations with a one-page speaking proposal *(Pinskey, Way 66)*
- [ ] Launch your quarterly newsletter — first issue using the 60/20/20 content formula *(Pinskey, Way 40)*
- [ ] Ask every satisfied client for a testimonial (written) and a referral (named) *(Edwards, Chapter 2)*
- [ ] Contact two joint venture partners; propose a co-branded email or cross-referral arrangement *(Pinskey, Way 54)*
- [ ] Review: track which tactics generated the most enquiries; double planned activity on those channels in Month 4
