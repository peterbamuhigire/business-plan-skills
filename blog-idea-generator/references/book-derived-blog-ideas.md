## From Minto — *The Pyramid Principle*

### B-MIN-01 — Why Your Business Plan Buries the Answer
**Angle:** Most business plans make the reader work too hard — they plod through context, analysis, and data before revealing the conclusion. The Pyramid Principle flips this.
**Target reader:** Entrepreneurs writing their first business plan; consultants preparing client reports
**Hook:** "A loan officer reads your 40-page business plan and closes it on page 3. Not because your plan was bad. Because it made them look for the answer."
**Draw from:** `pyramid-principle.md` — SCQA framework, governing thought, So What? test
**Format:** Practical how-to with before/after examples

### B-MIN-02 — The SCQA Formula: How McKinsey Opens Every Document
**Angle:** Situation, Complication, Question, Answer — the four-sentence opening that makes any business document immediately compelling
**Target reader:** Business plan writers, proposal writers, report authors
**Hook:** "Every compelling business document opens with four sentences. Here is the formula."
**Draw from:** `pyramid-principle.md` — SCQA section, four variation orders
**Format:** Step-by-step formula with Uganda business examples

### B-MIN-03 — Are You Making These Seven Business Plan Structure Mistakes?
**Angle:** Minto identifies seven common structural errors in analytical documents. Every one of them appears regularly in East African business plans.
**Target reader:** First-time entrepreneurs; business plan consultants
**Hook:** "Investors don't just reject weak plans. They reject well-researched plans with structural flaws that make the argument impossible to follow."
**Draw from:** `pyramid-principle.md` — Structural errors section
**Format:** Listicle with diagnosis + fix for each error

### B-MIN-04 — The 30-Second Test Every Business Plan Must Pass
**Angle:** The "elevator test" — if you cannot state your business's investment case in 30 seconds using the Pyramid structure, your executive summary is too long and your argument is not clear enough.
**Target reader:** Entrepreneurs preparing to pitch to investors or banks
**Hook:** "Before you send your business plan to the bank, read your executive summary aloud. Can you state the conclusion in the first sentence?"
**Draw from:** `pyramid-principle.md` — 30-second test; cross-reference `mckinsey-problem-solving.md` — elevator test
**Format:** Self-diagnostic checklist + rewrite guide

## From Rasiel — *The McKinsey Way*

### B-MCK-01 — MECE Thinking: The Framework That Separates Good Analysis from Great Analysis
**Angle:** Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive — the two words that McKinsey consultants use to pressure-test every framework. Most business plan market analyses are neither.
**Target reader:** Business analysts, plan writers, entrepreneurs who want consulting-quality plans
**Hook:** "Your market analysis has six customer segments. They overlap by 40%. You've been double-counting your TAM."
**Draw from:** `mckinsey-problem-solving.md` — MECE section with worked example
**Format:** Concept explainer with Uganda market segmentation example

### B-MCK-02 — How McKinsey Solves Problems: The Hypothesis-First Method
**Angle:** McKinsey starts every engagement with a hypothesis — a provisional answer — and then tests it. This is the opposite of how most entrepreneurs analyse their market.
**Target reader:** Entrepreneurs doing market research; consultants building business cases
**Hook:** "Most entrepreneurs collect data and then look for an answer. McKinsey starts with an answer and then looks for proof — or disproof."
**Draw from:** `mckinsey-problem-solving.md` — Hypothesis-driven analysis section
**Format:** Method explainer + "try this for your business plan" exercise

### B-MCK-03 — The 80/20 Rule for Your Business Plan Research
**Angle:** The McKinsey interpretation of 80/20 is not just a productivity tip — it is a diagnostic tool for knowing when you have done enough research to make a decision.
**Target reader:** Entrepreneurs overwhelmed by research; plan writers who "boil the ocean"
**Hook:** "You have been researching for six weeks. Your plan is still not written. This is a McKinsey problem they solved 40 years ago."
**Draw from:** `mckinsey-problem-solving.md` — 80/20 and key drivers sections
**Format:** Practical advice piece with actionable cut-off criteria

### B-MCK-04 — Fact-Based or Opinion-Based? How Banks Read Your Business Plan
**Angle:** Every claim in a business plan is either fact-based (cited, measured, tested) or opinion-based (assumed, estimated without basis). Banks and investors treat these very differently.
**Target reader:** Entrepreneurs submitting to banks and DFIs
**Hook:** "Your plan says 'the market is growing rapidly.' The bank officer reads this and discounts everything on the next five pages."
**Draw from:** `mckinsey-problem-solving.md` — Fact-based analysis section
**Format:** Diagnostic exercise + rewriting guide

## From Damodaran — *The Little Book of Valuation*

### B-DAM-01 — What Is Your Business Actually Worth? The Three Methods Every Founder Should Know
**Angle:** DCF, multiples, and options-based valuation — plain-English introduction to the three approaches, when each applies, and which one a Uganda SME founder is most likely to encounter.
**Target reader:** Entrepreneurs seeking equity investment; business owners planning a sale
**Hook:** "An investor asked: 'What's your valuation?' You said UGX 2 billion. They asked: 'How did you get there?' You didn't have an answer."
**Draw from:** `business-valuation-methods.md` — three approaches section
**Format:** Concept explainer with Uganda context

### B-DAM-02 — Why Growth Can Destroy Value (And What This Means for Your Business Plan)
**Angle:** Damodaran's counterintuitive finding: growth only creates value when ROIC exceeds WACC. Growth at below-cost-of-capital returns makes the business worth less, not more.
**Target reader:** Entrepreneurs projecting ambitious revenue growth; investors evaluating growth businesses
**Hook:** "Your five-year plan shows revenue growing 40% annually. An investor looks at it and sees value destruction. Here is why."
**Draw from:** `business-valuation-methods.md` — growth and value section
**Format:** Concept + worked numerical example

### B-DAM-03 — How to Value a Pre-Revenue Startup in Uganda
**Angle:** The three methods Damodaran recommends for businesses with no earnings — adapted for the Uganda context where comparable transactions are scarce and country risk is real.
**Target reader:** Tech startup founders; early-stage entrepreneurs seeking angel or impact investment
**Hook:** "You have 200 users, UGX 0 in revenue, and an investor meeting in two weeks. How do you value your startup?"
**Draw from:** `business-valuation-methods.md` — pre-revenue valuation section, Uganda/EA adjustments
**Format:** Step-by-step guide with worked UGX example

### B-DAM-04 — The Uganda Discount: Why East African Businesses Are Worth Less on Paper (and What to Do About It)
**Angle:** Country risk premium, illiquidity discount, and thin comparable data all reduce the calculated value of an East African business compared to a Western equivalent. But there are ways to counter each.
**Target reader:** Entrepreneurs negotiating with DFIs and impact investors
**Hook:** "Your business would be worth $5 million in the UK. In Uganda, the valuation model spits out $2.3 million. Here is where the gap comes from — and how to close it."
**Draw from:** `business-valuation-methods.md` — Uganda/East Africa context section
**Format:** Analysis piece with mitigation strategies

## From Feld & Mendelson — *Venture Deals*

### B-VD-01 — The Option Pool Shuffle: How VCs Quietly Lower Your Valuation
**Angle:** One of the oldest tricks in venture deals — placing the employee option pool inside the pre-money valuation to reduce what founders actually receive. Most first-time founders never see it coming.
**Target reader:** Entrepreneurs negotiating first equity round
**Hook:** "The term sheet says your pre-money valuation is $3 million. After the option pool shuffle, you're actually selling at $2.1 million. Here is how to spot it."
**Draw from:** `equity-term-sheets.md` — option pool shuffle section
**Format:** Explainer with worked numerical example

### B-VD-02 — Liquidation Preference: The Term That Can Make Your Exit Worthless
**Angle:** A participating liquidation preference with a 2× multiple means investors collect before founders in any exit scenario. At a modest exit price, founders receive nothing.
**Target reader:** Entrepreneurs at any stage of equity fundraising
**Hook:** "You built a company, raised funding, sold it for $10 million, and took home $0. Liquidation preference is how."
**Draw from:** `equity-term-sheets.md` — liquidation preference worked examples (four scenarios)
**Format:** Case study with four exit scenarios table

### B-VD-03 — Convertible Notes vs. SAFEs: Which Should You Use for Your Uganda Startup?
**Angle:** Pre-seed financing vehicles compared for East Africa context — where legal infrastructure for SAFEs may be limited but convertible notes are well understood by Ugandan lawyers.
**Target reader:** Early-stage founders raising their first UGX 50–200M from angels
**Hook:** "You need UGX 120 million to get to your first 1,000 users. Equity round is too early. Here are your two options — and which one to choose."
**Draw from:** `equity-term-sheets.md` — convertible notes and SAFEs section, Uganda/EA context
**Format:** Comparison guide with Uganda-specific legal notes

### B-VD-04 — The 10 Things Every Ugandan Founder Must Know Before Signing a Term Sheet
**Angle:** Direct adaptation of the Founder Quick Reference from `equity-term-sheets.md` for the East African investor landscape.
**Target reader:** Founders receiving first term sheet from an angel, VC, or impact investor
**Hook:** "You worked four years to get a term sheet. Don't lose your company in the next four days."
**Draw from:** `equity-term-sheets.md` — Founder Quick Reference, Uganda investor landscape
**Format:** Listicle with Uganda-specific commentary

## From Sinek — *Start with Why*

### B-SW-01 — Why Your Mission Statement Is Lying (And How to Write One That Isn't)
**Angle:** Most mission statements describe what a business does or how it does it. Sinek's Golden Circle shows why this is backward — and how starting with WHY produces statements that actually motivate people.
**Target reader:** Entrepreneurs writing company overviews; managers building company culture
**Hook:** "Your mission statement says 'to provide high-quality products at competitive prices.' So does every other business in your sector. Here is how to write one that only you could have written."
**Draw from:** `mission-vision-why.md` — Golden Circle, six-step writing process, common mistakes table
**Format:** Diagnostic + rewrite guide with Uganda business examples

### B-SW-02 — The Celery Test: A Simple Tool for Strategic Clarity
**Angle:** How do you decide which opportunities to pursue and which to decline? The Celery Test is a quick decision filter derived from your WHY — and it works for Uganda SMEs in crowded, tempting markets.
**Target reader:** Entrepreneurs facing scope creep; SME owners adding too many product lines
**Hook:** "Someone tells you to stock biscuits, celery, M&Ms, and chewing gum. You don't have money for all of them. How do you decide? The Celery Test is your answer."
**Draw from:** `mission-vision-why.md` — Celery Test section with Uganda business examples
**Format:** Concept + decision tool with four Uganda business case applications

### B-SW-03 — Why the Best Businesses in Uganda Are Built on Ubuntu, Not Value Propositions
**Angle:** Sinek's WHY framework resonates deeply with African business culture — because the most durable East African businesses are built around community purpose, family legacy, and collective uplift. These are authentic WHYs, not marketing slogans.
**Target reader:** East African entrepreneurs; business plan writers adapting Western frameworks for local context
**Hook:** "A Ugandan entrepreneur told me: 'I didn't start this business for profit. I started it so my mother would never worry about money again.' That is a WHY. Everything else is tactics."
**Draw from:** `mission-vision-why.md` — Uganda/East Africa application section, Ubuntu connection
**Format:** Opinion/essay with practical templates

### B-SW-04 — What Happens When a Business Loses Its Why (And How to Get It Back)
**Angle:** The Split — Sinek's analysis of what happens when growth, scale, or leadership change causes a company to drift from its founding purpose. Common in East African businesses transitioning from founder-led to professionally managed.
**Target reader:** Founders scaling beyond direct control; business owners feeling disconnected from their company
**Hook:** "Your business was growing. Then it started to feel like a job. That is not a motivation problem. It is a WHY problem."
**Draw from:** `mission-vision-why.md` — The Split section, purpose drift (cross-reference Kaza's objective function)
**Format:** Diagnostic + recovery guide
