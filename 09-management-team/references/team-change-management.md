# Team Development & Change Management Reference

Frameworks for understanding team dynamics, managing organisational change, and building the human capabilities needed for successful implementation — drawn from systems analysis methodology.

---

## Tuckman's Stages of Team Development (Dennis et al.)

Teams progress through five predictable stages:

### Stage 1: Forming

- Team members get to know each other
- Learning expectations, roles, and boundaries
- Polite, cautious interactions — avoiding conflict
- Dependence on the leader for direction
- Low productivity (team is orienting)

### Stage 2: Storming

- Conflict emerges over control, direction, and roles
- Members challenge authority and each other's ideas
- Frustration with pace of progress, differing work styles
- **This stage is normal and necessary** — avoiding it leads to superficial teamwork
- Key leadership action: facilitate constructive conflict, clarify roles, reinforce shared goals

### Stage 3: Norming

- Team establishes norms, working agreements, and shared expectations
- Relationships solidify, trust develops
- Members begin to appreciate each other's strengths
- Collaboration improves, conflict becomes constructive
- Group identity forms

### Stage 4: Performing

- Team works productively toward shared goals
- High autonomy, members anticipate each other's needs
- Effective problem-solving and decision-making
- Focus shifts from interpersonal issues to task achievement
- Not all teams reach this stage

### Stage 5: Adjourning

- Team transitions after project completion or phase ending
- Acknowledging accomplishments and contributions
- Managing knowledge transfer and documentation
- Emotional responses to disbanding (especially high-performing teams)
- Important for project-based businesses: plan for smooth handoffs

## Characteristics of a Jelled Team (Dennis et al.)

A "jelled" team is one that has reached peak performance. Indicators include:

| Characteristic | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| **Low turnover** | Members stay because they value the team, not just the job |
| **Strong identity** | Team has a shared sense of who they are and what they stand for |
| **Sense of eliteness** | Belief that they are doing something special and doing it well |
| **Joint ownership** | Success and failure belong to the team, not individuals |
| **Enjoyment** | Members genuinely enjoy working together |

**For business plans**: Describe how you will build these characteristics — shared mission, team rituals, collective accountability, and celebrating wins together.

## Six Analyst Skill Categories (Dennis et al.)

While framed for systems analysts, these skill categories apply broadly to any team member driving business change:

| Category | Skills | Business Plan Relevance |
|----------|--------|------------------------|
| **Technical** | Systems knowledge, programming, data analysis, tool proficiency | Technology implementation capability |
| **Business** | Industry knowledge, financial literacy, process understanding | Domain expertise that investors seek |
| **Analytical** | Problem decomposition, critical thinking, modelling, requirements gathering | Strategic planning and decision-making |
| **Interpersonal** | Communication, active listening, negotiation, conflict resolution | Team cohesion and stakeholder management |
| **Management** | Planning, organising, leading, resource allocation, risk management | Project execution and operational oversight |
| **Ethical** | Integrity, confidentiality, fairness, social responsibility | Governance, trust-building, investor confidence |

**For skills gap analysis**: Assess each team member across these six categories to identify where the team is strong and where external hires, advisors, or training are needed.

## Stakeholder Analysis for Change (Dennis et al.)

When implementing new systems, processes, or organisational changes, assess each stakeholder group:

| Assessment Factor | Questions to Answer |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Personal costs** | What does this person lose? (Comfort, status, autonomy, skills relevance, workload during transition) |
| **Personal benefits** | What does this person gain? (Easier work, new skills, career advancement, recognition) |
| **Certainty of each** | Are costs guaranteed while benefits are speculative? (This imbalance kills adoption) |
| **Transition costs** | What temporary pain is required? (Learning curve, dual systems, productivity dip) |
| **Actions to rebalance** | How can we increase perceived benefits or reduce perceived costs? |
| **Training needed** | What must this person learn to succeed in the new environment? |
| **Adoption likelihood** | Ready / Reluctant / Resistant (classify each stakeholder group) |

### Stakeholder Assessment Template

For each stakeholder group (e.g., sales team, warehouse staff, managers, customers):

```
Stakeholder group: [Name]
Current role in process: [Description]
Impact of change: [High / Medium / Low]
Personal costs: [List specific costs]
Personal benefits: [List specific benefits]
Net perception: [Positive / Neutral / Negative]
Adoption likelihood: [Ready / Reluctant / Resistant]
Actions needed: [Training, communication, incentive adjustment]
```

## Change Adoption Strategies (Dennis et al.)

### Informational Strategy

- Convince through evidence, demonstration, and logical argument
- **Best when**: Individual benefits of adoption are clear
- Tactics:
  - Share pilot results and success stories
  - Demonstrate the new system/process in action
  - Provide data on efficiency gains
  - Use testimonials from early adopters
  - **Stress "painkillers" over "vitamins"** — solving existing pain points is more compelling than offering nice-to-have improvements

### Political Strategy

- Use organisational authority and social influence
- **Best when**: Individual benefits are low but organisational benefits are high
- Tactics:
  - Executive mandate — make adoption expected, not optional
  - Peer pressure — leverage ready adopters as social proof
  - Remove alternatives — make the old way harder than the new way
  - Tie adoption to performance reviews and rewards
  - Make the new process the path of least resistance

### Choosing Between Strategies

| Situation | Recommended Strategy |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Clear personal benefits for adopters | Informational |
| Low personal benefits, high organisational benefits | Political |
| Mixed — some benefit, some do not | Informational for those who benefit, political for those who do not |
| Urgent timeline, cannot wait for voluntary adoption | Political |

## Adopter Distribution (Dennis et al.)

| Category | Percentage | Characteristics | Strategy |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| **Ready adopters** | 20-30% | See the benefits, enthusiastic, willing to try new approaches | Support them, provide early access, make them internal champions and proponents |
| **Reluctant adopters** | 40-60% | Wait and see, follow the majority, need reassurance that change is safe | They follow the flow — once ready adopters succeed visibly, reluctant adopters follow |
| **Resistant adopters** | 20-30% | Focus on costs, oppose change, may actively undermine adoption efforts | Generally ignore — do not let them consume disproportionate energy and attention |

**Key insight**: The natural instinct is to focus energy on converting resistant adopters. This is almost always a mistake. Instead:
1. Support ready adopters so they succeed visibly
2. Let their success influence the reluctant middle
3. The resistant minority either eventually follows or self-selects out

## Training Methods Comparison (Dennis et al.)

| Method | Development Cost | Delivery Cost | Impact | Reach |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|--------|-------|
| **One-on-one** | Low | High (per person) | Highest (personalised) | Low (one person at a time) |
| **Classroom** | Medium | Medium | High (interactive) | Medium (groups of 10-30) |
| **Computer-based** | High | Low (per person) | Medium (self-paced) | Highest (unlimited, asynchronous) |

### Training Recommendations

- **Focus on the 20% of functions used 80% of the time** — do not try to train everything
- Schedule training **close to go-live** — training delivered too early is forgotten
- Provide **multiple formats** — different people learn differently
- Plan for **ongoing support** after go-live: helpdesk, superusers, quick-reference guides
- Use a **train-the-trainer** approach for scalability
- Measure training effectiveness through observation, not just completion rates

## Ethical Considerations for Teams (Dennis et al.)

When implementing new technology or processes, leadership must consider:

| Issue | Description | Mitigation |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| **Deskilling** | Automation may reduce the skill level required for jobs, affecting employee satisfaction and career development | Retrain displaced workers for higher-value roles; redesign jobs to be more fulfilling, not just cheaper |
| **Employee monitoring** | New systems often increase visibility into individual performance | Be transparent about what is monitored and why; use data for improvement, not punishment |
| **Technostress** | Technology overload, constant connectivity, and rapid change cause stress and burnout | Set boundaries on availability; phase technology changes; provide adequate training time |
| **IT health effects** | Prolonged screen time, repetitive strain, sedentary work patterns | Ergonomic assessments; break policies; health and wellness programmes |

**For business plans**: Demonstrating awareness of these ethical considerations shows investors and lenders that the management team takes employee welfare seriously — which correlates with lower turnover, higher productivity, and sustainable growth.
