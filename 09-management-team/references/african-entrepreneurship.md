# African Entrepreneurship & Business Success Factors

*Sources: Beveridge & Oberschall, African Businessmen and Development in Zambia (Princeton University Press, 1979); Sardanis, A Venture in Africa (I.B. Tauris, 2007).*

---

## 1. What Actually Drives African Business Success (Beveridge & Oberschall)

The largest-scale empirical study of African entrepreneurship — surveying businessmen across Lusaka and rural Zambia — produced a counter-intuitive finding:

> **"Sound management and competitive position are the keys to business success. Ascribed characteristics like ethnicity and parental social class have low to negligible effects."**

Factors that DO matter, ranked by correlation with success:

| Factor | Strength | What It Means for Business Plans |
|---|---|---|
| **Innovation** | Highest | The single strongest predictor — see below |
| **Complete financial records** | Strong | Maintaining proper accounts vs. none |
| **Previous occupation level** | Strong | Demanding prior career builds skills + capital |
| **Sponsorship/mentorship at start** | Strong (Lusaka) | Help from banks, employers, agencies |
| **Education level** | Moderate | More schooling → more opportunities, but not a prerequisite |
| **Systematic skills training** | Moderate | Formal/apprenticeship > self-taught |
| **Full-time concentration** | Moderate (rural) | Undivided vs. part-time commitment |
| **Ethnicity** | Negligible | No meaningful group differences found |
| **Father's occupation** | Low–Moderate | Some capital/education advantage, but not deterministic |

**Key implication:** When writing the management team section, focus on what founders *can do* and *have done*, not who they are. A founder with hands-on business experience, proper financial discipline, and a willingness to learn outperforms a highly credentialled founder with poor management habits.

## 2. Innovation as the Core Success Driver

Beveridge & Oberschall found **innovation** had the highest zero-order correlation with business success of any variable measured — for both urban and rural businessmen.

### Definition: Derivative Innovation

Innovation in an African context does **not** mean inventing new technology. It means **adapting proven business models into new African market contexts** — what Redlich (1955) calls "derivative innovation":

> "The adoption and diffusion of original innovations into new contexts ... the adoption of technology, organisation, production, and marketing in a new social environment is an innovative action that makes an economic contribution."

### Types of Innovation Observed

| Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Marketing innovation** | Introducing a new product category to a neighbourhood where it never existed; being the first African-owned business of a particular type; targeting a new customer segment (e.g., professional Africans) |
| **Management/organisation innovation** | Introducing self-service layout to a township store; running a bar customers could bring their families to; separating business cash from personal cash |
| **Production innovation** | Refrigerated/inspected meat for African customers; branded packaged goods in informal markets |

### Innovation ≠ Guaranteed Success

Not all innovations succeed. One spectacular failure was a boutique importing high-end goods for the African elite — innovative, but the market was too thin. The lesson: **innovation must be matched to real demand from an accessible customer base.**

### The Innovation Process

Successful innovators were **analytic**, not impulsive:

> "When I looked around, everyone was doing the same kind of business. I tried to do something different, where there was less competition."

One bar owner described visiting every bar in the city before opening, identifying what drove losses (fights, waitresses, bands, spoilage), then designing a family-friendly alternative with low operating costs and high margins.

**Practical process:** Observe the market systematically → identify a gap or a practice that works elsewhere → adapt it to the local customer profile → test at small scale.

## 3. Failure Patterns — What Poor Managers Did Wrong

The contrast between successful and unsuccessful businesses at the same location, same size, was striking:

| Failure Pattern | Observable Signs |
|---|---|
| **Family mismanagement** | Incompetent relatives placed in management roles; inability to hold them accountable |
| **Poor stock control** | Empty shelves, out-of-stock events, purchasing at retail rather than wholesale |
| **Neglected maintenance** | Equipment broken for months (beer cooler out = lost sales) |
| **No financial records** | Inability to detect employee theft or assess profitability |
| **Part-time management** | Owner pursuing other income sources instead of concentrating on the business |
| **Credit to untrackable customers** | Giving credit without slips or systems to recover it |

The successful business in the same street had: near-constant owner presence, daily stock counting, full records, a policy on who got credit and under what terms, and immediate equipment repair.

## 4. Family Business Dynamics in African Context

Family relationships are both an asset and a risk — findings were nuanced:

**Family as asset:**
- Founding-period labour (cheap, loyal, trusted)
- Reinvestment of family savings as startup capital
- Capable family members in management roles (when genuinely competent)

**Family as liability:**
- Pressure to employ relatives regardless of capability
- Reluctance to discipline or dismiss underperforming family members
- Obligations to support kin from business funds (reduces reinvestable profit)

**Best practice observed:** Successful businessmen who employed family drew a hard line between business cash and family obligations. One large businessman gave family members a fixed salary from his own remuneration — never directly from the business. Another employed only one brother (by merit) while managing all other obligations from personal funds.

> "I maintain four sisters and their children. I give them money from the store." — rural trader, struggling

> "I give them money from the salary I pay myself, rather than giving away money directly from the business." — large Northern businessman, thriving

## 5. The ITM Operating Formula (Sardanis)

Andrew Sardanis built and led ITM (Investment Trade and Management) International, operating across 20+ African countries for 25 years. The group's most profitable period rested on a clear management formula:

> **"Local management, with only a sprinkling of expatriates. It worked well. They were Liberian in Liberia, Cameroonian in Cameroon and Malawian in Malawi — as they had always been Zambian in Zambia. This formula gave us widespread penetration of each market place and greater efficiency."**

### Why Local Management Worked

| Benefit | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Market penetration** | Locals understood neighbourhood dynamics, language, relationships |
| **Customer trust** | Clients felt they were dealing with one of their own |
| **Government relations** | Local managers navigated government obligations more effectively |
| **Efficiency** | Cultural fluency reduced misunderstandings and friction costs |
| **Cost** | Far cheaper than expatriate packages and housing allowances |

### The Mass Market Opportunity

ITM's banking subsidiary (Meridien BIAO) succeeded by serving the market that colonial banks ignored:

> "The mass African market that was crying for service but was deliberately ignored by the colonial banks, which concentrated on the multinational companies and the settler business sector."

The client profile: local businessman, professional, marketeer, jobber, small contractor, first-time bank account holder. This segment was underserved, had genuine need, and responded strongly to good service.

**Business plan implication:** In East Africa, many markets remain underserved at the base of the pyramid — not because demand is absent, but because established players focus on large/formal clients. Serving the mass market with appropriately sized products and local staff is a proven winning formula.

## 6. The Local Management Risk: Baronies

The same local management formula carried a risk Sardanis discovered in Angola and later in the BIAO merger:

> "You realised that, without meaning to, you had created a number of baronies and associations that you could no longer control ... The local baron has already made alliances with the local high and mighty, who will stand up for him and put obstacles in the way of the intruders."

When a delegated manager becomes entrenched:
- They build personal relationships with the key local stakeholders
- They become impossible to replace without political cost
- They badmouth the organisation to protect their position
- The business effectively declares independence from headquarters

**Mitigation:** Regular rotation of managers across locations; headquarters visits; financial oversight that does not depend solely on the local manager's reports; clear performance metrics visible to the centre.

## 7. The Case for Indigenous Enterprise

Both Beveridge and Sardanis reach the same conclusion from different angles:

Beveridge (research perspective):
> "Zambian innovators, however, have neither a uniform personality type, nor an identical social background ... African entrepreneurial performance is accounted for in much the same way as entrepreneurial performance elsewhere. Success depended on variables within the control of the businessman himself."

Sardanis (practitioner perspective):
> "Indigenous enterprise has to become a major factor in African economies before real progress can be made in emancipation, both political and economic. Only then will the countries develop home-grown policies, rather than vacillating between one foreign-imposed policy and another."

**Business plan implication:** Indigenous African founders are not at a disadvantage relative to their counterparts elsewhere. The factors that drive success — innovation, management quality, training, mentorship — are all actionable. A business plan that demonstrates these practices (proper records, full-time commitment, trained staff, innovative positioning) is credible precisely because these are the proven success factors.
