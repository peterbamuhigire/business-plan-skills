# The Hard Thing About Hard Things — Horowitz Framework

**Source:** Horowitz, Ben (2014). *The Hard Thing About Hard Things: Building a Business When There Are No Easy Answers*. HarperBusiness.

**Application:** Management team section (CEO/founder profile, leadership assessment, team quality), risk analysis (wartime contingency thinking), implementation planning (culture, processes, scaling), and any business plan for a venture expected to face competitive or operational crises.

**Core thesis:** Most management books describe what to do when things are going well. This book describes what to do when they are going badly — which, in any ambitious company, is most of the time. The hard thing is not setting a big, hairy, audacious goal. The hard thing is laying people off when you miss it.

---

## 1. The Struggle

> "The Struggle is when you wonder why you started the company in the first place. The Struggle is where greatness comes from."

Every founder CEO will enter the Struggle — the period when the product has issues, the market hasn't materialised as expected, key employees are leaving, cash is running low, and self-doubt becomes self-hatred. The Struggle is not failure. But it causes failure, especially in those who are not prepared for it.

**How the Struggle manifests:**
- Food loses its taste; going on holiday makes things worse
- You cannot hear a conversation because the Struggle is louder
- You feel surrounded by people and are completely alone
- Employees think you are lying and you think they may be right

**What helps in the Struggle:**

1. **Don't put it all on your shoulders** — share every burden you can. Nobody takes losses harder than the founding CEO; transferring awareness to the team doesn't make it worse, it gets more brains on the problem.

2. **This is chess, not checkers** — technology businesses are complex. There is always a move. Taking a company public with $2M in revenue and 340 employees is a move. There is always a move.

3. **Play long enough and you might get lucky** — if you survive long enough to see tomorrow, the answer that seems impossible today may arrive.

4. **Don't take it personally** — you hired the people; you made the decisions; you knew the job was dangerous when you took it. Evaluating yourself and giving yourself an F doesn't help.

5. **Remember what separates the great from the mediocre** — if you want to be great, this is the challenge. The great CEOs, without exception, say: *"I didn't quit."*

**Business plan implication:** A business plan that describes no downside scenario, no crisis pathway, and no founder resilience profile is not credible to sophisticated investors. Acknowledging that the Struggle is coming — and showing that the founding team has thought about it — builds more confidence than pretending it won't happen.

## 2. Tell It Like It Is — CEO Transparency

> "Build a culture that rewards — not punishes — people for getting problems into the open where they can be solved."

The single biggest personal improvement Horowitz made as CEO was stopping being too positive. He projected a sunny demeanour to protect employees from bad news — and was completely wrong.

**Three reasons why transparency is imperative:**

1. **Trust** — In any human interaction, the required amount of communication is inversely proportional to the level of trust. A company that communicates with its employees as if they cannot handle the truth generates an enormous communication tax that compounds daily.

2. **More brains on hard problems** — You hired smart people. A brain, no matter how big, cannot solve a problem it doesn't know about. "Given enough eyeballs, all bugs are shallow."

3. **Culture routing** — A healthy culture is like an old routing protocol: bad news travels fast; good news travels slow. If you investigate companies that have failed, you will find that many employees knew about fatal issues long before those issues killed the company. They didn't say anything because the culture discouraged it.

**Warning:** Beware management maxims like *"Don't bring me a problem without bringing me a solution."* If an engineer identifies a serious flaw in how the product is being marketed, you do not want them to bury that information.

## 3. Layoffs Done Right

When a company fails to hit its financial plan so severely that it must let people go, how the layoff is conducted determines whether the culture survives it. Horowitz led three consecutive large layoffs at Loudcloud/Opsware and achieved a $1.6B outcome anyway — because each layoff was done correctly.

**Five-step process:**

1. **Get your head right** — The CEO must stop grieving and focus on the future. People are depending on the remaining employees and they need a leader, not a victim.

2. **Don't delay** — Once the decision is made, execute quickly. Every day of delay allows leaks; leaks force managers to lie, stay silent, or confirm — each of which damages culture before a single person has been let go.

3. **Be clear in your own mind about why** — The reason must be simple, honest, and correct. *"The company failed to hit its plan"* is correct. Do not say the positions are being eliminated — that is usually false and employees know it. Be honest: the company overestimated growth and must correct.

4. **Train your managers** — Managers must deliver the news to their direct reports personally, not through HR. They must be briefed on why, on what happens next (severance, references, benefits), and on how to answer the questions their people will ask.

5. **Be honest and direct with individuals** — No platitudes. No euphemisms. Tell them clearly, treat them with dignity, give them a complete package, and say thank you for their contribution.

**Key principle:** It is possible to do something fundamentally wrong in the right way. Respecting people's dignity while making a brutal business decision is not a contradiction.

## 4. Firing an Executive

Firing an executive is among the most difficult things a CEO does. Horowitz outlines the process in detail.

**Why CEOs wait too long:** They like the person; they feel responsible for the hire; they hope the executive will improve. By the time the CEO has decided to fire, the organisation has already decided months ago — and is waiting for the CEO to catch up.

**Before the conversation — preparation:**
- Consult your board and HR to understand any legal obligations
- Think through the impact on the team: who does this person manage? What communication is needed?
- Prepare the transition plan: who takes over the function?
- Plan the exact words for the conversation — do not improvise this

**The conversation:**
- Be direct and clear. *"I've decided to let you go."* Not: *"I'm thinking about making a change."*
- Give the reason. One honest sentence. Do not over-explain — this invites negotiation.
- Treat the person with respect. They are not a criminal. They are a competent person who was wrong for this role at this stage.

**The aftermath:**
- Tell the team the same day
- Be honest with the team about what happened: *"I made a mistake in the hire / the role evolved beyond what we discussed / we needed different skills."*
- Do not throw the executive under the bus — the team will wonder what you say about them when they leave

## 5. People, Products, Profits — In That Order

> "My old boss Jim Barksdale was fond of saying, 'We take care of the people, the products, and the profits — in that order.' Taking care of the people is the most difficult of the three by far and if you don't do it, the other two won't matter."

**What a good place to work looks like (Horowitz's definition):**

In a good organisation, people can focus on their work and have confidence that if they get their work done, good things will happen for both the company and them personally.

In a poor organisation, people spend most of their time fighting organisational boundaries, infighting, and broken processes. They don't know what their job is; there is no way to know if they are doing it well; and when they muster the courage to tell management how dysfunctional their situation is, management denies there is a problem.

**Being a good company matters most when things go wrong** — and things always go wrong. In bad companies, when the economics disappear, the good employees leave. In technology businesses, when the good employees leave, the spiral begins and almost nothing reverses it.

**Being a good company is an end in itself** — not just a means to better economics. Every GO Corporation employee, despite the company eventually failing, counted it as one of the greatest work experiences of their lives. That is what makes a great CEO.

## 6. Why CEOs Must Train Their People

> "Training is one of the highest-leverage activities a manager can perform." — Andy Grove, *High Output Management*

Most startups recruit carefully and then stop investing in people the moment they join. This is irrational.

**Grove's leverage calculation:** If you deliver a four-lecture training series to ten employees (twelve hours total preparation), and that training produces a 1% improvement in each employee's performance over 20,000 person-hours of work the following year, you have generated 200 hours of additional output from 12 hours of input. Almost no other activity produces that ratio.

**Why managers must train — not training firms:** Outside training firms don't understand your business and teach things that aren't relevant. The manager who trains their people delivers knowledge that is specific, contextual, and immediately applicable. The manager who builds training into their operating rhythm multiplies their own effectiveness.

**The Good PM / Bad PM principle:** One page describing what excellent performance looks like — and what poor performance looks like, concretely — instantly calibrates a team. Horowitz wrote this for product managers; the principle applies to any function.

## 7. Minimising Politics in Your Company

Political behaviour almost always starts with the CEO — often unintentionally. The CEO who rewards an employee for agitating for a raise has just taught the company that agitation is the path to economic reward.

**How to minimise politics:**

**1. Hire people with the right kind of ambition**

> "The right kind of ambition is ambition for the company's success, with the executive's own success only coming as a by-product of the company's victory." — Andy Grove

The wrong kind of ambition is personal ambition regardless of the company's outcome. Interview signals:
- **"Me" prism** (wrong): *"My last job was my e-commerce play. I felt it was important to round out my résumé."* (personalises the company, treats it as a career accessory)
- **Team prism** (right): Rarely uses "I" when describing accomplishments; deflects credit to the team; asks how the company will win before asking about their compensation

**2. Build strict processes for politically sensitive activities — and do not deviate:**

| Activity | Political risk if unstructured |
|---|---|
| Performance evaluation and compensation | Employees agitate for raises; squeaky wheel wins |
| Organisational design and territory | Ambitious executives lobby for expanded scope |
| Promotions | Perceived as political favours rather than merit |

For each: establish a formal, visible, defensible process. Involve the board for executive-level decisions. Do not make exceptions — each exception becomes the new standard.

**On executive-to-executive complaints:**
- Behavioural complaints: bring both parties into the room together
- Competency complaints: shut down the conversation immediately; do not signal agreement or disagreement; reassess the executive independently within days

## 8. The Most Difficult CEO Skill — Managing Your Own Psychology

> "The other thing that is true is that it is this exact skill — keeping your mind in check — that will determine your success as CEO."

Nobody prepares you for the psychological reality of leading a company. Every CEO experiences it; almost nobody talks about it. The first rule of the CEO psychological meltdown is: don't talk about the CEO psychological meltdown.

**The two failure modes:**
1. Taking things too personally → terrorising the team (outward) or becoming unable to function (inward)
2. Not taking things personally enough → Pollyannaish rationalisation → failing to fix real problems → team frustration

**Practical techniques:**

- **Make some friends** — find other CEOs who have faced similar situations. You won't get better advice, but you will feel less alone.
- **Get it out of your head and onto paper** — writing down your reasoning separates you from your own psychology and enables clearer decision-making.
- **Focus on the road, not the wall** — race car driving: if you focus on the wall, you will hit it. Focus on where you are going, not on what you fear.
- **Don't punk out** — great CEOs face the pain. They experience the sleepless nights and the cold sweats. The consistent answer from successful CEOs when asked how they did it: *"I didn't quit."*

## 9. Ones and Twos — CEO Types and Succession

Most CEOs lean towards one of two core skill sets:

| Type | Strength | Weakness |
|---|---|---|
| **Ones** | Strategic direction — knowing what to do; comfortable making decisions with little information; great at eight-dimensional chess | Can neglect execution details: process design, goal setting, training, structured accountability |
| **Twos** | Operational execution — making the company perform; insists on clear goals; strong at running processes and holding people accountable | Can struggle with strategic thinking; becomes nervous when not "doing"; can bring decision-making to a halt on high-uncertainty choices |

**Most founding CEOs are Ones.** When founding CEOs fail, a significant reason is that they never invested time to become competent at Two tasks. The resulting companies become too chaotic to reach their potential.

**Succession challenge:** Most executive teams under a One CEO are populated with Twos (and Functional Ones). Replacing a One with a Two creates strategic drift; replacing a One with a deeper-level One causes major executive team turnover. There is no clean answer.

**Business plan implication:** A management section that profiles the founding CEO against the One/Two framework — and addresses how the missing skill set will be covered — demonstrates self-awareness that investors value.

## 10. Follow the Leader — Three Attributes of Great CEOs

> "The measure of the quality of a leader: the quantity, quality, and diversity of people who want to follow her."

**1. The ability to articulate the vision — The Steve Jobs Attribute**
Can the leader articulate a vision that is dynamic and compelling when things fall apart? Jobs's greatest leadership achievement was getting great people to stay at NeXT long after the patina was gone — and buying into his vision at Apple when the company was weeks from bankruptcy.

**2. The right kind of ambition — The Bill Campbell Attribute**
Great leaders create an environment where employees feel the CEO cares more about them than about herself. Employees who feel this way say "my company" — they set quality standards for future employees as a form of personal ownership. This attribute is the closest to "born not made" of the three.

**3. The ability to achieve the vision — The Andy Grove Attribute**
Competence — pure and simple. Do I trust that this leader can actually navigate us through the jungle? This is entirely learnable: Grove earned a PhD, wrote the best management book Horowitz has read, and taught management classes throughout his tenure as Intel CEO.

## 11. Peacetime CEO vs Wartime CEO

> "There are lots of good peacetime CEOs and lots of good wartime CEOs, but almost no CEOs that can function in both peacetime and in wartime."

**Definitions:**
- **Peacetime:** The company has a large advantage in its core market and that market is growing. Focus is on expanding opportunity and reinforcing strengths.
- **Wartime:** The company is fending off an imminent existential threat — from competition, market change, macro shock, supply chain disruption, or similar.

| Peacetime CEO | Wartime CEO |
|---|---|
| Encourages broad-based creativity | Has a single bullet in the chamber and must hit the target |
| Focuses on the big picture; empowers people to make detailed decisions | Cares about a speck of dust on a gnat's ass if it interferes with the prime directive |
| Builds scalable recruiting | Also builds HR capacity to execute layoffs |
| Defines culture through deliberate design | Lets the war define the culture |
| Tolerates deviations from plan when coupled with effort | Completely intolerant |
| Works to minimise conflict | Heightens the contradictions |
| Strives for broad-based buy-in | Neither indulges consensus building nor tolerates disagreements |
| Sets big hairy audacious goals | Too busy fighting the enemy to read management books |
| Trains employees for career development and satisfaction | Trains employees so they don't get their asses shot off in the battle |

**Mastering both requires understanding the many rules of management and knowing when to follow them and when to violate them.** Most management books describe peacetime techniques. Andy Grove's books are among the very few that describe wartime.

**Business plan implication:** Most early-stage Ugandan businesses are in wartime mode — fighting for market share against entrenched competitors, managing constrained cash, dealing with infrastructure and regulatory uncertainty. The management section should indicate whether the CEO is calibrated for the current environment.

## 12. Accountability vs Creativity Paradox

When an engineer discovers a serious architectural flaw, slips the schedule from three months to nine months, but is proved right about the problem — do you reward the creativity or hold her accountable for the slip?

**The framework:**

**Accountability for effort** — non-negotiable. World-class companies require world-class effort.

**Accountability for promises** — essential for routine commitments (marketing collateral, emails, processes). For complex technical commitments involving hard computer science, more nuance is required.

**Accountability for results** — depends on three factors:

| Factor | How it affects accountability |
|---|---|
| **Seniority** | More senior executives are expected to forecast results more accurately |
| **Degree of difficulty** | Some things are genuinely hard; that must be taken into account |
| **Amount of stupid risk** | Good risk that didn't pan out ≠ avoidable risk that shouldn't have been taken |

The goal is to be urgent yet not insane — to move aggressively without being emotionally culpable for every setback. The difference between mediocre and magical is often the difference between giving up after setback and staying disciplined enough to iterate.

## 13. Keys to Effective Feedback

**Why the "Shit Sandwich" fails (eventually):** The positive–negative–positive structure becomes predictable, feels scripted, and loses authenticity with experienced executives.

**Six keys to effective feedback:**

1. **Be authentic** — believe in the feedback; do not say anything to manipulate the recipient's feelings
2. **Come from the right place** — give feedback because you want the person to succeed, not because you want them to fail; if you are genuinely in their corner, they will feel it and listen
3. **Don't get personal** — if you decide to fire someone, fire them; don't use feedback to prepare them for firing while pretending otherwise
4. **Don't clown people in front of peers** — public humiliation rarely improves performance; it produces shame and resentment
5. **Feedback is not one-size-fits-all** — calibrate tone to the employee's personality, not your mood
6. **Be direct, but not mean** — watered-down feedback is worse than none; it is both deceptive and confusing

**High-frequency feedback:** As CEO, have an opinion on everything. Express it. If feedback is a rare and weighty event, people read meaning into each instance. If it is constant and expected, people focus on the issue, not the relationship.

## 14. Hiring for Strength, Not Lack of Weakness

> "Hire for strength rather than lack of weakness." — Colin Powell

Most hiring decisions are dominated by concern about a candidate's weaknesses. Horowitz learned to first identify the specific strengths that a role requires, then assess whether a candidate has those strengths — even if the candidate also has obvious weaknesses.

**The Mark Cranney lesson:** Every member of the executive team and board voted against the head of sales hire. The reasons were all about weakness: wrong university, uncomfortable manner, didn't look like a head of sales. Horowitz hired him anyway because the strengths were exactly what the company needed. Cranney became one of the most effective sales leaders in the business.

**Why this matters for the management section:** Investors and lenders who review management team bios will note gaps (wrong school, short tenure, no relevant sector experience). The business plan must acknowledge these — and explain why the specific strengths outweigh them. Hiding weaknesses loses more credibility than addressing them directly.

## 15. Uganda / East Africa Application Notes

| Horowitz Principle | Uganda / EA Application |
|---|---|
| **The Struggle** | Every serious Ugandan entrepreneur faces the Struggle — regulatory surprises, currency depreciation, late government payments, mobile money failures, power cuts, and competitor aggression. The business plan that acknowledges this and shows resilience thinking is more credible than the plan that assumes clear skies. |
| **Tell It Like It Is** | Ugandan business culture sometimes rewards deferential communication upward. But lenders and investors who get bad news late lose confidence permanently. Founders who communicate proactively — even bad news — build the trust that survives crises. |
| **Layoffs Done Right** | Retrenchment in Uganda is governed by the Employment Act 2006 (s.88–89): written notice, severance at one month per year served, and consultation requirements. Knowing the process — and communicating it to affected employees with dignity — protects the company's reputation in a tight-knit labour market. |
| **People, Products, Profits in Order** | In Ugandan businesses where the owner-manager is the entire management team, "taking care of the people" often means personally knowing each employee's situation — family illness, school fees, housing — and responding to it. This is not paternalism; it is the informal social contract that retains loyalty when formal compensation cannot compete with larger employers. |
| **Minimising Politics — Right Kind of Ambition** | In East African businesses, the distinction between personal-gain ambition and company-gain ambition maps onto the challenge of hiring family members. A family member whose ambition is personal (secure income for the family) rather than company-oriented will generate the same political dynamics Horowitz describes — and is harder to address because of social obligations. Clarity about role expectations and accountability processes from day one is essential. |
| **Peacetime vs Wartime CEO** | Most Ugandan SMEs operate in permanent wartime: fighting for every customer, managing multi-week payment delays, navigating EFRIS compliance, and competing against informal operators with no overhead. A founder who manages as if it is peacetime (consensus-building, tolerating deviations, avoiding conflict) in this environment will not survive. The management section should show a wartime posture where warranted. |
| **Training** | Most Ugandan SME employees have never received structured role-specific training. The business plan that includes even a basic training plan (job shadowing, documented SOPs, monthly role review) is demonstrably more serious than the typical plan that mentions training as an afterthought. |
| **Ones and Twos** | Most Ugandan founder-CEOs are Ones (vision-setters, deal-makers, relationship-builders) who are deficient in Two skills (process design, financial reporting discipline, accountability systems). The management section should name this explicitly and show how it will be addressed — a finance manager, an operations co-founder, an experienced COO hire. |
