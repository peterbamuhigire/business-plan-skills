# Uganda Labour Market 2025
**Source:** World Bank / Government of Uganda — *Uganda Human Capital Development and Growth Review* (UHCDGR), Chapter 6: Maximising Human Capital Utilisation in the Labour Market, 2025
**Relevance:** Hiring plans, wage benchmarks, skills gap analysis, HR planning, management team context, talent acquisition strategy

---

## 1. Labour Force Size and Utilisation

- **Working-age population (2021):** 23.5 million
- **In the labour market:** 20.5 million (87% labour force participation if subsistence included)
- **Working for pay or profit:** only **10 million** — a utilisation rate of **49%**
- **Utilisation-adjusted HCI:** 0.19 (when only paid workers counted) — meaning Uganda utilises less than 1 in 5 potential units of human capital productively
- **GDP impact:** Uganda's GDP per capita could be **3–5× higher** with full productive utilisation

### Unemployment and Underemployment
- **Formal unemployment rate (2021 NLFS):** 12% (excludes subsistence workers)
- **Unemployment — women (2021 LFS):** 14.1%
- **Unemployment — men (2021 LFS):** 10.4%
- **Underemployment (time-based, <28 hrs/week):** increased **4-fold between 2011 and 2019**
- **Highest unemployment subgroups:** urban areas, ages 18–25, more educated (paradox of the educated unemployed)
- **Underemployment by sector:** agriculture is the worst — indicates severe productive underuse

---

## 2. Youth and NEET (Not in Employment, Education or Training)

- **NEET (ages 18–30, 2024 census):** **5.25 million** — more than **half of all young people** in this age group
  - Women NEET: **52%**
  - Men NEET: **28%**
- **Labour force participation (ages 18–30):** declining over time (partly due to rising educational attainment, but structural demand gap remains)
- **School-to-work transition:** most Ugandans are out of education by their early 20s; unemployment peaks at ages 18–25 before dropping as people fall into agriculture or informal trade

**For hiring plans:** The pool of available youth labour is enormous, but training requirements are high. Budget 2–4 weeks for basic induction. Pre-employment literacy/numeracy screening is recommended for technical roles.

## 3. Sectoral Employment Distribution (2021 NLFS)

| Sector | Share of Working Population |
|---|---|
| Non-market agriculture, forestry & fishing | **50%** |
| Market-oriented agriculture, forestry & fishing | **12%** |
| Services | **25%** |
| Industry (manufacturing, mining, construction) | Remainder |

- Agriculture employment share has remained constant at **~64%** despite agriculture's falling contribution to GDP — productivity has declined as more people crowd into a fixed land base
- Services sector is the most dynamic, on track to surpass industry productivity by 2025
- **Urban subsistence economy:** Kampala has the highest work-for-pay share at **83.5%**; rural Eastern and Northern regions are predominantly subsistence (61.9% of subsistence economy concentrated there)

## 4. Occupation and Skills Structure

### Skill Level Classification (ISCO)
| Tier | Occupation Types | Trend 2011–2019 |
|---|---|---|
| **High-skilled (ISCO 1–3)** | Managers, professionals, technicians | Stagnant / small growth |
| **Medium-skilled (ISCO 4–8)** | Clerical, sales, skilled agric, craft, machine operators | **Growing** (shift from low to medium) |
| **Low-skilled (ISCO 9)** | Elementary occupations | Declining share |

### High-Skilled Occupation Distribution (2019)
- **84%** of high-skilled jobs are in the services sector (education, health, finance)
- Only **12%** of sampled youth (ages 15–25) have achieved upper secondary or tertiary education — yet the same group aspires to medical, engineering, and teaching jobs
- **25%** of individuals in high-skilled occupations have only a primary education (experiential knowledge)

### Skills Mismatch
- **~25% of high-skilled occupation holders** have only primary education (skills acquired through experience)
- **~10% of low-skilled workers** hold tertiary credentials — overeducated, underemployed
- This dual mismatch creates **labour market inefficiency** — gains available from better intermediation and matching
- Key reported skill shortages in Kampala informal sector: **customer service, managerial skills, communication skills** (especially cited by tertiary-educated owners)

## 5. Education-to-Wages: The Returns to Human Capital

| Education Level | Relative Median Wage (2019) |
|---|---|
| No formal education | **Baseline (1×)** |
| Primary complete | ~2–3× baseline |
| Secondary complete | ~4–5× baseline |
| Tertiary (university/TVET advanced) | **7×** baseline |

### Occupation-Level Wage Premium
| Occupation Skill Level | Median Earnings vs. Low-Skilled |
|---|---|
| Low-skilled | Baseline |
| Medium-skilled | Higher; stable 2011–2019 |
| **High-skilled** | **4× low-skilled median** |

- Low-skilled occupation earnings **fell by nearly half** between 2011 and 2019
- Medium- and high-skilled earnings remained **relatively stable** over the same period
- **Urban vs. rural premium:** Earnings higher in urban areas for equivalent education/skills

**For compensation planning:**
- Secondary-school-educated staff in Uganda expect 4–5× the wage of those with no education
- The return to tertiary education is high — use this to justify investment in tertiary-qualified managers
- Low-skilled wages are the most volatile — do not anchor business models to indefinitely cheap unskilled labour

## 6. Gender Gaps in the Labour Market

- Women are significantly less likely to hold high-skilled, high-paying jobs or to own a business
- Female employment is concentrated in agriculture under age 30; men have more access to industrial and services sector work
- Higher education is the strongest pathway for women into non-agricultural work (see `references/women-entrepreneurship-uganda.md` for full gender analysis)
- Constraints: time poverty (childcare, domestic duties), geographic mobility barriers, social norms
- **For management plans:** Short-term interventions that work — childcare access, information on education returns, tailored support for women entering traditionally male sectors

## 7. Firm Creation and Survival

### Uganda's Firm Landscape
- **>90% of firms** are micro, informal, and household-based enterprises
- **28% of adults** own or co-own a new business (Uganda ranked 3rd globally, GEM 2014)
- **Business discontinuation rates:** among the **highest in the world**
- Formal firms (manufacturing + services, 5+ employees): average lifespan **15 years** (vs. 22 years in Kenya)
- Only a **small portion** of the informal sector has real growth potential — the majority is subsistence-oriented

### What Drives Firm Failure
- High tax rates (cited by owners as top barrier)
- Corruption / regulatory friction
- Low human capital of both owners and workers
- Inadequate business skills: record-keeping, financial management, inventory control

### Education of Owner = Business Performance
| Owner Education Level | Productivity vs. Less Educated | Revenue Growth (past 3 years) |
|---|---|---|
| Secondary or above | **2× more productive** (controlled for sector, size, age) | **>50%** grew revenue |
| Below primary complete | Baseline | **32%** grew revenue |

- Informal firms with better-educated owners more likely to have at least **one paid employee**
- Firms with tertiary-educated owners report **managerial and communication skill shortages** — they have grown to need specialists they cannot find locally

**For business plans:** Founder educational background is a genuine predictor of success in Uganda's context. Present it prominently. Where there is a gap, the advisory board and hiring plan must demonstrably fill it.

## 8. Structural Transformation: Slow Progress

- Agriculture's **share of GDP** has fallen significantly, but its **employment share** remains ~64%
- Number of people in agriculture has actually **tripled since 2000** due to population growth — productivity per worker is declining
- Uganda's **value added per worker** is among the **lowest in Sub-Saharan Africa** and on a declining trend since 2000
- Agricultural productivity is well below industry and services productivity (see Figure 6.10 data)
- Productivity growth in mining, manufacturing, ICT, and professional services between 2016–2019 was modest but positive

### Sectoral Productivity Outlook
- Services sector is the most dynamic and will surpass industry productivity by 2025
- **Wholesale/retail trade:** largest single non-agriculture employer (30% of medium-skilled)
- **Manufacturing:** 6% of medium-skilled occupations
- **Education and health:** account for 84% of high-skilled employment — the primary vehicle for skilled job creation today

---

## 9. Labour Supply Pressure

- Uganda population growing at **>3% per year** — only Sub-Saharan country sustaining this rate since 2000
- **1.1–1.2 million new workers entering labour force annually**
- 14 million additional workers must be absorbed by 2040
- **Urban/rural split:** both are growing — urban areas (excluding Kampala) growing rapidly; rural population projected to grow **54%+ by 2030**
- **Implication:** Even with rapid urbanisation, rural agricultural labour remains abundant; urban labour markets will face increasing competitive pressure on wages for mid-level roles

## 10. Migration and Remittances

- **Ugandans migrating abroad annually:** approximately **0.78 million** (up from 0.3 million three decades ago)
- **Main destinations:** Kenya (333,789), South Sudan (145,607), UK (72,256), **Saudi Arabia (~150,000)**
- **Gulf Cooperation Council roles:** domestic work, construction, security, transport, hospitality
- **OECD-bound Ugandans:** tend to be highly educated, skilled professionals
- **Remittances received (2023):** US$1.4 billion — **~3% of Uganda's GDP**; **7th largest recipient in Sub-Saharan Africa** (World Bank, 2024)
- Uganda is negotiating bilateral labour agreements with UAE, Qatar, and Oman

### Brain Drain Risk
- Skilled professionals face strong pull factors from abroad (salary differentials, career progression)
- **Businesses in health, IT, engineering, and finance** face heightened retention risk
- Mitigation: competitive salaries at least matching public sector benchmarks + non-monetary benefits (training, career development, flexible working)

### Refugee Labour Pool
- Uganda hosts **nearly 1.7 million people** (1.4 million refugees — primarily South Sudanese and Congolese)
- Under Uganda's progressive refugee policy, refugees have the right to work and own businesses
- EAC nationals (Kenya, Tanzania, Rwanda, Burundi, South Sudan) can work in Uganda without a work permit (EAC Common Market Protocol, Art. 10)

## 11. Policy Recommendations Relevant to Business Owners

The World Bank (2025) identifies three priority areas for Uganda's labour market — each has direct implications for business planning:

### Area 1: Increasing Human Capital Utilisation (especially for youth and women)
- Short-term work-based skills development (apprenticeships, on-the-job training) — **businesses can participate and access trained staff**
- Female employment constraints addressed through childcare access, sector-crossing information, tailored support

### Area 2: Enhancing Worker Productivity
- Private sector involvement in TVET design is critical — the Skills Development Facility (with PSFU) is the primary vehicle
- Technical skills must be locally relevant and in demand — digital skills, green/blue economy, creative sector skills are flagged as future priorities
- Returns to tertiary education remain high — worth investing in staff upskilling

### Area 3: Improving the Entrepreneurship and Business Environment
- Labor market intermediation services (digital and physical) are underdeveloped — opportunity for HR-tech, job board, and staffing agency businesses
- Micro-enterprise owners need: business practice coaching, managerial skills, market access, network connections, and affordable finance
- Apprenticeship programmes can simultaneously train workers and reduce business skills mismatch

## 12. Practical Benchmarks for Business Plans

### Hiring Cost Estimates (Uganda, 2025)
| Role Category | Typical Monthly Salary Range (UGX) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Unskilled / elementary | 200,000–400,000 | Agricultural and general labour |
| Semi-skilled (P7 + some training) | 400,000–700,000 | Machine operators, shop assistants |
| Skilled secondary-educated | 700,000–1,500,000 | Artisans, technicians, admin staff |
| Degree-qualified professional | 1,500,000–5,000,000 | Accountants, engineers, teachers |
| Management / senior professional | 3,000,000–10,000,000+ | Varies significantly by sector |

*Note: These are indicative ranges. See `references/payroll-hr.md` for PAYE tax brackets, NSSF obligations, and full payroll calculation methodology.*

### Employer Cost Multiplier
- Employer's total cost = gross salary × **1.10–1.15** (to cover NSSF 10% contribution + payroll admin overhead)
- Factor in sick leave (malaria: budget ~3–5 days/employee/year), training, and PPE for manual roles

### Recruitment Lead Times
- Unskilled: 1–2 weeks
- Semi-skilled/skilled: 3–6 weeks (limited supply of trained candidates)
- Professional/specialist: 6–16 weeks; consider headhunting via professional networks or PSFU
- EAC nationals can fill specialist gaps quickly without permit complexity
