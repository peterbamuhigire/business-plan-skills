# Women's Entrepreneurship in Uganda — Policy Evidence

*Sources: World Bank/IFC, "Unlocking the Potential of Women Entrepreneurs in Uganda: A Brief of Policy Interventions" (Copley, Gokalp & Kirkwood; World Bank, 2022). Cite as (World Bank, 2022) in business plans. Additional evidence drawn from "Profiting from Parity: Unlocking the Potential of Women's Business in Africa" (World Bank, 2019).*

---

## 1. Overview — Women's Entrepreneurship in Uganda

Women are major economic actors in Uganda, yet face a persistent earnings gap driven by structural constraints rather than ability.

### Regional Context (Sub-Saharan Africa)
- **42%** of the non-agricultural labour force is self-employed or is an employer — the highest rate in the world
- Women are **more likely to be entrepreneurs than men** in Sub-Saharan Africa
- Women-owned businesses earn only **two-thirds** of the profits of male-owned counterparts

### Uganda-Specific Statistics
| Indicator | Value |
|---|---|
| Self-employed Ugandans | 52% of working population |
| Job-creating employers among self-employed | <4% |
| Gender profit gap | Women earn **30% lower profits** than male microentrepreneurs |
| Labour hours used | Women use **41% fewer hours** of labour in their businesses |
| Capital deployed | Women have **50% less capital** than male counterparts |
| Product innovation rate | Women **37% less likely** to have introduced a new product in past 12 months |
| Women in male-dominated sectors | Only **6%** of women entrepreneurs |

### The Sector Segregation Gap
- Women confined to traditionally female sectors earn significantly less
- Transitioning from female-concentrated to male-dominated sector: **141% increase in average monthly profits**
- Women who do cross over into male-dominated sectors are **just as profitable as men** in those sectors
- Women crossover entrepreneurs earn **twice the profits** of women who do not cross over
- **75%** of women interviewed who had not crossed over believed (incorrectly) they were earning equal to or more than crossover women

## 2. Gender-Specific Barriers to Enterprise Performance

### Legal and Structural Barriers
- No laws prohibiting gender-based discrimination by financial institutions
- **84%** of rural land parcels held under unregistered customary tenure (Uganda National Panel Survey 2013/14)
- Women's land access typically conditioned on relationship to a male spouse or relative — secondary use rights, not ownership
- Limited collateral access → locked out of growth-oriented loans
- No legal prohibition on gender discrimination in credit decisions

### Capital and Finance Barriers
- Lower asset ownership → smaller loan sizes relative to male counterparts
- Traditional microfinance typically insufficient for enterprise growth (small loan volumes)
- "Missing middle" phenomenon: growth-oriented women-owned enterprises whose credit needs are too large for microfinance but too small for commercial banks
- Unequal household bargaining power → household demands erode business cash flow reinvestment

### Time and Domestic Constraints
- Greater domestic and care responsibilities limit hours allocated to enterprise
- Women prioritise ability to combine work with domestic roles over sector earnings potential
- This drives preference for home-based microenterprises in female-concentrated sectors

### Network and Information Barriers
- Smaller business networks with fewer contacts in male-dominated sectors
- Limited exposure to apprenticeship opportunities in higher-value sectors
- Gender norms discourage entering sectors perceived as male domains
- Lack of information on relative earnings by sector (75% of women incorrectly estimate equal earnings)

## 3. Sector Map — Female-Concentrated vs Male-Dominated

### Typical Female-Concentrated Sectors (Uganda)
- Trade and textile manufacturing
- Food service and catering
- Accommodation
- Hair dressing and personal services
- Clay moulding, tailoring/knitting

### Male-Dominated Sectors (Uganda microentrepreneurs)
- Wood manufacturing and repair
- Electric and gas supply
- Leather manufacturing and repair
- Fitting and machinery
- Metal works and engineering
- Foundry

### Employment Pattern in Women-Owned Enterprises
- **75% of employees** in women-owned enterprises are women
- Only **20% of employees** in male-owned enterprises are women (even controlling for sector)
- This means growing women-owned enterprises multiplies employment for women more broadly

## 4. Evidence-Based Interventions

### 4.1 Skills — Psychology-Based Business Training

Standard business skills training shows minimal impact for women. Psychology-based training targeting non-cognitive skills shows significantly better results.

**Personal Initiative Training (evidence from Togo and Uganda):**
- Boosted women's profits by **40%** vs statistically insignificant 5% from standard business training
- Women invested more in businesses, more likely to introduce new products
- Women proactively pursued financing sources post-training
- Proven effective across multiple contexts (Uganda, Mozambique, Togo)
- In Mozambique: personal initiative training moved women farmers into more profitable crops and off-farm businesses

**Implication for business plans:** Where the founder/owner is a woman, flag non-cognitive skills development (personal initiative, resilience, growth mindset) as part of the management development plan rather than relying solely on traditional business skills training.

### 4.2 Capital — Larger Loans and Reduced Collateral Requirements

**Large individual loans (evidence from Ethiopia):**
- Women applying for larger individual liability loans (vs group microfinance): +40% profits after 3 years
- Employment growth: +55% compared to control group

**Business plan competitions (evidence from Nigeria):**
- Cash grants averaging US$50,000 increased likelihood of operating an enterprise and boosted employment, sales, and profits

**Microfinance limitations:**
- Typical microfinance volumes have poor track record for transformational business growth
- Group lending schemes may be insufficient to drive investment and growth

**Psychometric credit scoring (alternative to collateral, Ethiopia):**
- Customers who scored very well on psychometric test were **7× more likely to repay** loans
- Enables risk-averse lenders to invest in women without traditional collateral

**Secure savings mechanisms:**
- Combining business registration + bank account access → 28% increase in enterprise sales, 20% increase in profits (Malawi)
- Mobile money: preferred by women for privacy (less observable to other household members)
- M-PESA (Kenya): increased per capita consumption, lifted 194,000 households out of poverty; impacts larger for female-headed households
- Mobile savings + business training → compounding positive effect (Tanzania)

### 4.3 Sectoral Diversification — Getting Women into Higher-Value Sectors

**Role models and mentors:**
- Women with male role models in youth were **20–28% more likely** to cross over into male-dominated sector (Uganda study)

**Information on returns by sector:**
- Providing video information on returns increased likelihood of choosing male-dominated vocational training by **28.6%** (Republic of Congo)

**Vocational training:**
- ICT training in Nigeria → university graduates 26% more likely to work in ICT sector; larger impact on those initially biased against women in ICT

**Access to capital:**
- Higher start-up capital requirements of male-dominated sectors are a practical barrier — larger initial loans needed to enable crossover

### 4.4 Bundled Economic Inclusion Programmes (for Most Vulnerable Women)

**Niger programme (cash grants + savings groups + training + psychosocial support):**
- Strong increases in earnings from income-generating activities
- Savings and asset accumulation increased
- Improved psychological and social well-being
- Enhanced control over earnings and productive agency
- Positive impact on gender attitudes in the community

**Ghana programme (livestock asset grant + training + savings services):**
- 91% increase in non-farm income
- Significant gains in livestock revenue
- 58% more borrowing than comparison group
- 3× more savings than comparison households

## 5. Practical Implications for Business Plans

### Framing Women-Owned Businesses

When a business plan is for a women-owned enterprise, the plan should address:

1. **Gender gap awareness:** Acknowledge that women-owned businesses face structural constraints — but these are structural, not ability-based. Frame the business accordingly.

2. **Sector choice:** If the business operates in a female-concentrated sector, the plan should explicitly address either:
   - How the business differentiates to achieve above-average profitability despite the sector, OR
   - Whether there is a pathway to higher-value adjacent services/products

3. **Capital access strategy:** Women-owned businesses should identify DFI programmes, women-focused lending windows, and SACCO-based options as part of the funding mix. Examples in Uganda:
   - Uganda Development Bank (UDB) has lending windows targeting women and youth
   - aBi Finance, UNCDF, and various NGO-managed loan funds offer collateral-lite options
   - MFPED Women's Enterprise Fund (WEF) — grants and soft loans for women-owned enterprises

4. **Management team narrative:** For a woman-led team, highlight:
   - Personal initiative and resilience (validated by research as key success predictors)
   - Mentors and role models in or adjacent to the sector
   - Advisory board members (particularly any in male-dominated adjacent sectors)
   - Business networks and any sector associations

5. **Labour strategy:** Women-owned enterprises that grow create disproportionately more employment for women (75% of employees). This is a bankability and social impact narrative point for donor and DFI funders.

### Risk Analysis Considerations
- Flag the gender-specific capital access constraint and mitigation strategy
- Identify any collateral limitations and how they are addressed (movable assets law, psychometric scoring, group guarantee, etc.)
- Time allocation risk: if founder has significant care responsibilities, plan should show delegation, management structure, or flexible operations model
- Household income drain risk: document how business finances are kept separate from household finances (business bank account, mobile money account)

### 09-Management Team Section
Where the founder is a woman entering a male-dominated sector, the plan should explicitly note:
- The profit premium associated with crossover businesses (research evidence)
- Specific networks, mentors, and training that provide sector access
- Capital strategy to meet higher entry requirements

## 6. Uganda Policy Context

**NDP III Goals (2020/21–2024/25):**
- Increase household incomes by >50% over 2017/18 baseline
- Sustainable industrialisation for inclusive growth, employment, and wealth creation by 2024/25

**Gender gap closure as growth lever:**
- Closing the gender earnings gap is critical both for inclusion AND for aggregate growth
- 75% of women-owned enterprise employees are women — growing women-owned businesses creates a disproportionate employment multiplier for women in the workforce

**Key constraint acknowledged by government:** Currently low average enterprise productivity; need to help growth-oriented enterprises increase capacity for more and higher-quality jobs.
