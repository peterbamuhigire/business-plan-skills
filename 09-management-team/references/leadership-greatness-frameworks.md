# Leadership & Greatness Frameworks for Business Plans

> Reference: *Good to Great: Why Some Companies Make the Leap... and Others Don't* by Jim Collins (HarperBusiness, 2001)

---

## 1. Level 5 Leadership

*Source: Collins, Ch 2*

Collins discovered that every company which made the leap from good to great had what he calls a **Level 5 leader** at the helm during the transition. These leaders blend two seemingly contradictory qualities: deep personal humility and intense professional will.

### 1.1 The Five-Level Hierarchy

| Level | Title | Description |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| **1** | Highly Capable Individual | Makes productive contributions through talent, knowledge, skills, and good work habits |
| **2** | Contributing Team Member | Contributes individual capabilities to the achievement of group objectives; works effectively with others |
| **3** | Competent Manager | Organises people and resources toward the effective and efficient pursuit of predetermined objectives |
| **4** | Effective Leader | Catalyses commitment to and vigorous pursuit of a clear and compelling vision, stimulating higher performance standards |
| **5** | Level 5 Executive | Builds enduring greatness through a paradoxical blend of personal humility and professional will |

Level 5 leaders embody all five layers. You need not move sequentially — but fully developed Level 5 leaders display capabilities at every level.

### 1.2 The Humility + Will Paradox

Level 5 leaders are a study in duality:

| Professional Will | Personal Humility |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Creates superb results; a clear catalyst in the transition from good to great | Demonstrates compelling modesty; shuns public adulation; never boastful |
| Unwavering resolve to do whatever must be done, no matter how difficult | Acts with quiet, calm determination; relies on inspired standards, not inspiring charisma |
| Sets the standard of building an enduring great company; settles for nothing less | Channels ambition into the company, not the self; sets up successors for even greater success |
| Looks in the **mirror** to apportion responsibility for poor results | Looks out the **window** to credit others and good fortune for success |

### 1.3 The Window and the Mirror

This is Collins's most memorable metaphor for Level 5 behaviour:

- **When things go well** — Level 5 leaders look out the *window* to credit other people, external factors, and good luck.
- **When things go badly** — Level 5 leaders look in the *mirror* and take full responsibility, never blaming others or bad luck.

Comparison leaders do the opposite: they preen in the mirror when things go well and blame external factors when things go badly.

### 1.4 Applying Level 5 Leadership in a Business Plan

| Business Plan Element | How to Apply |
|----------------------|--------------|
| **Founder bios** | Emphasise track record of building things (not personal celebrity). Highlight what the team achieved, not "I" statements |
| **Leadership tone** | Use "we" language throughout. Credit the team, advisors, and mentors |
| **Succession planning** | Show that the venture does not depend on one genius. Describe how leadership capability is being built at multiple levels |
| **Risk section** | Take ownership of past failures honestly. Demonstrate the "mirror" quality — investors trust founders who own their mistakes |
| **Vision statement** | Express fierce ambition *for the company*, not for personal wealth or fame |

**Red flag for investors:** A business plan saturated with "I" language and personal hero narratives signals a Level 4 leader, not Level 5. Collins's research found that companies led by celebrity CEOs frequently failed to sustain greatness.

## 2. First Who, Then What

*Source: Collins, Ch 3*

The good-to-great leaders did not first set a vision and then recruit people to execute it. They did the opposite: **get the right people on the bus first, the wrong people off, and the right people in the right seats** — then figure out where to drive.

### 2.1 Three Practical Truths

| Truth | Explanation |
|-------|-------------|
| **1. Adaptability** | If people join because of *who else* is on the bus (not just *where* it is going), the team adapts more easily when direction must change |
| **2. Self-motivation** | The right people do not need to be tightly managed or "fired up." They are driven by an inner compulsion to produce the best results |
| **3. Vision without people is irrelevant** | If you have the wrong people, it does not matter whether you discover the right direction — you still will not have a great company |

### 2.2 Rigorous, Not Ruthless

Collins draws a sharp distinction:

- **Ruthless** = hacking and cutting wantonly, firing without thoughtful consideration.
- **Rigorous** = consistently applying exacting standards at all times and at all levels.

Three disciplines of rigorous hiring:

1. **When in doubt, don't hire — keep looking.** Never compromise on quality to fill a seat quickly.
2. **When you know you need to make a people change, act.** Letting the wrong person stay is unfair to all the right people.
3. **Put your best people on your biggest opportunities, not your biggest problems.** Managing problems makes you good; building opportunities makes you great.

### 2.3 Character over CV

Collins found that good-to-great companies placed greater weight on **character attributes** — work ethic, basic intelligence, dedication, values — than on specific educational background or technical skills. Skills are teachable; character is ingrained.

### 2.4 Applying "First Who" in a Business Plan

| Business Plan Section | How to Apply |
|----------------------|--------------|
| **Management team** | Lead with *who* the team members are (character, values, complementary strengths), not just their job titles and qualifications |
| **Hiring plan** | State: "We will not compromise on hiring standards even if it slows growth." Describe your hiring criteria and process |
| **Skills gap analysis** | Be honest. Acknowledging gaps signals rigour; pretending they don't exist signals delusion |
| **Compensation** | Explain that you pay fairly but that your team is motivated by the mission, not just money. Collins found no correlation between executive pay structures and great results |
| **Org chart** | Show that key seats are filled by A-players. If a seat is empty, say so — and explain the plan to fill it with the right person |

**Worked example (Uganda):**
> "Our Head of Operations, Aisha Nakamya, spent 8 years building the supply chain for a Kampala-based FMCG distributor, growing it from UGX 200M to UGX 3.4B in annual throughput. She joined our venture not for the salary — which is below her market rate — but because she believes smallholder farmers in the Luwero Triangle deserve access to cold-chain logistics. We would rather leave the CFO seat empty for six months than fill it with someone who lacks both the financial rigour and the mission alignment we require."

## 3. The Hedgehog Concept (Three Circles)

*Source: Collins, Ch 5*

This is arguably Collins's most important framework. Based on Isaiah Berlin's essay *The Hedgehog and the Fox*, it distinguishes between foxes (who pursue many strategies) and hedgehogs (who simplify the world into one organising idea).

A **Hedgehog Concept** is a simple, crystalline concept that flows from deep understanding about the intersection of three circles:

### 3.1 The Three Circles

```
                    +--------------------------+
                    | What can you be the BEST |
                    |    in the world at?      |
                    +----------+---------------+
                               |
                    +----------+---------------+
                    |     HEDGEHOG CONCEPT     |
                    |  (intersection of all 3) |
                    +----------+---------------+
                              / \
       +---------------------+   +----------------------+
       | What drives your    |   | What are you deeply  |
       | ECONOMIC ENGINE?    |   | PASSIONATE about?    |
       | (profit per X)      |   |                      |
       +---------------------+   +----------------------+
```

| Circle | Key Question | Pitfall |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Best in the world** | What could we potentially be the best at — and equally, what can we *not* be the best at? | Confusing core competence with "best in the world" potential. Having a competence does not mean you can be the best |
| **2. Economic engine** | What single denominator — profit per X — has the greatest impact on our economics? | Failing to identify the key economic driver. The "X" forces rigorous thinking about what truly drives profitability |
| **3. Deep passion** | What work ignites genuine passion in the team? | Trying to *stimulate* passion rather than *discovering* what already makes you passionate |

**Critical rule:** You need all three circles. Passion without economic viability produces a hobby. Economic viability without the potential to be best produces mediocrity. Being best without passion is unsustainable.

### 3.2 Hedgehog Concept vs Core Competence

A Hedgehog Concept is not the same as a core competence. You may be competent at something but lack the potential to be the *best in the world* at it. Abbott Laboratories was competent in pharmaceuticals but recognised it could never beat Merck. It shifted to becoming the best at creating products that make health care more cost-effective — and outperformed Merck's stock returns.

### 3.3 Worked Example: Ugandan Agri-Processing Business

**Business:** A company producing value-added matooke (banana) products in Mbarara, Western Uganda.

| Circle | Analysis |
|--------|----------|
| **Best in the world at** | Processing and packaging fresh matooke into shelf-stable flour and snack products. No global competitor has deep expertise in East African cooking banana varieties. We have proprietary low-temperature drying technology adapted for tropical conditions |
| **Economic engine** | **Profit per tonne of raw matooke processed.** Our analysis shows that each additional tonne processed above 40 tonnes/month drops UGX 1.2M to the bottom line due to fixed-cost absorption. The denominator forces us to maximise throughput, not just revenue |
| **Deep passion** | The founding team grew up in banana-farming families. We are genuinely passionate about transforming a crop that currently suffers 40% post-harvest loss into a product that feeds East Africa and earns foreign exchange |

**Hedgehog Concept statement:**
> "We will become the best company in East Africa at converting fresh cooking bananas into shelf-stable, nutritious food products, driven by profit per tonne processed, fuelled by our deep connection to banana-farming communities."

**What this means for the business plan:**
- **Strategy section:** Every strategic initiative must pass the three-circle test. If it does not sit at the intersection, it does not belong in the plan.
- **Product roadmap:** Only develop products that leverage our matooke processing expertise (Circle 1). Reject tempting diversifications into, say, coffee processing — we cannot be the best at that.
- **Financial projections:** Track and forecast the key denominator (profit per tonne). Build the financial model around this metric.
- **Stop-doing list:** Explicitly state what the business will *not* do, and why.

### 3.4 The Iterative Discovery Process

Collins warns that a Hedgehog Concept cannot be produced in a weekend offsite. It takes, on average, **four years** for good-to-great companies to clarify their Hedgehog Concept. The process is iterative:

1. Form a "Council" of the right people (5–12 members).
2. Engage in dialogue and debate guided by the three circles.
3. Make decisions. Observe results. Learn.
4. Repeat — refining understanding with each cycle.

**For a business plan:** If your Hedgehog Concept is not yet crystallised, say so honestly. Present your current best understanding of the three circles and describe the process you are using to refine it. Investors respect intellectual honesty far more than false certainty.

## 4. Confront the Brutal Facts / Stockdale Paradox

*Source: Collins, Ch 4*

### 4.1 The Core Principle

Good-to-great companies made better decisions because they began with an honest, diligent effort to determine the truth of their situation. Collins found that **the right decisions often become self-evident once you confront the brutal facts**.

### 4.2 The Stockdale Paradox

Named after Admiral Jim Stockdale, the highest-ranking US military officer held as a prisoner of war in Vietnam (1965–1973). When asked who did *not* survive the camps, Stockdale answered: "The optimists — the ones who said, 'We'll be out by Christmas.' Christmas would come and go. Then Easter. Then the next Christmas. They died of broken hearts."

The paradox:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Unwavering faith** | Retain absolute confidence that you *will* prevail in the end, regardless of the difficulties |
| **Brutal honesty** | Simultaneously confront the most brutal facts of your current reality, whatever they might be |

You must hold both simultaneously. Faith without facts is delusion. Facts without faith is despair.

### 4.3 Creating a Climate Where Truth is Heard

Collins identified four practices:

1. **Lead with questions, not answers.** Use meetings to gain understanding, not to impose your view.
2. **Engage in dialogue and debate, not coercion.** Create forums for vigorous, unscripted discussion.
3. **Conduct autopsies without blame.** When things go wrong, analyse *what* happened without pointing fingers.
4. **Build "red flag" mechanisms.** Create systems that surface uncomfortable truths automatically — do not rely on people volunteering bad news.

### 4.4 Applying the Stockdale Paradox in a Business Plan

| Business Plan Section | How to Apply |
|----------------------|--------------|
| **Risk analysis** | List the brutal facts. Do not sanitise. Then show your plan to prevail despite each risk. This *is* the Stockdale Paradox in action |
| **Market analysis** | Present the market as it truly is — including unfavourable data — then explain why you will succeed anyway |
| **Financial projections** | Include a downside scenario that reflects genuinely brutal assumptions. Show the business survives even under that scenario |
| **SWOT / competitive analysis** | Be ruthlessly honest about weaknesses. Investors will discover them anyway; better to show you already know |
| **Executive summary** | Express unwavering conviction in the mission, supported by evidence, not wishful thinking |

**Practical test:** Read your risk section aloud. If it makes you uncomfortable, it is probably honest enough. If it reads like a marketing brochure, rewrite it.

## 5. Culture of Discipline

*Source: Collins, Ch 6*

### 5.1 The Entrepreneurship + Discipline Matrix

Collins presents a two-by-two matrix:

| | Low Discipline | High Discipline |
|---|---------------|-----------------|
| **High Entrepreneurship** | Start-up Organisation (chaotic, creative, fragile) | **Great Organisation** (creative discipline) |
| **Low Entrepreneurship** | Bureaucratic Organisation (slow death) | Hierarchical Organisation (rigid, controlling) |

The goal is the upper-right quadrant: **a culture of discipline combined with an ethic of entrepreneurship**.

### 5.2 The Rinse-Your-Cottage-Cheese Principle

Named after Dave Scott, six-time winner of the Hawaii Ironman Triathlon, who rinsed his cottage cheese to remove extra fat — despite burning 5,000+ calories daily in training. The point: greatness comes from the disciplined accumulation of many small steps, each one contributing marginal improvement.

**For business plans:** Show that your team sweats the small stuff. Demonstrate concrete, measurable habits:
- "We review our unit economics weekly, not monthly."
- "Every team member tracks their own KPIs on a shared dashboard."
- "We reduced packaging waste by 12% through systematic daily audits."

### 5.3 Concrete Objectives — Never Fudge

Collins highlights Abbott Laboratories' practice: set objectives at the start of the year, record them in concrete, and measure yourself against exactly what you said — no editorialising, no retroactive adjustment, no redefining success after the fact.

**For business plans:**
- State measurable milestones with specific dates and numbers.
- In subsequent investor updates, report against *exactly* those milestones — not revised ones.
- If you missed a target, say so plainly and explain what you learned.

**Example:**

| Milestone | Target | Date | Metric |
|-----------|--------|------|--------|
| First production run | 5 tonnes processed | Q2 2026 | Tonnes of output |
| Break-even month | Revenue >= costs | Month 14 | Net monthly profit in UGX |
| Key hire: CFO | Appointed and onboarded | Q3 2026 | Role filled (yes/no) |

### 5.4 A Culture, Not a Tyrant

Collins warns against confusing **a culture of discipline** with **a tyrannical disciplinarian**. Companies led by brilliant dictators (e.g. Rubbermaid under Stanley Gault, Chrysler under Iacocca) produced spectacular short-term results that collapsed after the leader departed. True discipline is embedded in the culture and systems, not in one person's force of personality.

**For business plans:** Describe the *systems and processes* that enforce discipline, not just the founder's personal drive. Show that the discipline will persist even if any single leader departs.

## 6. The Flywheel and the Doom Loop

*Source: Collins, Ch 8*

### 6.1 The Flywheel

Imagine a massive metal flywheel — 30 feet in diameter, 5,000 pounds. Your task is to get it rotating. You push with enormous effort; it barely moves. You keep pushing. Slowly it completes one turn, then two, then ten. Gradually, momentum builds. Then — breakthrough. The flywheel's own weight works for you; it spins faster and faster with the same effort per push.

**The key insight:** No single push causes the breakthrough. It is the cumulative effect of consistent effort in the same direction. There is no "miracle moment."

### 6.2 Buildup Then Breakthrough

Every good-to-great company followed this pattern:

1. **Buildup phase** (often years): quiet, unglamorous accumulation of momentum — right people, brutal facts confronted, Hedgehog Concept clarified.
2. **Breakthrough point:** results accelerate dramatically. Outside observers see "overnight success" — but insiders know it was years of steady pushing.

Collins found that buildup phases ranged from 2 years (Pitney Bowes) to 10 years (Nucor). The median was approximately 5 years.

### 6.3 The Doom Loop

Comparison companies displayed the opposite pattern:

1. Launch a big programme, restructuring, or acquisition with great fanfare.
2. Fail to produce sustained results.
3. React by lurching to a new direction, new leader, new programme.
4. Repeat — never building cumulative momentum.

**Doom Loop characteristics:**
- Chronic inconsistency — lurching between strategies
- Skipping buildup and trying to jump straight to breakthrough
- New CEO = new direction = flywheel reset to zero
- Major acquisitions used to *create* momentum rather than *accelerate* existing momentum

### 6.4 Applying the Flywheel in a Business Plan

| Business Plan Section | How to Apply |
|----------------------|--------------|
| **Implementation timeline** | Present milestones as sequential turns of the flywheel. Show how each step builds on the previous one. Avoid presenting disconnected "big bang" initiatives |
| **Financial projections** | Model a buildup phase (modest early results) followed by acceleration. Hockey-stick projections are only credible if you show the buildup mechanics |
| **Growth strategy** | Describe how each phase of growth compounds on the previous phase. "Our first 50 customers validate the model; the next 200 come through referrals from the first 50; the next 1,000 come through partnerships unlocked by our proven track record" |
| **Investor narrative** | Set expectations: "We are in the buildup phase. Here are the turns of the flywheel we have already completed. Here is what breakthrough looks like" |
| **Acquisitions** | If you plan acquisitions, position them as accelerators of an *already spinning* flywheel — not as a substitute for organic momentum |

**Worked example (Ugandan logistics start-up):**

| Flywheel Turn | Activity | Outcome |
|--------------|----------|---------|
| 1 | Secure 3 anchor clients in Kampala | UGX 45M monthly revenue; prove the delivery model |
| 2 | Use performance data to negotiate lower fuel costs | Improve gross margin from 18% to 24% |
| 3 | Reinvest margin gains into a second delivery vehicle | Increase capacity by 40% |
| 4 | Higher capacity attracts 8 more clients via referrals | UGX 120M monthly revenue |
| 5 | Proven model unlocks UGX 500M asset finance facility | Acquire fleet of 5 vehicles |
| 6 | Regional expansion to Jinja and Mbale | Revenue exceeds UGX 300M/month — breakthrough |

Each turn builds upon the previous one. No single turn is "the answer." The cumulative effect creates unstoppable momentum.

## 7. Technology as an Accelerator

*Source: Collins, Ch 7 concepts; Ch 8 integration*

### 7.1 The Technology Accelerator Test

Collins found that good-to-great companies thought about technology differently from comparison companies. They applied a simple test:

> **"Does this technology fit directly within our Hedgehog Concept (three circles)? If yes, we must pioneer its application. If no, we either ignore it or match parity."**

Technology is an **accelerator** of momentum, not a *creator* of it. No technology — no matter how revolutionary — can turn a bad strategy into a good one.

### 7.2 Flywheel vs Doom Loop Responses to Technology

| Flywheel (Good-to-Great) | Doom Loop (Comparison) |
|--------------------------|----------------------|
| Harness technologies that fit the Hedgehog Concept to accelerate existing momentum | React to technology change in a panic, fearful of being left behind |
| Pioneer application selectively | Chase every technology trend |
| Technology follows strategy | Technology drives strategy (or replaces it) |

### 7.3 Applying the Technology Test in a Business Plan

When writing the technology or digital strategy section of a business plan, apply the three-circle filter:

**Step 1:** State your Hedgehog Concept clearly.

**Step 2:** For each proposed technology investment, answer:
- Does it help us become the best in the world at [Circle 1]?
- Does it directly improve our key economic denominator [Circle 2]?
- Does it align with what we are deeply passionate about [Circle 3]?

**Step 3:** If yes to all three, invest aggressively. If no, defer or ignore.

**Example — AI integration for a Ugandan agricultural exporter:**

| Technology | Circle 1 (Best at) | Circle 2 (Profit per X) | Circle 3 (Passion) | Decision |
|-----------|-------------------|------------------------|--------------------|---------|
| AI-powered crop quality grading | Yes — improves our grading accuracy beyond any competitor | Yes — reduces rejection rate, improving profit per tonne exported | Yes — directly serves smallholder farmers | **Pioneer it** |
| Blockchain traceability | Partially — adds transparency but does not make us "best" | Marginal impact on profit per tonne | Moderate | **Match parity only** |
| AI chatbot for HR | No connection to our three circles | No impact on key denominator | No | **Ignore for now** |

**Key message for investors:** "We do not adopt technology for its own sake. Every technology investment must pass our three-circle test. This discipline ensures that our limited capital goes to tools that directly accelerate our flywheel."

## 8. Applying Good to Great in a Business Plan — Practical Checklist

Use this checklist to audit your business plan against Collins's frameworks:

### 8.1 Section-by-Section Mapping

| Business Plan Section | Collins Framework | Key Question to Ask |
|----------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Executive Summary** | Level 5 Leadership; Stockdale Paradox | Does the summary express fierce ambition *for the company* while honestly acknowledging the challenges? |
| **Company Description** | Hedgehog Concept | Can you state your Hedgehog Concept in one clear sentence? |
| **Products / Services** | Hedgehog Concept (Circle 1) | Are you building products where you can be the *best*, or merely "good"? |
| **Market Analysis** | Confront the Brutal Facts | Have you presented the market as it truly is — including inconvenient data? |
| **Target Market** | Hedgehog Concept (Circle 2) | Have you identified your economic denominator (profit per X)? |
| **Competitive Analysis** | Hedgehog Concept; Brutal Facts | Are you honest about competitors who are better than you in certain areas? |
| **Marketing & Sales** | Flywheel | Does your growth model show cumulative, compounding momentum? |
| **Operations** | Culture of Discipline | Do your processes embed discipline in the *system*, not just in the founder? |
| **Management Team** | First Who Then What; Level 5 | Does the team section lead with *who* these people are, not just what they have done? Do bios use "we" language? |
| **Financial Projections** | Flywheel; Brutal Facts; Economic Engine | Do projections show a realistic buildup phase? Is the key denominator tracked? Is there an honest downside scenario? |
| **Funding Request** | Flywheel | Is the funding positioned as fuel for an already-turning flywheel, not a Hail Mary for a stationary one? |
| **Risk Analysis** | Stockdale Paradox | Does the risk section demonstrate both brutal honesty AND unwavering faith in the path to success? |
| **Implementation Plan** | Flywheel; Culture of Discipline | Are milestones sequential turns of the flywheel? Are targets concrete and unfudgeable? |
| **Technology / AI Strategy** | Technology Accelerator | Does every technology investment pass the three-circle test? |

### 8.2 Five "Stop Doing" Questions

Before finalising your business plan, ask:

1. **What activities are we including that do NOT sit within our three circles?** Remove them.
2. **Where are we using "I" language when "we" would be more honest?** Fix it.
3. **Where have we sanitised a brutal fact to make it more palatable?** Restore the truth.
4. **Where are we proposing a "big bang" initiative instead of incremental flywheel turns?** Restructure as sequential momentum-building steps.
5. **Where are we proposing technology adoption that does not pass the three-circle test?** Cut it or justify it rigorously.

### 8.3 The Ultimate Test

Read your business plan and ask:

> "If a Level 5 leader — humble, fiercely determined, allergic to hype, obsessed with results — read this plan, would they fund it? Or would they see through the bluster?"

If the answer is uncertain, revise until the plan earns the respect of the most rigorous reader you can imagine.
