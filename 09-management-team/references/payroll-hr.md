# Payroll and HR Management: Uganda/East Africa Reference

**Source:** Bragg, Steven M. *Essentials of Payroll: Management and Accounting*. John Wiley & Sons, 2003.

**Scope:** Bragg's text is US-focused (IRS, Social Security, FUTA). This reference extracts the universal payroll principles — system design, controls, accounting entries, compensation types, record-keeping — and translates them into Uganda/East Africa context using URA, NSSF, and the Uganda Employment Act 2006 as the governing framework. Uganda-specific rules are drawn from current legislation; Bragg's frameworks inform process and accounting discipline.

---

## 1. Payroll Fundamentals

### What Payroll Is and Why It Matters

Payroll is the total compensation process: calculating what employees are owed, making statutory deductions, remitting those deductions to the relevant authorities, and paying net wages to employees on time. Bragg describes it as a process that "must be completed the same way, every time" — reliability and consistency are not optional (Bragg, 2003).

For a business plan, payroll matters for four reasons:

1. **Legal compliance** — Late PAYE or NSSF remittances attract penalties and reputational damage with URA and the Ministry of Gender, Labour and Social Development.
2. **Cost accuracy** — The true cost of employment is 10–15% above gross salary once employer-side obligations are factored in.
3. **Employee trust** — Employees who receive correct, on-time pay are more productive and less likely to leave. Payroll errors create "possibly confrontational visits" and erode trust (Bragg, 2003).
4. **Audit readiness** — Banks, investors, and tax auditors will review payroll records. A well-maintained payroll register signals management competence.

### Payroll Cycle Options

| Cycle | Frequency | Uganda context |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| Weekly | 52 runs/year | Rare; used for casual/daily-paid labour |
| Fortnightly | 26 runs/year | Uncommon; used by some NGOs |
| Monthly | 12 runs/year | **Uganda standard** for permanent employees |

Monthly payroll is strongly recommended for SMEs because it minimises processing effort. Bragg notes that moving from weekly to monthly payroll eliminates 75% of processing runs (Bragg, 2003). PAYE and NSSF are both calculated and remitted on a monthly basis, making monthly payroll the natural cycle for statutory compliance.

### Gross Pay vs Net Pay

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| **Gross pay** | Total compensation before any deductions — basic salary + all taxable allowances |
| **Statutory deductions** | PAYE (employee's income tax) + employee NSSF (5%) |
| **Voluntary deductions** | Loan repayments, savings, union dues (if applicable) |
| **Net pay** | Gross pay minus all deductions — the amount deposited to the employee's account |

**Formula:**

```
Net Pay = Gross Pay − PAYE − Employee NSSF (5%) − Voluntary Deductions
```

The payroll register must show all columns separately. Employees must receive a payslip itemising each deduction. Never give an employee only the net figure without explanation — this is both bad practice and legally questionable.

### Components of Uganda Employee Compensation

Uganda compensation packages typically comprise:

| Component | Taxable? | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| Basic salary | Yes | Core fixed pay; basis for NSSF calculation |
| Housing allowance | Yes (unless provided in kind on employer premises) | Cash housing allowance is fully taxable |
| Transport allowance | Yes | Cash transport allowance is fully taxable |
| Medical allowance (cash) | Yes | Cash payment is taxable; employer-paid group medical insurance is a benefit |
| Airtime/communication allowance | Yes | Treated as additional income |
| Meals (provided on premises) | Generally exempt | Only if provided in kind on business premises for employer's convenience |
| Overtime pay | Yes | Calculated per Uganda Employment Act |
| Bonuses and commissions | Yes | Treated as regular wages for PAYE purposes (Bragg, 2003) |
| Per diem (within approved limits) | Exempt up to approved limits | URA prescribes approved per diem rates; excess is taxable |
| Employer NSSF contribution (10%) | Not income to employee | It is an employer expense, not a deduction from employee pay |
| End-of-contract gratuity | Taxable | Included in PAYE in the month paid |

## 2. Uganda PAYE Tax (URA)

### Uganda Income Tax Brackets (2025/26 Financial Year)

PAYE is governed by the Income Tax Act (Cap. 340) and administered by the Uganda Revenue Authority (URA). The following brackets apply to monthly employment income:

| Monthly Chargeable Income (UGX) | Rate |
|----------------------------------|------|
| 0 – 335,000 | 0% (nil) |
| 335,001 – 410,000 | 10% on the amount above 335,000 |
| 410,001 – 10,000,000 | 20% on the amount above 410,000 (plus UGX 7,500 from the second bracket) |
| Above 10,000,000 | 30% on the amount above 10,000,000 (plus the tax on 10,000,000) |

*Note: Verify current brackets on the URA website (ura.go.ug) each financial year as Parliament may adjust thresholds.*

**Annual equivalent threshold** for zero tax: UGX 4,020,000 per year (i.e., UGX 335,000/month × 12).

### PAYE Calculation Method — Worked Examples

**Example 1: Low-income employee**

- Basic salary: UGX 800,000/month
- Transport allowance: UGX 100,000/month
- Gross pay: UGX 900,000/month

PAYE calculation:
- First UGX 335,000: nil
- UGX 335,001 – 410,000 (i.e., UGX 75,000): 10% = UGX 7,500
- UGX 410,001 – 900,000 (i.e., UGX 490,000): 20% = UGX 98,000
- **Total PAYE = UGX 105,500/month**

---

**Example 2: Mid-level manager**

- Basic salary: UGX 3,000,000/month
- Housing allowance: UGX 500,000/month
- Transport allowance: UGX 200,000/month
- Gross pay: UGX 3,700,000/month

PAYE calculation:
- First UGX 335,000: nil
- UGX 335,001–410,000 (UGX 75,000): 10% = UGX 7,500
- UGX 410,001–3,700,000 (UGX 3,290,000): 20% = UGX 658,000
- **Total PAYE = UGX 665,500/month**

Net pay after PAYE and employee NSSF:
- Gross pay: UGX 3,700,000
- Less PAYE: UGX 665,500
- Less employee NSSF (5%): UGX 185,000
- **Net pay: UGX 2,849,500**

---

**Example 3: Senior employee above UGX 10M**

- Gross pay: UGX 12,000,000/month

PAYE calculation:
- Nil to UGX 335,000: nil
- UGX 75,000 at 10%: UGX 7,500
- UGX 9,590,000 at 20%: UGX 1,918,000
- UGX 2,000,000 at 30%: UGX 600,000
- **Total PAYE = UGX 2,525,500/month**

### Monthly PAYE Remittance to URA

- **Due date:** 15th of the month following the payroll month
- Example: January payroll PAYE must be remitted by 15 February
- **Method:** URA's e-tax system (etax.ura.go.ug) — generate a Payment Registration Number (PRN), pay via bank
- **Form:** Monthly PAYE return filed on e-tax
- **Late penalty:** 10% of the tax due, plus 2% per month interest on unpaid tax

### Annual Income Tax Return for Employees

Employers must file a **reconciliation return** at year-end (30 June). This reconciles total PAYE deducted for each employee against the annual tax liability. Employees with only one source of income generally do not need to file personal income tax returns — the employer's withholding is final tax. Employees with multiple income sources or rental income must file individual returns by 31 December.

### Employer Obligations

- Register as an employer with URA (obtain a Tax Identification Number if not already registered)
- File monthly PAYE return and remit by the 15th
- Issue each employee with a tax certificate (similar to a P9 in Kenya) at year-end showing total gross pay and total PAYE deducted
- Maintain payroll records for a minimum of **5 years** (Income Tax Act requirement)

### Penalties for Non-Compliance

| Offence | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Late filing of PAYE return | UGX 200,000 per month or 2% of tax due (whichever is higher) |
| Late payment of PAYE | 10% of tax due, plus 2% per month interest |
| Failure to deduct PAYE | Employer becomes personally liable for the tax |
| Understating wages | Tax fraud — criminal liability possible |

## 3. NSSF Contributions (Uganda)

### Legal Basis

The National Social Security Fund Act (Cap. 222) requires all employers with 5 or more employees to register and contribute. The contribution rate is:

| Party | Rate | Basis |
|-------|------|-------|
| Employer | 10% | Of employee's gross monthly wage |
| Employee | 5% | Of employee's gross monthly wage |
| **Total** | **15%** | Remitted by employer |

The employee's 5% is deducted from gross pay. The employer's 10% is an additional cost over and above gross pay — the employer does not recover this from the employee.

### NSSF Calculation — Worked Examples

**Example 1: Employee earning UGX 1,200,000/month**

- Employee NSSF (5%): UGX 60,000 — deducted from gross
- Employer NSSF (10%): UGX 120,000 — additional employer expense
- Total NSSF remittance by employer: UGX 180,000

**Example 2: Employee earning UGX 4,500,000/month**

- Employee NSSF (5%): UGX 225,000 — deducted from gross
- Employer NSSF (10%): UGX 450,000 — employer expense
- Total NSSF remittance: UGX 675,000

### Monthly NSSF Remittance Process

- **Due date:** 15th of the month following the payroll month (same as PAYE)
- **Method:** NSSF employer portal (nssfug.org) — employers file a schedule listing each employee's name, membership number, and contribution amount
- **Payment:** Bank transfer using the reference number generated by the NSSF portal
- Both employee and employer contributions are remitted together by the employer in a single payment

### NSSF Registration for New Employees

- New employees must be registered with NSSF within **30 days** of starting employment
- Employer applies on the NSSF portal; employee receives a membership number
- Employees who already have a membership number from previous employment must provide it — contributions must continue into the same fund, not a new account

### Penalties for Non-Compliance

| Offence | Consequence |
|---------|------------|
| Failure to register | UGX 1,000,000 fine |
| Late remittance | 5% of amount due per month, plus interest |
| Wilful non-remittance | Criminal prosecution; directors personally liable |

## 4. Allowances and Benefits in Uganda

### Common Uganda Employee Allowances

| Allowance | Typical amount (SME) | Tax treatment |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------|
| Basic salary | UGX 600,000–5,000,000 | Taxable |
| Transport | UGX 50,000–300,000/month | Taxable (cash) |
| Housing | UGX 200,000–1,500,000/month | Taxable (cash) |
| Airtime/data | UGX 30,000–150,000/month | Taxable |
| Medical allowance | UGX 50,000–300,000/month | Taxable if paid as cash |
| Hardship/field allowance | Variable | Taxable |
| Leave allowance | Common in Uganda (extra month's pay on annual leave) | Taxable |

### Tax Treatment of Allowances

Under the Income Tax Act, **all cash payments to employees are taxable** unless specifically exempted. The key exemptions are:

- Employer contributions to approved pension funds (NSSF is approved)
- Meals and accommodation provided on employer's premises for employer's convenience (not cash equivalents)
- Medical expenses paid directly to a medical provider under a group scheme (not cash reimbursements to the employee)
- Per diem payments within URA-approved limits when employee is travelling on business away from their usual workplace
- Terminal benefits on death or medical incapacitation (subject to limits)

### Benefits in Kind — Tax Implications

If an employer provides a benefit in kind (not cash), its **value must be imputed as income** for PAYE purposes:

| Benefit | Imputed value for PAYE |
|---------|------------------------|
| Company vehicle for private use | Market rental value of vehicle per month |
| Employer-provided housing | Market rental value of accommodation |
| Children's school fees paid by employer | Full amount paid |
| Personal club memberships | Full membership fee |
| Stock options | Market value at exercise date less amount paid by employee |

**Key principle from Bragg (2003):** "Such fringe benefits as tickets to entertainment events, free travel, and company cars should be recorded as employee gross income. The amount of incremental gross income added should be the fair market value of the fringe benefit, minus its cost to the employee." This rule applies equally under Uganda's Income Tax Act.

### Per Diem and Subsistence Allowances

URA publishes approved per diem rates by grade and location. Payments within these rates are **exempt from PAYE**. Payments above the approved rate must have the excess included in taxable income.

For business plans: use a conservative per diem of UGX 80,000–150,000/day for domestic travel (Kampala missions) and UGX 200,000–400,000/day for upcountry overnight travel, unless travelling to remote areas.

## 5. Payroll Register and Records

### What a Payroll Register Must Contain

Bragg (2003) prescribes the payroll register as the master document from which journal entries, tax reports, and employee inquiries are all resolved. A Uganda payroll register should contain the following columns:

| Column | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Employee number | Unique identifier |
| Employee name | Full legal name |
| NIN (National ID Number) | For URA identification |
| NSSF number | For NSSF reconciliation |
| Department / cost centre | For cost allocation |
| Basic salary | Fixed contractual pay |
| Taxable allowances | Each allowance listed separately |
| Gross pay | Sum of all taxable components |
| Employee NSSF (5%) | Deduction from gross |
| Taxable income for PAYE | Gross minus pre-tax deductions (if any) |
| PAYE | Computed per current tax tables |
| Other deductions | Loans, SACCO contributions, union dues |
| Total deductions | Sum of all deductions |
| Net pay | Gross minus total deductions |
| Bank account number | For direct transfer |
| Employer NSSF (10%) | Employer cost — not deducted from employee |

### Payslip Requirements

Every employee must receive a payslip on or before payday. The Uganda Employment Act 2006 (Section 51) requires that the employer provide a written statement of wages. A minimum payslip should show:

- Employer name and address
- Employee name and employee number
- Pay period (e.g., January 2026)
- Gross pay
- Each deduction itemised (PAYE, employee NSSF, loan repayments, etc.)
- Net pay
- Date of payment

### Record Retention Requirements

- Uganda: **minimum 5 years** for payroll records (Income Tax Act and Employment Act requirements)
- Records include: payroll registers, payslips copies, PAYE remittance receipts, NSSF schedules, employment contracts, and any deduction authorisation forms

Bragg (2003) emphasises that personnel files must be "kept under the strictest security at all times" — employees have a right to confidentiality of their pay and personal information. In Uganda, POPIA considerations (Personal Data Protection Office) reinforce this — payroll data is sensitive personal data.

### Payroll Register Template (Column Headings)

```
| No. | Name | NIN | NSSF No. | Dept | Basic | House | Trans | Other Allow | Gross | Emp NSSF 5% | PAYE | Loan Deduct | Total Deduct | Net Pay | Emplr NSSF 10% |
```

## 6. Payroll Accounting Entries

### Journal Entries for Monthly Payroll

Bragg (2003) describes the payroll journal entry as flowing directly from the payroll register into the general ledger. The entry records the expense on one side and the liabilities (amounts owed to employees and tax authorities) on the other.

**Step 1: Record the payroll expense and liabilities**

Assume the following payroll for a month:
- Gross salaries: UGX 15,000,000
- PAYE withheld: UGX 2,100,000
- Employee NSSF (5%): UGX 750,000
- Net wages payable: UGX 12,150,000
- Employer NSSF (10%): UGX 1,500,000

```
Dr  Salary Expense                    UGX 15,000,000
Dr  Employer NSSF Expense             UGX  1,500,000
    Cr  PAYE Payable (URA)                        UGX  2,100,000
    Cr  NSSF Payable (employee + employer)        UGX  2,250,000
    Cr  Net Wages Payable                         UGX 12,150,000
```

*Explanation:*
- Salary Expense = gross wages (UGX 15M)
- Employer NSSF Expense = employer's 10% of gross (UGX 1.5M) — this is an expense
- PAYE Payable = the tax withheld from employees to be remitted to URA
- NSSF Payable = employee NSSF (UGX 750K) + employer NSSF (UGX 1.5M) = UGX 2,250,000 — total to remit to NSSF
- Net Wages Payable = what employees will actually receive

**Step 2: Record payment to employees**

```
Dr  Net Wages Payable                 UGX 12,150,000
    Cr  Bank Account                              UGX 12,150,000
```

**Step 3: Remit PAYE to URA (by 15th of following month)**

```
Dr  PAYE Payable (URA)               UGX  2,100,000
    Cr  Bank Account                              UGX  2,100,000
```

**Step 4: Remit NSSF (by 15th of following month)**

```
Dr  NSSF Payable                     UGX  2,250,000
    Cr  Bank Account                              UGX  2,250,000
```

### Payroll Accruals

When a financial year or reporting period ends mid-payroll (e.g., 31 December falls mid-month), an accrual entry is required:

```
Dr  Salary Expense (accrued)         UGX [proportionate amount]
    Cr  Accrued Salaries Payable                  UGX [proportionate amount]
```

Reverse this entry at the start of the next period when the actual payroll is processed.

### Employer NSSF as an Expense

The employer's NSSF contribution (10%) is a **staff cost**, not a deduction from employee pay. It must appear on the profit and loss account as a component of employment costs. In a business plan financial model, always include it as part of the total payroll cost line:

```
Total payroll cost = Gross salaries + Employer NSSF (10%)
```

### Bank Reconciliation for Payroll Accounts

Bragg (2003) rates the bank reconciliation as "one of the most important controls anywhere in a company." For Uganda SMEs:

- Maintain a **separate payroll bank account** where possible — transfer the exact net wages total from the main account on pay day, then distribute from the payroll account
- Reconcile this account monthly: compare the list of transfers made to employee accounts against the payroll register net pay column
- Investigate uncashed or unprocessed payments immediately — these may indicate ghost employees or incorrect bank details

## 7. Payroll Controls and Audit

### Key Payroll Internal Controls

Bragg (2003) identifies the primary objective of payroll controls as preventing two categories of loss: *innocent errors* (wrong calculations) and *fraud* (ghost employees, inflated wages). The following controls are directly applicable to Uganda SMEs:

**Segregation of duties (most critical control)**

| Function | Should be performed by |
|----------|------------------------|
| Preparing payroll | Payroll/accounts clerk |
| Approving pay changes | HR manager or owner |
| Approving the payroll run | Finance manager or owner — different person from preparer |
| Signing cheques / authorising bank transfers | Director or senior manager — not the payroll preparer |
| Bank reconciliation | Accounts person — not the payroll preparer |

In very small businesses (1–3 staff), the owner should personally approve and authorise every payroll run and review the payroll register before payment.

**Pay rate controls**
- Store approved salary rates in signed employment contracts — compare payroll register rates to contracts quarterly
- Require written, authorised documentation for any pay change (increment, bonus, allowance addition)
- Bragg (2003) recommends that "approval of a high-level manager for all pay changes, which should include that person's signature on a standard pay change form"

**Time and attendance**
- For hourly or daily-paid workers, supervisor approval of hours worked each week
- Compare total hours to expected headcount; investigate departments where overtime is consistently high
- For monthly salaried staff, maintain an attendance register signed by each employee

### Ghost Employee Prevention

Ghost employees are the most common payroll fraud in East Africa. Prevention controls:

1. **Physical ID verification** — Periodically hand out payslips or payments directly to employees who must show national ID; Bragg (2003) notes this is "a useful control point in larger companies where the payroll staff may not know each employee by name"
2. **Manager roster check** — Issue a list of payroll recipients to each department manager monthly; they confirm every name is a current employee
3. **Address/bank account check** — If two employees share the same bank account, investigate immediately
4. **Termination procedure** — Remove terminated employees from the payroll register on the same day employment ends; require a signed termination form before the final payroll run
5. **New employee verification** — HR must supply a signed appointment letter and confirmed start date before any employee is added to the payroll
6. **Exception reports** — If using payroll software, generate a report each month of: new employees added, employees deleted, pay rates changed, and negative deductions — review all of these before authorising payment

### Payroll Audit Checklist

Conduct a payroll audit at least annually (or quarterly for larger businesses):

- [ ] Compare payroll register employee list to HR master list — verify every name has an employment contract on file
- [ ] Select 5–10 employees at random; trace their gross pay back to their signed employment contracts
- [ ] Verify PAYE calculations for selected employees — recalculate using current URA brackets
- [ ] Verify NSSF calculations: confirm 5% employee + 10% employer for each selected employee
- [ ] Compare PAYE remittance receipts from URA to the payroll register PAYE totals for the year
- [ ] Compare NSSF remittance receipts from NSSF to the payroll register NSSF totals for the year
- [ ] Review bank statements for payroll account — all credits from the main account, all debits to employee accounts
- [ ] Check for any employees with no NSSF number — investigate immediately
- [ ] Verify that terminated employees were removed from payroll in the correct month
- [ ] Confirm that all deduction authorisation forms are on file (loan deductions, union dues, etc.)

### Common Payroll Fraud Schemes and Prevention

| Scheme | How it works | Prevention |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| Ghost employees | Fictitious employees added; perpetrator collects salaries | ID verification; manager roster checks |
| Salary inflation | Payroll clerk increases own or accomplice's salary | Segregation of duties; signed pay change forms |
| Continued payments post-termination | Terminated employee remains on payroll | Prompt removal on termination date; manager approval |
| False overtime | Excessive overtime hours approved by conspiring supervisor | Two-level approval; exception reports |
| Duplicate payments | Same employee paid twice in a period | System-level duplicate detection; bank reconciliation |
| Fictitious deductions reversed | Negative deductions applied to increase net pay | Require approval for all negative deductions (Bragg, 2003) |

## 8. Payroll Software Options for Uganda SMEs

### Excel-Based Payroll (Template Approach)

Suitable for businesses with **fewer than 15 employees**.

**Structure:**
- One sheet per month: employee list, gross pay, PAYE calculation, NSSF, deductions, net pay
- A separate cumulative sheet tracking year-to-date gross and PAYE per employee
- A PAYE formula using IF statements to apply the Uganda tax brackets

**Advantages:** Free; flexible; owner understands it
**Risks:** No audit trail; formula errors propagate silently; difficult to scale; no automated URA filing

### QuickBooks (Desktop or Online)

Widely used by Uganda accountants. QuickBooks Payroll (set up manually) can handle:
- Monthly payroll register
- PAYE calculation (requires manual setup of Uganda tax tables)
- NSSF tracking as a liability account
- Payroll journal entries posted automatically to the general ledger

**Cost:** QuickBooks Desktop from approximately UGX 700,000–1,200,000 one-off; Online from approximately USD 30/month

### Sage Pastel Payroll

The dominant professional payroll solution used by medium-to-large Uganda businesses. Features:
- Built-in Uganda PAYE tax tables (updated annually)
- NSSF compliance
- Produces payslips, payroll registers, and PAYE schedules ready for URA submission
- Links to Sage accounting general ledger

**Cost:** Licence from approximately UGX 2,500,000/year for up to 20 employees

### Local Uganda HRMS Platforms

Several Ugandan or East African software vendors offer cloud-based payroll:

- **BrighterMonday Payroll** — Uganda-specific, mobile-first
- **Workpay** (Kenya-origin, used across EA) — cloud payroll with PAYE and NSSF tables for Uganda, Kenya, Tanzania, Rwanda
- **iPay HR** — used by some Uganda businesses

These platforms typically cost UGX 20,000–80,000 per employee per month and include statutory compliance updates automatically.

### What to Look for in Payroll Software

Based on Bragg (2003), the key requirements are:

1. **Statutory compliance** — auto-updated Uganda PAYE tables and NSSF rates
2. **Payroll register output** — downloadable in Excel for verification
3. **Payslip generation** — branded, emailed directly to employees
4. **Audit trail** — any change to employee data or pay rates is logged with username and timestamp
5. **Bank file export** — generates a file in the format required by Stanbic, Centenary, DFCU, or MTN Mobile Money for bulk payment
6. **URA return format** — exports data in the format required for URA e-tax PAYE return

## 9. Payroll for Different Employment Types

### Permanent Employees

Standard monthly payroll applies. All statutory obligations apply:
- PAYE on all taxable income
- NSSF: 5% employee + 10% employer
- Uganda Employment Act 2006 governs: notice periods, annual leave (21 days/year minimum), sick leave, maternity/paternity leave

### Casual Workers

Under the Uganda Employment Act 2006, a **casual worker** is employed on a day-to-day basis or for periods of less than one month. Employer obligations:
- Pay must be settled at the end of each day or week as agreed
- PAYE applies if daily/weekly pay, when annualised, exceeds the tax-free threshold
- **NSSF does not apply** to casual workers paid on a daily basis — they are not "employees in continuous employment" under the NSSF Act (verify current NSSF guidance as regulations may evolve)
- If a casual worker is retained for more than one month continuously, they become a regular employee and all obligations apply

**Practical approach:** Maintain a casual labour register with dates worked, daily rate, and payment made. Retain records for 5 years.

### Contract Workers and Consultants

When an individual is engaged as a **consultant** or independent contractor (not an employee):
- They are **not** on payroll — they issue invoices for services rendered
- **Withholding tax (WHT)** at 6% applies on payments to local individual consultants under Section 120 of the Income Tax Act
- The company deducts 6% WHT and remits it to URA; the consultant receives 94% of the invoice amount
- The company files a WHT return and issues a WHT certificate to the consultant

**Key test (adapted from Bragg, 2003):** The defining criterion for an employee is when the company controls not only *what* work is done but also *how* it is done. An independent consultant controls how the work is done and typically works for multiple clients.

**Caution:** Misclassifying employees as consultants to avoid NSSF and PAYE is illegal in Uganda. URA and NSSF are alert to this pattern, particularly for individuals working exclusively for one organisation over long periods.

### Part-Time Employees

Part-time employees working fewer than the standard hours (typically 45 hours/week in Uganda) are still entitled to:
- Pro-rated salary
- PAYE on their actual income
- NSSF on their actual earnings if they are on the payroll
- Pro-rated annual leave

### Expatriate Employees (Additional Considerations)

Expatriate employees working in Uganda are subject to:
- **Uganda PAYE** on all Uganda-sourced income (salary paid locally and any allowances)
- **Work permit** — a valid Class G work permit is required from the Directorate of Citizenship and Immigration Control; the employer is responsible for obtaining this
- **Withholding tax on foreign payments** — if the employer pays salary offshore, this may still be Uganda-sourced income if the services are rendered in Uganda
- **Double Taxation Agreements (DTAs)** — Uganda has DTAs with several countries (UK, Netherlands, Denmark, Mauritius, South Africa, among others); these may reduce withholding on certain payments but do not eliminate Uganda PAYE on employment income earned in Uganda
- **NSSF** — expatriates are generally included in NSSF unless exempt by a reciprocal social security agreement (Uganda has limited such agreements)
- **Benefits in kind** — accommodation and vehicles provided to expatriates are common; their market value must be included in taxable income

## 10. End-of-Year Payroll Processes

### Annual PAYE Reconciliation

At the end of each tax year (Uganda: 1 July to 30 June), employers must:

1. **Reconcile total PAYE** — sum all monthly PAYE remittances for the year; verify this matches the sum of individual employee PAYE computed from the payroll register
2. **File an employer annual return** with URA by 30 September following year-end (verify current deadline on URA website as deadlines are subject to change)
3. The return lists: each employee's name, TIN (Tax Identification Number if available), gross pay, taxable allowances, PAYE deducted, and any exemptions claimed

### Employee Tax Certificates

Each employee must receive a **tax certificate** (equivalent to a Kenya P9 or UK P60) showing:
- Total gross pay for the year
- Total PAYE deducted
- Total NSSF deducted (employee portion)

This certificate allows employees who file personal income tax returns to show PAYE already paid. Issue these by 31 August following the tax year-end (30 June).

### URA Annual Returns

If an employer has not reconciled monthly payments throughout the year, a difference will appear in the annual return. URA may raise assessments and penalties for any shortfall. It is therefore critical to:
- Keep monthly PAYE remittance receipts (PRN receipts) for all 12 months
- Reconcile the cumulative PAYE amount on URA e-tax to the payroll register total at each quarter

### NSSF Annual Statements

NSSF sends each member an annual benefits statement showing:
- Total contributions (employee + employer) for the year
- Cumulative balance in the member's NSSF account
- Projected benefit at retirement

Employers should remind employees that NSSF contributions are portable — if an employee leaves, their fund remains intact and continues to earn interest until withdrawal at retirement (age 55) or on other grounds (permanent emigration, permanent disability).

## 11. Maternity and Statutory Leave Payments

### Maternity Pay — Uganda Employment Act 2006 (Section 57)

| Item | Requirement |
|------|-------------|
| Duration | 60 working days (approximately 12 weeks) |
| Pay | Full pay — no deduction from salary during maternity leave |
| PAYE | Continues to apply on full pay during maternity leave |
| NSSF | Continues during maternity leave |
| Frequency | A female employee is entitled to maternity leave once every 24 months |
| Notice | Employee must give at least 1 month's notice of intention to take maternity leave |

Employer may not terminate a female employee because of pregnancy, childbirth, or maternity leave (Section 75(4) of the Employment Act 2006).

**Business plan implication:** When costing staff, provision for maternity leave should be built into HR budgets for businesses employing women of childbearing age, particularly in sectors with high female headcount. A simple provision of 2–4% of female payroll per year is a reasonable estimate for businesses planning ahead.

### Paternity Leave

Under the Employment Act 2006, male employees are entitled to **4 working days** of paid paternity leave within one month of the birth of their child. This is significantly shorter than maternity leave but is mandatory and paid at full salary.

### Sick Leave Payment Obligations

| Duration | Pay |
|----------|-----|
| First month of sick leave | Full pay |
| Second month of sick leave | Half pay |
| Beyond 2 months | No pay obligation (unless the employment contract is more generous) |

The employer may require a medical certificate from a registered medical practitioner for any sick leave beyond 3 days. Employers cannot dismiss an employee for illness during the first 6 months of continuous sick leave.

## 12. Payroll Budget for Business Plans

### How to Cost a Payroll in a Business Plan

A business plan must project the full cost of employment, not just the net salaries. Investors and bank lenders look at total employment cost. The following structure is recommended:

**Step 1: List all planned positions with gross monthly salary**

| Position | No. of people | Gross monthly salary (UGX) | Monthly total (UGX) |
|----------|---------------|---------------------------|---------------------|
| General Manager | 1 | 4,000,000 | 4,000,000 |
| Accounts Clerk | 1 | 1,200,000 | 1,200,000 |
| Production staff | 4 | 700,000 | 2,800,000 |
| Security guard | 1 | 450,000 | 450,000 |
| **Total** | **7** | — | **8,450,000** |

**Step 2: Add employer NSSF (10%)**

- Employer NSSF = UGX 8,450,000 × 10% = UGX 845,000/month

**Step 3: Consider other employer-side costs**

| Cost | Amount | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| Workers' Compensation Insurance | ~1–2% of gross payroll | Required under the Workers' Compensation Act; premium varies by industry risk |
| Recruitment costs | One-off per hire; typically 1–2 months' salary for skilled positions | Amortise over the employee's expected tenure |
| Uniforms / PPE | Varies by industry | Operational cost |
| Staff training | Variable | Human capital investment |

**Step 4: Annual payroll budget**

| Item | Monthly (UGX) | Annual (UGX) |
|------|--------------|-------------|
| Gross salaries | 8,450,000 | 101,400,000 |
| Employer NSSF (10%) | 845,000 | 10,140,000 |
| Workers' Comp Insurance (~1.5%) | 126,750 | 1,521,000 |
| Leave provisions (annual leave pay) | ~165,000 | 1,983,000 |
| **Total employment cost** | **9,586,750** | **115,044,000** |

### Employer-Side Cost Calculation

**Rule of thumb for Uganda:**

```
True cost of employment = Gross salary × 1.10 to 1.15
```

- The 1.10 factor covers employer NSSF (10%)
- The additional 0.05 covers workers' compensation insurance, leave provisions, and recruitment/training overhead
- For industries with high injury risk (construction, manufacturing), the factor may reach 1.18–1.20

**Example:**

An employee on UGX 1,500,000/month gross costs the employer:

| Component | Amount (UGX) |
|-----------|-------------|
| Gross salary | 1,500,000 |
| Employer NSSF (10%) | 150,000 |
| Workers' Comp (~1.5%) | 22,500 |
| Leave provision | ~28,850 (21/260 days × salary) |
| **Total monthly cost to employer** | **~1,701,350** |

This means the employer pays approximately **UGX 201,000 more** per month than the gross salary shown on the contract — or 13.4% above gross salary.

### Key Financial Ratios for Business Plans

| Ratio | Formula | Uganda benchmark |
|-------|---------|-----------------|
| Payroll-to-revenue | Total employment cost ÷ Revenue | 20–35% (service businesses); 10–20% (manufacturing) |
| Revenue per employee | Revenue ÷ FTE count | Varies widely by sector |
| PAYE compliance rate | PAYE remitted ÷ PAYE due | Should be 100% |
| NSSF compliance rate | NSSF remitted ÷ NSSF due | Should be 100% |

## Summary: Uganda Payroll Compliance Calendar

| Date | Action |
|------|--------|
| By the 25th of each month | Process payroll; distribute payslips |
| 15th of following month | File and pay PAYE to URA via e-tax |
| 15th of following month | File and pay NSSF contributions via NSSF portal |
| 30 June | Uganda tax year end |
| 30 September | File employer annual PAYE reconciliation with URA |
| 31 August | Issue employee tax certificates for the year ended 30 June |
| Annually | Renew workers' compensation insurance policy |
| Ongoing | Update NSSF membership for new employees within 30 days of hire |
