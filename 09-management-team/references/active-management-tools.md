# Active Management Tools: 45 Reproducible Tools for Leading and Improving Employee Performance

**Source:** Silberman, Mel (ed.). *The Active Manager's Tool Kit: 45 Reproducible Tools for Leading and Improving Your Employees' Performance*. McGraw-Hill, 2003.

**Cite as:** (Silberman, 2003)

This reference extracts all 45 tools from five parts of the book. Each tool is contributed by a practitioner-consultant. The tools fall into three types:
- **Questionnaire/Assessment** — scored instruments for self-reflection or team diagnosis
- **Job Aid** — reference guides, tip sheets, how-to frameworks
- **Exercise** — facilitated group or pair activities

---

## Part I: Assessing and Developing Your Leadership Skills (Tools 1–9)

### Tool 1: People Quotient (PQ) Assessment
**Type:** Questionnaire/Assessment
**Author:** Mel Silberman
**Source text:** Adapted from *PeopleSmart: Developing Your Interpersonal Intelligence* (Silberman)

**Purpose:** Measures interpersonal intelligence across eight skill areas: understanding people, expressing yourself clearly, asserting your needs, exchanging feedback, influencing others, resolving conflict, being a team player, and shifting gears.

**When to use it:** At the start of a leadership development programme; during annual performance self-review; when a manager wants to identify which interpersonal skills to prioritise for growth.

**How to use it:**
1. Complete the 40-item PeopleSmart Rating Scale (5 items per skill area, 1–5 scale).
2. Score each of the eight skill areas separately and note your lowest two or three.
3. Reflect on specific situations where each weak area has cost you or your team.
4. Identify one concrete action per low-scoring skill area to practise this month.
5. Re-take in three to six months to measure progress.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Interpersonal intelligence is highly valued in Ugandan business culture — particularly reading social cues, managing face-saving, and shifting communication style by hierarchy. Encourage managers to score themselves honestly on "asserting needs" and "exchanging feedback," as both are culturally suppressed in Uganda but critical for performance management.

### Tool 2: 360° Leadership Development Assessment
**Type:** Questionnaire/Assessment
**Author:** Joan Cassidy

**Purpose:** Provides a multi-rater view of 20 leadership competencies across five clusters: thinking strategically, influencing, achieving results, managing others, and leading with integrity. Each competency is rated by self and by others (direct reports, peers, manager).

**When to use it:** Annually or ahead of a major leadership transition; when a manager is being considered for promotion; as input to a structured development conversation.

**How to use it:**
1. Manager completes self-ratings for all 20 competencies on a 5-point scale.
2. Three to six raters (direct reports, peers, one level up) complete the same instrument independently.
3. Compile average scores per competency, noting items where self-score diverges from rater scores by more than one point.
4. Share results in a one-to-one coaching conversation focused on the two or three largest gaps.
5. Create an Individual Action Plan Worksheet targeting specific behaviours to change.
6. Follow up quarterly.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Ugandan workplaces are typically hierarchical. Direct reports may not give honest upward feedback unless anonymity is guaranteed and the culture of the review process is carefully prepared. Consider using an external facilitator for the initial roll-out, and frame feedback as "helping the manager serve the team better" rather than "rating the boss."

### Tool 3: Empowerment Patterns Inventory
**Type:** Questionnaire/Assessment
**Author:** Gaylord Reagan

**Purpose:** Identifies which of eight empowerment patterns dominates a manager's style. The patterns range from highly enabling to highly controlling or disengaged.

**The eight patterns:**
| Pattern | Brief description |
|---|---|
| Lifesaver | Over-rescues employees; prevents them learning from failure |
| Precisionist | Demands perfection; difficult to satisfy |
| Delighter | Energises and celebrates; may sacrifice rigour |
| Distancer | Emotionally withdrawn; low personal connection |
| Mutineer | Resists authority; models non-compliance |
| Attacker | Uses criticism as a management tool |
| Bureaucrat | Rule-bound; prioritises process over people |
| Intellectual | Values ideas over action; can be paralysed by analysis |

**When to use it:** When a manager receives complaints about leadership style; as part of a coaching programme; during team restructuring.

**How to use it:**
1. Complete the Empowerment Patterns Inventory (scored instrument, downloadable).
2. Identify your dominant pattern(s).
3. Use the Action Planning worksheet to draft three to five specific behaviour changes to shift toward a more empowering style.
4. Share results with a trusted peer or coach for accountability.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** The "Bureaucrat" and "Precisionist" patterns are common in Uganda's civil-service-influenced management culture. The "Lifesaver" pattern is common in family businesses where the founder cannot delegate. Naming the pattern non-judgementally creates space for productive coaching conversations.

### Tool 4: Leadership Style Self-Assessment
**Type:** Questionnaire/Assessment
**Authors:** Deborah Hopen and Laura Gregg
**Derived from:** Sayle's *Management Audits*

**Purpose:** Plots a manager's leadership style on a continuum from autocratic to democratic using 16 leadership characteristics. Helps managers see the full range of available styles and assess when each is appropriate.

**When to use it:** When a manager wants to understand their default style and its impact on team dynamics; when preparing to manage a team with different needs from previous experience.

**How to use it:**
1. Rate yourself on each of the 16 characteristics using the continuum scale provided.
2. Note where you cluster (left = autocratic, right = democratic/participatory).
3. Reflect on the situations your team faces: are they routine or novel? Do team members have high or low skill and will?
4. Identify two to three characteristics where you want to flex your style.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Many Ugandan managers have inherited an autocratic style from both colonial-era institutions and military-influenced management norms. Situational style-flexing — being directive with new staff, participatory with experienced staff — is a practical and culturally acceptable bridge.

### Tool 5: Motivating Others
**Type:** Questionnaire/Assessment
**Author:** Brooke Broadbent

**Purpose:** Helps managers assess how well they understand and apply motivational principles for each direct report. Identifies the six key motivators and how to activate them: enjoyment, feeling important, personal benefits, clarity, success, and positive thoughts.

**When to use it:** Before individual performance conversations; when a team member's performance is declining; when designing recognition programmes.

**How to use it:**
1. Complete the motivation assessment for yourself as a manager (how well you apply each of the six motivators in practice).
2. For each direct report, think about which motivators are most relevant to that individual.
3. Design one specific action per employee to activate their primary motivator this month.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** In Uganda, "feeling important" and "clarity" are often the most powerful but most neglected motivators. Many employees have never been told clearly what is expected of them, or have never been publicly acknowledged for good work. Simple, low-cost recognition — such as name-checking an employee's contribution in a staff meeting — can have an outsised effect on motivation.

### Tool 6: Improving Your Communications
**Type:** Job Aid
**Author:** Scott Parry

**Purpose:** A 25-tip self-assessment and reference guide for improving the quality of everyday management communication — listening, questioning, giving information, and managing conversations.

**When to use it:** As a personal checklist before important conversations; as a coaching prompt when reviewing a communication failure; as a foundation for communication-skills training.

**Key tips (sample):**
- Planning interaction: prepare your purpose, agenda, and desired outcome before the meeting
- Confirming attention: check that the other person is ready and able to engage
- Active listening: reflect back content and feeling, not just facts
- Allowing time to digest: pause after key points; do not rush to fill silences
- Funnel Technique: start broad, then narrow — open questions first, then closed
- Separating replies from responses: distinguish an immediate reply from a considered response
- Layer Method: give information in layers — headline first, detail second, context third
- Lost causes: know when to close a failing conversation and return to it later

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** In Ugandan communication culture, indirect speech, silence, and deference to elders are normal. The Funnel Technique and Layer Method work well cross-culturally; however, "planning interaction" may feel unnatural to staff accustomed to informal, context-dependent communication. Train managers to signal structure explicitly: "I want to discuss three things today."

### Tool 7: Ten Steps to Become an Effective Manager
**Type:** Job Aid
**Author:** Allen Truell

**Purpose:** A step-by-step checklist for new or developing managers to build their managerial effectiveness systematically.

**When to use it:** On appointment to a new management role; during the first 90 days in post; as an annual audit of management practice.

**The ten steps:**
1. Know the goals and objectives of the organisation — read the strategic plan, understand KPIs
2. Know your roles and procedures — clarify your authority, reporting lines, and decision rights
3. Assess your own authority — understand what you can approve without escalation
4. Build relationships inside and outside the organisation — identify your key stakeholders
5. Ascertain the criteria used to measure performance — for both yourself and your team
6. Ask about rewards or recognition — what motivates your team and what is available
7. Identify resources available to you — budget, equipment, support staff, training funds
8. Tap help and support — find a mentor, peer network, or coach
9. Receive feedback on your own performance — schedule it; do not wait for it to come to you
10. Continue learning — dedicate time to professional development monthly

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Steps 3, 4, and 5 are often skipped in Ugandan SMEs because roles are fluid and authority is informal. Making authority explicit — especially in writing — reduces friction and "who decides this?" disputes significantly.

### Tool 8: Building Organisational Trust
**Type:** Job Aid
**Author:** Barbara Pate Glacel and Emile Robert Jr.

**Purpose:** A 17-tip guide for building and sustaining trust as the foundation of effective management. Organised as a downloadable reference card.

**When to use it:** When taking on a new team; when trust has been broken (e.g., after redundancies, broken promises, or public criticism of an employee); during a cultural change programme.

**The 17 trust-building tips:**
1. Keep your word
2. Meet deadlines
3. Maintain confidentiality
4. Demonstrate belief in your values
5. Demonstrate belief in the organisation's values
6. Keep people informed
7. Give and receive feedback
8. Admit mistakes
9. Behave consistently
10. Be fair in your exercise of authority
11. Respond to ideas and requests for help
12. Stay in tune with informal communication networks
13. Perform beyond the norm
14. Communicate from the heart
15. Share information widely
16. Recognise the contribution of others
17. Be personally interested in your team members

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Tips 14, 16, and 17 deserve special emphasis. Ugandan team culture is relationship-first; managers who show personal interest in employees' lives (families, health, personal milestones) build trust far faster than those who focus only on task performance. Trust is also clan-and-tribe-contextual — be aware that fairness must be visibly practised across tribal lines in diverse teams.

### Tool 9: A Case Study of Empowerment
**Type:** Exercise
**Author:** Paul Lyons
**Suggested time:** 60–90 minutes

**Purpose:** Uses the fictional Avalanche Inc. case study to surface the real barriers to delegation and empowerment. Participants discuss a scenario where a manager's inability to delegate stifles organisational learning.

**When to use it:** In a management development workshop; when a team or department is discussing why delegation fails; when onboarding new managers.

**How to use it:**
1. Distribute the Avalanche Inc. case study (downloadable).
2. Ask participants to read individually (15 minutes).
3. In groups of three to five, discuss: What went wrong? What should the manager have done differently?
4. Bring groups back together and facilitate a plenary discussion on the lessons for delegation and empowerment.
5. Ask each participant to identify one delegation action they will take in the next week.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** The Avalanche Inc. scenario resonates strongly in Ugandan family businesses where the founder or senior manager holds all decision-making authority. Frame the discussion around "what is the cost to the business of not delegating?" — financial costs, staff frustration, and the bottleneck on growth.

## Part II: Coaching and Managing Employee Performance (Tools 10–19)

### Tool 10: Coaching Inventory — Self and Employee Versions
**Type:** Questionnaire/Assessment
**Author:** Scott Martin

**Purpose:** A dual 35-item inventory that assesses coaching competence from both the manager's perspective (self-assessment) and the employee's perspective (how they experience being coached). Organised into seven categories:
1. Commitment to Professional Development
2. Commitment to Performance Development
3. Assessment, Diagnosis, and Planning
4. Meeting Face-to-Face and Giving Feedback
5. Attending, Listening, and Responding
6. Implementation and Follow-Up
7. (Overall coaching relationship quality)

**When to use it:** As baseline and progress measure in a coaching skills programme; before a coaching conversation to identify what the manager should focus on; as part of a performance review process.

**How to use it:**
1. Manager completes the Self inventory.
2. Each direct report completes the Employee inventory independently (can be anonymous).
3. Compare self-scores with employee scores per category.
4. Identify categories with large gaps — especially where manager scores high but employee scores low.
5. Create a Personal Learning Journal: Coaching Insights to track development actions.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Category 5 (Attending, Listening, and Responding) is consistently underdeveloped in Ugandan management culture, where managers often use one-way directive communication. The Employee version may need to be administered anonymously with an explanation that honesty will not result in retaliation.

### Tool 11: How Do You Compare with People You Find Difficult?
**Type:** Questionnaire/Assessment
**Authors:** Mel Silberman and Freda Hansburg

**Purpose:** A reflective exercise that compares your communication style, gender, age, and cultural background with those of people you find difficult to work with. Helps managers recognise that perceived "difficult" behaviour often reflects differences rather than deficiencies.

**When to use it:** When a manager is having persistent conflict with a specific team member; as part of interpersonal skills training; when managing a diverse team.

**How to use it:**
1. Complete the Comparing Style, Gender, Age, and Culture worksheet (downloadable).
2. For a person you find difficult, map their profile alongside yours on each dimension.
3. For each difference, write one hypothesis: "Their behaviour might make sense if they value X."
4. Identify one thing you could do differently in your next interaction.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Generational, tribal, and gender differences all shape communication style in Uganda. Younger managers sometimes find older, more senior employees "resistant" — but the older employee may be showing deference to a process (not the person). The reverse is also common. This tool helps name the dynamic without blame.

### Tool 12: Are You a Love 'Em or Lose 'Em Manager?
**Type:** Questionnaire/Assessment
**Authors:** Beverly Kaye and Sharon Jordan-Evans
**Source text:** Based on *Love 'Em or Lose 'Em* (Kaye and Jordan-Evans)

**Purpose:** A 26-item retention assessment that reveals whether a manager's behaviours are likely to retain or drive away talented employees.

**Scoring interpretation:**
- 0–6: Alert — this manager's behaviours are likely contributing to talent loss
- 7–13: Caution — some positive behaviours, but significant gaps remain
- 14+: Kudos — this manager is generally a strong retainer of talent

**When to use it:** When a team is experiencing high staff turnover; during a manager's annual performance review; as a diagnostic before a staff engagement survey.

**How to use it:**
1. Complete all 26 items honestly.
2. Review individual items where you scored 0 and identify one behaviour change per item.
3. Identify the two or three items most relevant to your current team's situation.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Staff turnover is a serious cost in Ugandan SMEs — skilled workers in accounting, IT, and operations are scarce. This assessment helps managers see that retention is a management skill, not just an HR function. Low-cost retention actions (flexible hours, public recognition, investment in training) score highly on this instrument.

### Tool 13: Do You Support Star Performance?
**Type:** Questionnaire/Assessment
**Authors:** Frederick Miller and Corey Jamison

**Purpose:** Assesses whether a manager creates conditions for exceptional performance rather than merely adequate performance. Covers three dimensions:
- Self-expression (up to 30 points): does the manager bring their full talent to the work?
- Support for expression (up to 30 points): does the manager create space for employees to do the same?
- Orientation to growth (up to 30 points): does the manager prioritise development?

**When to use it:** As a development tool for experienced managers; when performance has plateaued and the manager wants to understand why; as input to a talent management programme.

**How to use it:**
1. Complete the three-section assessment.
2. Score each section and identify your lowest dimension.
3. Write three to five specific actions to improve your lowest-scoring dimension this quarter.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** "Support for expression" — giving employees permission to take initiative and be creative — is the dimension most commonly underdeveloped in Uganda's directive management culture. Managers often fear that empowering employees will undermine their own authority; this tool reframes empowerment as an indicator of management strength, not weakness.

### Tool 14: Moving from Boss to Coach
**Type:** Job Aid
**Author:** Barbara Pate Glacel

**Purpose:** A 10-tip guide for managers transitioning from a directive "boss" role to a coaching role. Particularly relevant in organisations shifting toward team-based working, participatory management, or performance coaching.

**When to use it:** When a manager is newly promoted and needs to stop doing the technical work themselves; when an organisation is introducing coaching as a management standard; during hypergrowth when the founder's "do it all" style is no longer sustainable.

**The 10 tips:**
1. Become a skilled observer of people — watch behaviour before intervening
2. Listen more than you speak — resist the urge to solve every problem immediately
3. Ask powerful questions — "What options have you considered?" not "Here's what you should do"
4. Give feedback frequently and specifically — small feedback loops, not annual surprises
5. Set clear performance standards — people cannot excel without knowing the target
6. Focus on what is right, not just what is wrong — notice and name good performance
7. Help people connect their work to the organisational mission — "why does this matter?"
8. Build on people's strengths — assign work that plays to existing strengths while developing new ones
9. Hold people accountable without micromanaging — inspect results, not activity
10. Model the behaviour you want — coaches must be coached themselves

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Tip 3 (asking questions rather than giving answers) is the single most impactful shift for Ugandan managers. Many employees are trained to wait for instructions; coaching questions feel unfamiliar at first. Begin with structured coaching questions — "Tell me three options you see" — rather than completely open prompts.

### Tool 15: Improving Morale and Enhancing Productivity
**Type:** Job Aid
**Author:** Barbara Glanz

**Purpose:** A list of 25 low-cost, practical ideas for improving team morale and motivation. Organised as a downloadable reference card.

**When to use it:** When morale is low after a difficult period (restructuring, lost contract, conflict); as a team leader planning the next quarter's engagement activities; when budget for formal rewards is limited.

**Sample ideas from the 25:**
- Give each team member personal business cards (even if not customer-facing) — signals professional identity
- Run a T-shirt design contest for the team/department
- Write handwritten thank-you notes to acknowledge specific achievements
- Create a bulletin board with photos and updates about team members' lives outside work
- Ask senior management to do something creative and visible for the team
- Use a "Red Plate" for a different team member each week to celebrate achievement
- Greet every employee by name every morning
- Add a meaningful quote or cartoon to team communications
- Create a "Four A's jar" (Appreciation, Accomplishment, Aspiration, Accountability) for team members to drop notes in weekly
- Celebrate "Laugh Day" with a humour bulletin board

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Many of these ideas are zero-cost and highly applicable in Uganda. Handwritten notes, public acknowledgement, and personal greetings resonate strongly in Uganda's relationship-oriented culture. The "business cards for all staff" idea has a powerful effect in Ugandan contexts where formal titles and visible status matter.

### Tool 16: Designing and Implementing an Effective Performance Management Programme
**Type:** Job Aid
**Authors:** Kammy Haynes and Warren Bobrow

**Purpose:** A three-phase framework for building a performance management system that is fair, developmental, and legally defensible. Covers preparation, the appraisal meeting, and follow-up.

**When to use it:** When designing or overhauling a performance management system; before rolling out annual performance reviews; when managers need a consistent process.

**Phase 1 — Preparation:**
- Conduct job analysis for each role
- Set performance standards in advance (not after the review period)
- Create a communication plan explaining the purpose and process
- Set goals with the employee (not just for the employee)

**Phase 2 — The appraisal meeting:**
- Open with context: the purpose is development, not punishment
- Focus on behaviour, not personality
- Document specific examples — not generalisations
- Explore the employee's perspective first before sharing the manager's view
- Agree on a development action plan with specific milestones

**Phase 3 — Follow-up:**
- Schedule 30/60/90-day check-ins after each review
- Monitor performance against the agreed plan
- Offer support proactively — do not wait for failure
- Identify consequences — positive and negative — of sustained performance and non-performance

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Ugandan HR practice often treats performance reviews as a compliance exercise rather than a development tool. The three-phase structure helps reframe the process. Pay particular attention to Phase 1: in many Ugandan SMEs, job descriptions do not exist or are out of date — the performance management process cannot be fair without clear, documented standards.

### Tool 17: Developing Active Listening Skills
**Type:** Exercise
**Author:** Sharon Bowman
**Suggested time:** 30 minutes

**Purpose:** A structured three-step exercise to teach active listening through modelling, partner practice, and reverse role play.

**When to use it:** In management development training; as a team-building exercise when communication breakdown is an issue; during onboarding for new supervisors.

**How to use it:**
1. **Model** (10 minutes): Trainer demonstrates active listening in a live conversation — reflecting content, naming emotion, using open questions, tolerating silence.
2. **Partner practice** (10 minutes): Pairs practise with a structured topic. One person speaks; the other listens using the modelled behaviours, then reflects back. Switch roles.
3. **Reverse role play** (10 minutes): Each person plays themselves in a difficult recent conversation, but the "other person" is played by a partner — giving the listener a new perspective on the dynamic. Use Reverse Role Play Scripts (downloadable) for guidance.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Role play can feel uncomfortable in hierarchical Ugandan settings. Normalise it by having the trainer model first and by using fictional characters or work-related scenarios rather than personal conflicts. The reverse role play step is particularly powerful for surfacing how managers come across when they think they are listening but are actually waiting to talk.

### Tool 18: Seeing through Another's Eyes
**Type:** Exercise
**Author:** Dave Arch
**Suggested time:** 10 minutes

**Purpose:** Uses an optical illusion (the classic duck/rabbit image) to illustrate that different people can look at the same situation and perceive something completely different — and that both perceptions can be correct. Creates a memorable hook for conversations about perspective-taking.

**When to use it:** As a quick opener for workshops on communication, conflict resolution, or change management; when teams are stuck in "we're right, they're wrong" thinking.

**How to use it:**
1. Show the duck/rabbit image (or another ambiguous image) to the group.
2. Ask: "What do you see?" Note that some see a duck, others a rabbit.
3. Facilitate a brief discussion: both are correct — the image contains both. Neither viewer is wrong.
4. Link to the current team situation: "Where might two people be seeing the same situation from different but equally valid perspectives?"

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** This exercise transcends literacy and language barriers — the image is the content. Effective with mixed-education-level teams. The learning point about "both can be right" is especially valuable in Ugandan team contexts where disagreement is often seen as disrespect.

### Tool 19: Managing Real Time
**Type:** Exercise
**Authors:** Steve Sugar and Bob Preziosi
**Suggested time:** 75–120 minutes

**Purpose:** A time management simulation in which participants plan their day, are interrupted by telephone messages, then reassess and replanning priorities in teams. Develops skills in distinguishing urgent from important, handling interruptions, and collaborative time management.

**How to use it:**
1. **Round 1 — Individual planning** (15 minutes): Each participant uses the Day Log (downloadable) and Schedule to plan their hypothetical workday, allocating time to tasks.
2. **Telephone message interruptions** (ongoing throughout the exercise): Distribute Messages (downloadable) — interruptions that add urgency, change priorities, or add new tasks.
3. **Round 2 — Team replanning** (20 minutes): Small groups share their plans and messages and develop a consolidated team schedule.
4. **Round 3 — Presentation and debrief** (30 minutes): Groups present their plans and discuss: How did interruptions change priorities? What was sacrificed? What does this reveal about time management habits?

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** The "message interruption" format mimics the reality of Ugandan office life — constant interruptions from phone calls (voice and WhatsApp), walk-in visitors, and overlapping demands. The exercise validates that time management in Uganda requires explicit "protected time" strategies rather than just personal discipline.

## Part III: Building Collaboration and Teamwork (Tools 20–29)

### Tool 20: What Does Your Team Need to Improve? (GRIP Assessment)
**Type:** Questionnaire/Assessment
**Author:** Kevin Lohan

**Purpose:** A 40-item team health assessment covering four dimensions — Goals, Roles, Interpersonal Relations, and Procedures (GRIP). Each dimension has 10 items rated on a 0–5 scale, giving a maximum of 50 per dimension and 200 overall.

**The four GRIP dimensions:**
- **Goals** — Are team objectives clear, shared, and compelling?
- **Roles** — Does each person understand their responsibilities and how they interrelate?
- **Interpersonal Relations** — Is there trust, openness, and constructive conflict?
- **Procedures** — Are meeting, decision-making, and communication processes functional?

**When to use it:** When team performance is declining; at the start of a new project or team composition; as an annual team health check.

**How to use it:**
1. Each team member (including the leader) completes the assessment independently.
2. Average scores per dimension across all raters.
3. Identify the lowest-scoring dimension — this is where to focus first.
4. Facilitate a team discussion: "What specifically is driving our low score on [dimension]?"
5. Agree on two or three specific actions to improve within 30 days.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** In Ugandan teams, "Interpersonal Relations" often scores paradoxically — social warmth is high but honest confrontation of performance gaps is low. "Procedures" is often the weakest dimension in SMEs where meetings are unstructured and decision-making is informal. GRIP gives a common vocabulary for discussing these without blame.

### Tool 21: Who's on Your Team? (Team Member Uniqueness Assessment)
**Type:** Questionnaire/Assessment
**Author:** Jon Stieber

**Purpose:** Identifies each team member's unique motivational profile using 14 motivators: Achievement, Attention, Autonomy, Challenge, Clarity, Camaraderie, Competence, Encouragement, Expertise, Harmony, Order, Stability, Supportive environment, and Variety. Helps team leaders and members understand what drives each person and how to structure work accordingly.

**When to use it:** When forming a new team; when re-deploying staff; when motivation is declining and the manager wants to understand individual drivers.

**How to use it:**
1. Each team member (including the leader) completes the Team Leader and Team Member Uniqueness Assessment (downloadable) — ranking their top five motivators.
2. Share results openly in a team session.
3. Map each person's top motivators against their current role — where are the gaps?
4. Discuss as a team: "How can we structure work so each person gets more of what motivates them?"
5. The leader uses this to inform task allocation and recognition style.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** "Stability" and "Harmony" often rank highly among Ugandan employees, reflecting a preference for predictability and positive relationships. "Autonomy" and "Challenge" may rank low but can be developed over time in a psychologically safe environment. Understanding individual motivators is particularly valuable in Uganda because monetary incentives are often limited.

### Tool 22: Is Your Team Functioning on All Cylinders?
**Type:** Questionnaire/Assessment
**Author:** Valerie MacLeod

**Purpose:** A diagnostic assessment of overall team functioning — the degree to which a team operates as a high-performing unit across multiple dimensions including shared purpose, mutual accountability, and productive processes.

**When to use it:** Quarterly team health review; when a new leader inherits an existing team; when team output has declined without an obvious cause.

**How to use it:**
1. All team members complete the Highly Functioning Team Assessment Tool (downloadable) independently.
2. Compile and average scores.
3. Present results to the whole team — transparency is key.
4. Discuss the lowest-scoring items: "What would it look like if we improved here?"
5. Assign action owners for the top two priority items.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Present results in a team setting with care for hierarchy — team leaders may feel publicly embarrassed by low scores. Frame it as "this is the team's score, not a score of any individual" and model vulnerability by sharing the leader's own contribution to the low score.

### Tool 23: Aligning Your Team
**Type:** Exercise
**Author:** Susan Solomon
**Suggested time:** 90 minutes to half-day (depending on team size and issues)

**Purpose:** A 16-question facilitated alignment process that surfaces hidden disagreements about direction, roles, and working norms, then drives consensus on a shared way forward.

**When to use it:** When a team has been through a major change; when there is a sense that "we all think we're aligned but we're not"; when launching a new project with a cross-functional team.

**How to use it:**
1. **Before the alignment session:** Collect individual written responses to the 16 alignment questions (covering goals, values, decision rights, communication norms, escalation processes, and success measures).
2. **During the session:** Facilitate structured discussion of each item where responses diverge. Do not rush consensus.
3. **Produce an Alignment Summary** capturing agreed positions on each dimension.
4. **After the session:** Distribute the summary to all participants and revisit at the next team meeting to confirm commitments are being honoured.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** The "before" step (anonymous written responses) is especially useful in Uganda, where team members are unlikely to voice disagreement with a senior colleague in a group setting. Written input given before the facilitated session equalises the voice of junior team members.

### Tool 24: Clarifying Team Roles and Responsibilities
**Type:** Job Aid
**Author:** Edwina Haring

**Purpose:** A concept-mapping methodology for clarifying roles within a team. Provides three design examples tailored to different team contexts:
- **Team A** — established team clarifying overlapping responsibilities
- **Team B** — newly formed team building role clarity from scratch
- **Team C** — project-specific team with members from multiple functions

**When to use it:** When team members are unclear about who does what; when role overlap is causing conflict or duplication; when building a new team or project group.

**How to use it:**
1. Choose the appropriate design template (A, B, or C) from the Roles and Responsibilities Clarification Activity Design Tool (downloadable).
2. Ask each team member to map their own responsibilities on the template.
3. In a facilitated session, lay all maps side by side. Identify overlaps, gaps, and unclear boundaries.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Role clarity is often missing in Ugandan SMEs because hiring happens reactively and job descriptions are not updated as the business grows. The concept-map approach is particularly useful for mixed-literacy teams — visual mapping is easier to engage with than a written job description.

### Tool 25: Building Distance Teams
**Type:** Job Aid
**Author:** Debra Dinnocenzo

**Purpose:** A practical guide to leading geographically dispersed teams, covering the five core challenges: nurturing the team, maintaining accessibility, developing interaction skills, delegating effectively, and building trust at a distance.

**When to use it:** When managing a team across multiple sites, districts, or countries; when moving to remote or hybrid working; when a key team member has relocated.

**The five core strategies:**
1. **Nurture the team** — schedule regular individual and team check-ins; acknowledge personal milestones; maintain social connection deliberately

### Tool 26: Becoming a Team Player
**Type:** Job Aid
**Author:** Mel Silberman

**Purpose:** A comprehensive guide to participatory team leadership — how to facilitate group participation, brainstorming, and consensus-building. Covers seven participation methods, two brainstorming techniques, and three consensus approaches.

**Participation methods:**
1. Open discussion — default; works when engagement is high
2. Response cards — written answers before verbal discussion; equalises participation
3. Go-around — everyone contributes in turn; prevents domination
4. Subgroups — small groups discuss before plenary; builds confidence
5. Calling on next speaker — current speaker designates next; more equitable than hand-raising

### Tool 29: A Game of Team Trust
**Type:** Exercise
**Author:** Ed Rose
**Suggested time:** 90 minutes

**Purpose:** A Prisoner's Dilemma-style bidding game that makes visible the dynamics of trust, cooperation, and self-interest within and between teams. ### Tool 32: Handling the Process Dimension of Meetings
**Type:** Job Aid
**Author:** Scott Parry

**Purpose:** A guide to managing the interpersonal dynamics that derail meetings — excessive participation, non-participation, side conversations, and antagonistic attitudes. Provides specific facilitation responses to each type of dysfunction.

**Four principles for managing meeting process:**
1. **Separate symptoms from problems** — "talking too much" is a symptom; the problem is "unequal airtime"; address the root cause
2. **Let the group handle it** — before intervening as a facilitator, invite the group to self-correct; this builds self-management skills
3. **Save face** — never humiliate a participant; make the intervention about the process, not the person
4. **Maintain adult-to-adult relationships** — avoid parent-child dynamics in facilitation; treat all participants as capable adults

**Specific interventions:**
- **Excessive participation:** Use structured go-around; name the behaviour neutrally ("I want to make sure we hear from everyone")
- **Non-participation:** Use response cards or direct (warm) invitation; never shame
- **Side conversations:** Pause; invite the side conversation into the main discussion
- **Antagonistic attitudes:** Acknowledge the feeling; redirect to the issue; do not allow personal attacks

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Non-participation is the most common dysfunction in Ugandan meetings — junior staff, women, and members of non-dominant tribal groups will often stay silent even when they have important input. Use response cards (written input before speaking) as a structural fix rather than relying on facilitation interventions alone.

### Tool 33: Using the Nominal Group Process
**Type:** Exercise
**Author:** Theresa Musser
**Suggested time:** 1–2 hours
**Source:** Based on DelBecq, Van de Ven, and Gustafson's Nominal Group Technique

**Purpose:** A highly structured seven-step group problem-solving process that equalises participation and produces a prioritised list of solutions or ideas that reflects the genuine views of the whole group — not just the most vocal members.

**The seven steps:**
1. **Silent individual brainstorming** (10 min): Everyone writes ideas independently — no discussion
2. **Round-robin listing** (20 min): Each person shares one idea at a time, rotating until all ideas are on the board. No discussion or evaluation yet.
3. **Group discussion** (30 min): Each idea is clarified and discussed, but not voted on yet
4. **First vote** (10 min): Each person privately ranks their top ideas (e.g., 5 for most preferred down to 1 for fifth)
5. **Discussion of first vote** (20 min): Results are shared; divergent votes are discussed
6. **Final vote** (10 min): Revised private ranking; final priorities emerge
7. **Wrap-up** (20 min): Confirm the top priorities; agree on next steps and owners

**When to use it:** When a group is stuck on a problem and needs fresh ideas; when a decision needs genuine buy-in from the whole team; when past group discussions have been dominated by one or two voices.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** NGP is the most powerful equalisation tool in this toolkit for Ugandan group settings. The silent individual brainstorming and private voting steps give all participants — including junior staff and women — an equal structural voice that bypasses social hierarchy. It does require a confident facilitator. Plan for a 90-minute session in Uganda as setup and voting tend to take longer.

### Tool 34: Improving Problem Solving in Meetings
**Type:** Exercise
**Author:** Edwina Haring
**Suggested time:** 3–3.5 hours

**Purpose:** The Problem-Solving Relay — a four-round exercise in which four groups rotate through the four stages of structured problem solving: Identify (the problem), Generate (solutions), Prioritise (solutions), and Implement (plan). Each group adds to the previous group's work.

**How to use it:**
1. Define a real team or organisational problem in advance.
2. Divide into four groups. Assign Round 1 (Identify) to Group 1; Round 2 (Generate) to Group 2, etc.
3. Each group has a defined time allocation (30–45 minutes) to complete their stage on chart paper.
4. Rotate groups clockwise — each group inherits and builds on the previous group's work.
5. After all four rounds, all groups review the final implementation plan together.
6. Debrief: What surprised you? What would you add?

**When to use it:** For complex problems that require structured thinking and wide participation; as a team development exercise that builds problem-solving skills alongside solving a real issue.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** The Problem-Solving Relay is highly effective for Ugandan management teams because it distributes the intellectual load — no one person or function "owns" the solution. It also builds cross-departmental understanding. Use real problems that participants care about; fabricated problems disengage Ugandan participants quickly.

### Tool 36: Breaking Stalemates in Groups
**Type:** Exercise
**Author:** Mel Silberman
**Suggested time:** 30–45 minutes

**Purpose:** A structured four-part process for resolving group deadlock when discussion has become circular, positions have hardened, and progress has stalled.

**The four-part stalemate-breaking framework:**
1. **The conflict we're having** — name the stalemate explicitly; what is the disagreement about?
2. **What concerns us** — each side articulates their underlying concerns, not just their position
3. **What we'd like to suggest** — each side proposes a solution that addresses both their concern and the other side's concern
4. **What we're willing to do** — concrete commitments to move forward

**When to use it:** When a team discussion has gone around the same loop more than twice; when two camps have formed and are no longer listening to each other; when a decision has been deferred for more than one meeting cycle.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Ugandan work culture often suppresses open conflict — groups may appear to agree when they do not. This tool works best when a trusted external facilitator or senior champion frames the stalemate-breaking process as "how we make better decisions" rather than "how we resolve the conflict you've been having." Steps 1 and 2 (naming the conflict and the underlying concerns) are the most transformative for Ugandan groups.

### Tool 37: Promoting the Value of "Yes"
**Type:** Exercise
**Author:** Kat Koppett
**Suggested time:** 4–8 minutes

**Purpose:** A brief improvisational exercise that demonstrates the power of "Yes, and" (building on ideas) versus "Yes, but" (negating ideas). Creates a visceral understanding of how blocking and accepting contributions affect team creativity and morale.

**How to use it:**
1. In pairs, Person A makes a suggestion for a fictional activity: "Let's go to the beach."
2. Person B responds with "Yes, but…" and gives a reason why not. Repeat for five exchanges. Notice how the energy feels.
3. Repeat with the same scenario, but Person B now responds with "Yes, and…" and builds on the idea. Repeat for five exchanges.
4. Debrief in plenary: What was different? How does "Yes, but" show up in our real team meetings? What is the cost?

**When to use it:** As a warm-up for any brainstorming or innovation session; when a team is stuck in a culture of shooting ideas down; before a creative problem-solving workshop.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** This tool works instantly across cultures and languages. It takes less than 10 minutes and requires no materials. Use it as a regular warm-up at the start of team meetings — over time it shifts the group norm from "find the problem with the idea" to "build on the idea."

### Tool 39: How Ready Are Your People for Change?
**Type:** Questionnaire/Assessment
**Author:** Randall Buerkle

**Purpose:** The Change Resiliency Profile (CRP) — a 20-item individual assessment of how a person perceives and adapts to change. Can be used individually for self-reflection or collectively to assess a team's overall change resilience.

**Scoring:**
| Score | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| 88–100 | Highly flexible; exemplary role model for change |
| 76–87 | Relatively flexible; comfortable in most change situations |
| 64–75 | Flexible in some situations; comfort depends on nature and magnitude of change |
| 52–63 | Relatively inflexible; tends to hold on to established ways until value of change is proven |
| Below 52 | Very inflexible; may feel helpless or victimised when facing change |

**What the assessment measures (sample items):**
- "I view disruption as a natural phenomenon"
- "I have a high tolerance for ambiguity"
- "I am able to renegotiate priorities during change"
- "I rely on nurturing relationships for support"
- "I can handle many tasks and demands at the same time"

**When to use it:** Before announcing a major organisational change; when assessing team readiness for a restructuring or new system implementation; as a coaching tool for individuals who are visibly struggling with change.

**How to use it:**
1. Each team member completes the CRP individually.
2. Aggregate scores across the team to identify overall team resilience and individual outliers.
3. Plan change communication and support based on where the team sits — teams below 64 need significantly more preparation time and more information before action is expected.
4. Use individual low-scorers' profiles to design targeted coaching support.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Uganda's business environment is characterised by frequent regulatory, political, and economic shifts — most Ugandan employees and managers have significant lived experience of navigating change. However, organisational change imposed by management (new systems, new reporting lines, restructuring) feels categorically different from external change — expect more resistance than individual resilience scores would predict.

### Tool 40: Is Your Organisation Ready for Change?
**Type:** Questionnaire/Assessment
**Author:** Duane Tway

**Purpose:** The Organisational Change Characteristics Survey — a 22-item survey that distinguishes first-order change (surface modifications that do not alter the core) from second-order change (deep transformation of processes, structures, and culture).

**Scoring:**
- Score even-numbered questions normally (answer = score)
- Score odd-numbered questions in reverse (score = 10 minus answer)

**Score interpretation:**
| Total score | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| 150+ | Organisation is exceptionally ready for or undergoing second-order planned change |
| 100–150 | Well placed to begin second-order change; if already started, reinforce preparation |
| 50–100 | People are aware of change effort but much work remains; if underway, stop and re-evaluate |
| Below 50 | Second-order change will be very difficult; extensive planning required before beginning |

**When to use it:** As a diagnostic before launching a change programme; as a mid-point check during a transformation effort; as a final evaluation of a completed change initiative.

**How to use it:**
1. Survey all members of the leadership team (or a representative sample of the organisation).
2. Calculate aggregate scores.
3. Share results with senior leadership — use to calibrate the ambition and pace of the change programme.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Most Ugandan SMEs operate in first-order change mode — tweaking structures and processes without changing underlying assumptions. Second-order change (e.g., transitioning from a founder-led to a professionally managed business) requires significantly more time, trust-building, and culture work than leaders typically anticipate. Score below 100 should prompt a pause and a strategy review.

### Tool 41: Coaching Employees through Change
**Type:** Job Aid
**Author:** Nancy Jackson
**Based on:** The Transtheoretical Model of Change (Prochaska, Norcross, and Diclemente)

**Purpose:** A six-stage Change Cycle model that helps managers identify where each employee is in their personal change journey and apply the appropriate coaching support at the right moment. The model was originally developed to support behavioural change in clinical settings and has been adapted for organisational use.

**The six stages:**
| Stage | Description | What the person might say |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Precontemplation | Denial or unawareness of need for change | "They can't make me do this." / "That will never happen here." |
| 2. Contemplation | Beginning to consider the change | "Some day I suppose I'll have to…" |
| 3. Determination | Decision made; beginning to plan | Scheduling training; researching options |
| 4. Action | Actively implementing the change | Using the new system; attending new team meetings |
| 5. Maintenance | Sustaining the change over time | Developing new habits; managing old ones creeping back |
| 6. Recycling | Relapse; returning to an earlier stage | Slipping back to old behaviours; needing to go through the cycle again |

**Coaching interventions by stage:**
- **Precontemplation:** Provide factual information without pressure — "here is the situation, what will you do?" Give choice, even if limited.
- **Contemplation:** Listen to ambivalence without pushing. Help identify pros and cons of both old and new ways. Reinforce motivation.
- **Determination:** Listen and support without directing. Help the person construct their own plan. Use visualisation — "how do you see yourself in the new role?"
- **Action:** Check in occasionally; most support is now practical. Answer questions as they arise.
- **Maintenance:** Provide soft-skills training to support the change long-term. Celebrate sustained progress.
- **Recycling:** Treat relapse as data, not failure. Re-enter at contemplation. Increase listening support and ambivalence resolution.

**When to use it:** Whenever managing individual employees through organisational change — new systems, role changes, team restructures, new line managers.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Many Ugandan change programmes fail because they launch at "Action" (distributing new forms, requiring new behaviours) while most staff are still in "Precontemplation." This model gives managers a practical diagnostic: "where is this specific person in the cycle?" and a coaching response calibrated to that person's stage rather than a one-size programme.

### Tool 42: Seeking Employee Opinion
**Type:** Job Aid
**Authors:** Kammy Haynes and Warren Bobrow

**Purpose:** A comprehensive guide to designing, administering, analysing, and acting on employee attitude surveys. Covers the full survey life cycle and the common pitfalls that cause surveys to damage rather than improve organisational trust.

**Key principle:** Conducting a survey creates an implicit commitment to act on the results. A survey that produces no action is worse than no survey — it signals that employee voices do not matter.

**Survey design guidance:**
- Use focus groups to identify critical issues before writing items
- One idea per item — avoid double-barrelled questions ("My supervisor is available AND willing")
- Mix closed-ended (Likert) and open-ended questions
- Include negative items sparingly to prevent response-pattern bias
- Be clear about what demographic data you need — more detail = less perceived anonymity

**Administration options:**
- **Mass administration** (group sessions): 80–100% response rate; highest logistical overhead
- **Mail-in** (to homes or internal mail): highest perceived confidentiality; 35% response rate typical
- **Online/intranet**: fastest data processing; risk of privacy concerns and low response

**Analysis guidance:**
- Report simple statistics (averages, frequencies) for individual items
- Use narrative storytelling: beginning (why we surveyed), middle (what we found), end (what we recommend)
- Do not overinterpret small score differences
- Do not mistake satisfaction with importance — add an importance scale if you need to know what matters most

**Action planning after the survey:**
- Tie every recommendation to a specific data point
- Assign owners and timelines to every action
- Publish the actions taken in response to the survey — close the feedback loop visibly

**When to use it:** When diagnosing causes of high turnover, low morale, or declining productivity; before a major change initiative (baseline); at regular intervals (annual or biannual).

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Employee surveys require a strong climate of trust to produce honest responses. In Uganda, where there is often concern about retaliation for honest feedback, anonymity must be both guaranteed and credibly demonstrated (e.g., external analysis, no surreptitious coding). Mass administration sessions run by an external facilitator produce the best results. Verbal or WhatsApp-based pulse surveys can supplement formal written surveys for lower-literacy workforces.

### Tool 43: Initiating and Managing Change
**Type:** Job Aid
**Author:** Nora Carrol

**Purpose:** A seven-step workbook framework for planning and managing organisational change systematically. Works as both a change planning guide and a diagnostic for organisations in the middle of a change that is not going well.

**The seven steps:**

**Step 1: Establish your mission and purpose**
- Is there a current mission statement? Does it accurately reflect the organisation?
- Who needs to participate in formulating or revising it?

**Step 2: Audit your current organisation**
- Products/services: do they reflect the mission?
- Market identification: who do you serve and who do you want to serve?
- Capacities and capabilities: what can you currently do? What are the gaps?
- Cost and revenue performance: are financial expectations realistic and aligned with mission?

**Step 3: Scan your external environment**
- Identify direct and indirect competitors
- Understand markets served by competitors
- Map regulatory and external requirements

**Step 4: Conduct market research**
- What question are you trying to answer?
- Quantitative (volume) or qualitative (depth) data?
- Internal or external research capability?

**Step 5: Create a short- and long-term goals continuum**
- What do you want to change, in what order, by when?
- Who will champion each change?
- What resources and processes are needed?
- Identify and mandate change agents — internal or external

**Step 6: Develop and implement an action plan**
- Operationalise each priority: internal vs. external resources; timelines; interdependencies
- Assess and evaluate implementation as it proceeds

**Step 7: Integrate change planning into your system**
- Set up an early-warning system: who in each function can scan the environment and report signals of change?
- Set up a feedback system: how will market signals, customer feedback, and competitor intelligence flow to decision-makers?
- Plan the negotiation and communication strategy for each planned change

**When to use it:** As a structured workbook for any planned change programme; as a diagnostic when a change effort is stalling; as input to a strategic planning retreat.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** Steps 3 and 7 (external scanning and early-warning systems) are significantly underdeveloped in Ugandan SMEs. Most founders and managers are too consumed by day-to-day operations to monitor the competitive environment systematically. Assigning one person — even part-time — to track market, regulatory, and competitor intelligence creates an early-warning function at minimal cost.

### Tool 44: Understanding Change through Other People's Eyes
**Type:** Exercise
**Author:** Vicki Schneider
**Suggested time:** 30–60 minutes
**Materials:** Three pennies, three drawings, three $20 bills, Form A (contestant cards), Form B (audience prediction cards)

**Purpose:** "The Great Giveaway" — an interactive exercise that makes viscerally real the lesson that what one person considers trivial (a drawing), another may value enormously (a child's first artwork). Applied to change management: what leaders ask employees to give up may seem trivial to the leader but highly significant to the employee.

**How to use it:**
1. Select three audience members as contestants. Give each a different Form A card — Contestant A values the $20 bill highest; Contestant B holds a rare penny; Contestant C has a drawing of deep sentimental value.
2. Audience members predict which object each contestant will keep, using Form B cards.
3. In sequence, each contestant gives up two objects. The audience's predictions are often wrong.
4. Debrief:
   - "What did the contestants know that you didn't?"
   - "As we ask people to change, we ask them to give up things — both tangible and intangible. How can we use this to understand why people resist change?"
5. Follow-up questions for small groups: What are you being asked to give up in the current change? What might replace that loss? Think of someone resisting change — what might they be giving up?

**When to use it:** When launching a change initiative and wanting leaders to develop empathy for resisters; when coaching a manager who cannot understand why their team is resisting a "clearly positive" change.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** The "sentimental value" dimension (the drawing) resonates strongly in Ugandan culture, where informal relationships, established routines, and relational history carry enormous personal weight. A restructuring that seems efficient to management may require employees to give up working with a trusted colleague of 10 years — a loss that is real and significant, even if invisible to the change leader.

### Tool 45: Discussing Change and Innovation
**Type:** Exercise
**Author:** Scott Simmerman
**Suggested time:** 20 minutes
**Materials:** Form A (Square Wheels One — a cartoon image); Form B ("It is dangerous to know The Answer")

**Purpose:** Uses a caterpillar-and-butterfly joke and the "Square Wheels" metaphor to surface the core innovation lesson: knowing "The Answer" closes down exploration and limits what a team can discover together.

**How to use it:**
1. Distribute or project Form A (Square Wheels One image — a wagon with square wheels being pushed, while round wheels lie unused on the side).
2. Tell the joke: "Two caterpillars are riding on a wagon. A butterfly floats by. One caterpillar says to the other: 'You'll never get me up in one of those things!'"
3. Ask if they "get the joke." Then: "There's a lesson in telling this joke — discuss it in pairs for two minutes."
4. Take responses. Typical answers: "Change is inevitable"; "Have a vision of the future." Respond that each is valid but none is The Lesson.
5. Reveal The Lesson: "It is dangerous to know The Answer." (Show Form B.)
6. Explain: "When we know The Answer, we stop looking. Leadership, innovation, teamwork, and change all require us to hold multiple possibilities simultaneously."
7. Link to the four-component change model:
   - Level of discomfort with the status quo
   - Attractiveness of the vision of the future
   - Individual/group's previous success with change
   - Peer and workgroup support for the change

**When to use it:** As an opener for any innovation or change workshop; when a leadership team is locked into a single "solution" before they have explored alternatives; as a creative energiser at a strategic planning retreat.

**Uganda/EA adaptation:** The "Square Wheels" image works powerfully in Ugandan contexts — many organisations have the round wheels (better practices) sitting unused alongside the wagon (the current operation) because "we've always done it this way." The exercise prompts teams to surface their own round wheels without external experts having to impose solutions.

### Adapting for a Low-Literacy Workforce

Several tools in this collection assume a workforce that is comfortable reading, writing, and completing scored questionnaires in English. Adaptations for lower-literacy contexts:

1. **Oral administration:** Read questionnaire items aloud and record responses verbally or with show-of-hands. This works for Tools 1, 3, 5, 12, 20, 21, and 39.

2. **Visual substitution:** Replace written scales with visual analogues — a line from "strongly disagree" (hand pointing down) to "strongly agree" (hand pointing up); coloured cards (red/yellow/green) instead of numerical scores.

3. **Group discussion format:** Facilitate open-ended oral discussion on the instrument's key themes rather than individual written completion. A scribe records key points.

4. **WhatsApp-based collection:** For employees with smartphones, voice-note responses to structured questions can replace written surveys. Transcription is straightforward.

5. **Peer-pair administration:** Pair a literate with a lower-literacy employee; the literate peer reads items and records responses. This works best when confidentiality is not critical (use for team discussions, not individual performance assessments).

6. **Exercise-based tools first:** Tools 17 (Active Listening), 18 (Duck/Rabbit), 26 (Becoming a Team Player), 37 (Yes, And), and 45 (Square Wheels) require minimal literacy and are often more engaging than questionnaire-based tools for mixed-literacy teams.

### Cultural Considerations in Uganda

**1. Hierarchical deference**
Ugandan organisations are typically high on power distance. Employees are unlikely to give candid feedback upward unless:
- Anonymity is structurally guaranteed (not just promised)
- The process is facilitated by an external or neutral party
- Senior leaders model vulnerability by sharing their own development areas first
- The framing is "helping us all improve" not "rating the boss"

**2. Collective identity and group harmony**
Ugandan teams often prioritise group harmony and avoid public confrontation. This means:
- Individual scoring and private reflection before group discussion (as in NGP, Tool 33) works better than open debate
- Tools that name a group dynamic without blaming an individual (GRIP, Tool 20; Team Alignment, Tool 23) are culturally safer than interpersonal feedback
- Frame conflict tools (Tool 36, Breaking Stalemates) as process tools, not interpersonal conflict tools

**3. Elder and seniority dynamics**
In Ugandan culture, age and seniority confer automatic authority. This creates challenges when:
- A younger or junior manager needs to give feedback to an older direct report (Tool 2, 10, 12)
- A facilitator needs to challenge a senior participant's view in a group exercise
- A peer-feedback process requires team members at different levels to rate each other

Mitigations: use written instruments (not verbal); frame feedback as "how can we serve the organisation better?" rather than "how am I rating you?"; have the most senior person complete instruments first and share results publicly before asking others to do so.

**4. Tribal and ethnic diversity**
In diverse Ugandan teams (common in Kampala and urban areas), tribal dynamics can shape who speaks, who is trusted, and who is listened to. Tools that equalise structural voice — response cards, go-arounds, anonymous voting — are particularly valuable. Be alert to coalition-forming in group exercises (Tools 27, 29) along tribal lines rather than strategic lines.

**5. Relationship-first communication**
Ugandan professional communication is relationship-first: greetings, personal enquiries, and social connection precede task discussion. Build this into meeting designs. Tool 28 (Getting to Know Your Teammates) is particularly powerful for Ugandan teams precisely because it gives formal permission for the relational investment that Ugandan culture already values.

### Remote and Distributed Team Management in Uganda

For Uganda's multi-site businesses (Kampala headquarters plus regional offices in Gulu, Mbale, Mbarara, Jinja, Fort Portal, etc.):

**Communication infrastructure:**
- WhatsApp Business is the primary management communication tool; treat it as your main "distance team" channel
- Voice notes are preferred over text in low-typing-fluency environments
- Video calls (Zoom, Google Meet) work well in district towns but may fail in rural areas — have audio-only fallback protocols
- Establish explicit norms: which decisions require video? which can be voice note? which need a written record?

**Applying Tool 25 (Building Distance Teams) in Uganda:**
- **Nurture:** Monthly in-person visits from headquarters to regional teams, where budget allows; quarterly regional manager in-person meetings; WhatsApp group for social connection (separate from the task group)
- **Accessibility:** Define response time SLAs per channel — e.g., WhatsApp voice note: respond within 4 hours; formal request: 24 hours
- **Interaction skills:** Train regional managers in video meeting participation; use voice calls when video fails
- **Delegation:** Give regional managers explicit authority for local decisions; require reporting on outcomes, not activity
- **Trust:** Regional staff can feel invisible and undervalued. Regular name-checking in headquarters communications ("The Gulu team hit their Q3 target") is a low-cost but high-impact trust builder.
