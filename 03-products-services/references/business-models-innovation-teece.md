# Business Models, Business Strategy, and Innovation

**Source:** *Business Models, Business Strategy and Innovation* — David J. Teece, Elsevier Ltd / Long Range Planning, 2010. (Originally published as: Teece, D.J. (2010). "Business models, business strategy and innovation." *Long Range Planning*, 43(2–3), 172–194.)

Teece is one of the most-cited scholars in strategic management; this is his definitive statement on business model theory.

**Relevance:** Business model design, innovation strategy, competitive advantage, value capture vs value creation. Core reading for strategic sections of a business plan. Applicable to any Uganda business defining how it creates, delivers, and captures value.

## Core Thesis

Teece argues that a business model defines **how a firm creates value for customers and then converts that value into revenues**. It is logically prior to strategy — you cannot develop a strategy without first having a working business model.

The central claim: *"Business model innovation can itself be a pathway to competitive advantage if the model is sufficiently differentiated and difficult to replicate."*

The key failure mode he identifies: firms that innovate products or processes but fail to capture value because they have not designed the right business model. "Value creation is not the same as value capture."

## Key Frameworks

### 1. The Business Model Definition

A business model consists of two core elements:

**Value Creation Architecture:**
- How the enterprise creates value for customers
- What customer segments are served
- What needs are met
- What technologies or capabilities underpin the offering
- How partners, suppliers, and channels contribute

**Value Capture Mechanism:**
- How the enterprise converts created value into revenue and profit
- The pricing model (per unit, subscription, licensing, freemium, usage-based, etc.)
- The cost structure
- How revenue flows and margins are achieved

*"A business model is more than a revenue model; it encompasses how a company creates value, delivers it to customers, and then captures some portion of that value as profit."*

---

### 2. Business Model vs Business Strategy

Teece is careful to distinguish the two:

| Business Model | Business Strategy |
|---|---|
| Describes the logic of how the firm creates and captures value | Describes how the firm competes, positions, and builds advantage over rivals |
| Is about the architecture of the business | Is about the competitive posture |
| Can be replicated by competitors | Is explicitly about differentiation |
| Necessary but not sufficient for competitive advantage | Built on top of the business model |

**Implication:** You can have an excellent business model (e.g., subscriptions) that is easily copied. Strategy is what makes your version of that model hard to replicate.

A firm needs:
1. A viable business model — one where value creation and capture are aligned
2. A strategy — that makes its business model difficult to imitate

---

### 3. Business Model Innovation

Teece identifies business model innovation as distinct from — and potentially more powerful than — product or process innovation.

**Types of business model innovation:**
- **New revenue models** — moving from unit sales to subscriptions, licensing, or usage-based pricing
- **New customer segments** — serving customers who were previously unserved or underserved
- **New value propositions** — redefining what benefit the product/service delivers
- **New delivery channels** — reaching customers through fundamentally different routes
- **New cost architectures** — fundamentally restructuring how costs are incurred (e.g., asset-light models)
- **Ecosystem reconfiguration** — changing the roles of partners, suppliers, and customers in value creation

**Historical examples:**
- Xerox: moved from selling photocopiers to leasing them (per-copy pricing) — unlocked access for customers who could not afford capital purchase
- Gillette: razors at a loss, blades at high margin (the classic razor-blade model)
- Amazon Web Services: converted internal infrastructure into an external service business
- Mobile money (M-Pesa, MTN MoMo): converted the telecommunications network into a financial services delivery channel

### 4. The Value Creation vs Value Capture Problem

Teece argues this is the central challenge of innovation-based competition.

**Value creation** = generating utility for customers (solving problems, improving lives, saving time or money)

**Value capture** = retaining enough of that value as profit for the creator (not having it all captured by customers, competitors, or complementors)

**Failure modes:**
- Invent a great product → competitors copy it → value captured by customers (not by inventor)
- Create a service → intermediaries capture most of the margin → business is not viable
- Build platform → third parties extract value without contributing proportionally

**How to capture value:**
1. **Intellectual property** — patents, trademarks, trade secrets (harder for services; relevant for branded products)
2. **Complementary assets** — own or control assets that are needed to commercialise the innovation (distribution networks, manufacturing capacity, customer relationships)
3. **First-mover advantage** — move fast enough that switching costs build before competition intensifies
4. **Network effects** — as more users join, the product becomes more valuable for all users
5. **Complexity and tacit knowledge** — embed the value in processes or relationships that cannot be easily observed or copied

### 6. Dynamic Capabilities and Business Model Renewal

Teece's broader body of work (including his 2007 paper on dynamic capabilities) argues that competitive advantage is not static. Firms must continuously **sense, seize, and reconfigure**:

- **Sense** — identify and assess market opportunities and threats (customer needs are changing; technology is shifting; competitors are entering)
- **Seize** — mobilise resources to address those opportunities (invest in new capabilities, adjust the business model)
- **Reconfigure** — transform the business as circumstances change (abandon old models, build new ones)

**Implication for business plans:** A business plan should not assume the current model will remain optimal indefinitely. It should identify the key assumptions that, if they changed, would require the model to evolve — and show how the management team has the capability to adapt.

---

### 7. Conditions That Favour Business Model Innovation

Teece identifies specific conditions where business model innovation is more valuable than product/process innovation alone:

1. **Nascent industries** — where customer habits and competitive structures are not yet fixed
2. **Technological transitions** — where new technology disrupts existing cost or delivery structures
3. **Regulatory changes** — where new rules create space for new models
4. **Shifts in customer preferences** — where existing models no longer match what customers want
5. **Underserved markets** — where conventional models do not serve large segments profitably

**Uganda application:** Uganda's financial sector (mobile money disrupting banking), agriculture (digital extension services disrupting physical input dealers), education (e-learning disrupting physical tuition), health (telemedicine disrupting clinic visits) — all are in conditions that favour business model innovation.

## Business Model Archetypes

Teece classifies recurring business model types:

| Model | Description | Uganda Examples |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Product seller** | Make and sell a physical product | Manufacturing, food processing, artisan crafts |
| **Service provider** | Sell time, expertise, or capability | Consulting, healthcare, legal, construction |
| **Platform/marketplace** | Connect buyers and sellers; take a cut | Jumia, SafeBoda, property listing sites |
| **Subscription** | Recurring fee for continued access | SaaS, magazines, gym membership, security services |
| **Freemium** | Basic free, premium paid | Mobile apps, professional networks |
| **Razor-blade (lock-in)** | Low-cost primary product; high-margin consumables/services | Printers and ink, razors and blades, generators and fuel |
| **Asset-light/franchise** | License brand and system; don't own assets | Fast food franchises, microfinance agent networks |
| **Aggregator** | Aggregate supply and demand; take margin | Agro-dealer networks, transport cooperatives |
| **Usage-based** | Pay per use rather than per unit | Electricity metering, pay-as-you-go solar (M-KOPA) |

## Practical Application to Business Plan Writing

### For Section 03 (Products and Services)
Use Teece's value creation/value capture distinction to:
- Clearly articulate the **value proposition** (what problem does the product/service solve?)
- Explain the **revenue model** (how does the business convert created value into cash?)
- Identify the **appropriability mechanisms** — what stops competitors from copying the offering? (IP, brand, relationships, location, regulatory approvals, exclusive supplier agreements)

### For Section 06 (Competitive Analysis)
Apply the business model vs strategy distinction:
- Who else has a similar business model?
- What is your strategic differentiation within that model?
- Can competitors replicate your model easily, or do you have proprietary elements?

### For Section 10 (Financial Projections)
Revenue model design (Teece):
- Does revenue come per transaction, per subscription, per usage, or per outcome?
- What is the customer lifetime value relative to acquisition cost?
- Are there complementary revenue streams (cross-sells, upsells, data, licensing)?

### For Section 14 (AI Integration)
Teece's dynamic capabilities framework directly applies:
- AI tools represent new capabilities that can be leveraged to sense (market intelligence), seize (operational efficiency), or reconfigure (new delivery models)
- A business plan should show awareness of how AI might disrupt current models and how the management team will respond

## Key Quotations for Business Plan Use

**On business model design:**
*"A business model articulates the logic, the data, and other evidence that support a value proposition for the customer, and a viable structure of revenues and costs for the enterprise delivering that value."*

**On value capture:**
*"Getting the business model right is a precondition for turning technological innovation into commercial success."*

**On innovation:**
*"Business model innovation can itself be a pathway to competitive advantage if the model is sufficiently differentiated and difficult to replicate."*

**On dynamic capabilities:**
*"Firms need to constantly evolve their business models, because competitive environments are never static."*
