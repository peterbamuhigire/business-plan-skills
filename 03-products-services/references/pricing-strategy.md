# Pricing Strategy Reference

> Reference: *Pricing for Profit: How to Develop a Powerful Pricing Strategy for Your Business* by Peter Hill (Kogan Page, 2013)

> Figures adapted to Ugandan context (UGX). Percentages and ratios from the source are universal.

---

## 1. The Five Ways to Grow a Business

Hill's central framework identifies five — and only five — levers that any business can pull to grow profit:

| # | Growth lever | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Generate more leads** | Increase the number of potential customers who enquire |
| 2 | **Improve conversion rate** | Turn a higher proportion of enquiries into paying customers |
| 3 | **Increase transaction value** | Get each customer to spend more per purchase |
| 4 | **Increase purchase frequency** | Get customers to buy more often |
| 5 | **Increase price** | Charge more for the same product or service |

### Why price is the highest-impact lever

Of these five, price improvement flows directly and entirely to the bottom line. The other four all carry incremental costs (marketing spend, sales effort, fulfilment capacity). A price increase has zero incremental cost.

**Mathematical proof (Ugandan worked example)**

| Metric | Current | +5% price | +5% leads |
|---|---|---|---|
| Leads | 200 | 200 | 210 |
| Conversion rate | 50% | 50% | 50% |
| Customers | 100 | 100 | 105 |
| Avg. transaction value (UGX) | 500,000 | 525,000 | 500,000 |
| Revenue (UGX) | 50,000,000 | 52,500,000 | 52,500,000 |
| Variable costs (UGX) | 35,000,000 | 35,000,000 | 36,750,000 |
| **Gross profit (UGX)** | **15,000,000** | **17,500,000** | **15,750,000** |
| **Profit increase** | — | **+16.7%** | **+5.0%** |

A 5% price increase delivers a 16.7% profit increase (at a 30% margin), while a 5% increase in leads delivers only 5.0%. The lower your current margin, the more dramatic the pricing effect becomes.

**Key insight:** Most businesses spend the bulk of their improvement effort on leads and conversion (the hardest, most expensive levers) and almost no time on pricing (the easiest, cheapest lever).

## 2. Cost-Plus Pricing

### What it is

Cost-plus pricing calculates a selling price by adding a fixed percentage mark-up to the cost of goods or service delivery.

`Selling price = Cost + (Cost x Mark-up %)`

**Example:** A Kampala furniture maker's chair costs UGX 180,000 in materials and labour. At a 50% mark-up: UGX 180,000 + 90,000 = UGX 270,000 selling price.

### Why it is common

- Simple to calculate.
- Feels "fair" — every product carries its share of cost recovery.
- Accountants and finance teams default to it.
- Gives a sense of control: "We know we cover costs."

### Fundamental flaws

1. **Ignores customer value.** The customer does not know or care what it cost you. They pay based on perceived value.
2. **Penalises efficiency.** If you reduce costs, your selling price drops even though the value to the customer has not changed.
3. **Rewards waste.** Higher costs lead to higher prices, passing inefficiency to the customer.
4. **Confuses mark-up and margin.** A 50% mark-up is only a 33.3% margin. Many owners overstate their profitability.
5. **Creates a race to the bottom.** Competitors with lower costs always undercut you.

### When cost-plus is acceptable

Use it only as a **floor** — the minimum price below which you must not sell. Then set the actual price based on value.

```
Cost-plus floor:   UGX 270,000   (minimum acceptable)
Value-based price: UGX 450,000   (what the customer will pay)
```

> **Action:** Eliminate cost-plus as your pricing *strategy* within six weeks. Retain it only as a profitability check.

## 3. Value-Based Pricing

### The Value Scales Model

Hill's core model pictures a set of scales:

```
        PRICE                    PERCEIVED VALUE
     (supplier side)            (customer side)
          |                          |
    ======+==========================+======
                    fulcrum
```

| Scenario | Scales tip towards | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Price = Perceived value | Balanced | **Fair deal** — sale likely |
| Price < Perceived value | Customer | **Bargain** — sale very likely |
| Price > Perceived value | Supplier | **No sale** — customer walks away |

### Post-purchase doubt

Even when a sale occurs at a price slightly above perceived value, the customer may experience **post-transaction doubt** — a lingering sense they overpaid. This erodes loyalty and leads to defection. Acceptance of the price does not mean happiness with it.

### Balancing the scales (without discounting)

When scales do not balance, most businesses default to dropping the price. Hill identifies better options:

1. **Explore the gap.** Discuss each side's perception openly to find where differences lie.
2. **Add value elements.** Include features the customer values but that cost you little (training, support, guarantees).
3. **Remove unwanted elements.** Strip out features the customer does not value and reduce the price accordingly.
4. **Communicate hidden value.** Many businesses offer excellent features (same-day delivery, technical expertise, stock availability) but never mention them. Customers cannot value what they do not know about.

### The Price Triangle

When a customer challenges your price, use the Price Triangle:

```
              QUALITY
               / \
              /   \
             /     \
           PRICE ------- SERVICE
           (speed/convenience)
```

The response: *"Which two of these three is most important to you?"*

- Good quality + fast service = cannot also be cheap.
- Good quality + cheap price = cannot also be fast.
- Fast service + cheap price = cannot also be high quality.

This reframes the negotiation from a pure price discussion to a value discussion.

**Ugandan example script (boda-boda courier service):**
> "We can get your package across Kampala in under 2 hours with full tracking and a delivery photo. We can do it cheaper if you are happy to wait until tomorrow's batch run, or we can do it fast and cheap but without the tracking guarantee. Which matters most to you?"

## 4. Tiered Pricing (Gold / Silver / Bronze)

### Psychology of choice architecture

Hill's research shows three critical principles:

1. **Offering choice increases total sales.** A single price forces a yes/no decision. Three tiers shift the question to "which one?" — keeping the customer inside your offering.
2. **Comparison stays internal.** With three options, customers compare your tiers against each other rather than against a competitor.
3. **Natural self-selection.** Some customers always choose the top tier (status, convenience, or indifference to price). Some always choose the bottom (budget constraint or frugal habit). The middle tier captures the majority.

### Optimal price-point spacing

| Principle | Guideline |
|---|---|
| Gap between Bronze and Silver | Larger (e.g. 40-60% above Bronze) |
| Gap between Silver and Gold | Smaller (e.g. 25-40% above Silver) |

**Decoy effect:** Adding a very expensive Platinum option (even if rarely sold) makes Gold appear much better value by comparison.

### How to construct 3-tier packages

**Step 1:** List every feature, service element, and deliverable you currently provide.

**Step 2:** Sort into three columns:

| Bronze (essentials) | Silver (+ convenience) | Gold (+ premium/peace of mind) |
|---|---|---|
| Core product/service | Everything in Bronze, plus: | Everything in Silver, plus: |
| Standard delivery | Faster delivery | Priority/same-day delivery |
| Basic support (email) | Phone support, business hours | Dedicated contact, extended hours |
| Standard warranty | Extended warranty | Full replacement guarantee |
| — | Training/onboarding session | Ongoing consultations |

**Step 3:** Price each tier using value logic, not cost-plus.

**Step 4:** Ensure the Gold-to-Silver price gap is smaller than the Silver-to-Bronze gap.

### Worked example: Ugandan IT support business (Kampala)

| Element | Bronze | Silver | Gold |
|---|---|---|---|
| Monthly device support | Up to 5 devices | Up to 15 devices | Unlimited devices |
| Response time | Next business day | Same day | Within 2 hours |
| Support channel | Email only | Email + phone | Email + phone + WhatsApp |
| On-site visits | None (remote only) | 1 per month | Unlimited |
| Data backup | Manual guidance | Weekly automated | Daily automated + offsite |
| Quarterly review | No | Yes | Yes + strategic IT advice |
| **Monthly price (UGX)** | **250,000** | **450,000** | **600,000** |
| Price uplift vs. Bronze | — | +80% | +140% (+33% vs. Silver) |

Note the Silver-to-Gold gap (UGX 150,000) is smaller than the Bronze-to-Silver gap (UGX 200,000), nudging Silver customers toward Gold.

## 5. Bundling and Cross-Selling

### Four ways to maximise each sale

1. **Sell greater volume** of the intended item (e.g. "buy the 5-litre tin — bring back the unused portion for a refund").
2. **Sell complementary products** at the same time (e.g. phone case + screen protector with a smartphone).
3. **Up-sell to a higher specification** (e.g. the Gold tier instead of Silver).
4. **Simply charge more** for the same item.

### Bundle pricing psychology

- **Bundling favours the seller.** Customers assess total value rather than scrutinising each line item.
- **Unbundling (menu pricing) favours the buyer.** It allows line-by-line cherry-picking.
- **Hybrid approach:** Show individual prices to demonstrate value, then offer the bundle at a discount to the sum of parts.

### The Price of Pain hierarchy

When a customer resists the price, rank your concession options from *least* painful to *most* painful for your business:

| Option | Customer pleasure | Your pain (cost) |
|---|---|---|
| Better selling — explain features/benefits | Greater confidence in fairness | Minimal (extra time only) |
| Free training or onboarding | Bonus perceived value | Low (labour, ~UGX 50,000) |
| Free service/maintenance agreement | Bonus ongoing support | Moderate (labour, ~UGX 100,000) |
| Free complementary products | Bonus goods | Higher (cost of goods, ~UGX 150,000) |
| **Cash discount off price** | **Money saved** | **Maximum — pound-for-pound loss** |

> **Rule:** Always start at the top of this list. Discounting the price should be the absolute last resort.

### When to bundle vs. unbundle

| Bundle when... | Unbundle when... |
|---|---|
| You want to increase average transaction value | Customers have very different needs |
| Items naturally complement each other | Individual items have strong standalone value |
| You want to obscure high-margin components | Transparency builds trust in your market |
| Competitor comparison is a threat | Customers demand line-item quotes (e.g. government tenders) |

## 6. The Hidden Cost of Discounting

### Margin erosion mathematics

A discount falls directly off profit, not off revenue. If your margin is 30% and you give a 10% discount:

- You have not lost 10% of your profit — you have lost **33%** of your profit.
- Revenue drops from UGX 500,000 to UGX 450,000 but costs remain at UGX 350,000.
- Profit drops from UGX 150,000 to UGX 100,000.

### Discount / volume breakeven table

**"How much additional volume do you need to sell just to get back to the same profit?"**

| Discount given | 20% margin | 30% margin | 40% margin | 50% margin | 60% margin |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2% | +11% | +7% | +5% | +4% | +3% |
| 5% | +33% | +20% | +14% | +11% | +9% |
| 10% | +100% | +50% | +33% | +25% | +20% |
| 15% | +300% | +100% | +60% | +43% | +33% |
| 20% | Impossible | +200% | +100% | +67% | +50% |
| 25% | Impossible | Impossible | +167% | +100% | +71% |
| 30% | Impossible | Impossible | +300% | +150% | +100% |

**How to read:** At a 30% gross margin, a 10% discount requires you to sell **50% more volume** just to return to the same gross profit. A 15% discount requires **double** the volume. A 20% discount requires **triple**.

> At typical Ugandan SME margins of 20-30%, even a 5% discount demands a 20-33% volume surge to break even. That volume surge almost never materialises.

### Two valid reasons to discount

1. **As a presentation tool** — when you have pre-inflated the list price to allow for the discount (the "was / now" technique). The discount is illusory.
2. **When your price is genuinely wrong** — based on proper market research, not guesswork or one customer's complaint.

## 7. Pricing Psychology

### Round sums vs. charm pricing

| Approach | Example (UGX) | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Round sum | 500,000 | Feels arbitrary, negotiable. Customers assume padding. |
| Charm pricing (ending in 9) | 499,000 | Feels "just under" a threshold. Works for small retail items. |
| Precise pricing | 487,300 | Feels carefully calculated. Customers trust it more and negotiate less. |

**Hill's finding:** For B2B and high-value items, precise prices (e.g. UGX 4,873,000 rather than UGX 5,000,000) dramatically reduce price challenges. The specificity signals that the price was properly computed, not invented.

### Handwritten vs. printed

The number 7 tested as the most "non-confrontational" and trusted digit in pricing experiments. One construction firm added 7 to the end of every quote (e.g. UGX 10,007,000 instead of UGX 10,000,000) and saw significantly improved quote conversion rates.

### Anchoring

The first price a customer sees becomes their reference point. Always present the highest-priced option first (Gold before Silver before Bronze). This makes every subsequent option feel like better value.

### Decoy pricing

Add an option that is intentionally unattractive to make the target option look superior:

| Option | Price (UGX) | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Basic website | 2,000,000 | Too bare — pushes buyers up |
| Standard website | 3,500,000 | **Target option** — best perceived value |
| Premium website | 3,800,000 | Decoy — barely more than Standard but positioned as "premium" |
| Enterprise website | 8,000,000 | Anchor — makes Standard look like a bargain |

### Price presentation methods

| Presentation | Underlying message |
|---|---|
| "The price is UGX 500,000" | Take it or leave it |
| "Was UGX 800,000, now UGX 500,000" | Great deal — you save UGX 300,000 |
| "This week only: UGX 500,000" | Worth more, but act now |
| "Your investment: UGX 500,000" | Long-term value, not a cost |
| "Our price: UGX 500,000" | Implies competitors may be higher |

> **Tip:** Use "investment" or "fee" for professional services. Use "today's price" to create urgency. Avoid "about" or "approximately" — vagueness destroys trust and leads to no-sale.

## 8. Competitive Pricing

### Why cheapest rarely wins

Hill demonstrates through multiple case studies that customers cite price as the reason for not buying far more often than it is the actual reason. The real drivers of lost sales are typically:

- Poor follow-up on quotes (no chase after 3 days).
- Failure to communicate features and benefits.
- Slow response times.
- Lack of trust or relationship.

### Aspirational purchasing

People buy for what a product helps them *become*, not merely for what it *does*. A Ugandan business owner buying a UGX 3,000,000 suit is buying credibility and confidence in investor meetings, not fabric.

### Value-added elements that justify premium pricing

- Stock availability / immediate fulfilment.
- Technical expertise and advice.
- Credit terms or flexible payment.
- Guarantees and warranties.
- Training and after-sales support.
- Convenience of location or delivery.

## 9. Directional Pricing

### Concept

Use price differentials to **steer** customers towards the products or services you *want* them to buy — typically those with higher margins, greater lifetime value, or strategic importance.

### Reasons to steer

| Objective | Method |
|---|---|
| Clear excess stock | Temporarily reduce price on overstocked item |
| Up-sell to higher margin | Narrow the price gap between low and high options |
| Increase lifetime value | Discount the entry product, profit on consumables/service |
| Shift from low-margin to high-margin item | Raise price on low-margin item to make the high-margin alternative more attractive |

### Worked example: Ugandan restaurant menu

| Item | Cost (UGX) | Selling price | Profit | Margin | % of sales |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Small chapati wrap | 2,900 | 5,000 | 2,100 | 42% | 50% |
| Large chapati wrap | 3,200 | 7,000 | 3,800 | 54% | 50% |

The large wrap costs only UGX 300 more to produce but sells for UGX 2,000 more. To steer customers toward the large:

**Option A:** Raise the small wrap to UGX 5,500 — makes the large look much better value for only UGX 1,500 more.

**Option B:** Add free extra filling to the large wrap (cost UGX 200) — differentiates value visibly.

**Option C:** Bundle the large wrap with a drink at UGX 8,500 (drink costs UGX 500, normally sells at UGX 1,500) — total profit UGX 4,800 per bundle.

> **Key principle:** It costs almost the same to produce small and large versions of many products. Direct customers to the larger/premium version where marginal cost is low but marginal revenue is high.

## 10. Implementing Price Changes

### Customer segmentation (A / B / C grading)

Grade every customer by total profit contribution:

| Grade | Description | Pricing approach |
|---|---|---|
| **A-grade** | Top 20% of customers by profit | Protect the relationship. Personal visit to explain changes. Offer value-adds. |
| **B-grade** | Middle 60% | Communicate changes clearly. Offer payment flexibility. |
| **C-grade** | Bottom 20% — low profit, high maintenance | Implement increases first. If they leave, accept it. |

> **Test price increases on C-grade customers first.** They generate the least profit, so the risk is lowest. Use their reactions to refine your approach before rolling out to A-grade clients.

### Payment options that ease price acceptance

- Instalments over 3-6 months (especially for amounts above UGX 1,000,000).
- Mobile money payments (MTN MoMo / Airtel Money) — the transaction feels less "real" than handing over cash.
- "Pay on completion" for service businesses — aligns payment with value delivery.
- Early-payment discount (e.g. 2% for payment within 7 days) — costs you less than a blanket discount.

### Handling objections — the scripted approach

**Customer:** "Your price is too high."

**Response framework:**
1. Acknowledge: "I understand — price is an important consideration."
2. Reframe: "Let me show you what is included in that price..."
3. Triangle: "Which two matter most — quality, speed, or price?"
4. Evidence: "Our last client in [similar situation] found that..."
5. Options: "I can offer our Silver package at [lower price] if you are comfortable with [reduced scope]."
6. Silence: After presenting the price, stop talking. Let the customer respond.

### Building pricing confidence in sales teams

| Problem | Solution |
|---|---|
| Fear of rejection | Role-play pricing conversations weekly |
| Bad-experience bias | Document the ratio: complaints vs. total sales (usually <1%) |
| Defaulting to discounts | Remove authority to discount on the spot; require 24-hour approval |
| Round-sum discounting | Ban multiples of 5%. Force specific figures (e.g. 17.4% not 20%) |
| Cost-focused selling | Remove cost data from sales materials; train on value language |

### Discount policy statement (adapt for your business)

> *"Our prices have been carefully determined to enable us to deliver high-quality products, exceptional service, and genuine value for money — while making a fair profit. Further discounts would only be possible by reducing quality or service standards, which we are not prepared to do. Our prices are already the very best we can offer."*

## 11. Pricing Action Checklist

Adapted from Hill's 42 actions, condensed to the most impactful items for a Ugandan SME.

### Immediate (this week)

- [ ] **Raise prices by 5% across the board.** Announce to your team tomorrow. Effective date: first of next month.
- [ ] **Audit your current pricing method.** Is it cost-plus, last year + inflation, or best-guess? Document it honestly.
- [ ] **List your top 10 products/services by profit contribution.** Focus all pricing work here first.

### Short-term (next 4 weeks)

- [ ] **Assemble a pricing team** — include sales, finance, and operations. Meet fortnightly.
- [ ] **Compile a competitor intelligence file** — brochures, websites, quoted prices. Assign one person to maintain it.
- [ ] **Create Gold / Silver / Bronze packages** for your top 3 services. Test on a few trusted customers.
- [ ] **List all hidden value features** (delivery, expertise, guarantees, availability). Ensure every salesperson communicates them.
- [ ] **Eliminate round-sum discounts.** Ban 5%, 10%, 15%, 20%. Force precise figures.
- [ ] **Remove cost data from sales materials.** Train salespeople to sell value, not cost.

### Medium-term (next 3 months)

- [ ] **Grade customers A / B / C** by profit. Implement targeted price increases starting with C-grade.
- [ ] **Develop a value strategy for price objections** — a ranked list of concessions from least to most painful.
- [ ] **Train all customer-facing staff** on the Price Triangle, Value Scales, and objection-handling scripts.
- [ ] **Add a Platinum option** to your top package — priced 50%+ above Gold. Its purpose is to make Gold look like excellent value.
- [ ] **Review quote management process.** Track conversion rates. Chase every quote within 3 days.
- [ ] **Set up payment options** — instalments, mobile money, early-payment incentives.

### Ongoing (quarterly)

- [ ] **Review the discount/volume breakeven table** against actual discounts given. Flag any product sold below margin.
- [ ] **Update competitor intelligence.** Adapt your value communication accordingly.
- [ ] **Revisit tier pricing and spacing.** Adjust based on customer uptake data.
- [ ] **Run pricing workshops** with external peers from non-competing businesses for fresh perspectives.
- [ ] **Measure and report:** average discount %, quote conversion rate, revenue per customer, and profit per product.
