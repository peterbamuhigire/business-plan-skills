> References: *The Portable MBA in Strategy* ed. Fahey & Randall (Wiley, 2000); *The Strategist's Toolkit* by Harris & Lenox (Darden, 2013); *Strategy Plain and Simple* by Evans (Pearson, 2018)

## 1. Value Chain Design

**Source:** Portable MBA Ch 3 — Gupta

The value chain is the set of interrelated activities, from upstream to downstream, that a business carries out — alone and in concert with suppliers, customers, and partners — to convert inputs into outputs. Its design has a direct and determining impact on customer value, competitive advantage, and shareholder value.

### Two Design Decisions

1. **Value chain architecture** — the basic value-creation system through which inputs become outputs.
2. **Internalisation vs outsourcing** — which activities the business performs itself and which it delegates to partners.

### Designing the Architecture

**Three criteria for choosing an architecture:**

| Criterion | Key Question |
|---|---|
| Effectiveness & efficiency | Will this architecture let us market, sell, and deliver to target customers in the most effective and efficient manner? |
| Flexibility | Will it let us scale up/down, expand/contract the product bundle, and switch to a superior architecture if needed? |
| Defensibility | Will it be difficult for competitors to imitate and neutralise? |

These criteria often conflict, forcing trade-offs. New technologies regularly enable radically superior architectures.

### Internalisation vs Outsourcing

The guiding principle: do not perform any activity that other firms can do more effectively and efficiently. However, outsourcing introduces three risks:

| Risk | Description | Safeguard |
|---|---|---|
| Non-performance | Partner's capabilities decline or priorities shift | Develop alternative suppliers; maintain partial in-house capability; take equity stake in supplier |
| Value expropriation | Partners skim most profits, leaving you marginally profitable | Retain critical non-substitutable activities; keep upgrading your competencies; acquire early equity stakes |
| Elimination | Partners squeeze you out of the chain entirely | Ensure your activities remain critical, superior, non-substitutable, and inimitable |

### Managing the Value Chain

Three management imperatives once the chain is designed:

1. **Excellence in individual activities** — maximise customer value, minimise cost, minimise asset investment by understanding the "drivers" of each activity.
2. **Internal integration across activities** — link product design, manufacturing, marketing, service, and distribution so that insights flow between them. This improves quality, slashes delivery cycle time, and reduces development cycle time.
3. **External integration** — tighten communication with supplier and customer value chains through electronic links, face-to-face interaction, and physical proximity.

### Application to a Business Plan

When describing your products/services delivery:

```
For each major activity in your value chain, state:
- Activity name (e.g. sourcing, production, distribution)
- Performed in-house or outsourced? Why?
- Key cost drivers
- How it contributes to customer value
- How it integrates with adjacent activities
```

**Ugandan example:** A Kampala-based coffee processor might design its value chain as: smallholder sourcing (in-house field agents) → wet processing (owned washing station) → dry milling (outsourced to specialist in Mbale) → export logistics (outsourced to freight forwarder) → direct sales to European roasters (in-house). The outsourcing of dry milling is justified because the specialist's equipment and throughput yield a lower cost per kilogram than building an in-house facility at UGX 800 million capital expenditure.

## 2. Core Competencies

**Source:** Portable MBA Ch 11 — Prahalad, Fahey & Randall

### What Is a Core Competency?

A core competency is more than a single technology or skill. It always consists of some combination of:

- **Multiple technologies** (hard and soft)
- **Collective learning** (multi-level, multi-functional)
- **Capacity to share** (across business and geographical boundaries)

The formula:

> **Competence = Technology x Governance Process x Collective Learning**

This multiplicative relationship means investment in technology alone will be under-leveraged without strong governance and learning culture.

### Composition of Competencies

1. **People-embodied knowledge** — both tacit (intuitive, experience-based) and explicit (codified, transferable).
2. **Capital-embodied knowledge** — both proprietary (developed in-house) and vendor-based (acquired from partners).

### How Core Competencies Differ from Simple Capabilities

| | Capability | Core Competency |
|---|---|---|
| Scope | Often unique to one function or business unit | Permeates the whole organisation |
| Competitive effect | "Table stakes" — necessary to play | Differential advantage — necessary to win |
| Imitability | Can often be acquired or copied | Difficult to imitate because it bundles technologies, customer knowledge, and organisational learning |
| Example | Just-in-time delivery (prerequisite for tier-1 auto suppliers) | Sony's miniaturisation (creative bundling of microprocessors, power management, packaging, ergonomics, and lifestyle insight) |

### Identifying Core Competencies: Three Tests

1. **Differentiation** — Does the competence generate distinct value and benefits for customers?
2. **Transcendence** — Does it cover a range of businesses, both current and new?
3. **Inimitability** — Is it hard for competitors to replicate?

Few firms sustain more than five or six core competencies.

### Leveraging Competencies for Growth

Five key management tasks (Prahalad):

1. **Absorb new knowledge** — recruit people with new skills; create legitimacy for new knowledge streams.
2. **Integrate knowledge streams** — set up cross-disciplinary project teams as carriers of new learning.
3. **Share across boundaries** — build cross-cultural and cross-unit collaboration skills.
4. **Learn to forget** — abandon dominant logic that worked in the past but constrains future development.
5. **Deploy across business units** — align administrative systems to support competence sharing.

### Application to a Business Plan

When describing what makes your business distinctive:

```
Core Competency Audit:
1. Name the competency
2. Technologies involved
3. Knowledge and skills bundled
4. How it creates customer value
5. Which products/services it underpins
6. Can competitors imitate it? Why not?
7. How will you invest in developing it further?
```

**Ugandan example:** A fintech firm in Kampala might identify its core competency as "mobile-first financial product design for unbanked populations" — bundling mobile UI/UX design, agent network management, local language localisation, and deep understanding of informal savings behaviour. This competency underpins its mobile wallet, micro-lending, and crop insurance products, and is difficult for traditional banks to replicate because it requires grassroots customer knowledge they lack.

## 3. Capabilities Analysis

**Source:** Harris & Lenox Ch 7

### What It Is

Capabilities analysis clarifies the major sets of activities, skills, and resources that drive value to customers. It is based on the resource-based view (RBV) of strategy.

### Tangible vs Intangible Assets

| Asset Type | Examples | Strategic Role |
|---|---|---|
| Tangible | Equipment, property, cash, inventory | Necessary but rarely sufficient for sustained advantage |
| Intangible | Tacit market understanding, supplier relationships, efficient back-end processes, brand reputation | Often the true building blocks of competitive advantage — harder to copy |

A true capability is the repeatable, tacit set of elements that enables the firm to perform consistently — not a one-off success.

### The Four-Step Process

**Step 1. Determine the value chain.**

Map activities that create value, working backwards from the customer (e.g. Component Supply → R&D → Production → Sales & Distribution → Service & Support). Identify key players, market shares, and bargaining power at each stage.

**Step 2. Isolate core capabilities.**

Most firms have only 3–6 capabilities that are truly central to customer value creation. Capabilities comprise three knowledge-based resources:

- **Processes** — codified routines; identify the few that account for the bulk of cost, time, and quality outcomes.
- **People** — critical skill areas most central to value creation and hardest to replace (focus on "A positions" not just "A players").
- **Systems/Technologies** — information systems, databases, proprietary technologies with operational, relational, and transformational impacts.

**Step 3. Determine alignment.**

- *Internal:* Are people, processes, and systems mutually reinforcing?
- *External:* Are capabilities aligned with the customer value proposition (product leadership, operational excellence, or customer intimacy)?

**Step 4. Determine sustainability.**

- **Imitability** — Can others copy it? Limiting factors: scarcity, social complexity, causal ambiguity.
- **Durability** — How long before it degrades? Consider patent expiry, talent attrition, technology obsolescence.

### Core vs Non-Core: Outsourcing Decisions

Activities that are only "table stakes" (necessary to play but not to win) are candidates for outsourcing or external partnering. Core capabilities — those that differentiate you — should be protected and invested in.

### SWOT Integration

Capabilities analysis feeds directly into the strengths and weaknesses components of SWOT. Strengths are capabilities that are valuable, rare, and hard to imitate. Weaknesses are capability gaps relative to what the chosen strategy requires.

### Application to a Business Plan

```
Capability Worksheet (per capability):
- Capability name:
- Core processes involved:
- Critical people/skills:
- Supporting systems/technologies:
- Internal alignment: [strong / moderate / weak]
- External alignment with value proposition: [yes / partial / no]
- Imitability: [high / moderate / low]
- Durability: [years]
- Core or non-core?
- If non-core, outsourcing candidate? To whom?
```

**Ugandan example:** A logistics firm operating the Kampala–Juba corridor might identify "cold-chain reliability" as a core capability — combining GPS-tracked refrigerated trucks (systems), trained drivers with border-crossing expertise (people), and a real-time temperature-monitoring protocol (process). This capability is hard to imitate because it took three years and UGX 2.4 billion in investment to build, and competitors lack the driver relationships and border-agent networks.

## 4. Competitive Advantage Through Products & Services

**Sources:** Portable MBA Ch 3 (Gupta); Harris & Lenox Ch 7

### How Products/Services Create Value

Competitive advantage exists at two levels:

- **Onstage** — what customers see: price, quality, delivery, features, image.
- **Backstage** — the resources and competencies that produce onstage advantages.

Onstage advantage cannot be sustained without backstage superiority.

### Five Principles of Competitive Advantage

1. **Think in relative terms** — advantage is measured against competitors, not in absolute terms.
2. **Be explicit about advantages AND disadvantages** — net advantage is what matters.
3. **Strive for multiple sources** — more layers of advantage = more sustainable position.
4. **Align onstage and backstage** — ensure internal capabilities support external claims.
5. **Expect the terms to shift** — customer priorities change; competitors achieve parity on yesterday's success factors.

### Linking Capabilities to Customer Needs

Analyse the entire customer purchase and consumption cycle — from realising the need, through choosing, ordering, paying, receiving, using, maintaining, and eventually replacing. Each stage is a potential differentiation point.

### Differentiation Sources

Differentiation is possible in any product, even apparent commodities. Not all differentiation increases cost. Sources: product performance, customisation, speed, service quality, brand image, customer relationships, convenience.

**Ugandan example:** Two Kampala-based printing firms might both offer business-card printing. Firm A competes on price (UGX 50,000 for 500 cards). Firm B charges UGX 80,000 but offers same-day delivery, a free design consultation, and WhatsApp-based order tracking. Firm B's multiple differentiation points (speed, service, convenience) command a premium and are harder for Firm A to replicate than a simple price cut.

## 5. Small Business Strategy

**Source:** Portable MBA Ch 5 — Duhaime

### Why Strategy Matters More for Small Firms

**Vulnerabilities:** little room for error; less negotiating power with suppliers and channels; dangerous dependencies on a single product, outlet, or customer group.

**Advantages:** closeness to customers (observe change faster); mind-set flexibility (fewer bureaucratic layers); marketplace agility (respond instantly to emerging change).

### SWOT for Small Firms

Key principles:

- **Strengths and weaknesses** must be assessed relative to rivals (including emerging ones), not your own history.
- **Opportunities and threats** should include analysis of industry fragmentation — highly fragmented industries that cannot easily be dominated by large players are more attractive for small firms.
- Always ask: How can we translate strengths into customer value? What customer needs represent opportunities?

### Focus and Niche Strategies

Most small businesses find that focus/niche strategies capitalise on their strengths and offer the best opportunities for success.

**Key questions for niche selection:**

1. What market segment can we serve?
2. What makes that segment attractive for our organisation?
3. How can we leverage our strengths to win in that segment?
4. How do we protect ourselves from current and potential rivals?

**Important warning:** Your most direct competitors are often other small firms like you. Do not focus your marketing strategy on why you are better than the large players — focus on why you are better than similar small firms serving the same niche.

### Value Chain Analysis for Small Businesses

Small firms should perform value chain analysis to assess costs and value added at each stage. Key questions:

- What are the key activities in our value chain?
- What costs are associated with each?
- What customer benefits accrue from each?
- How can we integrate activities to add further value?
- How can we manage our human and other resources to maximise value creation?

Small firms in traditional businesses face fundamental value chain shifts. Internet-based competitors may aim to reshape any activity in the overall chain. This is both a threat and an opportunity — small firms focused on being the best at one activity can outperform large vertically integrated rivals.

### When to Specialise

Specialise when:

- The industry has high segmentation of market demand.
- Small pockets of demand allow you to avoid head-to-head competition with the largest players.
- You can stay close to vendors, channels, and end-customers to continually adapt.
- Your limited resources would be spread too thin by a broad approach.

**Ugandan example:** A small Jinja-based food processor should not try to compete with large Kampala manufacturers across all product lines. Instead, it might specialise in dried fruit snacks for the school-canteen segment — a niche where its proximity to fruit farms (lower transport costs at UGX 200/kg vs UGX 450/kg from Kampala), relationships with local schools, and ability to adapt flavours to regional tastes give it advantages that national competitors cannot easily match.

## 6. Stakeholder Value Mapping

**Source:** Harris & Lenox Ch 6

### What It Is

Stakeholder analysis is a framework for understanding the key individuals, groups, or organisations that can significantly affect (or be affected by) the firm's activities. A focus on stakeholder interests clarifies what kind of value each stakeholder is looking for.

It is ultimately untenable to create value for one stakeholder by shortchanging another. Successful firms align stakeholder interests.

### Three-Step Process

**Step 1. Identify stakeholders.**

Create a stakeholder map distinguishing:

- **Primary stakeholders** — vital to continued growth (customers, employees, financiers, suppliers). Remove any of these and the business cannot sustain long-term advantage.
- **Secondary stakeholders** — can affect relationships between the firm and primary stakeholders (government, media, community groups, competitors, special interest groups).

**Step 2. Analyse stakeholder interests.**

Build a stakeholder priorities matrix:

| Issue | Employees | Customers | Government | Community | Shareholders |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Product safety | 3 | 1 | 1 | 1 | 3 |
| Job fulfilment | 1 | 5 | 5 | 3 | 5 |
| Financial returns | 3 | 5 | 5 | 5 | 1 |
| Environmental impact | 3 | 3 | 1 | 1 | 5 |

*(1 = critical importance; 5 = not very important)*

**Step 3. Assess impact of strategic actions.**

For each strategic alternative under consideration, evaluate the potential positive or negative impact on each stakeholder group. This facilitates "weighing out" different interests and can reveal innovative ways to align them.

### Application to a Business Plan

When describing your product/service offerings, show how they create value for multiple stakeholders:

```
Stakeholder Value Map:
- Customers: [specific value delivered]
- Employees: [skills development, fair wages, meaningful work]
- Suppliers: [reliable orders, fair payment terms]
- Community: [local employment, environmental stewardship]
- Investors: [return on investment, growth trajectory]
- Government: [tax revenue, regulatory compliance, sector development]
```

**Ugandan example:** A solar energy company in Gulu serving off-grid communities should map: customers (reliable lighting, phone charging, reduced kerosene spend of UGX 45,000/month); local technicians employed as installers (steady income, skills training); government (rural electrification targets, tax revenue); community (reduced indoor air pollution, children able to study after dark); investors (growing off-grid market, recurring revenue from pay-as-you-go subscriptions).

## 7. Capability Gap Analysis

**Source:** Evans Ch 2

### What It Is

Capability gap analysis identifies the distance between your firm's current capabilities and those needed to become the ideal player in your marketplace. It is undertaken in four stages.

### Stage 1: Envision Future Scenarios

Brainstorm how the marketplace might evolve over 3–5 years. Go beyond the most likely outcomes to include less expected but plausible developments. Under each scenario, ask: which success factors become more prominent?

### Stage 2: Profile the Ideal Player

Define the ideal player as the one achieving the highest possible rating against each success factor — today's and those emerging under future scenarios.

### Stage 3: Stretch Your Sights

Do you want to become a *good* player, a *strong* player, or a *leader*? Remember that goalposts shift — the ideal player in five years will have a different capability mix. Three traps to avoid:

| Trap | Description |
|---|---|
| Ideal Player of Yesterday Tomorrow | You are great today but fail to develop the extra capabilities customers will need tomorrow |
| Ideal Player of Tomorrow Today | You develop capabilities before customers need them — premature investment |
| Ideal Player of Tomorrow | The target — capabilities aligned with tomorrow's success factors, developed in time |

### Stage 4: Specify the Capability Gap

Revisit competitive position ratings against each success factor. Any shortfall below the ideal represents a capability gap. Set realistic but stretched targets and express them with active verbs (e.g. "improve distribution", "lower production costs").

**Key distinction:** *Targeting* the gap = specifying what needs to improve. *Bridging* the gap = choosing the strategic option to achieve it.

### Bridging the Gap

Once capability gaps are specified, identify profit growth options to bridge them:

| Gap Type | Short-term Options | Long-term Options |
|---|---|---|
| Segments for investment | Marketing push; lower pricing to gain share | Invest in assets, business processes, staff & training |
| Segments for holding | Reduce variable cost; tweak pricing | Reduce variable cost; recompete; form alliances |
| Segments for entry | Prepare robust project plan | Leverage existing strengths into related segments |
| All segments | Benchmark overhead; marketing | Reduce overhead; redesign processes; outsource; invest in core competences |

### Application to a Business Plan

```
Capability Gap Assessment:
1. Success factor:
2. Current rating (1-5):
3. Ideal player rating (5):
4. Target rating in 3 years:
5. Gap to bridge:
6. Bridging option (short-term):
7. Bridging option (long-term):
8. Estimated investment required:
```

**Ugandan example:** A Kampala hotel targeting business travellers rates itself 3/5 on "reliable high-speed internet" against an ideal of 5/5. The capability gap of 2 points requires: short-term — negotiate upgraded bandwidth with current ISP (UGX 3.6 million/month); long-term — invest in fibre-optic connection and backup satellite link (UGX 48 million capital). Without bridging this gap, the hotel will lose corporate clients to competitors who offer seamless connectivity for video conferencing.

## 8. Resource-Based Strategy

**Sources:** Evans Ch 2; Harris & Lenox Ch 7

The resource-based view (RBV) asserts that sustained competitive advantage comes from resources and capabilities that are difficult for competitors to replicate.

### VRIO Framework

A resource provides sustained competitive advantage only if it passes four tests:

| Test | Question | If "No" |
|---|---|---|
| **Valuable** | Does it enable the firm to exploit opportunities or neutralise threats? | Competitive disadvantage |
| **Rare** | Is it controlled by only a small number of firms? | Competitive parity only |
| **Inimitable** | Is it costly or difficult for competitors to obtain or develop? | Temporary advantage only |
| **Organised** | Is the firm organised to capture the value of this resource? | Unrealised potential |

Factors limiting imitation: scarcity, social complexity, causal ambiguity, and path dependence.

### Building Strategically Valuable Resources

For a resource to be strategically valuable (Collis & Montgomery, cited in Evans), it must be: (1) a distinctive competence — done better than others; (2) hard to copy; (3) long-lasting; (4) hard to appropriate (not lost when staff leave); (5) unlikely to be substituted in the medium term.

### Three Investment Strategies

1. **Invest in those you have** — deepen and strengthen existing strategically valuable resources.
2. **Leverage those you have** — deploy existing resources into adjacent markets or product categories.
3. **Upgrade those you should have** — build new resources that will be strategically valuable in the future.

**Caution:** Resource-building must be done with sharp awareness of the dynamic market context. Successful companies are not those with the greatest resources, but those that make best use of what they have.

### Application to a Business Plan

```
VRIO Assessment:
Resource/Capability:
- Valuable? [Yes/No — explain]
- Rare? [Yes/No — how many competitors have it?]
- Inimitable? [Yes/No — what makes it hard to copy?]
- Organised? [Yes/No — do we have systems to exploit it?]
→ Competitive implication: [disadvantage / parity / temporary advantage / sustained advantage]
→ Investment plan: [invest / leverage / upgrade]
```

**Ugandan example:** A Ugandan craft brewery's VRIO assessment of its "locally sourced ingredient network":
- *Valuable?* Yes — enables unique flavour profiles using Ugandan coffee, vanilla, and passion fruit, commanding UGX 8,000 premium per bottle.
- *Rare?* Yes — only two other craft breweries in Uganda, neither with comparable farmer relationships.
- *Inimitable?* Moderately — took four years to build trusted relationships with 35 smallholder farmers in Kasese; competitors would need similar time and local knowledge.
- *Organised?* Yes — dedicated sourcing manager, quality-testing protocols, and seasonal supply contracts in place.
- *Implication:* Sustained competitive advantage.
- *Investment plan:* Invest (expand farmer network to Bushenyi district); leverage (launch a "single-origin" seasonal range).
