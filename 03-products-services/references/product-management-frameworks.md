# Product Management Frameworks

> Reference: *Practical Product Management for Product Owners* by Verwijs, Overeem & Lennartz (Scrum.org, 2023)

> Adapted to Ugandan business plan context. Frameworks are universal.

---

## 1. Product Owner Stances Model

Six stances a product owner (or business founder) should adopt, mapped to business plan sections:

| Stance | Focus | Business Plan Section |
|---|---|---|
| **Customer Representative** | Understanding customer needs, personas, value | 05 Target Market, 03 Products & Services |
| **Visionary** | Product vision, roadmap, goals | 02 Company Overview, 03 Products & Services |
| **Experimenter** | Testing assumptions, innovation, pivots | 03 Products & Services (R&D), 12 Risk Analysis |
| **Decision Maker** | Prioritisation, trade-offs, accountability | 09 Management Team |
| **Collaborator** | Governance, budgeting, contracts | 10 Financial Projections, 11 Funding |
| **Influencer** | Stakeholder management, communication | 01 Executive Summary, 09 Management Team |

## 2. Customer Representative Stance

### Identifying Customer Value

Four layers of value (Verwijs et al, adapted from Bain & Company):

| Layer | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Functional** | Saves time, reduces cost, simplifies, integrates | Faster delivery, lower price, fewer steps |
| **Emotional** | Reduces anxiety, provides comfort, rewards | Peace of mind, enjoyment, belonging |
| **Life-changing** | Provides hope, self-actualisation, motivation | Education access, health improvement |
| **Social impact** | Self-transcendence, community benefit | Environmental sustainability, job creation |

**Business plan tip:** Map each product feature to at least one value layer. Investors respond to products that operate on multiple layers.

### Connecting Features to Outcomes

```
Company Goal → Impact on Market → Customer Outcome → Product Feature
```

Work backwards from the company goal:

1. **Goal**: Become the largest agritech platform in East Africa
2. **Impact**: 100,000 farmers using the platform by Year 3
3. **Outcome**: Farmers earn 20% more for their produce
4. **Features**: Price transparency tool, quality grading system, direct buyer connection

**Business plan application:** The R&D pipeline should show this chain for every planned feature.

## 3. Visionary Stance

### Product Vision Framework

Elements of an inspiring product vision:

| Element | Question | Example (Ugandan food processing) |
|---|---|---|
| **For** | Who is this for? | Smallholder farmers in Western Uganda |
| **Who** | What need do they have? | Who lose 30% of harvest to post-harvest waste |
| **The product** | What is it? | A solar-powered drying and packaging system |
| **That** | What key benefit? | That extends shelf life from 3 days to 6 months |
| **Unlike** | What is the alternative? | Unlike traditional sun-drying which is weather-dependent |
| **Our product** | What differentiates? | Our system includes quality certification for export markets |

### Product Vision Template (for business plans)

```
For [target customer]
Who [statement of need/opportunity]
The [product name] is a [product category]
That [key benefit, compelling reason to buy]
Unlike [primary competitive alternative]
Our product [primary differentiation statement]
```

### Connecting Vision to Mission and Values

| Level | Time Horizon | Answers | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mission** | Ongoing | Why do we exist? | To eliminate post-harvest food waste in East Africa |
| **Vision** | 5-10 years | Where are we going? | The leading agritech platform connecting 1M farmers to markets |
| **Product vision** | 2-3 years | What will the product become? | An end-to-end harvest-to-market solution |
| **Product goals** | Quarterly | What are we achieving now? | Onboard 500 farmers in Mbale district |

## 4. Product Roadmap Types

### Which Roadmap for Which Audience

| Roadmap Type | Best For | Level of Detail | Audience |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Goal-oriented** | Strategic alignment | Low — goals, not features | Investors, board, executive summary |
| **Now-Next-Later** | Agile teams, startups | Medium — themes by timeframe | Team, advisors |
| **User Story Map** | Development planning | High — detailed features | Development team |
| **Visual roadmap** | External communication | Low — milestones and themes | Customers, partners |
| **Gantt chart** | Regulatory/construction | High — dates and dependencies | Operations, implementation timeline |

### Goal-Oriented Roadmap Template (for business plans)

| Quarter | Goal | Key Metric | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Q1 Year 1 | Validate product-market fit | 50 paying customers | Development |
| Q2 Year 1 | Achieve operational breakeven | Revenue covers variable costs | Planning |
| Q3-Q4 Year 1 | Scale to first market | 200 customers, UGX 50M revenue | Planning |
| Year 2 | Expand to 2 districts | 1,000 customers, UGX 300M revenue | Concept |
| Year 3 | Regional expansion | 5,000 customers, UGX 1.5B revenue | Vision |

### Now-Next-Later Roadmap

| Now (This Quarter) | Next (Next Quarter) | Later (6+ Months) |
|---|---|---|
| Core product features | Integration with mobile money | AI-powered recommendations |
| Pilot with 10 customers | Expand to 50 customers | Enter second market |
| Manual operations | Semi-automated processes | Full automation |

## 5. Experimenter Stance

### Innovation Sources

| Source | Direction | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Inside-out** | From company to market | Build on internal capabilities | Engineer develops new feature from technical insight |
| **Outside-in** | From market to company | Respond to customer needs | Customer feedback drives product redesign |
| **Business model** | Structural | Change how value is captured | Switch from one-time sale to subscription |

### The Truth Curve — Selecting Experiments

Order experiments by cost and confidence:

```
Low cost / Low confidence ─────────────── High cost / High confidence

Desk research → Customer interviews → Surveys → Landing page →
Concierge MVP → Prototype → Pilot → Full launch
```

**Principle:** Start with cheapest experiments. Only invest more when cheaper tests confirm the hypothesis.

### Experimentation Techniques

| Technique | Cost | Time | Confidence | When to Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Desk research** | Very low | Days | Low | First screening of ideas |
| **Customer interviews** | Low | 1-2 weeks | Medium | Understanding the problem |
| **Smoke test** (landing page) | Low | 1 week | Medium | Testing demand |
| **Concierge MVP** | Medium | 2-4 weeks | Medium-High | Validating the service manually |
| **Wizard of Oz** | Medium | 2-4 weeks | Medium-High | Simulating automated features manually |
| **A/B test** | Medium | 2-4 weeks | High | Comparing two approaches |
| **Pilot programme** | High | 1-3 months | High | Full validation before scaling |

### Hypothesis Template

```
We believe that [customer segment]
Will [expected behaviour/outcome]
If we [product feature or change]
We will know this is true when [measurable metric]
Reaches [target threshold]
Within [timeframe]
```

**Example:**
```
We believe that smallholder coffee farmers in Mbale
Will use our mobile grading app weekly
If we provide instant quality scores with price estimates
We will know this is true when weekly active users
Reaches 100 farmers
Within 3 months of launch
```

## 6. Product Scaling Strategies

Eight strategies for scaling a product (Verwijs et al):

| Strategy | Description | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Geographic expansion** | Same product, new locations | Product-market fit proven in first market |
| **Customer segment expansion** | Same product, new customer types | Adjacent segments show demand |
| **Feature expansion** | More capabilities for existing customers | High retention, requests for more |
| **Platform strategy** | Enable third parties to build on your product | Strong user base, ecosystem potential |
| **Vertical integration** | Control more of the value chain | Supply chain is a bottleneck |
| **Horizontal integration** | Acquire complementary products | Market consolidation opportunity |
| **Licensing/franchising** | Others replicate your model | Capital-light growth preferred |
| **Partnership/distribution** | Leverage others' reach | Cannot afford to build own distribution |

## 7. Product Pricing Process

### Three-Step Pricing for Business Plans

1. **Understand value** — What is the product worth to the customer? (Value-based)
2. **Know your costs** — What does it cost to produce and deliver? (Cost floor)
3. **Check the market** — What do competitors charge? (Competitive reference)

### Pricing Alignment Check

| Element | Question | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| **Value alignment** | Does price reflect value delivered? | Price higher than perceived value |
| **Cost coverage** | Does price cover all costs + margin? | Gross margin below 30% |
| **Competitive position** | Is price defensible vs. alternatives? | Priced 50%+ above competitors without clear differentiation |
| **Customer segment match** | Can target customers afford it? | Price exceeds customer budget |
| **Scalability** | Does unit economics improve with scale? | Costs increase faster than revenue |

## 8. Key Value Areas (KVAs) for Product Metrics

Four areas to measure product success in a business plan:

| KVA | Measures | Key Metrics |
|---|---|---|
| **Current Value** | Value delivered to customers today | Revenue, customer satisfaction, usage frequency |
| **Unrealised Value** | Potential value not yet captured | Market share gap, unserved segments, feature requests |
| **Time to Market** | Speed of delivering new value | Release frequency, idea-to-launch time |
| **Ability to Innovate** | Capacity to create future value | R&D investment %, technical debt ratio, team capability |

**Business plan application:** Include at least Current Value and Unrealised Value metrics in financial projections. Ability to Innovate belongs in the management team section.
