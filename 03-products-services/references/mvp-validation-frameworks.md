# MVP & Product Validation Frameworks

> Based on Blank, S. & Dorf, B. (2012). *The Startup Owner's Manual*; Alam, M.M. *Transforming an Idea Into a Business with Design Thinking*.

## MVP Framework

### Core Principle
Build the smallest possible feature set that works as a standalone product. "No new features until you've exhausted the search for a business model" (Blank & Dorf, 2012).

### Low-Fidelity vs. High-Fidelity MVP

| Type | Purpose | Format | Investment |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Low-fidelity MVP** | Test whether you've identified a problem customers care about | Landing page, mockup, paper prototype, slide deck | Minimal (hours) |
| **High-fidelity MVP** | Test whether the product solves the problem well enough to buy | Working prototype, beta service, manual-behind-the-curtain | Moderate (days-weeks) |

### User Story Format (preferred over feature lists)

**Template:** "As a [customer type], I want to [action] so that [benefit]."

Examples:
- "As a restaurant owner, I want to see daily ingredient usage so that I can order supplies before stock-outs."
- "As a poultry farmer, I want automated feed scheduling so that I reduce waste and labour costs."

User stories keep focus on customer value rather than technical features.

### Product Vision Strategic Questions

When describing products/services in a business plan, address these strategic questions (Blank & Dorf, 2012):

1. **Network effects** — Does the product become more valuable as more people use it?
2. **Predictable pricing** — Can you predict revenue per customer? Is pricing transparent?
3. **Lock-in** — What switching costs keep customers from leaving? (data, training, integration)
4. **High margins** — Are gross margins >60%? If not, what drives volume?
5. **Organic demand** — Do customers find you, or must you find them?

## BFCE Use Case Evaluation Framework

Evaluate each product/service capability against four advantage types (Alam):

### The Four Advantages

| Advantage | Question | What to Measure | Business Plan Application |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Better** (Quality) | Does it deliver superior outcomes? | Customer satisfaction, error rates, success rates | "Our system reduces order errors by 85%" |
| **Faster** (Efficiency) | Does it save time? | Time-to-completion, throughput, cycle time | "Processing takes 2 minutes vs. 45 minutes manually" |
| **Cheaper** (Cost) | Does it reduce total cost? | TCO comparison, cost per unit, ROI | "Saves UGX 500,000/month vs. current method" |
| **Easier** (Experience) | Does it reduce effort/complexity? | Steps eliminated, learning curve, accessibility | "No training required; works on any smartphone" |

### Linking to Job-Goal-Vision Hierarchy

For each use case, connect the capability to:
- **Job** — the immediate task the customer needs done
- **Goal** — the broader objective the job serves
- **Vision** — the customer's aspirational outcome

Example:
- Job: Track daily restaurant sales
- Goal: Understand profitability by menu item
- Vision: Build a data-driven restaurant chain

## Experience Implications Template

For each product feature or service component, map dependencies across three dimensions (Alam):

| Future Experience Scene | People | Process | Technology |
|---|---|---|---|
| Customer places order | Counter staff trained on system | Order → Kitchen → Serve workflow | POS terminal, kitchen display |
| Owner checks daily report | Owner understands metrics | End-of-day reconciliation process | Dashboard, mobile app |
| Supplier receives reorder | Procurement staff authorised | Stock threshold → Auto-reorder flow | Inventory system, SMS gateway |

**Purpose:** Identify what must be in place (people, process, technology) for each product feature to work. This directly feeds into section 08 (Operations Plan) and section 13 (Implementation Timeline).

## Prototyping Taxonomy

Six prototype types for testing products/services before full build (Alam):

| Type | Best For | Cost | Speed |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Physical mockup** (cardboard, 3D print) | Physical products, packaging, store layouts | Low | Fast |
| **Handwritten wireframes** | App/web interfaces, forms, workflows | Minimal | Very fast |
| **Slide show / presentation** | Service concepts, multi-step processes | Low | Fast |
| **Storyboard** (comic strip) | Customer journey, use case narrative | Low | Fast |
| **Role-playing / acting** | Service delivery, customer interactions | Free | Immediate |
| **Technical prototype** (digital tools) | Interactive software, clickable demos | Medium | Moderate |

For Ugandan context: physical mockups, role-playing, and storyboards are often more effective than digital prototypes, especially when testing with customers who have limited digital literacy.

## Practical Application in Business Plans

When writing section 03 (Products & Services):

1. **Describe the MVP** — what is the minimum viable version? What features are included vs. deferred?
2. **Use the BFCE framework** — for each product/service, state which advantage(s) it delivers and quantify them
3. **Write user stories** — not just feature lists; show the customer value
4. **Map experience implications** — show you've thought through People, Process, and Technology requirements
5. **Address strategic questions** — network effects, pricing predictability, lock-in, margins, organic demand
6. **Show the product roadmap** — how does the MVP evolve? What comes in v2, v3?
7. **Link to validation** — reference Customer Development findings from meta-market-validation
