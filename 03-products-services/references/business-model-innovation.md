# Business Model Innovation Frameworks

*Source: Mangematin, V., Ravarini, A.M. and Sharkey Scott, P. (Guest Eds.) — Special Issue: Business Model Innovation, Journal of Business Strategy, Vol. 38 No. 2 (Emerald Publishing, 2017). Eight peer-reviewed articles covering business model performance, freemium strategy, crowdsourcing, open models, digital transformation, multi-sided platforms, and creative modelling.*

---

## What Business Model Innovation Is

Business model innovation is not about having a better product — it is about reconfiguring *how value is created, delivered, and captured*. Many businesses fail not because their product is weak but because their business model cannot sustain profitable growth. Equally, many new entrants defeat incumbents not through superior technology but through a more effective business model.

Three challenges managers face:
1. **Evaluating** current business models — whether based on traditional, freemium, crowdsourcing, or platform approaches
2. **Transforming** existing models in response to digitisation and market shifts
3. **Creating** business model innovations new to the firm (not necessarily new to the world)

---

## Framework 1: The 4V Business Model Components

Every business model consists of four interconnected elements (Holm et al., 2013; Teece, 2010):

| Component | What It Covers |
|---|---|
| **Value Proposition** | The bundle of products/services and their characteristics; what the firm offers and why customers should care |
| **Value Creation** | Resources, competencies, activities, processes, and partnerships required to create and deliver the offering |
| **Value Delivery** | Customer segments served; channels used to reach them; nature of customer relationships |
| **Value Capture** | Monetisation — how the firm makes money; revenue model, revenue streams, and cost structure |

**Business plan application:** Use the 4V framework to stress-test the business model before writing financial projections. If any element is weak, the model will underperform even with strong projections. Ask: *Is our value capture mechanism aligned with the value we actually create?*

## Framework 2: Five Business Model Performance Drivers

From a review of 38 studies on business model performance (Haggège, Gauthier and Rüling, 2017):

### Static Drivers (Configuration at a Point in Time)

**Driver 1: Rethinking Customer Engagement**
Strong business model performance requires carefully crafting customer relationships, not just targeting the right customers.

| Mechanism | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Shifting cost to customers | Self-service models that reduce firm costs while engaging customers | IKEA self-assembly; supermarket self-checkout |
| Increasing loyalty and attachment | Digital communities and platforms that create switching costs through engagement | LinkedIn professional network; loyalty apps |
| User-driven innovation | Involving customers in co-creation to align offerings with expectations | Crowdfunding pre-validation; open beta testing |

**Driver 2: Reconfiguring External Linkages**
Performance improves by building the right value network and creating lock-in effects.

| Mechanism | Description |
|---|---|
| Core competency focus + strategic partnerships | New ventures disrupt markets by partnering to fill gaps rather than building everything |
| Lock-in effects | Technology lock-in (Apple ecosystem), reputation lock-in (eBay seller ratings), network effects (Facebook) |
| Network-based innovation | Cross-industry partnerships that create new value (Nike + Apple → Nike iPod) |

**Driver 3: Optimising Internal Processes**
- **Time and cost efficiency:** Automation, standardisation, and advanced software (CRM, ERP, logistics)
- **Reducing time to market:** Faster R&D through prototyping, iteration, and 3D printing/digital tools

### Dynamic Drivers (Sustained Performance Over Time)

**Driver 4: Cultivating Strategic Awareness**
- **Opportunity sensing:** Proactively detecting new market opportunities and competitor threats before they materialise
- **Influencing uncertain environments:** Proactive strategy to redefine industry rules rather than react to them

**Driver 5: Developing Reconfiguration Capacity**
- **Organisational learning:** Trial-and-error experimentation embedded in the business model (MVP cycles, prototyping)
- **Business model portfolios:** Managing multiple, mutually reinforcing revenue streams to reduce risk and test alternatives

### Performance Drivers by Business Life Stage

| Life Stage | Emphasis |
|---|---|
| **Early venture** | Dynamic drivers — trial and error; business model portfolios; revenue diversification while hypotheses are tested |
| **Growth stage** | Static drivers — optimise customer engagement, create lock-in, build efficient processes |
| **Mature firm** | Dynamic drivers again — sense and respond to new entrants, digitisation, changing customer behaviour |

**Business plan implication:** An early-stage business plan should demonstrate dynamic capability (ability to learn and pivot) rather than over-optimised static configuration. A growth-stage plan should show how lock-in and process efficiency will defend the position.

## Framework 3: Freemium Business Model Strategy

From analysis of LinkedIn, Spotify, Box, Eventbrite (successes) and Chargify, Gasketball, TravelCrony (failures) — Holm and Günzel-Jensen, 2017:

**Freemium principle:** Provide a basic version free; convert a percentage to paying customers for enhanced value. Suitable for digital products where marginal cost of an additional user approaches zero.

**Critical freemium metrics:**
- Industry conversion rate from free to paid: **2–5%** — this means you need at least 20,000–50,000 free users to build a sustainable paying customer base
- Free users are not a cost burden — they are a **marketing asset** (word-of-mouth, product testing, B2B pathway through workplace adoption)

### Seven Strategic Requirements for Freemium Success

| Requirement | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **1. Attract vast user numbers** | Growth is the priority before monetisation; without sufficient free users, conversion volume is too low |
| **2. Sustain value of free and premium** | Continuously improve both tiers; low switching costs mean you must earn retention daily |
| **3. Maintain free/premium balance** | Too generous free tier = no upgrade incentive (Chargify failure); too restrictive = users leave |
| **4. Extract value from free users** | Treat as testers, advocates, and B2B seeds — not just cost |
| **5. Learn from user behaviour** | Analytics on how users engage drives segmentation and premium tier design |
| **6. Harness social sharing** | Integrate social sharing to multiply distribution at near-zero cost |
| **7. Bundle and integrate** | Partner with established platforms for distribution (Spotify + mobile operators; Box + IBM) |

**Freemium failure patterns:**
- Assuming excellent free product alone drives user acquisition (TravelCrony)
- Conversion rate too low due to wrong free/paid balance (Gasketball: 1% at $2.99)
- Removing free tier too early before proving premium demand (Chargify)

**Business plan application:** If proposing a freemium model, demonstrate: (a) total addressable free user base, (b) assumed conversion rate with justification, (c) cost structure at scale with mostly non-paying users, (d) pathway to profitability.

## Framework 4: Multi-Sided Platform Design

From Parmentier and Gandia (2017) — guidelines for moving from a one-sided to a multi-sided business model:

**Multi-sided platform definition:** A business that creates value by facilitating interactions between two or more distinct customer groups (e.g., Airbnb connects hosts and guests; Jumia connects sellers and buyers; Uber connects drivers and riders).

### Six Redesign Operations (One-Sided to Multi-Sided)

1. **Identify multiple customer groups** — who are the distinct sides, and what value does each seek?
2. **Establish a technological platform** — the infrastructure enabling interaction between groups
3. **Engage all sides** — each group must find enough value to participate; bootstrapping the "cold start" problem
4. **Structure the revenue model** — typically one side subsidises the other (e.g., buyers pay; sellers get low/free access) or advertising model (users free; advertisers pay)
5. **Create cross-side network effects** — value for each group increases as the other group grows
6. **Manage competitive dynamics** — multi-homing risk (users using multiple platforms); exclusivity strategies

**Network effects — the winner-takes-most dynamic:** Once a platform achieves critical mass on both sides, it becomes very difficult to displace. This creates a strong case for early investment in user acquisition at the cost of profitability. Reference: Facebook's advertising value increases with each additional user; eBay's seller reputation is non-transferable to a competing platform.

**Business plan application:** If the business is a platform, identify: which side bears cost and which pays; what the minimum viable scale is for each side; what prevents users from multi-homing to a competitor.

## Framework 5: Open Business Model Dimensions

From Benyayer and Kupp (2017) — five dimensions of open business models and strategic responses:

| Dimension | Traditional (Closed) | Open Model |
|---|---|---|
| **Motivation** | Proprietary advantage | Shared innovation ecosystem |
| **Object** | Products and IP | Knowledge, data, and platforms |
| **Community** | Internal teams | External contributors, crowds, partners |
| **Actions** | Controlled R&D | Open innovation, co-creation |
| **Governance** | Hierarchy | Distributed with rules |

**Three strategic responses for traditional businesses facing open model competitors:**
1. **Ignore** — viable only if open model cannot reach your customer segment
2. **Adapt** — selectively open parts of your model (API access, partner programmes) while protecting core
3. **Transform** — fully embrace open model architecture (requires significant business model redesign)

## Framework 6: Using Analogies for Creative Business Modelling

From Rumble and Minto (2017): business models can be designed by borrowing from distant industries, not just benchmarking competitors.

**Method:**
1. Choose a business model from an unrelated industry that solves a structurally similar problem
2. Identify *what the analogy does* (the mechanism) and *why it works* (the value logic)
3. Translate the mechanism into your industry context — adapt, don't copy
4. Stress-test the analogy for fit: does the underlying market logic transfer?

**Examples of cross-industry model transfers:**
- Gillette razor/blade model → printer/cartridge; subscription shaving (Dollar Shave Club)
- Airline frequent flyer → retail loyalty programmes → fintech rewards
- Newspaper advertising model → search engine advertising (Google)

**Uganda application:** Many successful East African business model innovations have been analogies — mobile money (M-Pesa) adapted banking logic for unbanked populations; solar leasing (BBOXX, Sun King) adapted utility model for off-grid households; agri-input credit adapted fintech credit scoring for smallholder farmers.

**Business plan application:** If proposing a novel business model, identify the analogue — a proven model from another industry or geography that demonstrates the logic works. "We are the [successful model] for [this market]" is more credible to investors than a claim of pure originality.

## Business Model vs. Competitive Strategy — Teece's Distinction

The Teece framework (referenced throughout the journal): business model and competitive strategy are distinct but interdependent:

- **Business model:** Describes the architecture of value creation, delivery, and capture — the *logic* of how the business works
- **Competitive strategy:** Describes how the business performs relative to rivals — the *positioning* of the business in its competitive environment

A business can have a strong competitive strategy (best product, lowest cost) but a weak business model (cannot capture the value it creates). Example: inventors who cannot prevent imitation have weak value capture even if their technology is superior.

**Business plan implication:** Validate both the business model (value capture is sustainable) and the competitive strategy (the offering is differentiated). Financial projections assume both are sound — investors will probe both.
