## 1. Overview and Framework

Cadle, Paul & Turner surveyed the full landscape of business analysis tools available to practising analysts and grouped them by stage in the BA lifecycle. The framework mirrors the BA process model from *Business Analysis* (Paul & Yeates, 2006):

| Stage | Chapter | Tools | Business Plan Relevance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Business strategy | 1 | 1–12 | High — sections 01, 04, 06 |
| Investigate situation | 2 | 13–22 | High — sections 00, 04, 05 |
| Consider perspectives | 3 | 23–32 | High — sections 05, 09 |
| Analyse needs | 4 | 33–40 | High — sections 03, 08 |
| Evaluate options | 5 | 41–49 | High — sections 01, 05, 10 |
| Define requirements | 6 | 50–65 | Medium — sections 03, 08 |
| Manage change | 7 | 66–72 | Medium — sections 13, 14 |

The book's key insight for business planning: **many BA techniques are essentially business planning tools used in reverse** — where a BA analyses an existing organisation to design a future state, a plan author analyses an imagined future state to justify its feasibility. The same analytical rigour applies in both directions.

## 2. Chapter 1 — Business Strategy and Objectives (Tools 1–12)

### External Environment Analysis

**Tool 1: PESTLE Analysis** *(aliases: PEST, PESTEL, STEEPLE, PESTLIED)*
Scans the macro-environment across six dimensions: Political, Economic, Socio-cultural, Technological, Legal, Environmental. Each dimension identifies forces that represent either constraints or opportunities for the organisation. The scan should be ongoing — not a one-off exercise. Use with Porter's Five Forces for a complete environmental picture.

**Tool 2: Porter's Five Forces**
Analyses industry attractiveness by examining five competitive pressures: (1) industry rivals/competitors, (2) threat of new entrants, (3) threat of substitutes, (4) buyer bargaining power, (5) supplier bargaining power. The first step is defining the industry boundary correctly — too narrow misses substitutes; too broad obscures real competition. The output is an assessment of structural profitability, not just competitor identification.

### Internal Capability Analysis

**Tool 3: MOST Analysis** *(alias: VMOST)*
Analyses internal strategic coherence across four levels: Mission (the organisation's purpose), Objectives (measurable targets), Strategy (approach to achieving objectives), Tactics (operational actions). VMOST adds Vision above Mission. Checks that tactics are aligned to strategy, strategy serves objectives, and objectives fulfil the mission. A misalignment at any level is a strategic failure.

**Tool 4: Resource Audit** *(alias: Resource Analysis)*
Catalogues internal resources across five dimensions: Physical (land, buildings, equipment), Financial (ratios: profit on sales, liquidity, gearing), Human (headcount, skills, motivation), Reputation (brand strength, awards, recognition), Know-how (systems, processes, intellectual capital). A structured alternative to informal SWOT-based "strengths" identification.

**Tool 5: Boston Box** *(aliases: BCG Matrix, Boston Consulting Group Matrix)*
Plots products or business units on a 2×2 matrix: market growth rate (y-axis) vs. relative market share (x-axis). Four quadrants: Stars (high growth, high share — invest), Cash Cows (low growth, high share — harvest), Question Marks (high growth, low share — decide), Dogs (low growth, low share — divest). Used for portfolio management and resource allocation decisions.

### Strategy Definition

**Tool 6: SWOT Analysis** *(alias: TOWS Analysis)*
Synthesises internal strengths/weaknesses (from Resource Audit) and external opportunities/threats (from PESTLE). TOWS reverses the sequence to generate strategic options: SO strategies exploit strengths to capture opportunities; ST strategies use strengths to neutralise threats; WO strategies improve weaknesses to capture opportunities; WT strategies minimise weaknesses to avoid threats. SWOT feeds into strategy definition; TOWS generates it.

**Tool 7: Ansoff's Matrix** *(alias: Ansoff's Box)*
Maps growth strategies against two dimensions: markets (existing/new) and products (existing/new). Four strategies: Market Penetration (existing products, existing markets — lowest risk), Market Development (existing products, new markets), Product Development (new products, existing markets), Diversification (new products, new markets — highest risk). Guides where to compete and how to grow.

### Strategy Implementation

**Tool 8: McKinsey's 7-S Model**
Diagnoses an organisation's capacity to implement a strategy by assessing seven interdependent elements: Strategy, Structure, Systems, Shared values (centre — the anchor), Staff, Skills, Style. The model's insight is that changing one S forces changes in the others. Implementing strategy without checking alignment across all seven creates implementation failure.

**Tool 9: Four-View Model** *(alias: Three-View Model)*
Provides a holistic picture of the business system from four angles: Customer (outside-in view of value delivered), Business Process (activities that deliver value), Organisation (who does what), and Technology (systems that enable processes). Ensures no perspective is omitted during strategy implementation planning.

### Performance Measurement

**Tool 10: Critical Success Factors (CSFs)**
Identifies the areas of performance that are essential to achieving objectives — the things the organisation *must* do well. Two types: industry-wide CSFs (table stakes for all competitors) and organisation-specific CSFs (differentiating capabilities). Always derived from the organisation's mission and strategic position.

**Tool 11: Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)**
Defines specific, measurable metrics that monitor whether CSFs are being achieved. KPIs are subordinate to CSFs — each CSF generates multiple KPIs. Example: CSF = "excellent customer service"; KPIs = complaint volume per month + percentage rating the service "very good" or "excellent". KPIs without CSFs are metrics without context.

**Tool 12: Balanced Business Scorecard (BBS)**
Norton & Kaplan's framework for balancing financial and non-financial performance across four perspectives: Financial (profitability, revenue growth), Customer (satisfaction, retention, acquisition), Internal Business Processes (efficiency, quality, cycle time), Learning and Growth (staff capability, innovation). Prevents over-emphasis on short-term financial metrics at the expense of long-term capability.

## 3. Chapter 2 — Investigate Situation (Tools 13–22)

### Qualitative Investigation

**Tool 13: Interviewing**
Structured conversation to gather information from stakeholders. Three phases: preparation (objectives, question list, logistics), conduct (open → probing → closed questions; active listening; managing silence), follow-up (notes issued promptly). Best for uncovering tacit knowledge, political sensitivities, and individual perspectives that surveys cannot capture.

**Tool 14: Workshops / Facilitated Workshops** *(aliases: JAD, Joint Requirements Planning)*
Group sessions to investigate situations, explore options, or build consensus. Five phases: scope and objectives, preparation, facilitation (focus, participation, record-keeping), output review, follow-up. Faster than interviewing when multiple perspectives need to be reconciled simultaneously. Proper facilitation is a prerequisite — unfacilitated workshops produce noise.

**Tool 15: Observation** *(aliases: Structured observation, STROBE, Shadowing, Protocol Analysis, Ethnographic Study)*
Direct observation of work as it actually happens — as opposed to how it is described in interviews. STROBE is a structured framework. Ethnographic study involves extended immersion in the working environment. Most useful when there is a significant gap between stated and actual processes. Time-consuming but reveals tacit practices invisible to other methods.

**Tool 16: Questionnaires** *(alias: Surveys)*
Structured instruments for gathering data from large populations. Four design elements: introduction (purpose, confidentiality, return date), instructions (how to complete), questions (open/closed/Likert/ranking), return instructions. Issues: low response rates, ambiguous questions, self-selection bias. Complement with interviews to interpret the numbers.

**Tool 17: Sampling** *(aliases: Activity Sampling, Work Measurement, Record Sampling)*
Statistical method for understanding how time or resources are distributed without continuous observation. Activity sampling records what is happening at random intervals over a period. Provides quantitative data on task frequency, time allocation, and workload distribution. Requires a sample size calculation to achieve statistical confidence.

**Tool 18: Special-Purpose Records** *(alias: Timesheets)*
Custom data collection forms designed to capture information not available from existing records. Used when activity sampling is impractical or when a specific type of event needs to be tracked. Document specification form captures: volumes, frequency, data items, users, storage, retention.

**Tool 19: Document Analysis**
Reviews existing documents (forms, reports, procedure manuals, organisational charts) to understand the current state. Produces a Document Specification Form for each key document: purpose, volumes, frequency, data items, users. Particularly useful in the early stages of investigation before stakeholders are available.

### Documenting Results

**Tool 20: Rich Pictures**
Free-form diagrams (Checkland's Soft Systems) that capture the situation holistically — people, processes, concerns, conflicts, and relationships — in a visual sketch. Deliberately informal to stimulate thinking rather than document conclusions. Useful for understanding complex, politically charged situations.

**Tool 21: Mind Maps** *(aliases: Semantic Networks, Webs, Concept Maps)*
Radial diagrams with a central topic and branching sub-topics. Used to structure thinking, summarise a business issue, or map the scope of an investigation. One of the authors' recommended "first eleven" tools — valuable for seeing the whole picture during complex investigations.

**Tool 22: Context Diagram**
Defines the system boundary by showing the system at the centre and all external entities (people, systems, organisations) that interact with it, connected by data/information flows. Establishes scope without internal process detail. Used at the start of process analysis to ensure boundary clarity.

## 4. Chapter 3 — Consider Perspectives (Tools 23–32)

### Stakeholder Identification

**Tool 23: Stakeholder Nomination**
Systematic process for identifying all parties affected by or involved in a business system. Uses prompts: customers, users, suppliers, regulators, competitors, strategic partners, employees, owners. Output is a raw stakeholder list for analysis.

**Tool 24: Background Research** *(alias: Background Reading, Report Analysis)*
Desk research using secondary sources to understand the context before primary investigation. Sources: annual reports, industry publications, regulatory filings, competitor websites, news archives. Avoids "reinventing the wheel" by building on existing knowledge.

**Tool 25: Stakeholder Wheel**
Visual framework showing the organisation at the centre surrounded by concentric rings of stakeholder groups: inner ring (directly involved in the system), outer ring (affected but not directly involved). More nuanced than a flat stakeholder list — the rings convey proximity and influence.

### Stakeholder Analysis

**Tool 26: Power/Interest Grid** *(aliases: P/I Grid, Power/Impact Grid, Influence/Interest Grid)*
Plots stakeholders on a 2×2 matrix: power (high/low) vs. interest (high/low). Four quadrants require different management strategies: High power/High interest = Manage closely; High power/Low interest = Keep satisfied; Low power/High interest = Keep informed; Low power/Low interest = Monitor. Extended version adds a "current vs. desired" position for each stakeholder.

**Tool 27: CATWOE** *(aliases: VOCATE, PARADE)*
Checkland's framework for understanding stakeholder worldviews. Six elements: Customer (beneficiary/recipient), Actor (performs the transformation), Transformation (core business process), Weltanschauung/World View (underlying beliefs that explain why the system exists), Owner (authority to change or close the system), Environment (external constraints). The "W" is the key insight — it reveals hidden agendas and value conflicts.

**Tool 28: Business Activity Modelling (BAM)** *(aliases: Conceptual Model, Logical Activity Model)*
A conceptual model of what an organisation must do to fulfil a given worldview (CATWOE). Shows the logical activities and their interdependencies without specifying how they are performed. Complements process models (which show how) by first establishing what (at a conceptual level). Derived directly from the CATWOE root definition.

**Tool 29: RASCI Charts** *(aliases: RACI Charts, ARCI Charts)*
Responsibility assignment matrix defining roles for each activity: Responsible (does the work), Accountable (owns the outcome), Supportive (provides support), Consulted (must be consulted), Informed (must be notified). RACI omits Supportive. Prevents accountability gaps and role confusion.

### Stakeholder Management

**Tool 30: Stakeholder Management Planning** *(alias: Stakeholder Map)*
Documents the management strategy for each stakeholder: current power/interest position, issues and concerns, current attitude (Champion/Supporter/Neutral/Critic/Opponent/Blocker), desired support, planned management actions. The Stakeholder Management Plan is the living document that drives stakeholder engagement through the project.

**Tool 31: Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument (TKI)**
Categorises five conflict-handling modes on two dimensions: assertiveness (desire to satisfy own concerns) vs. cooperativeness (desire to satisfy others' concerns). Five modes: Competing (high/low), Collaborating (high/high), Compromising (medium/medium), Avoiding (low/low), Accommodating (low/high). Each mode is appropriate in different contexts — the key is conscious mode selection.

**Tool 32: Principled Negotiation** *(BATNA — Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement)*
Fisher & Ury's four-principle negotiation framework: (1) separate people from the problem, (2) focus on interests not positions, (3) invent options for mutual gain, (4) insist on objective criteria. BATNA is the fallback if no agreement is reached — knowing your BATNA determines when to walk away.

## 5. Chapter 4 — Analyse Needs (Tools 33–40)

### Organisation Modelling

**Tool 33: Value Proposition Analysis**
Identifies the different value propositions the organisation offers from the customer's perspective. Four types of value proposition: operational excellence (lowest cost/best convenience), product leadership (best product), customer intimacy (best total solution), regulatory/societal (compliance or social mission). Forces clarity on what the business is actually selling — functional benefit, emotional benefit, or both.

**Tool 34: Value Chain Analysis** *(Porter's Value Chain)*
Maps the organisation's activities into a value system: primary activities (inbound logistics, operations, outbound logistics, marketing/sales, service) and support activities (infrastructure, HR, technology, procurement). Identifies where value is created and where cost is incurred. Used to find cost reduction and differentiation opportunities at the activity level.

**Tool 35: Organisation Diagram** *(alias: Organisation Model)*
Shows the organisation and its external environment — customers, suppliers, partners, regulators — and the high-level processes connecting them. Distinct from an organisational chart (which shows internal hierarchy). One of the authors' essential eleven: reveals the stakeholder landscape and the key processes to be explored.

### Business Process Analysis

**Tool 36: Business Event Analysis** *(aliases: System Event Analysis, Business Process Triggers)*
Identifies the events that trigger business processes. Three event types: external events (initiated by customers, suppliers, regulators), internal events (management decisions), time-based events (scheduled occurrences). Each event triggers one or more processes and has one or more desired outcomes. Event analysis provides the basis for comprehensive, systematic process modelling.

**Tool 37: Business Process Modelling** *(aliases: Process Maps, Swimlane Diagrams)*
Documents how business processes are performed, showing the sequence of tasks, decision points, and actors involved. Swimlane notation assigns tasks to lanes (one per actor/role), making cross-functional handoffs visible. Key quality checks: are there bottlenecks? Duplicate steps? Unnecessary loops? As-is models reveal improvement opportunities; to-be models define the desired future state.

**Tool 38: Business Rules Analysis** *(alias: Constraints Analysis)*
Documents the rules and policies that constrain or govern business processes. Two types: structural rules (how data is organised — e.g., a customer must have an account before placing an order) and operational rules (how processes behave — e.g., discounts apply only to orders above UGX 500,000). Business rules govern transitions between process steps and define eligibility, pricing, and exception handling.

**Tool 39: Decision Tables and Decision Trees**
Structured formats for documenting complex business rules with multiple conditions. Decision tables map all condition combinations to actions in a grid format — efficient for many conditions with many combinations. Decision trees use branching diagrams — better for sequential decisions. Both prevent missed conditions (business rules that are never tested).

### Business Change Identification

**Tool 40: Gap Analysis**
Identifies the gaps between the current state (as-is) and the desired future state (to-be). The gap becomes the basis for change options. Gap analysis is not a standalone tool — it requires both a thorough as-is analysis and a clearly defined to-be vision. Outputs are the specific changes needed to close each gap.

## 6. Chapter 5 — Evaluate Options (Tools 41–49)

**Tool 41: Options Identification**
Generates the full range of possible responses to a business problem or opportunity. Uses creative techniques (brainstorming, mind mapping) to avoid premature narrowing. Incremental options structure: do nothing → minimum change → phased improvement → full transformation. Business context analysis (PESTLE, Porter) informs which options are realistic.

**Tool 42: Feasibility Analysis**
Assesses whether each option is viable across three dimensions: Business feasibility (does it align with strategy and objectives?), Technical feasibility (do the required capabilities exist?), Financial feasibility (can it be funded and does it generate a positive return?). Feasibility gates prevent resources being committed to fundamentally unviable options.

**Tool 43: Force-Field Analysis**
Visualises the forces driving and restraining a proposed change. Driving forces (positive) are listed on the left; restraining forces (negative) on the right. The net force determines whether change is likely to succeed. Strategic insight: weakening a restraining force is often more effective than strengthening a driving force. Used to assess change readiness and design adoption strategies.

**Tool 44: Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA)** *(aliases: Benefit-Cost Analysis, BCA)*
Identifies and quantifies all costs and benefits of each option. Cost types: one-off initial costs, ongoing operational costs, intangible costs. Benefit types: tangible (quantifiable in financial terms), intangible (real but hard to monetise). The challenge is making intangible benefits credible — they must be described concretely and linked to measurable indicators. One of the authors' essential eleven.

**Tool 45: Impact Analysis**
Assesses the organisational impact of each option across multiple dimensions: process changes, technology changes, structural changes, interdepartmental relations, working practices, management style, recruitment policy. Prevents underestimating the human and operational change required to implement a solution.

**Tool 46: Risk Analysis** *(aliases: Risk Identification, Risk Management)*
Identifies, assesses, and plans responses to risks associated with each option. Risk identification generates a comprehensive risk register. Risk assessment rates each risk by likelihood and impact. Risk management assigns ownership and defines mitigation, contingency, and tolerance strategies. Risk analysis directly informs the business case recommendation.

**Tool 47: Investment Appraisal**
Applies three financial evaluation techniques: Payback/Breakeven Analysis (how quickly is the investment recovered?), Discounted Cash Flow/Net Present Value (what is the project worth in today's money, using a discount rate?), Internal Rate of Return (at what discount rate does NPV = 0? Accept if IRR > cost of capital). Each technique has different strengths — NPV is theoretically superior; payback period is most used in practice.

**Tool 48: Business Case Report Creation**
Structures the written business case: Executive Summary (3 paragraphs: findings, options considered, recommendation), Current Situation (problem/opportunity), Options Analysis (with CBA, risk analysis, and impact analysis for each), Recommendation (clear decision required), Appendices. The executive summary is the most important part — senior decision-makers often read only this.

**Tool 49: Business Case Presentation**
Oral presentation of the business case findings and recommendation. Key principles: know the audience (what matters to them?), structure for decision-making (not comprehensiveness), answer the question before giving the evidence, anticipate objections, end with a clear ask.

## 7. Chapter 6 — Define Requirements (Tools 50–65)

### Requirements Elicitation

**Tool 50: Scenarios**
Narrative descriptions of how a user interacts with a system in a specific context. Two types: normal (happy path), exception (edge cases and errors). Scenario coverage models check that all user types, environments, and frequencies are represented. One of the essential eleven — particularly effective for users who struggle to articulate abstract requirements.

**Tool 51: Storyboarding**
Visual sequences (like a comic strip or photo story) showing a user's interaction with a system step by step. Used to test the logic of a proposed workflow before development. More accessible to non-technical stakeholders than written scenarios.

**Tool 52: Prototyping**
Creates a working model (low-fidelity sketch or functional mockup) to test and refine requirements. Three categories: throwaway (explore and discard), evolutionary (refined into the final solution), incremental (delivers functionality in stages). Prototyping is particularly effective when users cannot articulate requirements in the abstract — show, don't tell.

**Tool 53: Hothousing**
Intensive 2-3 day workshop where small teams develop and demonstrate prototype solutions to a business problem. Similar to Agile sprint. Teams compete; the winning prototype is taken forward. Outputs: prototypes, process designs, priorities, cost-benefit analyses, metrics. Used when speed and stakeholder buy-in are critical.

**Tool 54: Timeboxing**
Agile time management technique: a fixed period (timebox) within which defined work must be completed. Outer timebox = full project; inner timebox = sprint (typically 1-6 weeks). If requirements cannot all be delivered within the timebox, lower-priority requirements are deferred. Used with MoSCoW prioritisation.

### Requirements Analysis

**Tool 55: MoSCoW Prioritisation**
Categorises requirements by priority: Must have (non-negotiable, project fails without them), Should have (important but can be managed around), Could have (desirable but easily excluded), Won't have this time (explicitly deferred to a later phase). Used in combination with timeboxing to manage scope. One of the essential eleven.

**Tool 56: Requirements Organisation**
Structures requirements into a logical hierarchy: functional requirements (by business area/process/use case), non-functional requirements (performance, security, availability, backup), general requirements (constraints, legal, look and feel), technical requirements (hardware, software, telecommunications). Structure prevents omissions and supports verification.

**Tool 57: Requirements Documentation**
Documents requirements in a requirements catalogue (one entry per requirement): unique ID, name, source, description, priority, status, owner, and linked models (use cases, entities, processes). The catalogue is the master reference — all other documents refer to it.

**Tool 58: Acceptance Criteria Definition**
Defines the specific conditions that must be met for a requirement to be considered satisfied. Provides the basis for testing. In Agile environments, acceptance criteria drive development (test-driven requirements). Must be defined before development starts — not retrospectively.

**Tool 59: Requirements Validation**
Ensures that documented requirements are correct, complete, consistent, unambiguous, verifiable, and necessary. Techniques: desk checking (individual review), peer reviews (structured group review), walkthroughs (author explains; reviewers comment), technical reviews (expert assessment), inspections (formal, independent).

**Tool 60: Requirements Management**
Controls changes to requirements after baselining. Change control process: (1) change request documented, (2) impact assessed, (3) approval authority reviews, (4) decision: approve/reject/defer, (5) if approved — update catalogue, traceability matrix, and affected models. Prevents uncontrolled scope creep.

### Requirements Modelling

**Tool 61: Requirements Traceability Matrix**
Tracks the links between requirements and other development artefacts (business objectives, test cases, design components). Supports completeness checking: every requirement should trace to a business objective; every test case should trace to a requirement. CRUD matrix (Tool 65) is a special case.

**Tool 62: Use Case Diagrams and Descriptions**
UML notation for defining the boundary and functions of a system from an actor's perspective. A use case diagram shows actors and use cases (system functions) connected by associations. A use case description details: name, actors, pre-conditions, normal flow, alternative flows, exception flows, post-conditions. One of the essential eleven.

**Tool 63: Entity Relationship Modelling (ERM)** *(aliases: Entity Relationship Diagrams, Logical Data Modelling)*
Models the structure of data: entities (things the system stores information about), relationships (associations between entities), and their cardinality (one-to-one, one-to-many, many-to-many). Business rules govern relationships. The ERM is the data blueprint — prevents data inconsistencies and duplication.

**Tool 64: Class Modelling** *(alias: Object Class Modelling)*
UML object-oriented equivalent of ERM. Classes represent both data (attributes) and behaviour (operations). Supports inheritance (generalisation/specialisation). Association classes capture information about relationships. Used when the development approach is object-oriented.

**Tool 65: CRUD Matrix** *(Create, Read, Update, Delete)*
Cross-references entities (rows) against processes (columns), marking which processes Create, Read, Update, or Delete each entity. Completeness check: every entity must be created by at least one process, and every process should use at least one entity. Catches orphan data and unreferenced processes.

## 8. Chapter 7 — Manage Change (Tools 66–72)

**Tool 66: Cultural Analysis** *(Johnson & Scholes' Cultural Web)*
Diagnoses organisational culture using six elements: stories (what gets talked about), symbols (physical artefacts), power structures (who really has influence), organisational structures (formal and informal), control systems (what is monitored), and rituals and routines. Culture is the hidden constraint on every change programme — diagnosing it before designing change prevents cultural rejection.

**Tool 67: Kurt Lewin's Model of Organisational Change**
Three-stage change model: Unfreeze (create the motivation to change — challenge current state assumptions), Change (implement new behaviours, processes, and systems), Refreeze (stabilise and embed the new state). The most common failure is refreezing prematurely — before the change is fully embedded — causing regression.

**Tool 68: SARAH Model** *(Shock, Anger, Rejection, Acceptance, Hope)*
Describes the emotional journey of individuals experiencing unwanted change. Five stages: Shock → Anger → Rejection → Acceptance → Hope. Change managers must recognise which stage each person is at and apply different interventions accordingly. People move through stages at different rates — forcing the pace creates resistance.

**Tool 69: Learning Cycle and Learning Styles** *(Kolb's Cycle, Honey & Mumford's Styles)*
Kolb's cycle: Concrete Experience → Reflective Observation → Abstract Conceptualisation → Active Experimentation — then repeat. Honey & Mumford's four learning styles: Activist (do first, think later), Reflector (observe before acting), Theorist (understand the model before trying), Pragmatist (focus on practical application). Training design must account for all four styles.

**Tool 70: Conscious Competence Model**
Four-stage learning progression: Unconscious Incompetence (don't know what you don't know), Conscious Incompetence (know you don't know), Conscious Competence (know how but must concentrate), Unconscious Competence (skill is automatic). Change implementation must move people from stage 1 to stage 4. Knowing which stage each person is at enables targeted support.

**Tool 71: Benefits Management**
Identifies expected benefits from a change programme, assigns benefit owners (responsible for securing each benefit), tracks progress, and reviews whether benefits are on track. Benefits plan: vision, benefits map, financial analysis, dependencies, tracking mechanisms, accountabilities. Distinct from project management — project management delivers the outputs; benefits management secures the outcomes.

**Tool 72: Benefits Realisation**
Post-implementation review: did the benefits actually occur? Three challenges: timing (when to review — cannot always wait years for lagging indicators), attribution (isolating the change's effect from other factors), and political culture (reluctance to audit failures). Benefits map helps by establishing enabling-change logic: if the changes happened, the benefits should follow.

## 10. Uganda/EA Application Notes

### External Analysis in Low-Data Markets

PESTLE and Porter's Five Forces assume data availability that may not exist for Uganda/EA SMEs. Practical adaptations:
- **PESTLE:** Supplement published sources with field observations and community knowledge. URA's tax briefings, KCCA business licence registers, and UBOS reports provide Political/Legal/Economic inputs. Social factors require primary research — surveys or focus groups in the target community.
- **Porter's Five Forces:** In fragmented informal markets, identifying "industry rivals" requires physical market surveys, not desk research. Buyer power is often concentrated in a small number of well-connected traders or supermarket chains; supplier power is understated in sectors with imported inputs (forex risk compounds it). See `references/market-strategy-frameworks.md` for Porter's Five Forces rating methodology.

### MOST Analysis and Mission Statements

Uganda's family-owned and founder-led businesses often have implicit missions that have never been articulated. MOST analysis forces the discipline of writing them down. The "MOST alignment check" — are tactics actually serving the mission? — frequently reveals drift in growing businesses where the owner has scaled up without revisiting strategy. See `../02-company-overview/references/mission-vision-why.md` (Sinek's Golden Circle) for the complementary "Why-first" approach.

### Resource Audit in the Uganda Context

Physical: note the difference between leased and owned assets (critical for collateral in bank applications). Human: factor in the informal skill base — trade experience and apprenticeship training are real human capital even without formal qualifications. Reputation: in Uganda's relationship-based economy, informal reputation (community trust, religious community standing, referral network) is often the most valuable and underreported asset.

### Stakeholder Analysis and CATWOE

The power/interest grid must reflect actual power, not formal authority. In Uganda's public sector and community institutions, informal power (elders, religious leaders, market associations, political connections) often exceeds formal authority. CATWOE's "Weltanschauung" dimension is particularly important in multi-stakeholder rural businesses where owners, workers, and communities hold genuinely different worldviews about what the business is for.

### Business Process Modelling and Informal Operations

Most Uganda SME operations exist as tacit knowledge in the owner's head — never documented. Business process modelling creates the operational blueprint that lenders and investors need to assess whether the business is systemised (and therefore scalable and manageable by someone other than the founder). The swimlane format is particularly accessible to non-technical readers. See `references/business-process-management.md` for the BPM lifecycle from Dumas et al.

### CBA and Investment Appraisal

Intangible benefits (improved community relationships, environmental stewardship, staff morale) are often under-stated in Uganda business plans but are genuinely material — particularly for NGO grant applications and impact investors (UDB, aBi Finance, UNCDF). Make them concrete: describe them specifically and link them to measurable proxies. For NPV calculations, use Bank of Uganda's commercial lending rate (currently 19–22%) as the discount rate unless a lower concessionary rate is applicable.

### Gap Analysis and Business Change

Gap analysis applied at business plan level: the "as-is" is the current business (or market status quo), the "to-be" is the proposed venture's operational state at full scale. The gap is the implementation challenge — exactly what Section 13 (Implementation Timeline) must address. Gaps that are not acknowledged in the plan are weaknesses that lenders will identify during due diligence.

### Change Management for Growth-Stage Businesses

Lewin's Unfreeze-Change-Refreeze model is directly applicable to Uganda businesses transitioning from informal to formal operations (NIN/BRN/TIN registration, EFRIS adoption, NSSF compliance, formalised HR). The "unfreeze" step — creating the motivation to formalise — is often the hardest, requiring concrete examples of lost contracts or blocked bank loans attributable to informality.
