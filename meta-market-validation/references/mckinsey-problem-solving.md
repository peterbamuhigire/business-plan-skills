# McKinsey Problem-Solving Framework

A practical distillation of McKinsey & Company's core analytical methods for business problem solving. These techniques are applicable directly to business plan construction — particularly for structuring analysis, validating market assumptions, and communicating conclusions clearly.

---

## 1. MECE — Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive

### Definition

MECE (pronounced "me-see") is the foundation of McKinsey's analytical structure. Every list of issues, recommendations, or analysis components must be:

- **Mutually Exclusive** — each item is a separate and distinct issue with no overlap
- **Collectively Exhaustive** — every relevant aspect of the problem is covered; nothing is missing

MECE is applied to every document, presentation, and communication — internal memos included. It is the primary test for whether thinking is clear and complete.

### How to Check for MECE Compliance

1. Take your list of issues or sections.
2. Ask: does any item overlap with another? If yes, the list is **not** mutually exclusive — consolidate or restructure.
3. Ask: is there any aspect of the problem that does not fall under one of these items? If yes, the list is **not** collectively exhaustive — add a category or refine existing ones.
4. A good McKinsey issue list contains **two to five top-line items** (three is optimal).

### MECE vs. Non-MECE: Worked Example

**Problem:** How do we increase widget sales at Acme?

**Non-MECE list (has overlap):**
- Change how we sell to retail outlets
- Improve marketing to consumers
- Reduce unit cost
- Reengineer the production process ← *overlaps with "reduce unit cost"*

The fourth item is not a separate issue — it belongs under "reduce unit cost" as a sub-issue.

**MECE list (corrected):**
- Change how we sell widgets to retail outlets
- Improve how we market widgets to consumers
- Reduce the unit cost of our widgets

Under "reduce unit cost," sub-issues include:
- Reengineer the production process to reduce unit cost
- Leverage the distribution system
- Improve inventory management

Under "improve marketing," sub-issues include:
- Reengineer the production process to improve widget quality

Each sub-issue sits under exactly one parent — no overlap, nothing missing.

### The "Other Issues" Safety Valve

If two or three important points do not fit the top-line MECE structure, group them under "Other Issues" — but only as a sub-issue category, not at the top line of a formal presentation. It is a MECE compliance device, not a substitute for clear thinking.

## 2. Issue Trees — Decomposing a Problem

### What an Issue Tree Is

An issue tree translates the Initial Hypothesis (see Section 3) into a structured map of sub-issues and required analyses. It shows, in visual form, every branch of the problem that must be investigated to prove or disprove the hypothesis.

### Structure and Branching Rules

- The top of the tree is the **problem statement** (e.g., "Increase widget sales")
- The first level of branches is the **top-line MECE issue list**
- Each branch then splits into **sub-issues** (the specific questions that must be answered)
- Each sub-issue maps to one or more **analyses** (the data and calculations that will answer the question)

Branching rules:
- Each branch must be MECE with its siblings at the same level
- Branches should describe actionable hypotheses, not just topics
- Drill down only as far as needed to identify the specific analyses required

### Acme Widgets Issue Tree (from the book)

```
Increase Widget Sales
├── Change Sales Strategy
│   ├── Sales Force Organisation
│   ├── Sales Force Skill Base
│   └── Promotion Strategy
├── Improve Marketing Strategy
│   ├── Product Quality
│   ├── Packaging
│   └── Consumer Advertising Strategy
└── Reduce Unit Cost
    ├── Raw Materials Sourcing
    ├── Production Process
    └── Distribution System
```

### How to Use an Issue Tree in Practice

1. Draft the top-line MECE issue list.
2. For each top-line issue, ask: "If this recommendation is correct, what sub-questions does it raise?"
3. For each sub-question, ask: "What analyses would prove or disprove this?"
4. Use the completed tree as your workplan — assign analyses to team members.

The issue tree is your **problem-solving map**. If the initial hypothesis is correct, the tree becomes the outline of your final report.

## 3. Hypothesis-Driven Analysis

### The Core Principle

McKinsey's third pillar of problem solving: **figure out the solution before you start, then prove or disprove it.** This is the Initial Hypothesis (IH).

> "The essence of the initial hypothesis is 'Figure out the solution to the problem before you start.'" — Rasiel

This seems counterintuitive but it is not. You use your knowledge of the industry and the problem to sketch a provisional answer. That answer then drives what you investigate — stopping you from boiling the ocean (see Section 4).

### Why Hypothesis-First Beats Data-First

- **Efficiency:** You only gather the facts needed to prove or disprove specific claims. Data-first analysis gathers everything, then looks for meaning — wasting time and creating noise.
- **Focus:** The hypothesis determines which analyses matter, preventing blind alleys.
- **Speed:** In McKinsey's brokerage client example, the issue tree defined exactly what data was needed before a single call was made.

### Generating an Initial Hypothesis

1. Absorb enough facts to understand the industry flavour — trade publications, industry insiders, internal documents. You do not need all the facts; you need enough for a good overview.
2. Identify the **key drivers** (the two or three factors that most determine the outcome — see Section 5).
3. For each key driver, formulate an **actionable recommendation** — not "the weather affects profits" but "we must reduce our vulnerability to weather."
4. Assemble the actionable recommendations into a MECE top-line list. That list is your IH.
5. Build the issue tree by decomposing each recommendation.

### Testing the Initial Hypothesis

Before beginning analysis, test the IH as a team:
- Is it the best possible hypothesis given current knowledge?
- Have all key drivers been considered?
- Are all recommendations actionable and provable?
- Does every branch of the issue tree lead to a real analysis?

Teams produce stronger hypotheses than individuals because they critique each other's assumptions. The team leader should propose a hypothesis but actively encourage challenges.

### The Critical Discipline: Don't Make the Facts Fit Your Solution

If the facts disprove your IH, **adjust the hypothesis — do not adjust the facts.** This is the most common failure mode in analysis. Rasiel cites an EM who spent weeks searching for "leakage" in an insurance client across multiple product lines because the data contradicted his hypothesis — he found none because it was not there.

Periodic reality checks: every week, ask "What have I learned? How does it change my hypothesis?" This prevents months of work down a blind alley.

## 4. The 80/20 Rule — McKinsey Application

### The Principle

> "80 percent of your sales will come from 20 percent of your sales force; 20 percent of a secretary's job will take up 80 percent of her time; 20 percent of the population controls 80 percent of the wealth." — Rasiel

The 80/20 rule is not a curiosity — it is a diagnostic tool. At McKinsey it appears in almost every engagement. Applied correctly, it reveals where to focus analytical effort and where the real levers of performance are.

### Worked Example (Brokerage Client)

A New York brokerage house asked McKinsey to improve profitability of its institutional equity business. The first question McKinsey asked: **"Where do your profits come from?"**

After analysing every account of every broker and trader, the data showed:
- 80% of sales came from 20% of brokers
- 80% of orders came from 20% of customers
- 80% of trading profit came from 20% of traders

These findings were not the answer, but they were the jump-start. They pointed to exactly where to focus: the allocation of staff resources. The team discovered that the three top brokers handled the ten biggest accounts — and by redistributing coverage, the client increased total sales from those accounts (not just redistributed them).

### How to Apply 80/20 in Practice

1. Get your data: sales by product, margin by product, sales by team member, success rate by segment, customer distribution by geography.
2. Sort data in multiple ways — by revenue, by margin, by volume, by customer.
3. Look for the clumps: which 20% of items produce 80% of the value?
4. Focus analytical energy on those items.
5. Look for opportunities, not just problems: 80/20 reveals where potential is concentrated, not only where waste exists.

### 80/20 and "Don't Boil the Ocean"

The 80/20 rule is the analytical basis for not boiling the ocean. Gather enough facts to prove or disprove a hypothesis — and only enough facts. Anything more is a waste when time and effort are limited.

> "Don't boil the ocean means don't try to analyse everything. Be selective; figure out the priorities of what you are doing. Know when you have done enough, then stop." — Rasiel

## 5. Find the Key Drivers

### Definition

Key drivers are the two or three factors (out of potentially hundreds) that most determine the outcome of a problem. Identifying them early allows concentrated, effective analysis.

> "There may be 100 different factors affecting the sales of our widgets — weather, consumer confidence, raw material prices — but the three most important ones are X, Y, and Z. We'll ignore the rest." — Rasiel

### The Square Law of Computation

Rasiel references the Square Law of Computation: for every additional component in a system, computation time increases by at least the square of the number of components. Doubling complexity quadruples work. By identifying key drivers and ignoring the rest, you apply the equivalent of astronomical simplification — astronomers model planetary motion by ignoring all but the dominant gravitational forces.

### Application in Business Plans

For each section of a business plan, identify the two or three key drivers before building the analysis:

| Section | Typical Key Drivers |
|---|---|
| Revenue projections | Price, volume, and one dominant channel |
| Cost structure | One or two dominant cost lines (often labour + one input) |
| Market size | Population segment × penetration rate × average spend |
| Competitive position | One or two differentiating factors that drive customer choice |
| Risk analysis | Two or three risks with highest probability × impact |

## 6. Fact-Based Analysis

### The McKinsey Standard

Every statement in a McKinsey document must be supported by a fact, not an opinion. Facts compensate for two weaknesses:

1. **Lack of industry gut instinct** — generalists need facts to reach conclusions that industry veterans might reach intuitively. Facts fill the credibility gap.
2. **Credibility with senior decision-makers** — a 27-year-old MBA presenting to a Fortune 50 CEO needs facts to be taken seriously. This is equally true of a business plan presented to a bank or investor.

### What Counts as a Fact

- Published data (industry reports, government statistics, company accounts)
- Primary research (interviews, surveys, field visits)
- Reasoned estimates with explicit assumptions — if you cannot find the exact figure, build it from components you can estimate (see Section 9 on "Don't Accept I Have No Idea")

### The "Don't Accept I Have No Idea" Method

When a key data point is unavailable, build it from components:

Rasiel's example: a publisher needed to show his board that a competitor was outspending him by 10×. His team had no direct data. So they estimated:
- Advertising spend: educated guess from market observation
- Production cost per issue: estimated cost per page × reported circulation
- Staff costs: headcount estimate × salary benchmark

The resulting P&L was "off by a factor of 2" — but accurate enough to make the business decision. The method is: **state your assumptions explicitly, build the estimate from observable components, and flag the margin of error.**

This is the standard McKinsey approach when data is unavailable — and it is the correct approach for business plan market sizing.

## 7. Presentation and the "Storyline" Approach

### The Governing Principle: Structure Reflects Thinking

A presentation is the external face of your analytical process. Sloppy structure signals sloppy thinking regardless of the quality of the underlying analysis.

McKinsey's structural standard for presentations:
1. **MECE structure** — every slide addresses one issue; issues are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive
2. **One message per chart** — the lead (caption) of every chart states the conclusion in one sentence; the chart illustrates it
3. **Source attribution** — every chart cites its data source
4. **Logical progression** — the audience is led step by step from problem to evidence to conclusion

### The "Tell 'em" Framework

The simplest acceptable presentation structure:
1. Tell them what you are going to tell them (executive summary / hypothesis)
2. Tell them (the analysis and evidence)
3. Tell them what you told them (conclusion and recommendations)

This structure works because it respects the audience's time and makes the logic visible.

### Prewiring: No Surprises in the Room

Before a formal presentation, walk every key stakeholder through the findings individually. This:
- Eliminates surprise objections in the room
- Identifies factual errors or overlooked information before the final meeting
- Builds consensus so the presentation is confirmation, not confrontation

> "It was very rare for us to do a presentation where we hadn't taken the various players through our findings beforehand. Otherwise, it was just too risky. In effect, the actual presentation became performance art." — Former McKinsey EM

In a business plan context: before submitting to a bank or investor, walk the relationship manager through the key numbers and assumptions in a preliminary meeting.

### Charts: One Message, One Source

- Keep charts simple — one message per chart, stated in the lead/caption
- Black and white is preferred: colour should not overpower the message
- Avoid 3D graphics (deceptive and distracting)
- Use the minimum number of charts needed to make your point
- Always include a source attribution in the lower left corner

**The waterfall chart** is McKinsey's signature tool for showing financial flows: starting value on the left, net income on the right, with positive items rising and negative items falling between them. Excellent for income statements, cash flow bridges, and showing how revenue becomes profit.

## 8. The Elevator Test

### Definition

Can you explain your conclusion — and sell it — to a senior decision-maker in 30 seconds (the duration of an elevator ride)?

> "The elevator test: In 30 seconds, can you tell the CEO your solution? Can you sell him your solution?" — Rasiel

If you cannot, you do not yet understand your own conclusion well enough.

### How to Pass the Elevator Test

1. Identify the two or three issues your analysis addressed.
2. State the recommendation for each issue in one sentence.
3. State the payoff (the "so what") — what outcome does this recommendation produce?
4. Drop all supporting data. That comes later.

**Example from the book:**
> "We think you can boost widget sales by 50% in three years if you reorganise your sales force by buyer category. We can talk about the details later."

That is the full elevator pitch. It states the action, the outcome, the timeframe, and the scope — nothing more.

### Application to Business Plans

The elevator test applies to:
- The **executive summary** — should pass the test in its opening paragraph
- **Funding requests** — the ask, the use of funds, and the repayment case in three sentences
- **Competitive positioning** — one sentence that captures the unique value proposition
- **Risk summary** — the top two or three risks and their mitigations

## 9. Common Analytical Mistakes McKinsey Avoids

| Mistake | McKinsey Principle |
|---|---|
| Accepting the problem as stated | "The problem is not always the problem" — dig to find the real issue before starting |
| Fitting facts to the hypothesis | Adjust the hypothesis when facts contradict it; never adjust the facts |
| Boiling the ocean | Stop gathering when you have enough to prove or disprove — not before, not after |
| Cookie-cutter solutions | Every client is unique; validate gut instinct with fact-based analysis every time |
| Recommending the theoretically optimal solution | Know what your client can actually implement; tailor solutions to organisational capability |
| Accepting "I have no idea" | Push through it — estimate from components, state assumptions, flag uncertainty |
| Hiding problems from the boss | Keep information flowing upward; surprises cost more than problems disclosed early |
| Overloading a presentation | Use the minimum charts needed; more slides dilute, not strengthen, the message |
| Faking it when you don't know | "Just say I don't know" — then find out; bluffing destroys credibility faster than ignorance |
| Ignoring politics | Politics is people acting in their own interests; understand incentives, build consensus |

### The Problem Is Not Always the Problem

Rasiel's medical analogy: a patient self-diagnoses and tells the doctor what he thinks is wrong. A good doctor does not trust the patient's diagnosis — she takes the history, asks questions, and then makes her own determination.

At McKinsey, clients frequently presented the wrong problem. One division brought McKinsey in to "evaluate expansion opportunities" — after several weeks of data gathering the team concluded the division needed to be closed or sold, not expanded.

The protocol: before beginning analysis, get enough facts to confirm you are solving the right problem. This is the first 10% of time well spent.

## 10. The Forces at Work Framework (Environmental Scan)

McKinsey uses a structured scan of the competitive environment at the start of an engagement to build a snapshot of external pressures and likely future changes.

**The four categories:**
1. **Suppliers** — what changes are occurring? What impact (positive or negative) on the client?
2. **Customers** — what changes in their behaviour, power, or needs?
3. **Competitors** — what moves are competitors making?
4. **Substitute products** — what alternatives could replace the client's offering?

**Plus: internal changes** — what is happening inside the business and industry that could cause major changes to how the client designs, manufactures, distributes, sells, and services its products?

This framework is directly applicable to the competitive analysis and market analysis sections of any business plan. It produces both a current snapshot and a view of how the environment might change — which is the basis for strategic positioning.
