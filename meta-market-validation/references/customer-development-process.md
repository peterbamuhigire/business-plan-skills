# Customer Development Process

> Based on Blank, S. & Dorf, B. (2012). *The Startup Owner's Manual*. K&S Ranch.

## Core Philosophy

"A startup is a temporary organisation in search of a scalable, repeatable, profitable business model." Startups **search** for a business model; existing companies **execute** a known one. Different tools, metrics, and organisational structures apply to each phase.

"No business plan survives first contact with customers." Business plans are collections of untested hypotheses. The Customer Development process converts hypotheses into facts through systematic testing.

## The Four Steps

### Step 1: Customer Discovery
Translate the founder's vision into business model hypotheses, then test them with customers.

**Four phases:**
1. **State hypotheses** — deconstruct vision into 9 Business Model Canvas boxes; write 1-2 page briefs for each; define pass/fail tests
2. **Test the problem** — get out of the building; do customers have the problem you think? How important is it?
3. **Test the solution** — present MVP to customers; do they see it as solving their problem? "When can I get it?" is the ideal response
4. **Verify and pivot/proceed** — full understanding of customers' problems? Value proposition validated? Sizeable market? Customers will pay?

### Step 2: Customer Validation
Prove the business model is repeatable and scalable with real orders.

**Four phases:**
1. **Get ready to sell** — craft positioning, build sales/marketing materials, hire sales closer, develop channel plan, refine sales roadmap, formalise advisory board
2. **Get out and sell** — test-sell at near full price to earlyvangelists; track everything; build probability-adjusted sales forecast
3. **Develop positioning** — test product and company positioning with analysts and broader audience
4. **Pivot or proceed** — assemble all data; validate business model box by box; run "metrics that matter" analysis

### Step 3: Customer Creation
Build end-user demand and drive it into the sales channel. Begins only after validated business model. Varies dramatically by market type. This is where serious spending begins.

### Step 4: Company Building
Transition from startup (search) to company (execution). Swap informal Customer Development team for formal departments. Scale the organisation.

**Steps 1-2 = Search. Steps 3-4 = Execute.** Most business plans are written during the Search phase.

## Business Model Canvas as Weekly Scorecard

Use Osterwalder's nine-box canvas as an iterative scorecard throughout Customer Development:

1. Value Proposition
2. Customer Segments
3. Channels
4. Customer Relationships
5. Revenue Streams
6. Key Resources
7. Key Activities
8. Key Partners
9. Cost Structure

**Weekly update method:** changes shown in red, then integrated into black. Each box contains testable hypotheses, not facts.

## 9 Deadly Sins of New Product Introduction

Common failure modes that validation must catch:

1. **Assuming "I know what the customer wants"** — founder's vision ≠ customer reality
2. **"I know what features to build"** — feature lists without customer feedback
3. **Focus on launch date** — shipping before understanding customers
4. **Emphasis on execution over learning** — executing an untested plan
5. **Traditional business plans presume no trial and error** — static plans in dynamic markets
6. **Confusing job titles with startup needs** — hiring VPs before finding a business model
7. **Sales and marketing execute to a plan** — selling before understanding the market
8. **Premature scaling** — the #1 startup failure mode; hiring/spending before business model is validated
9. **Management by crisis** — reacting to problems instead of systematic discovery

## Customer Development Manifesto: 14 Rules

1. There are no facts inside your building — get outside
2. Pair Customer Development with Agile Development
3. Failure is an integral part of the search
4. Make continuous iterations and pivots
5. No business plan survives first contact with customers
6. Design experiments and test to validate hypotheses
7. Agree on market type — it changes everything
8. Startup metrics differ from existing company metrics
9. Fast decision-making, cycle time, speed and tempo
10. It's all about passion
11. Startup job titles differ from large company titles
12. Preserve all cash until needed, then spend
13. Communicate and share learning
14. Customer Development success begins with buy-in

## Pivot-or-Proceed Decision Framework

The toughest startup question. Three-step process:

### 1. Assemble and Review All Key Findings (the "War Room")
Gather all customer interview notes, test results, metrics, and feedback in one place. Create physical or digital walls showing all evidence.

### 2. Review Business Model Hypotheses
Evaluate each Business Model Canvas box:
- Has this hypothesis been tested?
- What was the evidence?
- Is the hypothesis validated or invalidated?

### 3. Focus on "Metrics That Matter"
Three key questions:
- **Can it scale?** — $1 spent on customer acquisition produces > $1 in revenue?
- **Is there a repeatable sales roadmap?** — Can the sales process be repeated by others?
- **Is the sales funnel predictable?** — Can you forecast conversion at each stage?

## Revenue Growth Curves by Market Type

Different market types produce fundamentally different growth patterns:
- **Existing market** — relatively predictable growth based on market share capture
- **New market** — slow initial adoption, typically 3-7 years to scale
- **Re-segmented market** — similar to new market curve initially; assess switchable customer subset
- **Clone market** — depends on local market dynamics

## Search vs. Execute: Implications for Business Plans

| Dimension | Search Phase | Execute Phase |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| Goal | Find business model | Scale business model |
| Plan | Business Model Canvas (iterative) | Business plan (detailed) |
| Metrics | Discovery metrics, burn rate | Revenue, profit, ROI |
| Organisation | Customer Development team | Functional departments |
| Spending | Preserve cash | Invest for growth |
| Failure | Expected (pivot) | Problematic |

**For business plan writers:** Acknowledge which phase the venture is in. Search-phase plans should emphasise validated hypotheses and planned experiments. Execute-phase plans should emphasise proven metrics and scaling strategy.
