# Customer Discovery: 8 Practical Steps

> Based on Cooper, B. & Vlaskovits, P. (2010). *The Entrepreneur's Guide to Customer Development*. Cooper-Vlaskovits.

## Overview

Customer Discovery validates that a real problem exists for an identifiable group of people willing to pay for a solution. These 8 steps operationalise the process.

## Step 1: Document C-P-S Hypotheses

Write testable hypotheses for three dimensions:

- **Customer**: Who specifically has the problem? Demographics, psychographics, role, context.
- **Problem**: What specific problem do they have? Why do existing solutions fail?
- **Solution**: What specific solution addresses the shortfalls of current alternatives?

**Refinement questions:**
- How are people dealing with the problem today?
- Are they using competitors? Workarounds? Ignoring it?
- What does life look like before and after the solution?
- What is the cost (time, money, frustration) of the old way?

**Anti-pattern:** Don't mistake guesses for facts. Write specific assumptions, not vague hopes.

## Step 2: Brainstorm Business Model Hypotheses

Three sets of follow-on hypotheses:

1. **Business assumptions** — distribution channels, partnerships, monetisation model
2. **MVP assumptions** — features required per customer type, objective of each feature, "currencies" exchanged (attention, commitment, money)
3. **Funnel assumptions** — customer acquisition and conversion stages

## Step 3: Find Prospects to Talk To

Three concentric circles of interview targets:

1. **Broad search** (friends, family, LinkedIn, networks) — qualifying questions, ask for referrals
2. **Possible users / industry veterans** — market insights, C-P-S feedback
3. **Target users / early adopters** — product-market fit insight, acquisition strategy

**Anti-pattern:** Don't survey only friends and family. They tell you what you want to hear.

## Step 4: Reach Out to Prospects

**Intro email template (referral-based):**
> "Hi [Name], [Referrer] suggested I reach out. I'm researching [problem area] and would value your perspective as someone who [relevant experience]. I'm not selling anything — just gathering insights. Could you spare 20 minutes this week?"

**Phone call script:**
> "[Referrer] mentioned you'd be a great person to talk to about [problem]. I'm exploring [area] and would love to learn from your experience. This isn't a sales call — I'm genuinely trying to understand the problem better."

**Follow-up email template:**
> "Thank you for your time today. Key takeaways: [summary]. Next steps: [actions]. Would you be comfortable introducing me to [type of person]? Here's a blurb you can forward: [one-paragraph description]."

## Step 5: Engaging Prospects

### Problem/Current-Situation/Solution Presentation (Steve Blank)

Three-column presentation technique:
1. **Present the Problem** — discuss, get reactions, observe body language
2. **Reveal Current Solution/Workaround** — validate the pain is real
3. **Present Your Solution** — observe response

### "IPO" Questions
- What is the biggest pain about how you [work/do X]?
- If you could wave a magic wand and change anything, what would it be?
- How much time/money does this problem cost you?

### Vitamin vs. Painkiller Test
Is the solution a vitamin (nice-to-have) or a painkiller (must-have)? Painkillers are far more viable businesses.

## Step 6: Phase Gate I — Compile/Measure/Test

First gate evaluation after initial interviews:

- Are responses trending clearly? If so, you've talked to enough people
- **Stop criterion:** "When all the data begins to look the same" (Blank)
- Two outcomes:
  - (a) Right customer/problem/solution confirmed → proceed to Step 7
  - (b) One or more C-P-S assumptions wrong → return to Step 1 and pivot

**Anti-patterns:** Don't be "knee-jerk anti-Customer Development" when hearing negative feedback. Beware confirmation bias. Be cognisant of others' assumptions masquerading as expert opinions.

## Step 7: Problem-Solution Fit / MVP

Second iteration cycle:
- Get early product in front of earlyvangelists
- Test for **suitability** (not bugs or usability)
- Explore pricing through **value-based** approach — don't ask "how much would you pay?" but propose a specific price/range and observe reaction
- **Resource allocation heuristic** for multiple segments: 70% segment A / 20% segment B / 10% opportunistic

**Anti-pattern:** Beware feature creep. MVP means minimum.

## Step 8: Phase Gate II — Compile/Measure/Test

Exit criteria for Customer Discovery:
- Strong hypothesis for Product-Market Fit
- Representative MVP with paying (or committed-to-pay) users
- Understanding of business model mechanics
- Readiness to proceed to Customer Validation

## Business Ecosystem Mapping (5-Step Whiteboard Exercise)

For complex multi-stakeholder businesses:

1. **Map entities** — users, customers, channel partners, technical partners, strategic partners, advertisers, customers' customers
2. **Define value propositions** for each entity — what does each give and receive?
3. **Posit a final MVP** — define currency (attention/commitment/money), metric, and value determinants for each entity
4. **Identify risks** — technology risk vs. market risk; create risk table (risk type, severity, entity affected)
5. **Create a Value Path** — sequence intermediate MVPs to test gating factors first; catch roadblocks early

## Funnel Matrix

Six-column matrix for documenting customer acquisition/conversion assumptions:

| Funnel Stage | Buyer's Process | Business Task | Desired Response | Metric | Validation Question |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Suspect | Unaware of problem/solution | Create awareness | Visit/enquire | Reach, impressions | Where do suspects congregate? |
| Lead | Aware, exploring options | Educate, demonstrate value | Sign up / request info | Conversion rate | What triggers exploration? |
| Prospect | Evaluating your solution | Prove fit, remove objections | Trial / demo request | Trial rate | What objections arise? |
| Customer | Deciding to purchase | Close the sale | Purchase | Close rate, ASP | What triggers purchase? |
| Reference | Using and advocating | Delight, request referrals | Refer others | NPS, referral rate | What drives advocacy? |

## MVP Evolution Model

Three-stage progression from hypothesis to validated product:

| Stage | Customer Interaction | Objective | Currency |
|---|---|---|---|
| MVP 1 | Landing page / concept description | Test whether problem resonates | Attention (clicks, time) |
| MVP 2 | Demo / prototype / wireframe | Test whether solution approach works | Commitment (sign-ups, LOIs) |
| MVP 3 | Working product / service | Test whether customers will pay | Money (purchases, deposits) |

## Product-Market Fit Measurement

Three-criteria test (Cooper/Vlaskovits):
1. Customer willing to pay
2. Cost of acquisition < revenue per customer
3. Sufficient evidence market is large enough

**Sean Ellis 40% Rule:** Survey users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?" If ≥40% say "very disappointed," you have product-market fit.

## AARRR Metrics for Tracking Progress

Dave McClure's framework for measuring Customer Development progress:
- **Acquisition** — users arrive from various channels
- **Activation** — users have a positive first experience
- **Retention** — users come back
- **Referral** — users refer others
- **Revenue** — users pay
