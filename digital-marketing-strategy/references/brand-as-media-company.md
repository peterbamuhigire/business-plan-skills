# Brand as Media Company — Content Strategy and Brand Narrative

*Source: Brito, M. — Your Brand, The Next Media Company: How a Social Business Strategy Enables Better Content, Smarter Marketing, and Deeper Customer Relationships (Que Biz-Tech/Pearson, 2013).*

---

## The Core Concept

Every business is now a media company. The question is not whether to publish content but how to build a systematic content operation that tells a consistent brand story across all channels. Brands that master this transition attract, retain, and grow customers more efficiently than those relying on interruption advertising.

> "Your brand is now a publisher. The only question is whether you are a good one or a bad one." (Brito, 2013)

---

## The Three Pillars of Social Business

Transforming a brand into a media company requires alignment across three pillars:

| Pillar | What It Means | What Must Change |
|---|---|---|
| **People** | Leadership, employees, and culture must embrace content creation and sharing as part of work | Behaviour change from top; managers must model the culture they want |
| **Process** | Workflows, approvals, content calendars, and governance structures that make publishing consistent and fast | From ad-hoc posting to editorial discipline |
| **Platforms** | The technology stack (CMS, social media management, analytics, CRM) that enables creation, distribution, and measurement | Tools that connect content to customer data |

**Business plan implication:** A digital marketing plan should address all three pillars. Buying platforms (ads, tools) without fixing people and process will produce inconsistent results.

## Building Your Brand Content Narrative

Before deciding *where* to tell your story, determine *what* story to tell. The content narrative is built from five inputs:

### The Five Narrative Inputs

| Input | Description | Questions to Answer |
|---|---|---|
| **Brand messaging and product benefits** | What you offer and why it matters | What problems do we solve? What outcomes do we create? |
| **Non-business issues important to your brand** | Values, causes, and topics your brand cares about beyond products | What does the brand stand for beyond what it sells? |
| **Media perceptions of your brand** | How journalists, bloggers, and influencers currently describe you | What story is already being told about us? |
| **Customer-generated content** | What customers say about you in reviews, posts, and conversations | What language do customers use to describe the value we provide? |
| **Real-time and cultural context** | Trending topics, seasonal events, and cultural moments relevant to your audience | When and where do we have permission to join the conversation? |

**Output:** These five inputs combine into a **hero content narrative** — a single, clear, consistent story that every piece of content serves. The hero narrative answers: *What is this brand here to do for its customers, in plain language they would use?*

## Brand Tone of Voice

Once the content narrative is established, define the brand's tone of voice — how the brand sounds across every channel. For a business plan, document:

- **3–5 personality traits** the brand voice should convey (e.g., "warm but expert", "direct and practical", "aspirational but grounded")
- **2–3 traits the brand voice avoids** (e.g., "not corporate", "not preachy", "not aggressive")

**Example for a Uganda SME food brand:**
- Voice: Local, warm, knowledgeable, proud of quality
- Avoid: Generic global marketing language, over-formal tone, English that alienates Luganda/Swahili speakers
- Application: WhatsApp messages sound like a trusted friend; Instagram captions sound like the founder; invoices sound professional but approachable

## Content Channel Strategy

Different channels require different content tiers. Map your hero narrative to each channel with channel-specific execution:

| Tier | Channel Type | Content Role | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1 — Hero** | Website/blog, YouTube, long-form | Deep storytelling; the authoritative source | Weekly–monthly |
| **Tier 2 — Hub** | Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn | Regular engagement; amplify Tier 1 | Daily–weekly |
| **Tier 3 — Hygiene** | WhatsApp, Twitter/X, Stories | Real-time; quick responses; reactive content | Multiple daily |

**EA context:** For most East African SMEs, WhatsApp Business is the Tier 3 workhorse (customer service, order updates, community groups); Facebook is Tier 2 (reach and engagement); a website or Facebook page serves as Tier 1 (authority and depth).

**Laser-focused storytelling:** Each channel has a native behaviour. Content created for one channel rarely works on another without adaptation. A detailed blog post becomes a carousel on Instagram, a voice note on WhatsApp, a short video clip on TikTok — same narrative, different format.

## Converged Media — Making POEM Work Together

Converged media is the integration of Paid, Owned, and Earned media into a single coherent strategy where each channel amplifies the others.

> "Consumers must interact with your brand message three to five times before they start to believe. Converged media ensures your story reaches them consistently across multiple touchpoints." (Brito, 2013)

| Media Type | Definition | Examples | Strength | Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Paid** | Media you purchase | Facebook ads, Google ads, influencer fees, sponsored posts | Reach and speed | Cost; trust deficit |
| **Owned** | Media you control | Website, blog, email list, WhatsApp group, app | Trust; long-term asset | Slow to build audience |
| **Earned** | Media others create about you | Reviews, shares, PR coverage, word of mouth | High credibility | Cannot control |

**Converged media principle:** Owned media is the foundation (own the relationship). Paid media amplifies owned content to grow the earned media flywheel. Earned media then reduces the need for paid media over time.

**Practical converged media model for Uganda SMEs:**
1. Create quality content on your owned channel (Facebook page, WhatsApp broadcast)
2. Boost best-performing owned content with small paid budget to reach new audiences
3. Encourage sharing and reviews (earned) through excellent service and explicit asks
4. Retarget engaged audiences with paid ads to deepen the relationship

## Employee and Customer Advocacy

For small businesses without large ad budgets, employee and customer advocates are the most cost-effective media assets:

### Employee Advocacy
- Staff who share company content on personal accounts extend reach authentically
- Authenticity: employee posts receive 8× more engagement than brand posts on average
- Enable advocacy: make content easy to share; celebrate employees publicly; allow personality

### Customer Advocacy
- Identify customers who are already enthusiastic — they require small prompts, not incentives
- Ask for reviews (Google, Facebook, TripAdvisor) immediately after a positive experience
- Feature customer stories in content — user-generated content (UGC) builds trust and reduces content production costs
- Create exclusivity for advocates: early access, unlisted specials, VIP communication channels

**Business plan application:** Include an advocacy programme structure in the marketing plan. Identify how many advocates you target in Year 1, what engagement mechanism you will use, and what content support you will provide.

## Content Governance

Even small content operations need minimum governance structures to maintain consistency:

| Governance Element | Minimum Viable Version for SME |
|---|---|
| **Brand voice guide** | 1-page document: narrative, tone, what we say/don't say |
| **Content calendar** | Monthly plan with themes, post types, and responsibilities |
| **Approval workflow** | Who approves content before publishing (owner sign-off or delegated) |
| **Response protocol** | How and when to respond to comments, complaints, DMs |
| **Password security** | Who has access to social accounts; what happens when staff leave |
