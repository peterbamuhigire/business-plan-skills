## 1. Suns & Clouds Risk Chart

Evans's visual framework for plotting risks (clouds) and opportunities (suns) on a single chart, enabling an at-a-glance assessment of whether a strategy is backable.

### How It Works

The chart uses two axes:
- **X-axis: Likelihood** — low, medium, high (probability of occurrence)
- **Y-axis: Impact** — low, medium, large (effect on firm value if it occurs)

Risks are plotted as **clouds**; opportunities as **suns**. The diagonal from top-left to bottom-right divides the chart: items above the diagonal are most significant.

```
                        Likelihood
                Low         Medium        High
           +------------+------------+------------+
    High   |            |            |  THUNDER-  |
  Impact   |   Sun C    |  Cloud A   |  CLOUD !!  |
           |            |  Sun A     |            |
           +------------+------------+------------+
   Medium  |            |            |            |
           |            |  Cloud B   |  Sun B     |
           |            |            |            |
           +------------+------------+------------+
    Low    |  Cloud D   |            |            |
           |            |  Cloud C   |            |
           |            |            |            |
           +------------+------------+------------+

Key:  [cloud] = risk    [sun] = opportunity
      Items near the thundercloud corner = most critical
      Parabola zone (above diagonal) = priority items
```

### Reading the Chart

1. **Look at the thundercloud corner** (high likelihood + high impact) — any cloud there is a showstopper risk; the strategy is unbackable unless mitigated.
2. **Examine the parabola zone** (above the diagonal) — risks and opportunities here are significant. If suns are better placed than clouds, the strategy is backable.
3. **Below the diagonal** — lower priority; either unlikely or low-impact.
4. **Dynamic use** — add arrows to show how mitigation shifts clouds away from the thundercloud, or how strategy enhances opportunities (shifts suns towards it).

### Worked Example: Kampala Craft Brewery

A microbrewery planning to launch premium craft beer in Kampala:

**Clouds (Risks):**
- Cloud A: Excise duty increase by URA (medium likelihood, high impact)
- Cloud B: Established breweries launch competing craft lines (medium likelihood, medium impact)
- Cloud C: Supply chain delays for imported hops (medium likelihood, low impact)
- Cloud D: Consumer preference remains with mainstream lager (low likelihood, high impact)

**Suns (Opportunities):**
- Sun A: Growing middle-class demand for premium beverages (medium likelihood, high impact)
- Sun B: Tourism recovery drives on-premise consumption (high likelihood, medium impact)
- Sun C: East African trade agreements reduce raw material costs (low likelihood, high impact)

**Assessment:** Sun A and Sun B are well placed above the diagonal. Cloud A is the most concerning risk (near the parabola zone). Overall, the suns outweigh the clouds — the strategy appears backable, provided excise duty risk is mitigated through pricing flexibility and product mix.

## 2. Risk Categories for Business Plans

Every business plan should systematically address risks across seven categories. Each category below includes typical risks in a Ugandan / East African context.

### 2.1 Market Risk

Risks from shifts in demand, market size, or timing.

- Demand contraction during economic downturns (e.g. UGX depreciation reducing purchasing power)
- Market saturation in key segments (e.g. too many boda-boda delivery apps in Kampala)
- Seasonal volatility (e.g. agricultural businesses exposed to rainfall patterns)
- Currency risk on imported inputs (USD/UGX fluctuations)

### 2.2 Competitive Risk

Risks from competitor actions or new entrants.

- Price wars from established players with deeper pockets
- Regional competitors entering Uganda via EAC trade agreements
- Disruptive innovation making current offering obsolete
- Competitor acquiring your key supplier or distribution partner

### 2.3 Operational Risk

Risks from internal processes, systems, and supply chains.

- Power outages disrupting manufacturing (unreliable grid supply)
- Supply chain bottlenecks at Mombasa port or Malaba border
- Quality control failures damaging brand reputation
- Dependency on a single supplier for critical inputs

### 2.4 Financial Risk

Risks relating to cash flow, funding, and financial management.

- Cash flow shortfalls during growth phase (receivables from government contracts)
- Interest rate spikes on UGX-denominated loans (Bank of Uganda rate changes)
- Inability to secure follow-on funding at acceptable terms
- Foreign exchange losses on USD-denominated debt or imports

### 2.5 Regulatory Risk

Risks from government policy, taxation, and compliance obligations.

- URA tax assessments or changes to excise duty / VAT rates
- KCCA licensing requirements or zoning changes in Kampala
- UNBS standards compliance costs for manufactured goods
- Sector-specific regulation (e.g. NITA-U for technology, UCC for telecoms, NDA for pharmaceuticals)
- Environmental compliance requirements from NEMA

### 2.6 Technology Risk

Risks from technology failures, obsolescence, or dependencies.

- Platform dependency (e.g. reliance on a single mobile money API)
- Cybersecurity breaches exposing customer data
- Technology obsolescence requiring costly upgrades
- Internet connectivity disruptions in upcountry operations

### 2.7 Key-Person Risk

Risks from dependency on specific individuals.

- Founder/CEO incapacitation with no succession plan
- Loss of a key technical specialist to a competitor or diaspora
- Relationship-dependent sales (clients follow the person, not the company)
- Knowledge concentration — critical processes undocumented

## 3. Risk Containment Strategies

Evans identifies four approaches to containing risk, mapped to the Suns & Clouds chart. Strip out the suns, keep only the clouds, and overlay four containment zones.

### The Four Strategies

```
                        Likelihood
                Low         Medium        High
           +------------+------------+------------+
    High   |            |  TRANSFER  |   AVOID    |
  Impact   |            |            |            |
           +------------+------------+------------+
   Medium  |            |   REDUCE   |  TRANSFER  |
           |            |            |  / REDUCE   |
           +------------+------------+------------+
    Low    |   ACCEPT   |   ACCEPT   |   REDUCE   |
           |            |            |            |
           +------------+------------+------------+
```

### 3.1 Avoid

**Eliminate the risk entirely by changing approach.** Used for risks that are both highly likely and highly impactful — the showstopper zone.

| Example Risk | Avoidance Action |
|---|---|
| Entering a market segment dominated by a monopoly player | Choose a different segment or geography |
| Building a factory in a flood-prone area near Lake Victoria | Select an alternative site on higher ground |
| Launching a product requiring a licence you cannot obtain | Pivot to a product category that does not require it |
| Taking on USD-denominated debt with UGX revenues | Structure financing in UGX or secure a natural hedge |

### 3.2 Transfer

**Shift the risk to a third party** — typically through insurance, outsourcing, or contractual arrangements. Used for high-impact risks that cannot be avoided.

| Example Risk | Transfer Action |
|---|---|
| Fire or theft destroying inventory | Comprehensive business insurance (e.g. through UAP, Jubilee) |
| Product liability claims | Professional indemnity / product liability insurance |
| Transport risk on goods from Mombasa | Insure cargo; use reputable clearing agents with liability cover |
| IT system failure | Outsource hosting to a cloud provider with SLA guarantees |
| Foreign exchange exposure | Forward contracts or options through a commercial bank |

### 3.3 Reduce

**Lower the probability or impact through preventive action.** The workhorse strategy for medium risks.

| Example Risk | Reduction Action |
|---|---|
| Single-supplier dependency | Qualify two or three alternative suppliers |
| Power outages halting production | Install backup generator; negotiate priority industrial supply |
| Cash flow gaps from slow-paying clients | Require 50% advance payment; offer 2% early-payment discount |
| Key-person departure | Cross-train staff; document all critical processes |
| Data loss | Automated daily backups to off-site / cloud storage |
| Quality failures | Implement quality checks at three production stages |

### 3.4 Accept

**Acknowledge and monitor low-impact, low-likelihood risks.** Do not spend resources mitigating risks that would cost more to address than the potential loss.

| Example Risk | Acceptance Action |
|---|---|
| Minor seasonal demand fluctuations | Monitor monthly; adjust staffing flexibly |
| Small competitor entering a niche segment | Track their progress quarterly; no immediate action |
| Incremental regulatory paperwork changes | Comply as required; no structural change needed |

### Key Principle

Risk containment must not become paralysing. Evans warns that purely risk-focused tools can seem like "unremitting gloom." Always balance risk management with the pursuit of opportunities — this is why the Suns & Clouds chart plots both together.

## 4. Extraordinary Risk / Black Swans

### What They Are

Extraordinary risks sit in the thundercloud corner of the Suns & Clouds chart: **very likely and very large impact**, or alternatively, **very unlikely but catastrophic** (tail risks / Black Swans, per Nassim Taleb).

Evans's example: in 2001, advising on an airport operations investment, his team flagged "major air incident" as a low-likelihood, high-impact risk — days before 9/11. The risk was not precisely predicted, but having identified the category enabled the deal to be renegotiated.

### Identifying Tail Risks

Ask these questions:
- What single event could destroy the business entirely?
- What has happened in our industry elsewhere that "could never happen here"?
- What are we assuming will remain stable that historically has not?
- What systemic risks affect the entire sector (not just our firm)?

### Ugandan Context Examples

| Tail Risk | Potential Impact | Building Resilience |
|---|---|---|
| Government ban on a product category (e.g. plastic bags in 2018) | Total revenue loss for affected product lines | Diversify product range; monitor policy signals early |
| Regional conflict disrupting trade routes (e.g. South Sudan, DRC border) | Supply chain collapse; loss of export markets | Maintain buffer stock; develop alternative routes via Tanzania |
| Pandemic-level disruption (COVID-19 precedent) | Forced closure; demand collapse | Build cash reserves covering 3-6 months of fixed costs; develop digital channels |
| Banking sector crisis or mobile money platform failure | Inability to transact; frozen working capital | Spread funds across multiple banks; maintain petty cash reserves |
| Major cyber-attack on critical infrastructure | Operational paralysis; data loss | Offline backup systems; incident response plan; cyber insurance |

### Building Resilience

1. **Cash reserves** — maintain a liquidity buffer (minimum 3 months of operating costs)
2. **Diversification** — across products, customers, suppliers, and geographies
3. **Scenario rehearsal** — regularly ask "what if?" for low-probability, high-impact events
4. **Insurance** — transfer catastrophic risks where policies are available
5. **Relationships** — strong banking relationships and credit lines provide crisis funding access

## 5. Scenario Planning

Harris & Lenox define scenario planning as constructing a range of **plausible futures** to use as a backdrop for discussing and testing strategies.

### Core Concepts

- **Predetermines** — trends that are reasonably certain within the planning timeframe (e.g. Uganda's population will exceed 50 million by 2030; urbanisation will continue)
- **Uncertainties** — factors that will significantly influence success but whose outcomes are unknown (e.g. government policy on digital taxes; regional trade dynamics)
- **Scenarios are not forecasts** — they are distinct, plausible stories about the future, not predictions

### Two Uses of Scenario Planning

| Use | When | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Generative** | Early in strategy development | Spur creative identification of strategies you would not otherwise consider |
| **Robustness testing** | After a strategy is developed | Test whether the strategy holds up across multiple plausible futures |

### The 2x2 Matrix Method

The most common approach: select two critical uncertainties and map their outcomes to create four scenarios.

**Step-by-step process:**

1. **Gather information** — brainstorm environmental factors; interview stakeholders; research trends
2. **Identify predetermines and uncertainties** — sort factors into "reasonably certain" vs "uncertain but important"
3. **Select the two most critical uncertainties** — these become the axes
4. **Structure four scenarios** — one per quadrant; give each a memorable name
5. **Write vivid scenario narratives** — include headlines at 20%, 50%, and 100% of the time horizon
6. **Test strategy implications** — ask "What are the implications for our strategy in each scenario?"

### Worked Example: Ugandan Agri-Tech Startup (5-year horizon)

**Predetermines:**
- Uganda's farming population remains large (70%+ of labour force)
- Mobile phone penetration continues to rise
- Climate variability increases

**Critical Uncertainties:**
- **Axis 1:** Government investment in rural digital infrastructure (high vs low)
- **Axis 2:** Smallholder willingness to adopt digital tools (high vs low)

```
                    Government Digital Investment
                      HIGH                LOW
                +-----------------+-----------------+
   Farmer       |  "Digital       |  "Grassroots    |
   Adoption     |   Harvest"      |   Innovation"   |
   HIGH         |  Rapid growth;  |  Demand exists  |
                |  public infra   |  but poor infra;|
                |  supports scale |  offline-first   |
                +-----------------+-----------------+
   Farmer       |  "Build It and  |  "Status Quo"   |
   Adoption     |   They'll Come" |                 |
   LOW          |  Infra exists   |  Slow growth;   |
                |  but farmers    |  pivot to B2B   |
                |  resist change  |  or exit market |
                +-----------------+-----------------+
```

**Strategy implications:**
- A robust strategy works across at least three quadrants — e.g. an offline-first mobile app that can scale on public infrastructure when available, but does not depend on it.
- A fragile strategy bets everything on "Digital Harvest" — high growth only if both conditions are met.

### Time Horizon Guidance

- **Under 2 years** — useful for high-uncertainty situations (e.g. regulatory change pending)
- **3-5 years** — standard for business plans
- **5-10+ years** — infrastructure, energy, agriculture sector planning

## 6. Hypothesis Testing for Strategy

Harris & Lenox's framework for unearthing and testing the key assumptions on which a strategic choice depends.

### Why It Matters

Every strategic choice is a hypothesis: "If we do X, then Y will result." Strategies fail when reality differs from assumptions — customers do not want the offering, the firm cannot execute, or competitors respond faster than expected.

### The Four Core Tests

Every strategic hypothesis must pass these tests:

| Test | Question | Example (Kampala Delivery Service) |
|---|---|---|
| **Value test** | Will customers want this at a price that works? | Will Kampala restaurants pay UGX 3,000-5,000 per delivery for a reliable third-party service? |
| **Execution test** | Can we create and deliver it at a viable cost? | Can we recruit and manage 50 boda-boda riders with quality-controlled delivery at UGX 2,000 cost per trip? |
| **Scale test** | Can it be sustained and leveraged for future value? | Can we expand to 500 riders across Kampala and then replicate in Jinja, Mbarara, Gulu? |
| **Defensibility test** | Can competitors copy it easily? | What stops SafeBoda or Bolt from adding food delivery? Our restaurant relationships and integration? |

### Step-by-Step Process

**Step 1: Identify core assumptions**
List every assumption underlying your strategy. Be explicit. Common categories:
- Assumptions about **customers** (what they value, willingness to pay, segment size)
- Assumptions about **your organisation** (capabilities, costs, partnerships)
- Assumptions about **competitors** (their likely response, ability to copy)

**Step 2: Prioritise ruthlessly**
You cannot test everything. Identify the handful of assumptions that make or break the idea. In early stages, the **value test** and **execution test** matter most. Scale and defensibility come later.

**Step 3: Sort your data**

| Category | Description | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **What you know** | Facts already in your possession | Beware of beliefs masquerading as facts |
| **What you don't know but could** | Knowable data you have not yet gathered | Design experiments to get this data efficiently |
| **What you can't know** | True uncertainty (macro trends, black swans) | Make explicit predictions; acknowledge the uncertainty |

**Step 4: Design and run tests**
- **Thought experiments** — use existing data to test assumptions analytically
- **Field experiments** — go out and gather new data (customer interviews, pilot tests, MVPs)
- Focus on data that could **prove you wrong**, not just confirm your beliefs

**Step 5: Go/No-Go decision**

| Signal | Decision |
|---|---|
| Core assumptions validated by evidence | **Go** — proceed to next stage of investment |
| Mixed results; some assumptions unresolved | **Conditional go** — proceed but design further tests; set review milestones |
| Core assumptions invalidated | **No-go** — pivot strategy or abandon this path |

### Worked Example: Assumption Register

For a proposed UGX 500M investment in a cold-chain logistics business in Uganda:

| # | Assumption | Test | Data Source | Result | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Farmers will pay UGX 200/kg for cold storage | Value test | Interviews with 40 farmers in Luwero | 32/40 willing at UGX 150/kg | Partially validated — adjust pricing |
| 2 | We can maintain cold chain at < UGX 100/kg cost | Execution test | Pilot with 2 trucks over 3 months | Actual cost UGX 130/kg | Failed — redesign route density |
| 3 | No competitor offers this in central region | Defensibility test | Market scan; UNBS registry | One competitor in Mukono only | Validated |
| 4 | Supermarkets will pay 30% premium for fresh produce | Value test | Interviews with 5 Kampala supermarket buyers | 3/5 confirmed 20-25% premium | Partially validated — lower premium |

## 7. Strategy Risk-Reward Evaluation

Day (in *The Portable MBA in Strategy*) frames the ultimate strategic question: **will credible forecasts of revenues, costs, and investments yield returns that warrant taking these risks?**

### The Shareholder Value Approach

Conventional metrics (return on sales, revenue growth) ignore risk and timing. A more defensible approach:

1. **PV of cash flows during planning period** — discount future cash flows at the cost of capital, adjusted for the riskiness of the strategy. A radical departure carries a larger risk premium than a continuation strategy.
2. **Plus residual value** — the present value of cash flows after the planning period (what the business is worth at the end). For strategies requiring heavy up-front investment, this is often the largest value component.
3. **Minus market value of debt** — assigned to the business.
4. **Equals total shareholder value created** by the strategy.

### Risk-Adjusted Discount Rates (Illustrative)

| Strategy Type | Risk Premium Above Base Cost of Capital | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Continuation of current strategy in stable market | +0-2% | Existing restaurant adding a lunch menu |
| Moderate expansion into adjacent segment | +3-5% | Kampala retailer opening a second branch in Entebbe |
| New product in existing market | +5-8% | Manufacturer launching a new product line |
| New market entry (geographic or demographic) | +8-12% | Ugandan firm entering Rwandan market |
| Radical innovation / unproven model | +12-20% | First-of-kind fintech product in rural areas |

### Robust vs Fragile Strategies

| Characteristic | Fragile Strategy | Robust Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Scenario dependence** | Achieves results in only one scenario | Succeeds across a variety of scenarios |
| **Assumption sensitivity** | Highly sensitive to key assumptions being correct | Tolerates assumption errors without collapse |
| **Upside potential** | May outperform if expected scenario unfolds | May underperform in the best case |
| **Downside protection** | Withers under adversity | Won't be nearly so damaged by undesirable events |
| **Recommended for** | High-conviction bets with strong evidence | Most business plan contexts; investor preference |

### Worked Example: Scenario-Based Valuation

A Kampala-based solar energy installer evaluating a UGX 800M expansion:

| Cash Flow Component | Optimistic Scenario | Base Case | Pessimistic Scenario |
|---|---|---|---|
| Annual revenue (Year 3) | UGX 1.2B | UGX 900M | UGX 600M |
| Annual operating costs | UGX 700M | UGX 650M | UGX 600M |
| Annual free cash flow | UGX 500M | UGX 250M | UGX 0 |
| Discount rate | 18% | 22% | 28% |
| PV of 5-year cash flows | UGX 1.56B | UGX 714M | UGX 0 |
| Residual value (PV) | UGX 1.1B | UGX 450M | UGX 100M |
| Less: debt | (UGX 500M) | (UGX 500M) | (UGX 500M) |
| **Shareholder value created** | **UGX 2.16B** | **UGX 664M** | **(UGX 400M)** |

**Decision:** Base case creates value (UGX 664M > UGX 800M investment? Marginal). Pessimistic scenario destroys value significantly. The strategy is somewhat fragile — consider whether the pessimistic scenario is plausible and what mitigation could shift the base case upward.

## 8. Risk Mitigation Plan Template

A practical table format for documenting risks, their assessment, and mitigation actions in the business plan.

### Full Template

| # | Risk | Category | Likelihood (1-5) | Impact (1-5) | Risk Score (L x I) | Mitigation Strategy | Type | Owner | Trigger / Early Indicator | Contingency Plan |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | | | | | | | Avoid / Transfer / Reduce / Accept | | | |

### Scoring Guide

**Likelihood:**
1 = Very unlikely (<10%), 2 = Unlikely (10-25%), 3 = Possible (25-50%), 4 = Likely (50-75%), 5 = Very likely (>75%)

**Impact:**
1 = Negligible (<2% revenue), 2 = Minor (2-5%), 3 = Moderate (5-15%), 4 = Major (15-30%), 5 = Catastrophic (>30% or existential)

**Risk Score Interpretation:**
- **20-25:** Critical — must avoid or transfer; requires board-level attention
- **12-19:** High — must reduce aggressively; assign dedicated owner
- **6-11:** Medium — reduce where cost-effective; monitor quarterly
- **1-5:** Low — accept and monitor

### Worked Example: Kampala Food Processing Company

| # | Risk | Category | L | I | Score | Mitigation Strategy | Type | Owner | Trigger | Contingency |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | URA increases excise duty on processed foods by >10% | Regulatory | 3 | 4 | 12 | Monitor budget announcements; build 8% price flexibility into margins | Reduce | Finance Director | Budget speech signals; UMA industry briefings | Pass through 50% to customers; absorb 50% from margin |
| 2 | Key supplier (packaging) goes out of business | Operational | 2 | 4 | 8 | Qualify two alternative suppliers; maintain 4-week safety stock | Reduce | Operations Mgr | Supplier delivery delays >2 weeks; supplier financial distress signals | Switch to pre-qualified backup supplier within 2 weeks |
| 3 | Kampala factory fire | Operational | 1 | 5 | 5 | Comprehensive fire insurance (UAP); fire suppression system; quarterly drills | Transfer | Factory Mgr | N/A (insured event) | Claim insurance; temporarily outsource to co-packer in Jinja |
| 4 | USD/UGX depreciation >15% in one year | Financial | 3 | 3 | 9 | Negotiate 6-month fixed-price contracts with import suppliers; explore local substitutes | Reduce | Procurement Mgr | UGX weakens past 4,000/USD; Bank of Uganda signals | Accelerate local sourcing programme; renegotiate pricing |
| 5 | Production manager resigns | Key-person | 2 | 3 | 6 | Cross-train assistant manager; document all SOPs; competitive retention package | Reduce | HR / MD | Disengagement signals; competitor poaching activity | Promote assistant manager; engage interim consultant |
| 6 | Seasonal demand dip in Q1 | Market | 4 | 2 | 8 | Flexible staffing (casual workers in Q2-Q4); promotional campaigns in Q1 | Accept | Sales Mgr | Q1 orders 20% below forecast by mid-January | Extend promotions; reduce production shifts |
| 7 | UNBS rejects a product batch | Regulatory | 2 | 3 | 6 | In-house quality lab; pre-submission testing protocol | Reduce | Quality Mgr | Internal QC test failures | Quarantine batch; rework or dispose; expedite re-submission |
