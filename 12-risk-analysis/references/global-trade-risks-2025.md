# Global Trade Risks 2025 — Uganda Business Context

*Reference for §12 Risk Analysis — Uganda context*

Source: Uganda World Business Journal 2025 (WBJAC International); UBOS Kenya Economic Indicators Q1 2025/26; AfDB Africa Economic Outlook 2025; WTO Global Trade Report 2025; EU Deforestation Regulation 2023/1115; Houthi Red Sea shipping data 2024–25.

## 1. Overview

Uganda's position as a landlocked, import-dependent, commodity-exporting economy makes it structurally exposed to global trade disruptions. Business plans must assess which of these risks materially affect the specific venture — not every risk applies to every business. This file provides verified 2025 data for the most significant trade-related risk categories.

**Risk screening guidance:** Apply each section below only if the business has material exposure (supply chain, export revenue, regulatory market access, or financing dependency). Flag non-applicable risks as "out of scope" to demonstrate analytical discipline.

---

## 2. US Tariff Policy

**Current position (2025):** The US announced broad tariff increases in 2025, establishing a **base tariff rate of 10%** on most imports, with higher sector-specific rates. US trade policy is under active review, including the future of preferential frameworks such as AGOA.

**AGOA renewal risk:**
- AGOA (Africa Growth and Opportunity Act) provides duty-free access to the US market for >6,000 products; Uganda qualifies
- The programme is subject to periodic US Congressional renewal; the current review cycle introduces uncertainty about continuation and eligibility criteria
- Businesses building an export strategy around AGOA market access should model a downside scenario in which AGOA is curtailed or Uganda is removed from the eligible country list

**Uganda-specific exposure assessment:**
- Gold ($3.16B): primarily transited through UAE intermediaries before reaching end buyers — largely insulated from direct US tariff impact
- Coffee ($990M): primarily EU-bound (67%); US direct exposure is limited but exists for specialty coffee exporters
- Garments and horticulture: businesses using AGOA for US market access face the highest renewal risk

**Business plan action:** Manufacturing businesses with planned US export revenue must model a tariff-adjusted price competitiveness scenario. If AGOA duty-free access is assumed, include a sensitivity analysis showing margins if the preference lapses.

## 3. Red Sea and Suez Canal Disruptions

**Current position (2024–2025):** Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping (ongoing from late 2023) forced most commercial vessels to reroute around the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10–14 days to Europe–Asia transit routes.

**Impact on Uganda imports via Mombasa:**

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| Additional transit time (Europe/Middle East) | +2–3 weeks |
| Shipping cost increase (Europe origin) | +15–25% |
| War risk insurance premium (Red Sea routes) | +50–80% |
| Impact from Asia origin | Smaller — some Asia routes already used Cape |

**Working capital implication:**
Businesses sourcing from European suppliers (machinery, specialised inputs, medical equipment, chemicals) must hold **3–4 weeks of additional safety stock** compared to pre-2023 norms. This has a direct impact on:
- Working capital requirements (increase inventory buffer in §10 Financial Projections)
- Warehouse space needs (increase in §08 Operations Plan)
- Cash flow timing (import payment cycles lengthen)

**Business plan action:** Any business with European import dependency should note this in §12 under supply chain risk, quantify the additional inventory holding cost, and include the working capital uplift in §10 cash flow assumptions.

## 4. EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR)

**Effective date: 31 December 2025** — this is now an active market access requirement.

**Products in scope:** coffee, cocoa, soy, palm oil, cattle and beef products, wood and timber, rubber, and all derived products (e.g., chocolate, furniture, tyres).

**Compliance requirements:**
- GPS coordinates for every farm or land parcel in the supply chain
- Satellite imagery confirming no deforestation occurred after 31 December 2020
- Full supply chain due diligence documentation submitted to EU customs authorities
- Annual risk assessment by EU operators importing these commodities

**Uganda exposure:**

| Commodity | EU Market Share | Annual Value |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------|
| Coffee | 67% of Uganda's coffee exports | ~$660M/year |
| Timber/wood products | High EU relevance | — |
| Fish (aquaculture feed inputs) | Moderate | — |

**Risk:** Producers and exporters unable to provide EUDR-compliant documentation lose **EU market access entirely** — not a tariff penalty but a complete exclusion.

**Opportunity:** EUDR-compliant producers can command a **traceability premium** from EU buyers seeking verified, deforestation-free supply chains. Early compliance is a competitive advantage.

**Business plan action:** Coffee, cocoa, timber, and palm oil businesses must treat EUDR compliance as a baseline operational requirement. GPS farm registration, satellite monitoring, and supply chain traceability systems belong in §08 Operations and §10 Financial Projections (as a compliance cost line). Businesses that cannot achieve compliance should not project EU export revenue.

## 5. DRC Instability — Western Trade Corridor

**Current position:** The M23 conflict in eastern DRC (ongoing 2022–2025) continues to affect Uganda's western trade corridor.

**Trade exposure:**
- Uganda–DRC bilateral trade: approximately **$300M annually** at pre-conflict levels
- The Kasese/Fort Portal corridor to eastern DRC is intermittently disrupted by security conditions
- Cross-border traders in Kasese, Bundibugyo, and Kanungu districts face the most direct impact

**Refugee and humanitarian pressure:**
- Uganda hosts **1.5 million+ refugees**, with DRC conflict driving continued inflows
- Refugee settlements (primarily in South-West and North Uganda) create localised market demand but also fiscal pressure on host communities

**Business plan action:** Businesses dependent on DRC import or export routes should include corridor disruption as a rated risk in §12, with a contingency routing via Tanzania (longer and more expensive). Businesses in western Uganda districts adjacent to DRC should assess whether customer base or supply chain is conflict-affected.

## 6. Sudan Crisis — Northern Corridor

**Current position:** Sudan's civil war (ongoing since April 2023) has disrupted Uganda's northern trade route and damaged South Sudanese market access indirectly.

- Uganda–Sudan formal trade: largely disrupted
- South Sudan market access: affected by South Sudanese economic deterioration linked to Sudanese instability
- Refugee flows from South Sudan and Sudan continue into northern Uganda (Adjumani, Yumbe, Moyo districts)

**Business plan action:** Businesses targeting South Sudan or northern corridor transit should treat this as a high-probability, ongoing disruption and model revenue from that corridor conservatively.

## 7. EAC Integration Risks

Despite the EAC's legal framework, operational friction remains significant:

**Standards mutual recognition:** Kenya (KEBS) and Uganda (UNBS) mutual recognition of product standards is incomplete. Ugandan exporters to Kenya face periodic rejection of goods at the border when standards certification is not accepted. This is particularly common for food and beverage products.

**DRC and Somalia integration:** DRC joined EAC in 2022; Somalia in 2023. Both countries are at an early stage of integration — customs harmonisation, standards alignment, and tariff schedules are not yet fully operational.

**Tanzania land border:** Uganda exporters to Tanzania report inefficiencies at Mutukula and other crossing points. Delays of 1–3 days are common for goods requiring phytosanitary or quality certificates.

**Business plan action:** Businesses projecting EAC export revenue should not assume frictionless market access. Build border delay time and periodic standards disputes into the supply chain risk register.

## 8. Global Commodity Price Risks

### 8.1 Coffee

- Arabica prices reached multi-decade highs in 2024–25; robusta prices (Uganda's primary variety) followed
- Uganda robusta farmers and exporters are benefiting from elevated prices in the near term
- Risk: commodity prices are cyclical — a market correction would reduce export earnings and farm-gate prices sharply
- Business plan action: Coffee-related businesses should model revenue at current prices and at a 30–40% downside price scenario

### 8.2 Gold

- Spot gold price has been in the **$2,000–$3,000/oz range** (2024–25); prices elevated by geopolitical risk and central bank buying
- Uganda's gold exports ($3.16B) are highly sensitive to global price movements
- Gold is also Uganda's single largest export — a price correction would significantly affect the national balance of payments and UGX exchange rate

### 8.3 Petroleum

- Brent crude at **$70–85/bbl** range (2025); moderating from 2022 peak
- Uganda has no domestic oil refinery until at least 2027 (EACOP construction ongoing)
- Uganda imports all refined petroleum; fuel costs are exposed to both crude price and UGX/USD exchange rate movements
- Transport-intensive businesses (logistics, agriculture, FMCG distribution) carry high fuel cost exposure

### 8.4 Agricultural Inputs

- Fertiliser prices normalised from the 2022 peak (driven by Russia-Ukraine war) but remain elevated relative to pre-2020 levels
- Imported fertilisers (DAP, urea, NPK) are priced in USD; UGX depreciation amplifies cost increases
- Business plan action: Agribusiness and horticulture plans should use current fertiliser prices plus a 15–20% buffer for continued depreciation risk

## 9. East African Inflation Context (2025)

| Country | Approximate Inflation Rate (2025) |
|---------|----------------------------------|
| Uganda | 3.2% (UBOS CPI, Feb 2026) |
| Kenya | 4–6% |
| Tanzania | 3–5% |
| Rwanda | 4–6% |

Uganda's relatively contained inflation is a positive operating environment signal. However, cross-border price differentials with Kenya and Rwanda affect competitiveness for businesses selling into those markets.

**Business plan action:** Financial projections should apply Uganda's 3–4% inflation rate to operating cost escalation. Revenue projections for EAC markets should use the destination country's inflation rate when pricing increases are being modelled.

## 10. Risk Register Template — Trade Exposure

Business plan authors should include a trade risk register in §12 covering whichever of the following applies:

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-------------|--------|------------|
| AGOA lapse (US export revenue) | Medium | High | Diversify to EAC/EU; monitor AGOA renewal |
| Red Sea disruption (import delay) | High | Medium | Hold 4-week safety stock; dual supplier |
| EUDR non-compliance (EU market loss) | Low–Medium | Very High | GPS farm registration; traceability system |
| DRC corridor disruption | High | Medium | Alternate Tanzania routing; reduce DRC dependency |
| Coffee price correction (−30%) | Medium | High | Hedge via forward contracts; diversify crops |
| UGX depreciation (+15%) | Medium | Medium | USD-denominated price clauses for exports |
| EAC standards rejection | Medium | Medium | Pre-export UNBS/KEBS dual certification |

*Probability and impact ratings should be adjusted to the specific business context. Use the Suns and Clouds framework (Evans) or scenario planning (Harris/Lenox) for structured analysis.*
