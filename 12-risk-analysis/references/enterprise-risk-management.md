# Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) Frameworks

*Sources: Olson & Wu, Enterprise Risk Management Models, 2nd ed. (Springer, 2017); Murray-Webster & Pullan, Making Risk Management Work, 2nd ed. (Routledge); Raydugin, Project Risk Management (Wiley, 2013).*

---

## 1. The COSO ERM Framework (Olson & Wu)

The Committee of Sponsoring Organizations (COSO) defines ERM as:
> "A process designed to identify potential events that may affect the organisation, and manage risk to be within that organisation's risk appetite in order to provide reasonable assurance of accomplishing the organisation's objectives."

### COSO Risk Management Framework

| Concept | Question to Answer |
|---|---|
| Mission, strategy, objectives | What are the organisation's goals? |
| Risks | What are the significant risks? |
| Risk appetite | What is the organisation willing to tolerate? |
| Likelihood | What is the probability of occurrence? How can it be measured? |
| Impacts | What is the potential impact if it occurs? |
| Risk mitigation | What defence strategies are available? |
| Residual risk | What risk remains beyond control? |
| Risk response effectiveness | How well does the organisation manage individual risks? |
| Risk maturity | How robust is the current ERM programme? |

### Risk Management Process Cycle

```
1. Risk Identification
         ↓
2. Risk Assessment & Evaluation (Likelihood × Impact)
         ↓
3. Selection of Risk Management Strategy
         ↓
4. Implementation of Response
         ↓
5. Monitoring & Mitigation
         ↓
Back to Step 1 (continuous cycle)
```

## 2. Enterprise Risk Categories (Olson & Wu)

Every business faces risks in five categories. Systematically check each:

### Strategic Risks
- Is there a formal process to identify changes in markets, economic conditions, regulations, and demographics?
- Is new product/service innovation assessed for short-run and long-run impact?
- Is R&D/product development investment adequate to keep up with competitors?
- Are controls in place to satisfy regulatory requirements?

### Operations Risks
- Are decision-making processes rational and documented?
- Is there a master list of supplier/vendor relationships?
- Is there adequate segregation of duties (who authorises vs who executes)?
- Are financial models and controls documented and tested?

### Legal Risks
- Are intellectual property rights protected (trademarks, patents, copyrights)?
- Is there an inventory of legal agreements and regular compliance auditing?
- Does the business address litigation patterns?
- Is there adequate product/service quality to avoid consumer complaints?

### Credit Risks
- Are key financial metrics monitored (accounts receivable ageing, debtor days)?
- How are settlement risks managed?
- Is there sufficient collateral for credit facilities?
- Is incentive compensation aligned with profitability rather than volume?

### Market Risks
- Is there a documented funding plan for growth?
- Is there a contingency plan for extreme market events?
- Are pricing and cash flow assumptions regularly stress-tested?
- Are core customers analysed for price sensitivity?

## 3. Risk Appetite and Risk Tolerance

*Murray-Webster & Pullan; Olson & Wu*

**Risk appetite** = the amount and type of risk an organisation is willing to pursue or accept to achieve its objectives.

**Risk capacity** = the maximum amount of risk an organisation can absorb without threatening its existence.

**Risk tolerance** = the acceptable variation around specific objectives — the degree of risk accepted for a particular risk event.

**Risk attitude** lies on a continuum:
```
Risk Averse ←────────────────────────→ Risk Seeking
(want certainty)        (neutral)        (comfortable with uncertainty)
```

### Setting Risk Appetite for a Business Plan

| Business Stage | Typical Risk Appetite | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Startup (pre-revenue) | Risk tolerant → Risk seeking | Must take risks to establish market |
| Early growth | Risk neutral | Balance growth with cash conservation |
| Established SME | Risk averse → Risk neutral | Protect established revenue; preserve capital |
| Turnaround/distress | Risk seeking (forced) | Must act boldly or fail |

> **Business plan implication:** Define the organisation's risk appetite explicitly. State which risks are acceptable (within appetite) and which must be mitigated or transferred.

## 4. Risk Types for Business Plans

Adapt the COSO categories to business plan context:

| Risk Category | Business Plan Section | Key Questions |
|---|---|---|
| **Market risk** | Market Analysis | Are growth assumptions realistic? Is competition underestimated? |
| **Operations risk** | Operations Plan | Can the business execute? Are supply chains reliable? |
| **Financial risk** | Financial Projections | Can cash flow support operations? Is leverage manageable? |
| **Regulatory/legal risk** | Regulatory Environment | Are all licences and compliance requirements met? |
| **Strategic risk** | Business Model | Is the value proposition truly differentiated? |
| **People risk** | Management Team | Are key skills present? What is the key-person dependency? |
| **Environmental/climate risk** | Industry context | For agriculture, construction, tourism — weather/climate exposure |
| **Technology risk** | AI/Operations | Is technology reliable and scalable? |

## 5. ERM in Projects (Olson & Wu)

When a business plan involves a significant implementation project (e.g., construction, product launch, system rollout), apply project ERM:

**Project-specific risk types:**
- **Scope risks:** requirements change, scope creep
- **Schedule risks:** delays in approvals, contractor performance, resource availability
- **Cost risks:** cost overruns, input price inflation, currency risk
- **Quality risks:** rework, defects, non-compliance
- **Stakeholder risks:** resistance to change, key person departure

**Key project ERM principle (Olson):** Set a risk appetite for the project budget and schedule variance that management will tolerate before escalation. Anything beyond this threshold requires a formal risk response decision.

## 6. Natural Disaster & Environmental Risk (Olson & Wu)

Particularly relevant for Uganda/East Africa context:

**Three categories of crisis (Mitroff & Alpaslan):**
1. **Natural disasters** — earthquakes, floods, drought, volcanic eruptions — inherently uncertain but can be prepared for
2. **Malicious activities** — intentional acts by competitors, thieves, disgruntled employees
3. **Systemic failures** — breakdowns in complex human systems (e.g., power grid, banking, supply chain)

**Mitigation principle:** Build resilience through redundancy, diversification, and insurance — not by assuming disasters won't happen.

**Uganda-specific natural disaster risks:** Flooding (lakeshore areas), drought (Karamoja, dry corridor), crop disease, livestock epidemic, landslides (Mount Elgon region).

## 7. Balanced Scorecard for Risk Monitoring (Olson & Wu)

A Balanced Scorecard (BSC) can embed risk monitoring into ongoing management, tracking risks across four perspectives:

| BSC Perspective | Risk KPIs to Monitor |
|---|---|
| **Financial** | Debt ratio, cash burn, debtor days, cost variance vs budget |
| **Customer** | Customer retention rate, complaint rate, NPS |
| **Internal Process** | Defect rate, delivery time, supplier reliability, regulatory compliance |
| **Learning & Growth** | Staff turnover, training completion, technology uptime |

> **Business plan implication:** Include a risk monitoring table showing which metrics flag emerging risks, and the threshold that triggers action.

## 8. Risk Management Maturity

*Olson & Wu*

| Maturity Level | Characteristics | Typical Business Stage |
|---|---|---|
| 1 — Ad hoc | No formal process; reactive only | Startup, informal SME |
| 2 — Repeatable | Some processes exist but not documented | Growing SME |
| 3 — Defined | Documented risk framework; regular reviews | Established SME, seeking finance |
| 4 — Managed | Quantitative risk measurement; integrated into decisions | Mid-size company |
| 5 — Optimising | Continuous improvement; risk culture embedded | Large organisation |

> **Business plan implication:** Lenders and investors expect at least Level 2–3. A business plan that demonstrates Level 3 (documented risks + response plans + monitoring metrics) is far more credible.
