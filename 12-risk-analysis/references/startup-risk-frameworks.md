# Startup Risk Frameworks

> Based on Blank, S. & Dorf, B. (2012). *The Startup Owner's Manual*; Alam, M.M. *Transforming an Idea Into a Business with Design Thinking*.

## 9 Deadly Sins of New Product Introduction

The most common failure modes that business plans should explicitly address (Blank & Dorf, 2012):

### Sin 1: Assuming "I Know What the Customer Wants"
**Risk:** Building products based on founder intuition rather than customer evidence.
**Mitigation:** Customer Development interviews, validation with real customers, evidence-based personas.
**In the business plan:** Show the validation process used. Reference specific customer conversations and their findings.

### Sin 2: "I Know What Features to Build"
**Risk:** Feature lists driven by engineering excitement rather than customer needs.
**Mitigation:** User stories based on customer interviews; MVP approach; BFCE evaluation.
**In the business plan:** Present features as solutions to validated customer problems, not as technology showcases.

### Sin 3: Focus on Launch Date
**Risk:** Treating launch as the endpoint rather than the beginning of learning.
**Mitigation:** Define launch as the start of Customer Validation, not the finish line.
**In the business plan:** Frame milestones around validation achievements, not just product launches.

### Sin 4: Emphasis on Execution Over Learning
**Risk:** Executing an untested plan with maximum efficiency — efficiently going in the wrong direction.
**Mitigation:** Build learning milestones into the implementation timeline; pivot criteria defined in advance.
**In the business plan:** Include "decision gates" where the plan is reviewed against customer evidence.

### Sin 5: Traditional Business Plans Presume No Trial and Error
**Risk:** Static plans in dynamic markets; no mechanism for course correction.
**Mitigation:** Business Model Canvas as a living document; iterative planning with quarterly reviews.
**In the business plan:** Acknowledge assumptions explicitly; show how they'll be tested.

### Sin 6: Confusing Job Titles with Startup Needs
**Risk:** Hiring VPs of Sales and Marketing before understanding the market.
**Mitigation:** Founders do initial customer discovery and sales; hire functional roles only after validation.
**In the business plan:** Show founding team doing discovery work; hiring plan tied to validation milestones.

### Sin 7: Sales and Marketing Execute to a Plan
**Risk:** Assuming the customer acquisition strategy will work as designed.
**Mitigation:** Test-selling to earlyvangelists; experiment-based marketing with 30-day review cycles.
**In the business plan:** Present marketing as a set of experiments with expected outcomes, not a fixed plan.

### Sin 8: Premature Scaling (the #1 Failure Mode)
**Risk:** Hiring, spending, and building infrastructure before the business model is validated.
**Mitigation:** Stage spending to validation milestones; burn rate increases only after proof points.
**In the business plan:** Show a staged hiring and spending plan with clear trigger points for each increase.

### Sin 9: Management by Crisis
**Risk:** Reacting to problems instead of systematic discovery; founder burnout.
**Mitigation:** Structured Customer Development process; regular decision gates; accountability systems.
**In the business plan:** Include a structured review cadence (weekly, monthly, quarterly).

## Premature Scaling Risk Assessment

Premature scaling is the single most common cause of startup failure. Check for these warning signs in any business plan:

| Warning Sign | What It Looks Like | Corrective Action |
|---|---|---|
| Hiring before validation | Team of 10+ before first paying customer | Reduce to founding team; validate first |
| Office before revenue | Fancy office, long lease, expensive equipment | Work from home/co-working; short leases |
| Full product before feedback | Building all features before testing any | Strip to MVP; test core hypothesis |
| Marketing spend before message | Big ad budget before knowing what resonates | Small experiments; test messaging first |
| Multi-city before one city | Expanding geographically before dominating locally | Focus on one market; prove unit economics |

## Assumptions Tracking & Risk Score

### Assumptions Tracking Template (Alam)

| # | Assumption | Impact | Assignee | Due Date | Status | Comments |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "Customers will pay UGX X" | Critical | Founder | Week 3 | Validated | 5/7 paid in preselling test |
| 2 | "Market size is Z units/year" | Major | Analyst | Week 2 | In Progress | Checking UBOS data |
| 3 | "Supplier delivers at cost Q" | Minor | Ops | Week 4 | Disproved | Actual cost 15% higher |

**Impact classifications:**
- **Minor** — if wrong, can adjust without major consequence (weight: 1)
- **Major** — if wrong, requires significant business model changes (weight: 5)
- **Critical** — if wrong, the entire venture may not be viable (weight: 25)

**Status lifecycle:** New → In Progress → Validated / Disproved

### Risk Score Formula

```
Risk Score = (Minor unvalidated × 1) + (Major unvalidated × 5) + (Critical unvalidated × 25)
```

**Target:** Keep Risk Score below 100. Track the trajectory over time — a declining Risk Score shows systematic de-risking.

**Stage-level breakdown:**
- Desirability risk score (do customers want it?)
- Feasibility risk score (can we build/deliver it?)
- Viability risk score (will it make money?)

### Risk Score in Business Plans

Present the Risk Score trajectory to investors:
- Starting Risk Score (all assumptions unvalidated)
- Current Risk Score (after Customer Development activities)
- Remaining risks and validation plan

This quantifies risk management rather than just listing risks narratively.

## Reversible vs. Irreversible Decisions

Framework for classifying business decisions by reversibility (Blank & Dorf, 2012):

| Decision Type | Characteristics | Approach | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Reversible** | Low cost to undo; can learn and adjust | Decide fast; bias toward action | Pricing changes, marketing copy, feature toggles |
| **Irreversible** | High cost or impossible to undo | Decide carefully; gather evidence first | Long-term leases, equipment purchases, hiring, partnerships |

**Application in risk analysis:** Classify each major business plan decision as reversible or irreversible. For irreversible decisions, require stronger validation evidence before committing. This prevents premature scaling while maintaining speed on low-risk decisions.

## Risk Type Distinction

Two fundamental types of startup risk (Blank & Dorf, 2012):

| Risk Type | Definition | Mitigation Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Technology/invention risk** | Can we build it? Does the technology work? | Prototyping, technical proof of concept, pilot projects |
| **Customer/market risk** | Do customers want it? Will they pay? Is the market large enough? | Customer Development, preselling, market validation |

Most Ugandan startups face primarily **customer/market risk** (the technology usually exists; the question is whether the market will adopt it). Business plans should allocate validation effort proportional to the dominant risk type.
