# Operational Due Diligence — Scharfman (PE Standards)

> Scharfman is the Managing Partner of Corgentum Consulting and former head of ODD at Morgan Stanley Graystone Research. The book is the field's leading practitioner reference. All citations below are from this source.

---

## 1. What Is Operational Due Diligence?

### Definition
Operational due diligence (ODD) is the systematic assessment of **non-investment risks** in a fund or business. Where investment due diligence asks "Will this generate returns?", ODD asks "Is the organisation operationally capable of executing its strategy without losing money to fraud, systems failure, poor controls, or regulatory breach?"

ODD is described as a **diagnostic process** — ongoing, not a one-time event. Scharfman emphasises that it is "more art than science" and that thorough analysis "allows investors to detect funds that will have an increased likelihood for underperformance or failure in the event of unexpected stresses."

### The Complete Due Diligence Equation
Total Due Diligence = Investment Due Diligence + Operational Due Diligence

Both halves must be present. An investment review without ODD leaves the equation "unbalanced and incomplete."

### Core Operational Risk Categories (Exhibit 1.1)
The ten standard ODD risk categories are:

| Category | What it covers |
|---|---|
| Cash controls | Authorisation, segregation of duties, transfer protocols |
| Trade life cycle processing | Execution, confirmation, settlement, reconciliation |
| Valuation | Methodology, consistency, independence |
| Transparency and fund reporting | Frequency, accuracy, format |
| Liquidity management | Working capital, cash forecasting, stress |
| Technology and systems | Software, hardware, data integrity |
| Legal and compliance | Regulatory status, compliance infrastructure |
| Counterparty oversight | Vendor and banking relationships |
| Quality of service providers | Auditor, legal counsel, administrator |
| Business continuity and disaster recovery | BCP/DR plans, data backup, succession |

## 2. The ODD Process: Sequence and Scope

### Process Stages
1. **Goal self-assessment** — investor defines what they are trying to learn and why
2. **Programme design** — scope (core vs. expanded), resources, framework type
3. **Document collection** — DDQ, financial statements, legal documents, policies
4. **On-site visit** — management interviews, physical inspection, question design
5. **Analysis and conclusions** — operational opinion, red flags, scoring
6. **Ongoing monitoring** — periodic re-review, event-triggered reassessment

### Core vs. Expanded Review
Scharfman distinguishes two levels:

**Core review** includes:
- General firm overview and AUM
- Trade flow analysis
- Cash oversight, management, and transfer controls
- Compliance infrastructure
- Valuation policies and processes
- Legal and regulatory status
- Service provider quality
- Reporting

**Expanded review** adds:
- Technology and systems
- Information security
- Human capital depth
- Business continuity and disaster recovery
- Operations connectivity
- Tax practices
- Insurance coverage
- Counterparty management
- Administration agreement review

### Document Collection: What Investors Request
A thorough ODD document request should include:

- [ ] Due diligence questionnaire (DDQ)
- [ ] Audited financial statements (minimum 3 years)
- [ ] Private placement memorandum (PPM) or equivalent
- [ ] Limited partnership agreement or shareholders agreement
- [ ] Compliance manual or compliance policies
- [ ] Valuation policies and procedures document
- [ ] Business continuity and disaster recovery plan
- [ ] Organisation chart
- [ ] List of service providers (auditor, legal counsel, administrator, custodian)
- [ ] Insurance certificates
- [ ] Employee non-compete and confidentiality agreements
- [ ] Historical performance track record
- [ ] Background investigation consent forms
- [ ] Any existing side letters from other investors
- [ ] Technology systems inventory
- [ ] Details of any past or pending litigation

### Signalling Effects
How a manager responds to document requests is itself an ODD data point. Delays, redactions, or "pass-the-buck" deflections to service providers are early red flags. Scharfman notes: **"Investors cannot simply take the private equity manager's word for it."**

## 3. ODD Red Flags: Warning Signs That Concern PE Investors

Scharfman identifies red flags throughout the book. The most critical are:

### A. Valuation Red Flags
- Manager resists quarterly valuation or pushes back on transparency: **"Investors should be wary of any GP that pushes back on valuation frequency and transparency."**
- No written valuation policies and procedures document
- Same person makes investment decisions and sets valuations (no separation)
- Valuation methodology inconsistent with IPEV guidelines or GAAP/IFRS
- Historical performance restatements — a significant red flag
- Level 3 assets (illiquid, unobservable) held without third-party valuation verification

### B. Cash Controls Red Flags
- Sole signatory authority for cash transfers (no dual authorisation)
- Management fees withdrawn "at will" or in advance without board approval
- Cash transfers to related-party or principal-owned accounts
- No audit trail linking cash movements to investment decisions
- Commingling of fund assets with manager's personal or operating accounts

### C. Documentation and Compliance Red Flags
- No compliance manual or compliance officer (even part-time)
- No written BCP/DR plan
- Historical regulatory sanctions, even if resolved
- Absence of a due diligence file (no documented process for how investment decisions were made)
- Legal documents (PPM, shareholders agreement) drafted without professional counsel
- Indemnity and exculpatory clauses that are unusually broad or miss standard exceptions

### D. Governance and Personnel Red Flags
- Key person risk unmitigated — no successors named, no key person clause
- High employee turnover, especially in CFO or COO roles
- Senior management cannot be contacted post-departure as a reference
- Outside business interests of principals competing with the fund
- No independent board oversight of management decisions
- Background investigation reveals litigation, regulatory sanctions, or misrepresentation of credentials

### E. Reporting and Transparency Red Flags
- Audited financial statements unavailable, overdue, or inconsistent across years
- Unqualified audit opinion treated as "clean" without reading the notes — Scharfman warns: **"An unqualified opinion can in fact reveal that everything is not okay."**
- Performance estimates distributed without audit basis
- No independent administrator calculating NAV
- Investor reporting frequency or format less than quarterly

## 4. Valuation — Techniques, Standards, and Scrutiny

### The Two Valuation Questions PE Investors Ask
1. How does the fund calculate its own overall performance (fund-level NAV)?
2. How does the fund value individual portfolio company holdings? (The primary ODD focus)

### Applicable Valuation Standards

**IPEV Guidelines** (International Private Equity and Venture Capital Valuation Guidelines): the principal methodology framework for PE fund valuation. Central concept: **fair value** = "the price at which an orderly transaction would take place between market participants at the reporting date."

**GIPS** (Global Investment Performance Standards, CFA Institute): provides IRR calculation standards and performance reporting norms. The annualised IRR formula:

```
IRR formula: Solve for rIRR where
Sum of [CFi / (1 + rIRR)^(ti/TD)] + VE / (1 + rIRR) = VB
```

**FAS 157 / FASB ASC 820** (US GAAP fair value measurement): defines fair value as the **exit price** — "the price that would be received to sell an asset in an orderly transaction between market participants at the measurement date." Critical shift: exit price, not cost/entry price.

FAS 157 three-level hierarchy:
- **Level 1**: Observable market prices (e.g., listed stocks)
- **Level 2**: Observable inputs from similar assets
- **Level 3**: Unobservable inputs (most private equity assets fall here)

Level 3 assets require **expanded disclosures** of methodology and assumptions.

### IPEV Valuation Methodologies

| Method | Appropriate Use |
|---|---|
| Price of recent investment (at cost) | Start-up, seed, early-stage — accuracy decays over time |
| Multiples (EV/EBITDA, P/E, EV/EBIT) | Established businesses with identifiable income |
| Net assets | Property-holding companies, fund-of-funds |
| Discounted cash flows (underlying business) | Established and early-stage companies |
| DCF from the investment | Near-realisation or pending flotation |
| Industry valuation benchmarks | Limited use; cross-check only |

### IPEV Enterprise Value Adjustments Required
The Enterprise Value must:
- Be adjusted for surplus assets or excess liabilities
- Deduct instruments ranking ahead in liquidation priority
- Allocate appropriately across the capital structure

### Milestone Analysis for Early-Stage Companies (IPEV Exhibit 5.2)
Valuations should be reconsidered when milestones occur, including:

| Financial | Technical | Market |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue growth | Development phases | Customer surveys |
| Profitability trajectory | Patent approvals | Market introduction |
| Cash burn rate | Testing cycles | Market share changes |
| Covenant compliance | | |

### Valuation Process Requirements
For valuation to survive ODD scrutiny, the following must be in place:

- [ ] Written valuation policies and procedures document
- [ ] Valuation committee with documented terms of reference
- [ ] Separation of the person making investment decisions from the person setting valuations
- [ ] Quarterly valuation process (at minimum)
- [ ] Consistent methodology applied across periods (changes documented and justified)
- [ ] Third-party valuation consultant engaged for Level 3 assets (or at minimum periodically)
- [ ] Valuation output documented: methodology used, inputs, comparable data, conclusion
- [ ] Board or committee sign-off on valuations before investor reporting

### Use of Third-Party Valuation Consultants
PE investors increasingly expect third-party validation for illiquid or complex assets. Criteria for evaluating such consultants include independence from the investment decision-makers, appropriate professional qualifications, and methodology consistent with IPEV/FAS 157.

## 5. Liquidity Analysis

### What PE Investors Mean by Liquidity Risk
In a PE/DFI context, liquidity risk includes:
1. **Fund-level liquidity**: Whether the fund/business has sufficient cash to meet capital calls, operational expenses, and obligations at all times
2. **Portfolio company liquidity**: Whether underlying businesses have adequate working capital
3. **Stress liquidity**: Whether the entity can survive a period of severely reduced revenues or unexpected cash demands

### Cash Management System Requirements
A business seeking PE-level investment must demonstrate:

- [ ] Segregation of duties between those who authorise payments and those who execute them
- [ ] Dual authorisation for wire transfers or cash disbursements above a defined threshold
- [ ] Bank reconciliations completed monthly (and independently reviewed)
- [ ] A defined cash reserve policy (minimum operating runway, typically 3–6 months)
- [ ] Capital call management process (for fund structures)
- [ ] No commingling of business funds with personal or related-party accounts
- [ ] All cash movements traceable to authorised investment or operating decisions

### Cash Controls as an ODD Category (Exhibit 1.1)
Scharfman places "cash controls" as the first item in the ODD risk category list. In the case studies, the Onyx Capital fraud illustrates the consequences: the manager "withdrew management fees at will... double-billed funds for certain fees... and treated the pension funds' investments as their own pot of cash." The red flag was the absence of dual authorisation and independent oversight.

### Liquidity Stress Testing
While Scharfman addresses this primarily in the fund management context, the principle applies to portfolio companies: investors want to see that management has considered liquidity under adverse scenarios, not merely a base-case cash flow.

## 6. Financial Controls and Documentation

### Audit Standards Accepted by PE Investors
For US-based funds: **US GAAS** (Generally Accepted Auditing Standards, AICPA); post-Sarbanes-Oxley, PCAOB for public companies and ASB for private.
For non-US funds: **International Standards on Auditing (ISA)**, issued by IFAC/IAASB.

**Financial statements must be presented under GAAP or IFRS** — modified or non-standard formats are not acceptable to PE investors: "It is not likely that investors would be willing to provide capital to funds without audited financial statements routed in GAAP or IFRS."

### Key Financial Statement Components Reviewed in ODD
A PE-level review of financial statements examines:

1. **Statement of Assets and Liabilities** (Balance Sheet) — asset composition, leverage level, off-balance-sheet items
2. **Statement of Operations** (P&L) — revenue recognition policies, expense categorisation, management fees as an expense line
3. **Statement of Cash Flows** — operating vs. investing vs. financing activities; under IAS 7 this is required; under US GAAP exemptions exist but are scrutinised
4. **Statement of Changes in Net Assets** — capital flows, distributions, valuations
5. **Schedule of Investments** — detailed listing of portfolio holdings with valuation methodology per holding
6. **Financial Highlights** — per-unit performance data, IRR history
7. **Notes to Financial Statements** — accounting policies, related-party transactions, contingent liabilities, legal proceedings, significant subsequent events

### Audit Opinion Types
- **Unqualified opinion**: Auditor found no material misstatement — but **does not mean "clean."** Investors must read the notes carefully.
- **Qualified opinion**: Auditor took exception to one or more accounting treatments — a red flag requiring explanation
- **Adverse opinion** or **disclaimer**: Major failure — generally disqualifying

**Scharfman's key warning**: "An unqualified opinion is often thought to mean that everything is okay, whereas it can in fact reveal that everything is not okay, and that more due diligence [is] required."

### Audit Materiality Considerations
Auditors set materiality thresholds — items below the threshold may not be specifically identified in the audit opinion even if present. ODD analysis should look beyond the opinion to the notes and supporting schedules.

### Internal Controls Framework
Scharfman references COSO (Committee of Sponsoring Organizations of the Treadway Commission) as the implied standard for internal controls assessment. Key requirements for PE-readiness:

- [ ] Documented authorisation matrix (who can approve what, at what values)
- [ ] Segregation of duties: the person recording a transaction is not the same as the person authorising it or reconciling it
- [ ] Regular internal controls review (at least annual)
- [ ] Conflict-of-interest policies in writing
- [ ] Compliance manual covering employee conduct, personal trading, MNPI (material non-public information)
- [ ] Records retention policy aligned with applicable regulatory requirements

### Audit Certification Standards by Jurisdiction (Exhibit 12.1)
| Country | Standard |
|---|---|
| United States | SAS No. 70 / SSAE 16 |

## 7. Governance and Compliance

### Board Composition: What PE Investors Expect
Scharfman's Chapter 10 is dedicated to advisory boards. At the portfolio company level, PE investors look for:

- [ ] A board with genuinely independent members (not all controlled by the founder/CEO)
- [ ] Key person clause naming critical individuals and defining succession triggers
- [ ] A compliance function: ideally a dedicated CCO; for smaller firms, at minimum an outsourced compliance adviser
- [ ] Advisory board meeting minutes — documented decisions, quorum, and votes
- [ ] Documented conflicts of interest policy
- [ ] Information flow process: how does management report to the board, and how frequently?

### Key Person Risk Mitigation
PE investors require explicit answers to:
- Which individuals are named as key persons?
- Does the key person clause require one or multiple named individuals to trigger?
- What is the scope of the clause (death, incapacitation, departure)?
- Is there a succession plan?
- Does the GP/business provide penalty-fee redemptions or capital return if a key person clause triggers?

### Compliance Infrastructure Checklist
- [ ] Written compliance manual (updated at least annually)
- [ ] Employee personal account dealing policy
- [ ] Restricted list maintained for insider trading prevention
- [ ] Pre-trade approval process for employee trades
- [ ] Post-trade review process
- [ ] Electronic communication monitoring policy
- [ ] Compliance training conducted at least annually
- [ ] Regulatory registration confirmed and up to date
- [ ] No pending or historical regulatory sanctions (or full explanation if present)

### Legal Documents Required for ODD
- [ ] Fund formation documents (limited partnership agreement, articles of association)
- [ ] Private placement memorandum (PPM) or investment memorandum
- [ ] Subscription documents
- [ ] Side letters (all existing ones disclosed)
- [ ] Service provider contracts (audit engagement letter, legal retainer, administrator agreement)
- [ ] Insurance certificates (D&O, E&O, key person)

## 8. Operational Infrastructure

### Technology and Systems
ODD at the expanded level reviews:
- What accounting/ERP system is used? Is it appropriate for the business size and complexity?
- Are systems customised and by whom? Is there vendor support?
- What is the data backup process? How frequently are backups made and tested?
- Is there information security (firewalls, access controls, password policies, penetration testing)?
- What happens if a system goes down — is there a tested recovery procedure?

### Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery (BCP/DR)
PE investors specifically ask:
- [ ] Written BCP/DR plan exists
- [ ] Plan has been tested (most recently when?)
- [ ] Technology AND personnel testing conducted
- [ ] Alternative gathering location designated
- [ ] Data backup facilities confirmed
- [ ] Backup power generation available
- [ ] Cross-office coordination process documented (if multiple locations)

### Service Provider Quality
PE investors evaluate each service provider, not just their existence. Key questions:

**Auditor**:
- Is the firm independent and reputable?
- Is the engagement team experienced in this asset class?
- How long has this auditor been engaged?

**Legal Counsel**:
- Does the firm have appropriate jurisdictional expertise?
- Are they independent from management?

**Fund Administrator / Independent Administrator**:
- Is NAV calculated independently by the administrator?
- Does the administrator reconcile transactions independently?
- What is the data feed process from the fund to the administrator?

**Custodian**:
- Are assets held at an independent custodian?
- Can the investor confirm balances directly with the custodian?
- Is there a service level agreement (SLA)?

### Insurance Coverage
PE investors expect coverage for:
- Directors and Officers liability (D&O)
- Errors and Omissions (E&O)
- Key Person life and disability insurance on critical principals
- Fidelity/crime insurance

The fund should be able to explain how coverage amounts were determined, who the carriers are, and whether any claims have been filed.

## 9. Compensation and Alignment of Interest

PE investors examine compensation as a proxy for alignment:

**Three core compensation questions**:
1. How are principals and key employees compensated? Is it linked to fund performance?
2. Are compensation packages competitive enough to retain talent?
3. Does compensation vest over time, aligning long-term interests?

**Skin in the game**: Investors want principals to have invested meaningfully in the fund/business. A vague answer ("a significant portion of liquid net worth") is unacceptable — Scharfman calls this "effectively useless to investors." A percentage or dollar figure is required.

**Vesting mechanisms**:
- **Time vesting**: Compensation vests as long as the employee stays
- **Performance vesting**: Vesting triggered by achieving specific fund or portfolio metrics (e.g., EBITDA targets)
- **Cliff vesting**: Nothing received if departure occurs before the vesting cliff is reached

## 10. Business Plan Operational Standards

### What Operational Evidence to Include in a Business Plan Targeting PE/DFI Investment

#### Section 08 — Operations Plan
Include:
- Documented process maps for core operational workflows (production, service delivery, procurement)
- Technology and systems inventory: accounting software name, banking platform, reporting tools
- Cash management policy statement: authorisation levels, dual-signatory thresholds, bank reconciliation frequency
- Business continuity summary: what happens to operations if a key system, facility, or person is unavailable?
- Service provider list: auditor, legal counsel, bank, insurance carriers
- Quality control process and how it is documented

#### Section 09 — Management Team
Include:
- Full CVs for all key principals including previous employer references
- Explicit key person risk statement: who are the critical individuals and what succession provisions exist?
- Board composition description: independent directors named, their backgrounds
- Compliance officer or function identified
- Compensation structure described (including any performance-linked elements)
- Conflict-of-interest disclosure and policy reference
- Any regulatory registrations or professional licences

#### Section 10 — Financial Projections
Include:
- Audited financial statements for prior years (or at minimum management accounts with a note on audit timeline)
- Written assumptions document for all projections
- Explicit valuation methodology: how is the business valued and what comparable evidence supports it?
- Working capital analysis demonstrating adequacy for the projection period
- Stress scenario: what happens to cash flow under a 20–30% revenue shortfall?
- Schedule of all related-party transactions (any transactions between the business and entities or individuals connected to the founders)
- Internal controls description: who approves expenditures, who reconciles accounts

## 11. Valuation Defence — Presenting Valuations That Survive PE Scrutiny

### Core Principle
PE investors view the exit price (what a willing buyer would pay today) as fair value — not what you paid for assets and not what you hope they will be worth. Valuations must be supportable by observable market data or clearly documented assumptions.

### Cross-Reference with Damodaran
Damodaran (Damodaran on Valuation, see skills suite) emphasises that DCF models are only as good as their assumptions. Scharfman echoes this: DCF terminal value assumptions can result in "insufficiently reliable" fair values if not grounded in defensible inputs. Both sources converge on the same point: **document every assumption and show your comparables.**

### Valuation Defence Checklist for Business Plan Appendices
- [ ] State the valuation methodology used (e.g., EBITDA multiples, DCF)
- [ ] Provide the source and date of comparable transaction or trading multiples used
- [ ] Show the EBITDA or revenue base being multiplied
- [ ] Apply a documented discount for illiquidity and minority interest if applicable
- [ ] Show sensitivity analysis: how does the valuation change if the key variable shifts ±20%?
- [ ] Confirm the valuation has been reviewed by an independent professional (accountant, valuation specialist)
- [ ] Include a statement of consistency: "This methodology has been applied consistently with prior periods"
- [ ] Disclose any factors that may cause the valuation to be overstated (e.g., current losses, pending litigation)

### Common Valuation Manipulations PE Investors Look For
- Keeping the "price of recent investment" methodology too long after the initial investment, even when fundamentals have materially changed
- Selecting revenue multiples from transactions in different markets, geographies, or time periods without adjustment
- Including "synergy" or "control premium" values that are speculative
- DCF models that use terminal growth rates substantially above industry norms
- Failing to deduct debt or adjust for working capital deficits when deriving equity value from enterprise value
- Valuing assets at cost when cost exceeds current market value (overoptimism)

## 12. Documentation Minimum Standards Table

| Document Type | What It Must Contain | Red Flag If Absent or Deficient |
|---|---|---|
| Audited financial statements | GAAP/IFRS standard; auditor's opinion letter; notes to accounts; schedule of investments; related-party disclosures | No audited statements; overdue audit; non-standard format |
| Valuation policies document | Methodology hierarchy; approval process; frequency of revaluation; how disputes are resolved | No written policy; valuation done by the same person who invests |
| Compliance manual | Code of conduct; personal trading policy; MNPI/insider trading policy; complaints process | No manual; no designated compliance officer |
| BCP/DR plan | Triggers, responsibilities, alternative site, data backup, last test date | No plan; never tested |
| Shareholder/partnership agreement | Economic terms; key person clause; voting rights; exit provisions; anti-dilution | Draft only; missing key provisions; no independent legal review |
| Cash transfer authorisation policy | Dual-signatory thresholds; approval chain; wire transfer controls; documentation required | Single signatory for all amounts; no audit trail |
| Organisational chart | Reporting lines; clear identification of who controls what functions | Informal or absent; roles overlap dangerously |
| Service provider agreements | Scope, fees, SLA, termination rights for each key provider | Verbal-only arrangements; no SLA; no conflict-of-interest provisions |
| Board/advisory board minutes | Decisions recorded; quorum confirmed; frequency of meetings stated | No formal meetings; no minutes; "founder decides everything" |
| Insurance certificates | D&O, E&O, key person, fidelity/crime; current and from rated carriers | No insurance; coverage inadequate for business size |
| Background check documentation | Verification of prior employment, qualifications, litigation history for all principals | Not performed; refused |
| Due diligence file | Record of the investment decision-making process | No record; decisions made informally without documentation |

## 13. Uganda / East Africa Adaptation Notes

### The Core Challenge
Scharfman wrote for US/UK institutional investors reviewing mature fund managers. In Uganda and East Africa, most SMEs approaching PE or DFI investment operate in an environment with:
- Thin compliance infrastructure (no SEC equivalent for private companies)
- Limited availability of Big Four audit firms outside Kampala
- A majority of firms relying on informal or mobile-money-based financial records
- Low penetration of sophisticated accounting software
- Governance structures that are typically founder-controlled, with no independent directors

This does not mean ODD standards are lowered — it means Ugandan businesses must demonstrate **equivalent intent and process** using proportionate means.

### Adaptation Guidance by Category

**Audited financial statements**: ICPAU-member auditors are acceptable. IFRS is the required standard in Uganda. If the business is too small for a full IFRS audit, a Review Engagement (lower assurance) from a qualified accountant is the minimum. Mobile money statements, bank reconciliations, and EFRIS-generated records can supplement.

**Valuation**: Apply IPEV multiples methodology using East African comparables (regional M&A databases: AVCA, BoU reports, sector-specific research). Apply an illiquidity discount appropriate to the Ugandan market (typically 15–30% for unlisted equity). Document the source of every comparable.

**Cash controls**: Dual authorisation is achievable at any scale using mobile money (two-signatory requirements on MTN MoMo business accounts or Airtel Money Business). Demonstrate this in the business plan.

**Compliance**: A written "ethics and compliance policy" of even 2–3 pages covering employee conduct, conflicts of interest, and cash handling is far better than nothing. DFI investors (AfDB, IFC, UDB) now require this.

**Board governance**: Even a single independent non-executive director — a retired banker, sector expert, or respected professional — transforms the risk profile from a PE investor's perspective. Document their role, attendance, and contribution.

**Key person risk**: In Uganda, with a shallow management talent pool (see ugandan-labour-market-2025.md), PE investors will focus heavily on key person risk. Identify a deputy or clear succession path. Purchase key person insurance through UAP, APA Insurance, or another rated carrier.

**BCP/DR**: For a small Ugandan business, a BCP can be a 1–2 page document addressing: what happens if the owner is incapacitated (who takes over), if the main facility is unavailable (alternative site), if data is lost (backup). Cloud-based accounting systems (QuickBooks Online, Sage 50) provide basic DR automatically.

**Service providers**: List all service providers explicitly. Use firms that are members of recognised professional bodies (ICPAU for accountants, Uganda Law Society for lawyers, URSB for company secretaries). This signals quality to investors unfamiliar with the local landscape.

### Three Red Flags That Kill PE Deals for Ugandan Businesses
See Section 3 of this document for the full red flag list. The top three most common deal-killers for East African businesses approaching PE/DFI capital:

1. **No audited financial statements** — or statements that are several years out of date, unaudited, or prepared by an unqualified person. This is the single most common disqualifying factor. DFI investors (IFC, UDB, AfDB) will not proceed without IFRS-compliant financial statements for at least the past 2–3 years.

2. **Founder-as-sole-signatory cash controls** — where all financial decisions flow through one person with no oversight, no reconciliation process, and no record. This is the structural feature most associated with the fraud cases described by Scharfman (Onyx Capital, AA Capital, etc.). It signals both operational weakness and governance failure.

3. **No written governance documents** — no shareholders agreement, no board minutes, no valuation policy, no compliance manual. PE investors interpret the absence of written governance as evidence that informal arrangements exist which may disadvantage new investors. This is especially critical for Uganda, where customary practices and verbal agreements are common in family businesses.

## 14. Ongoing Monitoring — What Investors Expect Post-Investment

Scharfman devotes Chapter 9 to ongoing monitoring. For businesses that have received PE/DFI investment, ongoing ODD monitoring typically requires:

- **Quarterly financial reporting** (P&L, balance sheet, cash flow statement) against budget
- **Annual audited financial statements** delivered within 90–120 days of fiscal year end
- **Board meeting minutes** provided to investors within 30 days of each meeting
- **Material event notification**: immediate notification of any litigation, regulatory action, key person departure, or material adverse change
- **Annual operational review**: investor or their consultant revisits key ODD areas
- **Covenant compliance certificates**: if debt is involved, regular DSCR confirmation
- **Valuation updates**: quarterly or semi-annual, methodology consistent with prior periods

The Minimum Operational Risk Regime (MORR) model in Scharfman's Chapter 9 formalises this: total operational risk across a portfolio is minimised by actively managing the individual risk categories — not by ignoring them once the initial investment is made.

## 15. Summary Checklist: "PE-Ready" Operations Standard

A business is operationally ready for PE/DFI investment when it can demonstrate:

### Governance
- [ ] Independent board member(s) in place and attending documented meetings
- [ ] Shareholders/partnership agreement in place, professionally drafted
- [ ] Key person clause and succession identified
- [ ] Conflict-of-interest policy in writing

### Financial Controls
- [ ] IFRS-compliant audited financial statements (2–3 years minimum)
- [ ] ICPAU-member auditor engaged
- [ ] Dual-signatory cash authorisation above defined threshold
- [ ] Monthly bank reconciliations reviewed by someone other than the preparer
- [ ] No commingling of personal and business funds

### Valuation
- [ ] Written valuation methodology document
- [ ] Enterprise value calculated using observable comparables
- [ ] Discounts applied for illiquidity and minority interest where relevant
- [ ] Valuation reviewed by independent professional
- [ ] Sensitivity analysis prepared

### Compliance and Legal
- [ ] Written compliance/ethics policy
- [ ] All regulatory registrations current (URA TIN, URSB, sector licences)
- [ ] No undisclosed litigation or regulatory sanctions
- [ ] Service provider agreements in writing

### Operational Infrastructure
- [ ] Accounting system in use (even QuickBooks or Sage)
- [ ] Data backed up (cloud or external drive, tested)
- [ ] Basic BCP/DR plan written and communicated to key staff
- [ ] Written job descriptions for key roles (segregation of duties)

### Reporting
- [ ] Ability to produce monthly management accounts within 15 days of month end
- [ ] Budget vs. actual variance analysis available
- [ ] Capital expenditure tracked separately from operating expenditure
- [ ] Related-party transactions fully disclosed and at arm's length
