# Due Diligence in Business Transactions — Berkman

## Overview

Berkman is a venture capital and business lawyer. This book presents due diligence through eight fictional case studies covering loans, asset purchases, business buyouts, private equity placements, real estate, partnerships, licensing deals, and franchise investments. Its core argument: due diligence is not an M&A-only tool — it applies to any significant business transaction, and sellers should conduct it on themselves before buyers do.

The book is structured as a practitioner's guide. It builds from definition → questionnaire design → transaction-specific tailoring → deal-killing issues → deal improvement through DD findings → resolution mechanisms → pragmatics and seller self-assessment.

---

## 1. What Due Diligence Is — Berkman's Definition

> "Due diligence involves the processes of examining and developing the necessary level of understanding of four factors — the company and its business operations (its products, assets, and/or services and how the company functions); determining an appropriate valuation of the business, assets, and transactions; and vetting management capabilities, key personnel and employment matters — before entering into a business transaction with, investing in, licensing assets or services of, or purchasing all or part of a business or its assets."

The **four business cornerstones** of every DD investigation:

| Cornerstone | DD Goal |
|---|---|
| The Company | Understand legal and financial structure; identify organisational/structural risks |
| Business Operations | Understand products, assets, services; assess operational aspects; identify legal, financial, and business risks |
| Valuation | Determine appropriate valuation; identify financial risks |
| Personnel | Identify key people; ascertain capability to operate the business and execute plans |

Every DD question maps back to one or more of these four cornerstones. This is the analytical lens Berkman applies throughout.

## 2. Transactions That Require Due Diligence

Berkman explicitly lists transactions where DD is **routine**:
- Loan transactions
- Purchase of a business or its assets
- Company purchase (merger or business buyout)
- Purchase of stock/membership interests (private placement)
- Purchase of real estate

Transactions where DD is **equally important but often overlooked**:
- Purchasing a franchise from a franchisor
- Starting a company with a partner
- Licensing a product, service, technology, or IP from a third party
- Licensing out to a third party
- Entering a joint venture
- Engaging a third party to design/develop a product or technology
- Engaging a third party to manufacture or supply
- Entering into material long-term contracts

**Key insight:** The size of the deal does not determine the scope of DD. The **nature of the transaction** determines the scope. A $500,000 loan and a $5,000,000 loan secured by the same type of collateral require the same DD questions.

## 3. The Business Plan as a DD Document

Berkman frames this directly:

> "Think of due diligence as a process of first hearing the company's business story — as it may have been provided in an investment presentation or business plan — and then conducting an investigation to see if the story could be corroborated."

> "The questionnaire is used to ask the company owners and other employees a series of questions designed to test the veracity of the story the owners have presented regarding their business."

The **due diligence plan** is constructed directly from the claims in the business plan and deal documents. Every material assertion — about revenues, ownership, contracts, market position, management capability — becomes a line item in the DD questionnaire.

### How Investors Read a Business Plan Through a DD Lens

An investor does not read a business plan to be convinced; they read it to identify what must be independently verified. The business plan generates the DD plan. The DD plan generates the questionnaire. The questionnaire generates the findings that determine whether the deal proceeds, on what terms, and at what price.

Claims that become DD investigation targets:
- Revenue figures and growth assumptions
- Customer relationships and contract status
- Ownership of key assets (IP, software, equipment)
- Management track record and background
- Regulatory compliance status
- Market size and competitive position assertions
- Financial projections and their stated assumptions

### The "Verification-Ready" Business Plan

A business plan that anticipates DD scrutiny will:
1. Source every material claim (market data, financial benchmarks)
2. Maintain documents that corroborate ownership (contribution agreements, IP assignments, incorporation documents)
3. Ensure contracts are assignable to a new owner
4. Have financial records that reconcile to tax returns
5. Have written employment agreements and IP assignment clauses with all employees
6. Be able to produce, on request, the evidence behind every projection assumption

## 4. The Due Diligence Plan

Before writing a questionnaire, Berkman insists on drafting a **due diligence plan** — a strategy document that:
- Identifies the key aspects of the proposed transaction
- Maps each aspect to the four business cornerstones
- Flags known issues at the early stages of the deal
- Guides scope and focus of the questionnaire

**Structure of a DD Plan (from the KMF Franchise case study):**

```
I. Corporate Structure
   - Legal formation and governance documents
   - Ownership structure and capitalization
   - Approval rights and restrictions

II. Business Operations
   - Nature of the business and revenue model
   - Products, services, assets
   - Key contracts and assignability
   - Operational workflows and policies

III. Valuation
   - Financial statements (3–6 years)
   - Revenue by source and by location
   - Top customers by revenue
   - Asset valuations
   - Growth assumptions

IV. Personnel
   - Organisational chart
   - Key employee terms and retention risk
   - Compliance with employment laws
   - IP/confidentiality agreements with staff
```

> "No matter how small the transaction, create some version of a due diligence plan. Even an outline that contains key issues can help you draw out the initial issues and keep you on track."

## 5. Standard Due Diligence Questions — All Transaction Types

### Topic 1: Basic Company Information

- Legal name, assumed names (d/b/a), and jurisdiction of formation
- Good standing certificates for all operating jurisdictions
- All formation documents (articles of incorporation, operating agreement, bylaws, amendments)
- Corporate records books (minutes, resolutions, consents)
- All changes to identity or corporate structure
- Capitalization table: all shareholders/members, options, warrants, convertible rights
- Shareholder agreements, investor rights agreements, voting agreements
- Rights of first refusal affecting assets or equity

**Why this matters:** A tax lien filed under an assumed name will not appear if you search only under the registered name. A right of first refusal unknown to the buyer can void a transaction after closing.

### Topic 2: Ownership of Key Assets

- Proof of ownership (title, bill of sale, contribution agreement) for all material assets
- Confirmation that all founder/partner contributions have been formally assigned to the company
- IP ownership documentation: invention assignment agreements, copyright registrations, patent filings
- Any liens, encumbrances, or third-party claims against assets
- UCC lien searches against the company name and all assumed names

### Topic 3: Financial and Tax Matters

- Financial statements for past 3–6 years (balance sheets, P&L statements, cash flow)
- Tax returns for past 3–6 years, all jurisdictions
- Accounts receivable ageing schedule
- List of 10 largest customers by revenue for each of the past 5 years
- Top 5 revenue sources by product/service for each of the past 5 years
- Any notices of financial difficulty from major customers (including bankruptcy filings)
- Any outstanding tax liens, unpaid taxes, open audits
- All loan agreements, lines of credit, personal guarantees
- Capital expenditure plans

### Topic 4: Contracts and Undertakings

- All material agreements (revenue-generating or operationally critical)
- Key customer agreements: are they assignable? Are they terminable on change of control?
- Supplier/vendor agreements
- Lease agreements (real property and equipment): expiry dates, renewal terms, assignability
- Licence agreements (in and out): assignability, change of control provisions
- Any agreements granting third parties rights in the business or its assets
- Any non-compete or exclusivity agreements that bind the company or its key people

### Topic 5: Legal Claims and Proceedings

- All pending or threatened civil, criminal, or administrative actions
- Any monetary judgments against the company or its assets
- Any equitable judgments (orders prohibiting or requiring actions)
- Government investigations (tax authorities, environmental regulators, labour inspectors)
- Bankruptcy filings (voluntary or involuntary) in the past 10 years
- All settlements and consent decrees

### Topic 6: Employees and Key Personnel

- Organisational chart with titles and responsibilities
- Employment agreements for all key personnel
- Confidentiality, non-disclosure, invention assignment, and non-compete agreements for all employees, contractors, and former staff who had access to IP
- Compliance with payroll laws and benefits obligations
- Any pending or threatened employment claims
- Worker classification (employee vs. contractor)
- Retention risk for key employees post-transaction

## 6. Financial Due Diligence — Berkman's Approach

### Does the Financial Story Support the Valuation?

The central question of financial DD is: **do the financials support the valuation underpinning the transaction?**

For a lender: can the borrower repay? Is the collateral sufficient?
For a buyer: is the purchase price justified by current and projected financial performance?
For an investor: do projected returns rest on supportable assumptions?

### Common Financial DD Findings That Change Deal Terms

| Finding | Effect |
|---|---|
| Liabilities greater than represented | Reduce loan amount; demand personal guarantee; escrow holdback |
| Revenues declining | Reduce purchase price; require milestone-based payment (earn-out) |
| Operating expenses excessive | Expense covenants; lender approval threshold; management salary deferral |
| Profit margins shrinking | Renegotiate or walk |
| Collateral value unsupported by appraisal | Reduce loan principal; require additional collateral |
| Revenue concentrated in one customer | Concentration risk discount; earn-out tied to retention |
| Management salaries inconsistent with company stage | Salary reduction or deferral as deal condition |
| No financial controls | Require implementation as preclosing or postclosing condition |
| Tax issues (audits, unpaid taxes, sales tax exposure) | Tax opinions; escrow; representations and warranties |

### Financial Projections — How DD Investigators Evaluate Them

Berkman is explicit: unsupported valuation assumptions justify renegotiation or termination.

A DD investigator examining financial projections will ask:
1. Are revenue assumptions consistent with historical performance?
2. Are growth rates supported by market data or customer contracts?
3. Are cost assumptions complete (including indirect costs, taxes, depreciation)?
4. Are working capital requirements accounted for?
5. What is the sensitivity of projections to the departure of key customers or key staff?
6. Are there any one-time items in historical financials that inflate the apparent earnings baseline?
7. Do the projections assume continuation of relationships that are not contractually locked in?

**Earn-out structures** are the deal mechanism when projections are disputed: the seller receives the full price only if post-closing performance meets the projected targets.

## 7. Commercial and Market Due Diligence

### Customer Analysis

Berkman's enhanced financial questionnaire (his "Example 2") requests:
- Monthly revenues by location
- Top 10 customers by revenue for each of the past 5 years
- Bankruptcy notices or financial difficulty notices for top customers
- Top 5 revenue sources by product/service for each of the past 5 years

This reveals:
- Customer concentration risk (too many eggs in one basket)
- Revenue trend shifts (are the key customers changing, and why?)
- Financial fragility of the customer base
- Whether growth assumptions are realistic given customer trajectory

### Market Position Verification

DD investigators go beyond the business plan's assertions. They look for:
- Independent market data corroborating the TAM/SAM claims
- Whether the competitive advantages described are durable and documented
- Whether key contracts support the claimed market position
- What happens to revenues if one major contract is lost

### Revenue Model Stress Testing

When assessing value, DD goes beyond financials to ask:
- What is the demand for the company's products or services in the current market?
- Is there opportunity for growth, or is the market saturated?
- Is the revenue model dependent on a competitor's absence or a regulatory condition that may change?

## 8. Legal and Compliance Due Diligence

### Contract Review — What to Look For

The most common contract-related deal killers:
1. **Assignment clauses**: Material contracts that require consent before assignment to a buyer. Without consent, the contract may not transfer — the buyer gets the business but not the key relationships.
2. **Change of control clauses**: Contracts that terminate automatically on a change of ownership. Common in franchise agreements and many commercial contracts.
3. **Exclusivity provisions**: Contracts granting the company exclusive rights in a market that would lapse on assignment.
4. **Expiry risk**: Key contracts or leases near expiry — will they renew on the same terms?

Berkman's ABB case study illustrates contract assignment risk clearly: the seller assured the buyer the retail chain contracts were assignable; in fact, each required consent, and no retailer was obligated to give it.

### IP and Intangible Asset Verification

Key IP questions:
- Has a formal invention assignment been executed for all software, patents, or creative works developed by founders or early employees?
- Are there any former partners who contributed IP without a written contribution agreement?
- Have all employees and contractors signed IP assignment agreements?
- Are patents registered and current? (Search the relevant IP registry)
- Are there any conflicting claims, third-party licences, or liens against the IP?
- Is all licensed IP assignable or does it revert on change of control?

**Berkman's opening case study turns entirely on this point**: a founder developed software before incorporating the company, contributed it informally, but never executed a written assignment. The buyer's lawyer discovers no document proves the company owns the software — the deal nearly collapses.

### Regulatory Compliance

- Does the business hold all licences and permits required to operate?
- Are there pending inspections, violations, or investigations by regulatory authorities?
- Are there environmental liabilities (buried tanks, hazardous substances, remediation orders)?
- Has the company complied with employment and labour laws (payroll taxes, benefits, worker classification)?
- Are there data privacy obligations (especially if the business holds customer personal data)?

### Environmental Liabilities

Environmental issues are treated as potentially catastrophic:
- Phase 1 and Phase 2 environmental assessments required for any real property transaction
- Sellers must indemnify buyers and escrow funds against unknown environmental claims
- An environmental issue can make a transaction unviable if remediation costs are uncertain

## 9. Management and People Due Diligence

### Background Verification

Berkman's personal DD questionnaire asks all directors, managing members, and major shareholders:
- Criminal history (felony or misdemeanour, including nolo contendere pleas)
- Bankruptcy history
- Pending lawsuits (personal and spousal)
- Government investigations (regulatory, securities, administrative)
- Tax liabilities beyond current year; prior audits
- Material disputes with potential liability over $10,000
- All companies in which they hold equity of 5% or more
- Personal guarantees currently in force
- Non-compete or non-solicitation agreements currently in force
- Consent to credit check and full background check

### Track Record Verification

- Historical performance of the management team at this and prior companies
- References from customers, suppliers, and prior business partners
- Reputation in the industry (particularly relevant for franchise businesses where franchisor reference is available)

### Retention Risk

Berkman identifies this as a deal-specific DD priority: what happens to the business if key personnel leave after the transaction?

Solutions:
- Employment agreements with post-closing obligations
- Holdback of purchase price contingent on key employee retention for a defined period
- Non-solicitation agreements
- Equity grants or profit-share arrangements to incentivise retention

### Conflict of Interest

- Are any directors, officers, or shareholders parties to agreements with the company (related-party transactions)?
- Are there personal guarantees of company debt that create incentive misalignment?
- Are there non-compete agreements that could prevent founders from assisting post-closing?

## 10. Transaction Structuring After DD — How Findings Shape Terms

Berkman provides systematic tables of DD findings and corresponding deal responses. The key mechanisms:

### Preclosing Conditions

Issues that must be resolved **before** the deal closes. Examples from Berkman's case studies:

| DD Finding | Preclosing Condition |
|---|---|
| No operating agreement for the LLC | Execute operating agreement before closing |
| Fired bookkeeper still has bank signatory authority | Company must adopt resolution removing signatory authority |
| Franchise requires ownership change approval | Company must obtain franchisor consent |
| Health code violations outstanding | Company must address violations before closing |
| Former partner threatening to compete | Require execution of non-compete agreement |

### Postclosing Covenants

Issues resolved **after** closing, documented as obligations in the purchase agreement:

| DD Finding | Postclosing Covenant |
|---|---|
| Cross-marketing agreement not yet finalised | Seller must assist buyer to finalise after closing |
| Software update underway with third-party vendor | Seller pays all developer fees; cooperates until completion |
| Invention assignment missing from travelling partner | Obtain assignment within two weeks; purchase price held in escrow |
| Key employees may depart after payout | Holdback of purchase price, released if employees stay past defined date |
| Weak budgeting and financial planning | Company must prepare financial plans and budgets on schedule for investor review |
| No written workflow protocols | Company must prepare written policies by set date |

### Representations and Warranties

- Seller makes formal written statements about the state of the business at closing
- If any statement proves false, buyer has a claim against seller for breach of warranty
- Indemnification clauses back up warranties: seller agrees to compensate buyer for losses arising from undisclosed liabilities or misrepresentations
- Escrow holdbacks (typically 10–20% of purchase price) secure the indemnification obligation for a defined period

### Earn-Out Structures

When financial projections are disputed or uncertain, the purchase price is split:
- Upfront payment at closing based on verified historical performance
- Earn-out payments contingent on post-closing revenues or profit hitting agreed milestones
- Earn-out effectively transfers valuation risk from buyer to seller

### Purchase Price Adjustments

- Net working capital adjustments: if working capital at closing differs from the agreed target, purchase price adjusts accordingly
- Post-closing purchase price adjustments require a detailed mechanism in the agreement to avoid disputes

## 12. Business Plan Verification Table

For each major section of a business plan, a DD investigator will seek to independently verify the claims made. This table maps plan sections to DD investigation targets.

| Plan Section | Key Claims to Verify | Evidence Required |
|---|---|---|
| **01 Executive Summary** | Business concept, market opportunity, financial headline | Cross-check against all other sections for consistency |
| **02 Company Overview** | Legal structure, ownership, history, good standing | Formation documents, good standing certificate, capitalization table, corporate minutes |
| **03 Products & Services** | IP ownership, product/service description, uniqueness | Invention assignment agreements, patent/trademark registrations, product demonstrations or certifications |
| **04 Market Analysis** | Market size, growth rate, trends | Independent market research; cite sources; investor will cross-check |
| **05 Target Market** | Customer segments, purchasing behaviour | Customer references, sales data, market research sources |
| **06 Competitive Analysis** | Competitor identification, competitive advantage claims | Independent research; customer references confirming preference |
| **07 Marketing & Sales** | Sales pipeline, marketing effectiveness, CAC | Sales reports, CRM data, historical conversion rates |
| **08 Operations** | Production capacity, operational systems, workflow | Site visit; workflow documentation; equipment titles and valuations |
| **09 Management Team** | Qualifications, track record, relevant experience | CVs, reference checks, background checks, prior company performance data |
| **10 Financial Projections** | Revenue forecasts, cost assumptions, profitability timeline | Historical financial statements; reconciliation to tax returns; documented assumptions; sensitivity analysis |
| **11 Funding Request** | Amount needed, use of funds, repayment capacity | Detailed use-of-funds schedule; DSCR calculation; collateral documentation |
| **12 Risk Analysis** | Risk identification and mitigation | Cross-check against legal, financial, and market DD findings |
| **13 Implementation Timeline** | Milestone realism, resource requirements | Contracts with suppliers/contractors; staffing plan; regulatory timeline |
| **14 Appendices** | Supporting documents | Actual documents behind the plan's claims |

**The investor's test**: take the most optimistic statement in the plan and ask: what document, data point, or independent source would prove or disprove this? If the answer is "nothing" — the claim is unverifiable and will be treated as a risk.

## 13. Red Flags — 20 Most Common DD Findings That Kill Deals or Reduce Valuation

These are drawn from Berkman's case studies and explicit discussion of material issues:

1. **IP not formally assigned to the company** — founder or contractor created the IP but no written assignment exists; the company may not own what it is selling (Berkman's opening case study)
2. **Key contracts are not assignable** — buyer cannot inherit the customer or supplier relationships without consent that may not be forthcoming, or at substantial cost
3. **Change-of-control clauses in critical contracts** — franchise agreement, key customer contract, or technology licence terminates automatically on change of ownership
4. **Revenue concentrated in one or two customers** — loss of a single relationship destroys the business model
5. **Revenue or profit declining in the period just before the transaction** — the seller may be rushing to transact before the deterioration becomes fully visible
6. **Financial records not maintained or inconsistent with tax returns** — undermines all projections and may indicate undisclosed liabilities or fraud
7. **Undisclosed liabilities** — tax arrears, pending lawsuits, personal guarantees of third-party debt, contingent warranty claims
8. **No corporate governance documents** — no operating agreement, no shareholder agreement; ownership rights and management authority are ambiguous
9. **Former partner with unresolved claims** — a departed co-founder, partner, or employee who has not signed a release, or has asserted IP ownership, employment claims, or equity rights
10. **Personal tax liabilities of founders** — a tax lien against a director personally can extend to business assets in some jurisdictions; investors check personal tax compliance
11. **Environmental liabilities** — buried fuel tanks, hazardous materials disposal, contaminated land; can create unlimited future remediation obligations
12. **Management compensation inconsistent with company stage** — founders drawing market salaries from a pre-revenue startup signals misaligned incentives to investors
13. **No financial controls** — one person with unrestricted access to all accounts; no approval thresholds; pricing policies unenforced; revenue leakage is systemic
14. **No documented workflows or operating procedures** — the business exists in the founder's head; it is not transferable without the founder and cannot be scaled
15. **Key employee retention risk post-transaction** — the business value resides in one or two people who are not contractually committed to remain
16. **Pending or threatened litigation** — especially government regulatory actions, employment disputes, or IP infringement claims; even unproven claims create contingent liabilities
17. **Unsupported financial projections** — assumptions that are not documented, not grounded in historical performance, and not tested against market reality
18. **Undisclosed related-party transactions** — owner or director is also a supplier, customer, or landlord; transactions may not be at arm's length
19. **No inventor/employee IP assignment agreements** — software developers, designers, and technical staff whose work products are central to the business have not assigned rights to the company
20. **Corporate structure not suited to the transaction** — no share classes that allow investment; no ability to issue preferred stock; operating agreement blocks investor protections

## 14. Pre-Transaction Preparation Checklist

*Actions for a business owner in the 90 days before presenting to investors or beginning a sale process.*

Berkman's "self-imposed seller due diligence" section (Chapter 9) provides the framework. The following checklist operationalises his guidance.

### Corporate Housekeeping (Days 1–30)

- [ ] Obtain good standing certificates for all jurisdictions where the business is registered or operates
- [ ] Review and update all corporate formation documents (operating agreement, shareholders' agreement, articles)
- [ ] Confirm the capitalization table is accurate and up to date (all equity, options, warrants accounted for)
- [ ] Identify all rights of first refusal, drag-along rights, or supermajority approval requirements — understand how they affect a transaction
- [ ] Confirm that all equity issued to founders, employees, and advisors is properly documented and vested schedules are current
- [ ] If operating under an assumed name (d/b/a), ensure this is registered in all relevant jurisdictions
- [ ] Obtain UCC lien search results for the company under all names used — identify and resolve any unexpected liens

### Intellectual Property (Days 1–30)

- [ ] List every piece of IP the business relies on: software, brand names, patents, trade secrets, creative works
- [ ] For each item, confirm that a written assignment or contribution agreement transfers ownership to the company
- [ ] Collect signed invention assignment agreements from all current and former employees and contractors who worked on IP
- [ ] Search the relevant IP registry (URSB in Uganda; ARIPO; national patent office) to confirm registrations are current and free of claims
- [ ] Review all IP licences (in and out) for change-of-control clauses that could affect assignability
- [ ] If any IP was contributed by a former partner, confirm a written assignment exists — if not, obtain one now

### Contracts (Days 15–45)

- [ ] List all material contracts (key customers, suppliers, leases, licences, service agreements)
- [ ] Review each contract for: (a) assignability clause; (b) change-of-control clause; (c) expiry date; (d) renewal terms
- [ ] For contracts with problematic assignment clauses, approach counterparties now — before a deal is in sight — to negotiate amendments; leverage is much weaker once a deal is known
- [ ] Check that lease agreements are assignable and confirm the landlord's consent requirements
- [ ] Identify any contracts that will expire within 12 months; initiate renewal discussions

### Financial Records (Days 15–60)

- [ ] Engage an accountant to review financial statements for the past 3 years and confirm they reconcile to tax returns
- [ ] Prepare a clean accounts receivable ageing schedule and resolve outstanding disputes
- [ ] Prepare a revenue breakdown by customer and by product/service for the past 3–5 years
- [ ] Identify and document any one-time items in historical financials that inflate apparent earnings
- [ ] Resolve any outstanding tax issues: arrears, unpaid PAYE, VAT, withholding tax
- [ ] If the business has never filed tax returns, prepare and file all outstanding returns before approaching investors
- [ ] Document all assumptions behind financial projections — do not leave any assumption implicit
- [ ] Confirm that all bank accounts are held by the company (not a founder personally); remove any individuals who should no longer have signatory authority

### Employment and HR (Days 30–60)

- [ ] Confirm all employees have signed employment agreements (including IP assignment and confidentiality clauses)
- [ ] Prepare an organisational chart with titles, responsibilities, and compensation (salary, benefits, equity)
- [ ] Prepare an employee manual if one does not exist
- [ ] Confirm payroll tax compliance for all employees (PAYE, NSSF in Uganda)
- [ ] Review worker classification — are any workers classified as contractors when they are functionally employees? Resolve before DD
- [ ] Identify key employees and assess retention risk; consider employment agreements or retention bonuses if key people are likely to leave in a transaction

### Legal Compliance (Days 30–75)

- [ ] Confirm all operating licences and permits are current (trading licence, sector-specific licences, environmental permits)
- [ ] Address any outstanding regulatory violations, health code notices, or inspection reports
- [ ] Prepare a summary of all pending or threatened litigation; engage counsel to assess exposure and resolution options
- [ ] Check for any government tax notices, audit letters, or compliance demands — do not let these surface for the first time during a buyer's DD
- [ ] In Uganda specifically: confirm NIN/BRN compliance, TIN registration, EFRIS enrolment (if applicable), UNBS certification for any standardised products

### Operations Documentation (Days 45–90)

- [ ] Document key operational workflows (how orders are taken, how products are made/delivered, how returns are processed, how inventory is managed)
- [ ] Create an organisational chart
- [ ] Prepare written policies for: pricing authority, expense approval limits, bank account access, data handling
- [ ] Back up all electronic files, software, and data to at least one off-site or cloud location
- [ ] If the business relies on proprietary software, confirm the source code is backed up and access is controlled
- [ ] Prepare a data room (physical or virtual) with organised copies of all key documents under the categories: corporate, financial, contracts, IP, employment, legal, permits

### Personal Affairs of Founders/Directors (Days 60–90)

- [ ] Confirm personal tax compliance (no outstanding personal tax liabilities)
- [ ] Identify any personal guarantees currently in force that could affect the transaction
- [ ] Identify any non-compete agreements from prior employers that could limit post-investment activities
- [ ] Prepare a personal net worth statement
- [ ] If there is any criminal, civil, or regulatory history, brief your lawyer now — surprises discovered by a buyer destroy trust

## 15. Execution Readiness — What DD Reveals About the Gap Between Planning and Execution

Berkman's Chapter 6 (operational/management issues) addresses this directly. A business plan describes intentions; DD reveals whether the business has the infrastructure to execute those intentions.

### What "Executable to the Letter" Looks Like

A plan is executable when:
1. **Workflow documentation exists**: The process by which every major business function is performed is written down. Customer orders can be filled, inventory managed, returns processed, and staff hired without the founder being present.
2. **Financial controls are in place**: Pricing authority is defined and enforced. Expense approval thresholds are documented. Bank account signatories are limited and appropriate. Management cannot unilaterally spend without oversight.
3. **Key functions are not concentrated in one person**: The departure of any single employee — including the founder — does not collapse the business. There is a documented backup for every critical function.
4. **Revenue is contractually supported**: Key customer relationships are documented in contracts. Revenue projections are not dependent on informal relationships that may not transfer with the business.
5. **Financial records are maintained in real time**: Books are closed monthly. Bank reconciliations are current. The accountant (or accounting software) is up to date. There is no six-month catch-up exercise required.
6. **Compliance is proactive, not reactive**: Licences are renewed before expiry. Tax filings are current. No regulatory violations are outstanding.

### How DD Exposes Execution Gaps

| Execution Gap | DD Finding |
|---|---|
| No workflow documentation | Revenue leakage; customer complaints; inventory shrinkage; inability to fill orders |
| No financial controls | Pricing varies without authorisation; employees have unrestricted bank access; management unaware of key financial events |
| Key-person dependency | Key employee departure risk is identified; no transition plan exists; value resides in one person's relationships |
| Plans not backed by contracts | Revenue projections based on informal customer assurances that are not contractually committed |
| Operations exist in founder's head | Business cannot be replicated, scaled, or transferred; buyer must depend entirely on founder post-closing |
| Informal bookkeeping | Financial statements cannot be reconciled; tax returns diverge from management accounts; projections have no credible foundation |

### Berkman's Warning on Financial Controls

> "It is not unusual to see situations when one or more employees or officers have unrestricted access to a company's bank accounts."

> "The absence of written policies or clear directives has cost the music store significant revenues and not even the owner was aware of the pricing issue and the impact on the income of the business."

The absence of financial controls is not just a management weakness — it is a material risk that investors will price in or treat as a deal-killing finding.

## 16. Uganda and East Africa — Context Notes

East African businesses, particularly in the informal and SME sector, typically present with several structural features that create predictable DD vulnerabilities. These map directly to Berkman's framework.

### What Informal Economy Businesses Typically Lack (and Why It Matters to DD)

| Gap | Berkman's Framework | Investor/Lender Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Unregistered business or operating under personal name | No formal legal entity; no clear ownership structure; no ability to execute contracts as a company | Cannot enter binding agreements; lender has no clear collateral target |
| No written partnership or shareholding agreement | Berkman: operating agreement/shareholder agreement is a precondition to any investment | Ownership disputes likely post-investment; exit rights undefined |
| IP informally owned by founder, not by company | Berkman's opening case study; invention assignment never executed | Buyer/investor cannot rely on IP ownership; entire deal collapses |
| Mobile money records as primary financial records | Revenue cannot be independently verified; no audit trail | Projections unverifiable; tax compliance unclear |
| No employment contracts | No IP assignment; no non-compete; no confidentiality protection | Key employees can leave and take customers, trade secrets, or client relationships |
| Key contracts oral or on WhatsApp | Not assignable; no defined terms; terminable at will | Revenue projections have no contractual basis |
| Land in customary tenure (particularly for agribusiness) | Title cannot be transferred; cannot be pledged as collateral | Collateral basis of loan disappears; asset ownership disputed |
| Outstanding tax liabilities (PAYE, VAT, corporate tax) | Tax lien discovered in DD; escrow required; transaction delayed | Adds cost; reduces net proceeds; may block transaction |
| No documented operating procedures | Berkman: revenue leakage; cannot scale; key-person dependency | Business is not transferable; post-investment growth plan is not credible |
| NSSF non-compliance | Employment law violation; contingent liability | Creates undisclosed liability; may require regulatory clearance |

### Priorities for an East African Entrepreneur Preparing for Investment

Based on Berkman's framework, the highest-priority actions for a Ugandan SME preparing for investor DD, in order of deal impact:

1. **Formalise the legal entity**: Incorporate (URSB) or ensure the existing company is in good standing. Obtain TIN, trading licence, and EFRIS enrolment if applicable.
2. **Assign all IP to the company in writing**: Draft and sign a contribution or assignment agreement for any software, brand, product formula, or creative work contributed by founders or early staff.
3. **Obtain signed invention assignment and confidentiality agreements from all employees and contractors**: This is non-negotiable for any technology, creative, or brand-led business.
4. **Review all key customer and supplier contracts for assignability**: If oral, convert to written agreements now. If written but non-assignable, begin re-negotiation before investors are engaged.
5. **Get financial records in order and reconcile to tax returns**: Engage an accountant. Ensure mobile money records, if used, are reconstructed into formal accounts. File any outstanding tax returns.
6. **Document operational workflows**: Create a simple written manual for the three most critical business processes. This signals to investors that the business is transferable and scalable.
7. **Resolve any outstanding regulatory violations**: Address KCCA notices, health certificates, UNBS compliance, environmental permits — do not let these surface during DD.
8. **Prepare a capitalization table**: Know exactly who owns what. If shares or equity have been promised informally, document them now and ensure any rights of first refusal are mapped.

### Uganda-Specific DD Considerations

- **NIN/BRN gate**: Since 2025, a National Identification Number (NIN) or Business Registration Number (BRN) is required before a TIN, trading licence, or bank account can be obtained. DD investigators will confirm this chain of compliance.
- **EFRIS**: Uganda Revenue Authority's electronic fiscal receipting system. Non-compliance creates contingent liabilities (UGX 8M/month penalties). A buyer or lender will check EFRIS enrolment status.
- **Land tenure**: Customary tenure is not registrable title and cannot serve as collateral without conversion. For agribusiness and rural enterprises, this is a common collateral gap.
- **NSSF and PAYE**: Employment tax compliance is a standard DD item for any business with employees. Outstanding NSSF contributions and PAYE are treated as contingent liabilities.
- **Related-party transactions**: In family-owned businesses, the owner may be simultaneously the landlord, the main supplier, and the primary customer. Related-party transactions at non-arm's-length rates will be identified in financial DD and will require adjustment to normalised EBITDA.

## 17. The No-Shop / Exclusivity Clause

Once a buyer or investor decides to conduct DD, they require the target company to stop soliciting other offers during the investigation period. Berkman provides six worked examples. The key variables:

- **Duration**: Typically 45–90 days
- **Scope**: What constitutes a prohibited solicitation or negotiation
- **Remedy for breach**: From cost reimbursement (legal fees, DD costs) to specific performance and injunctive relief
- **Good faith obligation**: Whether the clause imposes a duty to negotiate — not just to refrain from shopping

**Practical guidance for a seller**: The no-shop restricts your ability to run a competitive process. Negotiate the shortest reasonable period. Ensure there is a clear process for what happens if the buyer fails to proceed within the exclusivity window.

## 18. The Data Room

Berkman introduces the concept of a virtual data room: the target business uploads electronic copies of all DD documents to a secure system for review by the buyer's team.

For a Ugandan SME, this may be as simple as a structured shared Google Drive folder. The key is organisation:

```
Data Room Structure
├── 01_Corporate
│   ├── Certificate of Incorporation
│   ├── Operating Agreement / Shareholders Agreement
│   ├── Good Standing Certificate
│   ├── Capitalization Table
│   └── Corporate Minutes / Resolutions
├── 02_Financial
│   ├── Financial Statements (3 years)
│   ├── Tax Returns (3 years)
│   ├── Management Accounts (recent 12 months)
│   ├── Revenue by Customer (5 years)
│   └── Loan Agreements
├── 03_Contracts
│   ├── Key Customer Agreements
│   ├── Supplier Agreements
│   ├── Lease Agreements
│   └── Licence Agreements
├── 04_IP
│   ├── Invention Assignments
│   ├── IP Registrations
│   └── Licence Agreements (IP)
├── 05_Employment
│   ├── Employment Agreements (key staff)
│   ├── Organisational Chart
│   ├── NSSF / PAYE Compliance
│   └── Employee Manual
├── 06_Legal

    ├── Director CVs
    ├── Personal Background Questionnaires
    └── Net Worth Statements
```

A well-organised data room signals operational maturity and accelerates the DD process, which reduces costs for both parties.

## 19. Key Quotes for Business Plan Use

"Think of due diligence as a process of first hearing the company's business story — as it may have been provided in an investment presentation or business plan — and then conducting an investigation to see if the story could be corroborated." (Berkman, 2013, p. 15)

"The due diligence questionnaire is used to ask the company owners and other employees a series of questions designed to test the veracity of the story the owners have presented regarding their business." (p. 142)

"Whether a company is in a position to be sold or raise money is often a function of adopting measures along the way to avoid major issues rather than trying to clean them up in response to due diligence." (p. 228)

"Having the right team improves the value of the due diligence investigation dramatically and substantially reduces the risk of discovering costly or devastating legal, financial, or operational issues after the deal has closed." (p. 217)

"Companies that are vigilant about enforcing good corporate policies and procedures reduce substantially the chance that the buyer's due diligence will uncover legal, financial, or business issues that either undermine dramatically the value of the company or result in a termination of the sale." (p. 228)
