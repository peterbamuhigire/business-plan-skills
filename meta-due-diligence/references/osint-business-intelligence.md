# OSINT for Business Intelligence — Hetherington Framework

Extracted and adapted from Hetherington (2024) for application in business planning, partner vetting, supplier due diligence, competitive intelligence, and investor-readiness preparation. All techniques described are open-source and legal. The book's primary methodology is CRAWL: Communicate, Research, Analyse, Write, Listen.

---

## 1. The CRAWL Methodology — Core Framework

Hetherington's CRAWL methodology is the foundational framework for all due diligence investigations. It distinguishes professional OSINT from amateur guesswork.

### C — Communicate
- Define the scope before starting: Who is being investigated? Why? What is the budget and timeline?
- Create a Statement of Work (SOW) and engagement letter before any investigation begins
- Establish what you **can** and **cannot** find, and communicate this to the client upfront
- The driving question: **Why** — why am I doing this, and why am I not doing something else?

### R — Research
- Research is 70% of the work; it covers internet searches, databases, social media, court records, and on-the-ground interviews
- Apply the **Goldilocks Principle**: not too narrow (misses relevant data), not too broad (produces noise) — find the "just right" search scope
- Build a **taxonomy** (keyword list) at the start: initial terms expand as the investigation progresses
- Use **tracking** to monitor changes over time and **common-sense research** to prioritise leads
- Never fabricate or extrapolate — only report what is verifiable

### A — Analyse
- Analysis transforms data into knowledge; it is distinct from summarising
- 80% of investigation time is research; 20% is the core analysis that delivers insight
- Use structured frameworks (SWOT, CARA, Supply Chain, Value Chain) rather than ad hoc impressions
- Corroborate every finding: a fact should be confirmed in at least two independent sources
- Distinguish between **evidence** (high confidence), **intelligence** (trusted secondary sources), and **hearsay** (unverifiable — cite as such)

### W — Write
- Reports must be non-biased, organised, and professionally formatted
- Key Findings appear first (most unfavourable first), followed by Recommended Next Steps
- Use qualifying language for social media findings: "It appears that..." not "He is..."
- Date-stamp and location-stamp all research — search results vary by time and location

### L — Listen
- Follow up after delivery: Did the report address the client's real question?
- Listen to experts, former employees, local contacts for nuance that databases miss
- Continuous listening enables Phase Three (ongoing monitoring) when a situation is still evolving

## 2. The Three Phases of Due Diligence

### Phase One — Online Intelligence
The foundation of every investigation. Conducted entirely using open sources and databases.

**Sources available in Phase One:**
- Internet research (search engines, news archives, company websites)
- Social media intelligence (Facebook, LinkedIn, X/Twitter, Instagram, TikTok)
- Individual government databases (business registries, court systems, licensing boards)
- Local, national, international, and industry-specific media
- Online litigation records
- Non-traditional internet resources (forums, Wayback Machine, domain registries)

**Data points to compile:**
- Academic records and credentials
- Board appointments and non-profit participation
- Business and personal affiliations
- Civil and criminal filings
- Corporate records and property records
- Financial records (SEC, FINRA, state securities filings)
- Government litigation history
- Intellectual property holdings
- Liens, Judgments, and UCC filings
- Media history and adverse press
- National sanctions and regulatory watch lists
- Vendor and supplier relationships
- Global compliance records

**Phase One report structure:**
1. Subject's Personal Identifiers and Assets (name, address, education, property)
2. Subject's Financial History (personal debt, bankruptcy, real property)
3. Subject's Civil and Criminal Filings
4. Subject's Reputational Standing (social media, adverse statements)

### Phase Two — Boots-on-the-Ground
Moves beyond online to discreet interviews, reference calls, and on-site court document retrieval. Required when:
- Phase One raises unresolved red flags
- The investigation involves foreign entities with sparse online records
- Nuance requires direct human intelligence (former employees, suppliers)

**Key insight:** 30% of courts do not provide criminal and civil court online access to historical dockets. Physical retrieval is often essential for complete records (Hetherington, 2024, Ch. 3).

### Phase Three — Ongoing Monitoring
Activated when Phase One or Two findings remain inconclusive or a situation is still evolving.
- Monitor online forums, social media groups, employee review sites
- Track regulatory and court databases for new filings
- Set up media alerts for the subject's name and associated entities

## 3. Five Types of Business Investigations

### 3.1 Competitor Investigation
Used when a competitor enters a market, outperforms your business, or is suspected of fraud.
- Map supply chain vendors, clients, and revenue streams for the past 6–12 months
- Identify current market trends and project the next 6–12 months
- Produce a SWOT analysis emphasising the competitor's market position
- Recommend ongoing monitoring to prevent surprises

### 3.2 Competitive Intelligence (Market Intelligence)
Examines an entire industry, not just one company. Requires statistical and analytical depth.
- Evaluate multiple players simultaneously; assess global industry dynamics
- Use D&B Hoovers, SCIP (scip.org), Kompass, and industry journals
- Relevant for validating **market size claims** in business plan market analysis sections

### 3.3 Potential Acquisition Investigation
- First: Search media and internet for malpractice, misconduct, or reputational damage
- Review ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) reports — both self-published and from oversight bodies
- Examine financial health (venture capital dependency, cash burn rate)
- Analyse marketing strategy and website for competitive positioning
- A company with rave reviews but poor sales is a warning sign — investigate the management

### 3.4 Fraudulent or Defaulted Company Investigation
- Track all tangible assets that could have been moved into personal funds of shareholders
- Identify all responsible parties: their wealth, vehicles (including watercraft and aircraft), homes, other business interests, families, associates
- Intellectual property (trademarks, patents, copyrights) is also a traceable asset class

### 3.5 Background Checks
- On a **company**: ownership, who runs it, who invests in it, corporate actions taken
- On a **person**: professional history, credentials, criminal/civil history, assets, reputational standing
- On a **location**: property ownership, taxes, local compliance adherence

## 4. Analytical Frameworks for Business Intelligence

### 4.1 SWOT Analysis
**Best for:** Competitor investigations, market positioning, acquisition targets.

| Quadrant | Business Application |
|----------|---------------------|
| Strengths | What the subject company does well; unique assets, market position |
| Weaknesses | Revenue gaps vs. industry benchmarks; compliance issues; management deficiencies |
| Opportunities | Adjacent markets, property for acquisition, under-exploited distribution |
| Threats | Regulatory exposure, aggressive competitors, reputational risks |

**Key insight:** If a company is showing revenue 50% below similar-sized industry peers, that is a weakness or threat indicator. Do not accept company-provided revenue without cross-referencing D&B or industry reports (Hetherington, 2024, Ch. 6).

**SWOT limitation:** Not suitable for fraud or deception cases where creative/critical thinking is required.

### 4.2 CARA Analysis
**Best for:** Vetting individuals — partners, key hires, suppliers, investors.

| Element | What to Look For |
|---------|-----------------|
| **C**haracteristics | Professional rank, lifestyle signals, litigation history, civic awards |
| **A**ssociations | Socioeconomic status, closest friends, colleagues, family members |
| **R**eputation | Trustworthy? Reliable? Combative? Media portrayal? Employee accounts? |
| **A**ffiliations | Professional associations, board memberships, alma mater, charitable contributions |

**Application:** A CEO whose LinkedIn connections include an associate under investigation for the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act, and who attended a gala for a nonprofit under IRS investigation, warrants serious scrutiny — even without direct evidence of wrongdoing (Hetherington, 2024, Ch. 6 case study).

**Important:** Present CARA findings with qualifying language: "Based on today's research, it appears that..." Never make direct accusations based solely on associations.

### 4.3 Supply Chain Analysis
**Best for:** Vetting suppliers, assessing operational resilience, pre-investment manufacturing due diligence.

Seven components to investigate:
1. **Inbound logistics** — How are inputs sourced, handled, and stored? Any regulatory violations?
2. **Outbound logistics** — Who packages and ships? Are distribution partners vetted?
3. **Operations** — Who actually manufactures the product? Is there a subcontractor hidden in the chain?
4. **Support teams** — Research and development, unions, immigration compliance (for labour-intensive operations)
5. **Human resources** — Does management provide adequate tools, safe working conditions, leadership?
6. **Infrastructure** — Location risks, disaster recovery, business continuity plans, ISO compliance
7. **Technology** — CRM systems, tracking tools, data management

**Red flag:** A supplier with no documented contingency plan for a single-point failure in their infrastructure is a high operational risk (Hetherington, 2024, Ch. 6).

### 4.4 Value Chain Analysis
Examines the specific functions that add value at each stage of production — from raw material to consumer. Useful for:
- Identifying the weakest link in a multi-stage supply relationship
- Assessing which partner is creating or destroying value
- Pre-acquisition evaluation of one specific element of an operation

## 5. Company Research — What Is Publicly Accessible

### 5.1 Business Entity Types and Research Approach

| Entity Type | Key Research Sources |
|-------------|---------------------|
| Publicly traded corporation | SEC EDGAR (edgar.sec.gov) — 10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, DEF-14A (executive pay), proxy statements |
| Privately held company | D&B, Kompass, Hoover's, Secretary of State filings; data is often self-reported — treat with caution |
| Non-profit / NGO | IRS Form 990 (via GuideStar/guidestar.org); reveals executive compensation, donor lists, grant activity |
| LLC / Partnership | Secretary of State filings; check for original handwritten documents — corrections and cross-outs not visible in digital versions |
| Foreign corporation | Moody's Orbis (462M+ companies), SkyMinder, Sayari, Bureau van Dijk; local investigators required for full records |

### 5.2 Key Databases for Company Research

**Dun & Bradstreet (D&B / dnb.com)**
- 100+ million companies globally; self-reported data — use for leads, not gospel
- Search by principal's name to find connected entities (reveals hidden business interests)
- Search by fax number — fraudulent companies share fax numbers even when generating new phone numbers
- Execute address searches — multiple companies at one address is a red flag

**Kompass (kompass.com)**
- 35+ million companies in 75+ countries; reasonable pricing
- Free searches: by company, executive, product/service, trade name, region
- Useful starting point for East African and emerging market companies

**Sayari (sayari.com)**
- Indexes public records across 150+ country sources including corporate formations, litigation, real property, IP filings, trade activity
- Includes sanctions screening: EU, UN, US Consolidated Sanctions, US Treasury OFAC
- Useful for cross-border investigation in markets with multi-language records

**Moody's Orbis (formerly Bureau van Dijk)**
- 462+ million companies; 29 million in Middle East and Africa
- Detailed corporate ownership structures, M&A data, financial statements, patent links

**SkyMinder (skyminder.com)**
- 230+ million companies; credit reports, payment history, banking relationships, litigation
- Reports are sometimes produced by in-country researchers in real time — valuable for markets with thin online records

**OpenCorporates (opencorporates.com)**
- Free; maps connections between companies and entities across jurisdictions
- Useful for tracing corporate family trees and ownership structures

**ZoomInfo (zoominfo.com)**
- Excellent for finding people within companies; aggregates from websites, public filings, directories
- Best free tool for finding business contacts and company-to-person links

### 5.3 Critical Research Technique: Domain and Web Presence Verification
- Use **Domaintools.com** or **Who.is** to identify who owns a website domain
- Check **creation date**, **registrar**, and **registered address** — these should align with claimed business location
- Multiple companies registered at the same address = red flag (possible shell entity or mail-drop fraud)
- A prestigious website does not equal a legitimate business: "On the Internet, nobody knows you're a dog" (Hetherington, 2024, Ch. 8, citing Steiner, 1993)
- Co-working spaces (WeWork equivalents) and short-term office rentals are legitimate — but also used by fraudulent entities to appear established

## 6. People Research — Vetting Partners, Hires, and Suppliers

### 6.1 Four Types of People in Business Investigations

**The Privacy Seeker** — Keeps a low profile; difficult to research but not necessarily suspicious. Requires deeper database and court record searches.

**The Limelighter** — CEOs and public-facing executives who actively cultivate media profiles. Key caution: company press releases and CEO quotes on websites are highly crafted marketing content, not independent intelligence. Read what *others* write about them — especially older articles from before they were prominent.

**The Fraudulent One** — Often charismatic, claims specialised knowledge, runs seminars or investment pitches. Typically also a Limelighter. Always investigate before any money changes hands. Criminal history may be in another jurisdiction under a slightly different name.

**The Incompetent One** — Not malicious but dangerous. Subtypes:
- *Overextended*: Assumes others are managing details; things fall through the cracks
- *Unaware*: Enters unfamiliar territory without knowing applicable compliance laws
- *Overachiever*: Ignores legal/ethical constraints to hit targets

### 6.2 Key Sources for Individual Research

**Professional credentials:**
- Licensing boards — verify professional licence status, complaints, disciplinary actions
- University registrars — confirm degrees directly (not from CV or LinkedIn)
- FINRA BrokerCheck (brokercheck.finra.org) — for financial professionals
- Medical and legal disciplinary databases (state-specific)
- A missing licence where one is required is a significant red flag — could indicate tax evasion, a criminal record, or lack of qualifications

**Court and litigation history:**
- PACER (pacer.gov) — US federal court records
- State-specific eCourts systems (vary by state/country)
- Search civil AND criminal records; civil records often reveal financial disputes, defaults, and pattern behaviour
- Cross-reference multiple jurisdictions — a subject may have clean records in one area but history in another

**Assets and financial indicators:**
- Property records — local county/parish tax assessor offices; also reveals financial pressure if property is under lien
- UCC (Uniform Commercial Code) filings — reveal business loans secured against assets; searchable by state Secretary of State
- Patent and trademark filings — USPTO (uspto.gov) reveals intellectual property holdings often overlooked in asset searches
- IRS Form 990 — for any nonprofit association the subject is involved with; reveals executive compensation and grant relationships

**Board positions and affiliations:**
- SEC EDGAR DEF-14A proxy statements — list all board members and compensation for public companies
- Look for collusion: Did board members join together (possible prior relationship)? Are board members also vendors? Do family members serve on the same board?
- IRS 990 forms expose nonprofit kickback schemes — grants from a subject's own foundation flowing to projects the subject was awarded contracts to build

**Political and charitable contributions:**
- FEC (fec.gov) — political donation data
- OpenSecrets.org — origin of political contributions
- FollowTheMoney.org — state-level political donations
- Charitable affiliations can be sincere or a front for money laundering or influence-buying

### 6.3 Social Media Intelligence (SOCMINT)

**Platforms and applications:**

| Platform | Primary Intelligence Value |
|----------|---------------------------|
| LinkedIn | Professional history verification, board connections, business associations, former employees |
| Facebook | Lifestyle indicators, assets (cars, boats, holidays), personal associations, group memberships, behaviour patterns |
| X/Twitter | Public statements, political leanings, business commentary, character signals |
| Instagram | Location data (geo-tagged posts), lifestyle, assets, business activities |
| TikTok | Behaviour patterns, associations, public statements |
| Venmo / payment apps | Social connections (friends feed), spending patterns (where privacy settings are misconfigured) |

**Key techniques:**
- Search by name across platforms using consistent handle variations: LinkedIn/RobertSmith, Facebook/BobSmith, X/BobbySmith
- Use `site:facebook.com "Bob Smith"` in Google to search without logging in
- Check old, abandoned accounts (Myspace, Foursquare) — early profiles often contain unguarded information
- Search hashtags related to subject's industry — may reveal business activity or associations
- Photo metadata (EXIF data) can reveal location information on some platforms (Instagram in particular)
- Venmo "friends feed" — if set to public, reveals social connections and payment partners

**Critical warnings:**
- Social media profile fraud is rampant — verify profiles before using them as evidence
- Qualify all social media findings: "Based on social media and internet postings, it appears..."
- Do not "friend" or make contact with a subject during investigation — view only public content
- In some jurisdictions (notably Germany), employers cannot view employee Facebook profiles pre-employment — check local laws

**Competitor digital footprint audit (applying to own business):**
- What does your company look like from an OSINT perspective? Run a CARA analysis on your own business
- Check what former employees have written on Glassdoor, Indeed, and Vault — this is what investors will find
- Audit your domain registration information — does it match your registered business address?
- Search your company name + "complaint," "scam," "lawsuit," "fraud" in Google

## 7. Financial Intelligence — Reading Public Financial Signals

### 7.1 Financial Fraud Red Flags
Common financial fraud types detectable through OSINT (Hetherington, 2024, Ch. 14):

- **Market manipulation**: Artificial inflation/deflation of a stock or asset value through misleading information
- **Pump-and-dump schemes**: Inflate value through false claims, sell at peak, leave investors with worthless assets
- **Ponzi schemes**: Returns paid from new investor funds, not from genuine business activity; no underlying profitable enterprise
- **Backdating**: Recording transactions at an earlier date to improve financial appearance
- **Fraudulent revenue inflation**: Claiming revenues far above actual performance (classic Theranos pattern)
- **Shell company schemes**: Creating a façade of multiple entities to obscure the movement of funds

**OSINT signals of financial distress:**
- Company revenues significantly below industry benchmarks for similar-sized peers
- Frequent changes in registered address or business name
- Multiple companies sharing the same fax number, phone number, or registered address
- CEO or CFO with FINRA disciplinary history, SEC enforcement action, or prior bankruptcy
- Annual reports not filed on time with the company registry
- No verifiable client list or reference customers
- A business that is "too hard to find" in any database despite claiming significant revenues

### 7.2 Assets and Liens — What Is Publicly Traceable
- **Property records**: County/parish tax assessor offices; reveals ownership, liens, purchase history
- **UCC filings**: Business loans secured against assets — searchable at Secretary of State offices; reveals what assets the company has pledged as collateral
- **Court judgments**: Unpaid judgments appear in state court civil records; evidence of financial default
- **Bankruptcy records**: Federal court system; PACER (pacer.gov)
- **Vehicle/vessel registration**: State motor vehicle agencies (cars and pleasure boats); US Coast Guard (commercial vessels over five tons)
- **Intellectual property**: USPTO (uspto.gov) — patents, trademarks, copyrights; intangible assets often more valuable than physical ones
- **Ownership of 5%+ of a publicly traded stock** is a public record — searchable via SEC EDGAR

### 7.3 Regulatory Compliance Signals
- **Licensing board disciplinary actions**: Available by state/country; reveals professional misconduct
- **OSHA violations**: For manufacturing and food processing companies — signals operational risk
- **FDA/regulatory citations**: For food, pharmaceutical, and medical device companies
- **SEC/FINRA enforcement actions**: For financial services companies and investment advisors
- **Import/export compliance**: PIERS database; Panjiva, Zepol, ImportGenius for trade data
- **Government excluded parties**: SAM.gov (sam.gov) — entities excluded from doing business with the US government; equivalent lists exist for UN, EU, and OFAC

## 8. Competitor Intelligence Checklist

Use this checklist when conducting a competitor investigation for a business plan market analysis section:

### Company Identity and Structure
- [ ] Confirm legal name, registration number, and registered address via company registry
- [ ] Identify all trading names, DBAs, and brand names
- [ ] Determine entity type (sole trader, limited company, partnership, LLC equivalent)
- [ ] Trace corporate family tree: Is this company a subsidiary? Who is the ultimate parent?
- [ ] Check domain registration — does it match the stated company address?
- [ ] Identify all directors and principal officers; cross-reference their other business interests

### Financial Health Signals
- [ ] Review available business credit reports (D&B, Kompass, Orbis)
- [ ] Compare stated/estimated revenues against industry benchmarks for similar-sized companies
- [ ] Check for outstanding court judgments, liens, or UCC filings against the company
- [ ] Look for patterns of late payment to suppliers (credit report signals)
- [ ] Search for past bankruptcies of the company or its principals
- [ ] Identify any SEC, FINRA, or equivalent financial regulatory actions

### Operations and Supply Chain
- [ ] Identify key suppliers (import/export databases, trade directories, Thomasnet)
- [ ] Assess operational capacity — number of employees, locations, equipment (if visible in media)
- [ ] Check for regulatory compliance (licensing, health and safety, environmental permits)
- [ ] Look for ISO certification (indicates documented operational standards)
- [ ] Review OSHA, EPA, or local equivalent citations for manufacturing companies

### Reputation and Adverse Media
- [ ] Google search: [company name] + "lawsuit" / "complaint" / "fraud" / "fine"
- [ ] Search news archives (Factiva, LexisNexis, local press) for adverse media
- [ ] Review employee opinions on Glassdoor, Indeed, Vault
- [ ] Check industry forums and trade association complaint channels
- [ ] Review Better Business Bureau equivalent or local consumer protection records

### Social Media and Digital Footprint
- [ ] Audit all active social media accounts — content consistency, engagement, tone
- [ ] Identify key personnel's personal social media — character signals, business relationships
- [ ] Look for customer complaints or praise in unmoderated spaces (Facebook groups, Twitter/X mentions, Reddit)
- [ ] Note whether social media activity is consistent with claimed company size and revenue

### People Behind the Company
- [ ] Run CARA analysis on all directors and key management
- [ ] Verify academic credentials through university registrar (not from company's own materials)
- [ ] Check professional licence status (licensing board databases)
- [ ] Search court records in all jurisdictions where the principal has lived or worked
- [ ] Examine board composition — look for conflicts of interest, family clusters, vendor-board member overlaps
- [ ] Review political donations and charitable affiliations for influence relationships

## 9. Business Plan Due Diligence Preparation

**What investors, banks, and due diligence professionals will find when they OSINT your business — and how to prepare.**

### What a Bank or Investor Will Search
Before approving a loan or making an equity investment, any competent bank or investor will conduct at minimum a Phase One OSINT investigation. Based on Hetherington (2024), they will search:

1. **Company registry records** — Does the registration match what you claim? Are annual returns filed on time?
2. **Director and owner background** — Criminal, civil, and regulatory history of all principals
3. **Credentials verification** — Academic qualifications, professional licences, claimed prior employment
4. **Court records** — Judgments, bankruptcies, defaults, litigation history of the company and principals
5. **Domain and web presence** — Does your website registration match your company address? When was it created?
6. **Social media footprint** — Personal accounts of directors; company accounts; what employees say about you
7. **Adverse media** — News searches for complaints, fraud allegations, regulatory breaches
8. **Sanctions and watch lists** — Are any directors on international sanctions lists (OFAC, UN, EU)?
9. **Financial signals** — Revenue claims plausible against company size? Any UCC filings or liens?
10. **Nonprofit/charity affiliations** — Any questionable charity connections or governance issues?

### How to Prepare Your Business for OSINT Scrutiny

**Registry and corporate records:**
- Ensure all company registry filings are current and accurate
- Filed addresses must match actual operating address (or explain any discrepancy)
- All director changes must be filed promptly — discrepancies between what you tell a lender and what the registry shows are a serious red flag

**Credentials and professional background:**
- Prepare certified copies of academic qualifications — do not rely on investors taking your word
- Ensure all professional licences are current and in good standing
- Brief all co-founders and key management to expect background checks; any undisclosed history should be disclosed proactively, not discovered by the investor

**Digital footprint — clean up before fundraising:**
- Google your own name and company name — review the first three pages of results
- Search your name + "complaint," "scam," "lawsuit," "bad debt" — address anything that appears
- Audit your company's social media: inconsistent claims about size, revenue, or client relationships are easily flagged
- Review personal social media accounts of all directors — remove or contextualise content that could raise questions about character or professional conduct
- Ensure your domain registration reflects your legitimate business address (not a mail drop)

**Court and regulatory history:**
- Pull your own court record across all relevant jurisdictions — if you find something, know how to explain it before the investor does
- Check licensing compliance — all required operating licences, trade permits, and regulatory registrations should be current
- If a director has a prior regulatory action, prepare a clear, factual explanation

**Financial records:**
- Ensure your company's financial records are reconcilable — revenue claims in your business plan must be supportable by bank statements and tax filings
- Outstanding court judgments or unpaid tax debt will appear in public records searches — resolve before fundraising
- If your company has taken business loans, ensure UCC filings match your actual secured assets

**Reference and reputation management:**
- Identify three to five credible reference customers or clients you can offer — they will be contacted
- Brief references on what to say and what context to provide
- Check what former employees have said publicly on job review platforms

## 10. Supplier and Vendor Vetting — Red Flag Signals

### Primary Red Flags (High Risk — Do Not Proceed Without Investigation)
- **Multiple companies at one address** — especially if the address is a mail drop, UPS store, or co-working space
- **Shared fax numbers** across multiple companies — fraudulent entities frequently share fax numbers while generating new phone numbers
- **Newly registered company** with no verifiable operating history but large revenue claims
- **Director with prior Ponzi scheme, wire fraud, or securities fraud conviction** — even under a slightly different name or in a different jurisdiction
- **Professional licence not found** in the licensing board database for a claimed profession
- **No import/export records** for a supplier claiming significant cross-border trade
- **Domain registered recently** (under 1–2 years) despite claims of being a long-established business
- **SEC, FINRA, or international financial regulator enforcement action** against principals
- **Sanctions list match** — OFAC, UN, EU; always screen against consolidated lists before any significant payment
- **IRS Form 990 irregularities** in nonprofit affiliations — grants flowing to projects controlled by the same party

### Secondary Red Flags (Investigate Further Before Proceeding)
- Revenue per employee implied by company size significantly above or below industry benchmarks
- CEO or director with no verifiable prior employment history (LinkedIn or professional databases)
- Company registered in a known tax-haven jurisdiction (British Virgin Islands, Cayman Islands, Bahamas) with no evident operating rationale
- A very polished website with no traceable physical office, no named staff, and no verifiable references
- Director who simultaneously sits on the board of a key client or key supplier (conflict of interest)
- No adverse media does NOT mean clean — it may mean the company has simply not been investigated yet; absence of information is not validation

### Behavioural Red Flags in Supplier Relationships
- Reluctance to provide references, company registration documents, or audited accounts
- Requests for full payment upfront before goods are delivered with no escrow arrangement
- Prices significantly below market rate without credible explanation
- Pressure to close quickly before due diligence is completed
- Changes in payment instructions at the last moment (business email compromise pattern)
- Inability to explain inconsistencies in their own materials when questioned

## 11. East Africa and Uganda OSINT — Limitations and Workarounds

### What Is Different in Markets with Thin Public Records

Hetherington's framework was developed primarily for US and Western investigations. Applying it to Uganda and East Africa requires understanding significant structural differences:

**Company registries:**
- Uganda Registration Services Bureau (URSB) at ursb.go.ug maintains the Companies Register, business names, and partnerships
- Online search functionality is limited — basic name searches are possible but full filing history may require in-person requests at URSB Kampala
- Annual returns are frequently not filed on time — absence of recent filings does not necessarily mean the company has ceased operations (but is a yellow flag)
- Company records in Kenya are more accessible via the Business Registration Service (BRS); Kenya is a useful regional comparator for East African operators

**Court records:**
- Uganda court records are largely not digitised; the Uganda Judiciary maintains some online cause lists but full case records require physical court access
- Kenya's eCitizen portal has better online court record access — useful for investigating Kenyan counterparties
- International courts (ICC, WIPO) are relevant for intellectual property and cross-border disputes

**Financial records:**
- Private companies (the vast majority in Uganda) have no public filing requirement equivalent to SEC EDGAR
- Bank of Uganda licensing data (for financial institutions) is publicly available at bou.or.ug
- Capital Markets Authority (CMA) Uganda maintains records on listed securities at cmauganda.co.ug
- Uganda Revenue Authority (URA) data is not publicly accessible, but tax clearance certificates can be requested and verified

**Regulatory and licensing:**
- Uganda Investment Authority (UIA) maintains investment licence data
- Uganda National Bureau of Standards (UNBS) maintains product certification records
- NEMA maintains environmental compliance records
- Professional body records (e.g., Institute of Certified Public Accountants of Uganda — ICPAU) can confirm professional credentials

**Available OSINT workarounds for thin-record markets:**

1. **Mobile money intelligence** — Mobile money transaction patterns (M-Pesa, MTN Mobile Money, Airtel Money) are not publicly accessible, but supplier payment behaviour can be assessed informally through trade references. Consistent mobile money payment patterns are a positive signal; requests for unusual payment routing are a red flag.

2. **Social media is richer than formal records** — Facebook is the dominant platform in Uganda; business pages, group memberships, and public posts often reveal operational reality, customer relationships, and reputation signals not visible in any formal database. Search the company name and principal names on Facebook, WhatsApp group rosters (via screenshots shared publicly), and LinkedIn.

3. **Physical verification** — In markets where digital records are sparse, Phase Two boots-on-the-ground investigation is more important, not less. A visit to the stated business address, observation of actual operations, and interviews with nearby businesses about a supplier are standard practice.

4. **Trade reference networks** — Business associations (Uganda Manufacturers Association, Uganda Chamber of Commerce, KACITA for small traders) maintain informal reputation networks. A call to the relevant association about a supplier can surface reputation intelligence not available online.

5. **Regional databases with East Africa coverage:**
   - **Moody's Orbis / BvD**: Lists some Ugandan entities, particularly larger firms
   - **Sayari**: Increasingly covers East African company records
   - **OpenCorporates**: Partial coverage of Kenya; growing East Africa data
   - **Kompass**: Lists some East African companies in its international directory
   - **African Development Bank** and **World Bank procurement** databases list companies that have accessed development finance — useful for vetting larger suppliers
   - **OFAC SDN List**: Screen against this for any counterparty with significant cross-border exposure (applicable to companies connected to sanctioned countries or individuals)

6. **Diaspora and informal networks** — For East African markets, investigative journalists and local correspondents are often more effective than formal databases. Journalists at Monitor Publications, Daily Nation (Kenya), or The East African have investigated specific companies and individuals and their archives are searchable.

7. **Import and export data** — Uganda Revenue Authority generates import/export data; TradeMap.org (International Trade Centre) provides aggregate trade flow data by commodity and partner country. Panjiva and ImportGenius have limited but growing East Africa coverage.

**Specific cautions for Uganda context:**
- Company age is less verifiable — date of incorporation is on file at URSB but pre-2010 records are often not digitised
- Director names may appear with spelling variations across documents — apply Soundex/phonetic awareness when searching
- Multiple businesses at a single address is common (shared offices, business centres) and less diagnostic of fraud than in Western markets — requires contextual judgment
- Conduct the investigation in local context: a business registered at a Kampala Chamber of Commerce shared address is different from one registered at a fictional address

## 12. Report Writing Standards for Due Diligence

### Report Structure (Hetherington Standard)
1. **Cover sheet** — Date, report title, investigating party, client name
2. **Confidentiality statement** — Scope of investigation (online only? In-person interviews?)
3. **Key Findings** — Most unfavourable first; then neutral; then positive
4. **Recommended Next Steps** — What further investigation is warranted
5. **Detailed findings by category** — Organised by subject area (identity, financial, legal, reputational, social media)
6. **Exhibits** — Screen captures, copies of public documents, referenced sources
7. **Disclaimer** — Standard disclaimer that database information may be incomplete or contain errors

### Language Standards
- Always cite the source: "Based on proprietary databases, [subject] appears to reside at..."
- Use qualifying language for social media and non-verified information: "It appears that..." / "According to..."
- Never make direct accusations based solely on associations or circumstantial evidence
- If a name is common, add: "Due to the ambiguity and commonality of the name, it is not possible to confirm or deny if the results pertain to the subject"

### Step-by-Step Investigation Sequence (from Hetherington, 2024, Ch. 20)
1. Pull comprehensive profile reports from public record vendors (TLO, LexisNexis, CLEAR, or equivalent)
2. For companies: pull corporate database reports (D&B, Kompass, Orbis)
3. Search key online databases within the state/country and county where the company is located
4. Cross-reference addresses across multiple databases for consistency — consistent address = higher confidence
5. Identify personal connections: relatives, work associates, known friends (via social media and profile reports)
6. Locate and verify assets (property records, UCC filings, patent records)
7. Research historical data: criminal, regulatory, litigation, media, and social media history
8. Draft report with Key Findings first, then Recommended Next Steps

## 14. Ethical and Legal Boundaries

All OSINT techniques in this document are legal and ethical when applied to publicly available information. Hetherington (2024) is emphatic about the following limits:

**Always legal:**
- Viewing publicly available social media profiles, posts, and pages
- Accessing public company registry records
- Reading court records that are publicly filed
- Searching news archives and media databases
- Reviewing Form 990 (US nonprofit) and equivalent public financial disclosures
- Domain ownership lookups via public WHOIS databases
- Viewing publicly listed patent and trademark filings
- Accessing government sanctions and excluded-party lists

**Legal limits to respect:**
- **Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA)**: Prohibits accessing bank accounts or credit files without a legally permissible purpose (e.g., employment screening under FCRA, judgment collection)
- **GDPR (EU) and equivalent data protection laws**: Limit use of personal data for background checks in certain jurisdictions; Germany prohibits viewing Facebook profiles for pre-employment screening
- **No "friending" or contacting subjects** during an investigation — view only public content
- **No GPS tracking, hacking, or accessing protected systems** — these are illegal regardless of the purpose
- **Foreign jurisdictions**: Privacy rules in China, and increasingly other markets, make standard OSINT investigations legally risky; always consult local counsel before conducting Phase Two investigations abroad

**The trust test (from Hetherington, quoting CIA Deputy Director Medina):** Would the subject, if they knew what you were researching and how, consider it a reasonable exercise of due diligence? If not, reconsider the method. Ethical OSINT is transparent in method, even if not disclosed to the subject in real time.

## 15. Glossary of Key OSINT Terms

**CARA** — Characteristics, Associations, Reputations, Affiliations. Individual profiling framework.

**Compliance check** — Minimal background check for non-management employees; confirms basic eligibility without deep investigation.

**CRAWL** — Communicate, Research, Analyse, Write, Listen. Hetherington's due diligence methodology.

**DBA (Doing Business As)** — A trade name under which a company operates that differs from its legal registered name.

**EDGAR** — Electronic Data Gathering, Analysis, and Retrieval system (SEC). Free public access to US public company filings.

**FCRA** — Fair Credit Reporting Act. US law governing permissible uses of consumer credit and financial information.

**FINRA** — Financial Industry Regulatory Authority. Self-regulatory body for US broker-dealers; BrokerCheck is its public discipline database.

**Limelighter** — A public-facing executive or business figure who actively cultivates media presence; treat company-produced content about them with caution.

**OFAC SDN List** — Office of Foreign Assets Control Specially Designated Nationals list. Key US sanctions list; essential screening tool for any international transaction.

**Phase One** — Online-only due diligence investigation.

**Phase Two** — Boots-on-the-ground: interviews, physical verification, court document retrieval.

**Phase Three** — Ongoing monitoring of a subject or situation.

**Ponzi scheme** — Returns paid to existing investors from funds contributed by new investors; no underlying profitable business.

**SCIP** — Strategic and Competitive Intelligence Professionals (scip.org). Professional association for competitive intelligence practitioners.

**Shell company** — A business façade with little or no operational activity, used to obscure ownership, move funds, or give false appearance of an established entity.

**SOCMINT** — Social Media Intelligence. The subset of OSINT focused on social media platforms.

**SOW** — Statement of Work. Document outlining investigation scope, approach, and reporting format; agreed before work begins.

**SWOT** — Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats. Structured analytical framework for business and competitor assessment.

**Taxonomy** — Initial keyword list developed at the start of an investigation; expands as the investigation progresses.

**UCC Filing** — Uniform Commercial Code filing. A business loan recorded against assets; publicly searchable at state Secretary of State offices. Reveals what assets a company has pledged as collateral.
