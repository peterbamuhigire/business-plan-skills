# Due Diligence Framework — Howson (M&A)

> **How to use this file:** This reference covers the full due diligence (DD) framework that investors, lenders, and acquirers use to evaluate businesses. For a Ugandan entrepreneur, read it from the sell-side: every question an investor asks is a gap you must close in your business plan or data room. The Business Plan Section Mapping table at the end cross-references each DD area to the relevant skill (01–15).

---

## 1. What Due Diligence Is — and Why It Matters

Due diligence (DD) is the systematic investigation of a business before a financial transaction — acquisition, equity investment, or lending. It has three practical outputs for business owners:

1. **Negotiating ammunition** — findings drive price and terms
2. **Deal breaker identification** — what makes investors walk away
3. **Post-deal integration planning** — what must be fixed immediately after closing

Howson's central insight: *"The aim of due diligence should be to give the buyer an assessment of the risks and benefits of the deal both as an aid to negotiation and as an aid to delivering value post-completion."*

Half of all mergers and acquisitions fail to achieve their intended purpose; in most cases failure is traced not to bad strategy but to poor post-deal integration. The same principle applies to investment: a capital injection into a badly understood business destroys value. DD is therefore as much about future management as it is about verifying the past.

**DD is not an audit.** Key distinction (Howson's Table 4.1):

| Dimension | Audit | Financial Due Diligence |
|---|---|---|
| Aim | Verify results; true and fair picture | Establish underlying profit; flush out black holes |
| Focus | Past | Past and future |
| Scope | Defined by statute | Often limited to key issues |
| Access | Unrestricted | May be restricted |
| Evidence | Substantive and compliance testing | Interview-based |

## 2. DD Programme Structure

### 2.1 The Two Decisions

Before any DD begins, the investing team must decide:
- **Which areas to cover** — not everything; priority areas where risk is greatest
- **How deeply to investigate** — a function of deal size, industry risk, and existing knowledge

Howson's ordered priority list (surveys of acquirers, ranked by importance):
1. Market / Customers
2. Management
3. Financial records and projections
4. Competition
5. Product / technology
6. Legal issues
7. Environmental investigations

### 2.2 Areas Typically Covered (Full DD Programme)

| DD Type | Focus | Who Leads |
|---|---|---|
| Commercial DD | Market size, competitive position, customer quality, future prospects | Strategy consultants, CDD specialists |
| Financial DD | Quality of earnings, balance sheet integrity, cash flow, forecasts | Reporting accountants |
| Legal DD | Contracts, liabilities, IP, regulatory compliance | Lawyers |
| Human Resources DD | Employment terms, culture, key person risk, pensions | HR specialists or lawyers |
| Management DD | Management capability, leadership depth, succession | Independent assessors |
| Tax DD | Tax liabilities, compliance, deferred tax, VAT | Tax specialists |
| Environmental DD | Site contamination, regulatory exposure, remediation costs | Environmental consultants |
| IT DD | Systems adequacy, scalability, cyber risk, data ownership | IT specialists |
| Pensions DD | Scheme funding, hidden liabilities, actuarial assumptions | Actuaries |

### 2.3 Non-Financial Deal Breakers (Howson's "Key Exposures")

These are the areas most likely to be deal-specific and non-financial:
- IT systems — incompatibility with acquirer/investor systems
- Contract commitments — onerous or undisclosed
- Employment terms — promised changes, redundancy exposure
- Financial instruments and hedging
- Environmental matters
- Compliance with existing laws
- Outstanding litigation

### 2.4 DD Timeline

- **Commercial DD:** 3–4 weeks (can be done before Heads of Terms — recommended)
- **Financial DD:** 4–8 weeks (runs concurrently with legal)
- **Legal DD:** 4–8 weeks
- **Full programme:** typically 6–12 weeks from Heads of Terms to report delivery

### 2.5 Data Room Requirements

A data room is the controlled information repository the seller provides to investors. Its contents directly mirror the information request list (Appendix A2 below). The objective from the seller's perspective is to put in as much non-sensitive information as possible while protecting genuinely sensitive data until a shortlist of bidders is established.

**Data room must include at minimum:**
- 3 years audited financial statements + latest management accounts
- Business plan / information memorandum
- Corporate structure documents (articles, share register, board minutes)
- Key customer and supplier contracts
- Employment contracts for directors and senior managers
- IP registrations and licences
- Regulatory permits and licences
- Tax computations for last 3–6 years
- Any known litigation or disputes
- Environmental assessments (if applicable)
- Insurance schedules

## 3. Commercial Due Diligence (CDD) — Highest Priority

Commercial DD is the investigation of a company's **markets** and **competitive position**, and from those two inputs, its **future prospects**. It is the primary forward-looking DD discipline. Howson: *"A company is acquired not for its past performance but for its ability to generate profits in the future."*

### 3.1 The CDD Framework

CDD feeds two inputs into financial forecasting:

```
Market analysis      →  Prospects  →  Financial forecasts  →  Valuation
Competitive position →  ↗
```

The three traditional aims of CDD:
1. **Reduce risk** — understand if revenue is under threat (customers migrating to competition)
2. **Assist valuation** — DCF requires a realistic growth rate; CDD provides market-based validation
3. **Aid integration planning** — identify what must be fixed to capture synergies

### 3.2 Market Analysis Framework

**Table 6.1 (Howson) — CDD Headings:**

**PRICE dimension — market factors:**
- Degree of industry rivalry: number of firms, relative shares, degree of differentiation, stage of industry life cycle, demand/supply balance, fixed cost intensity, height of exit barriers
- Buyer power: relative size of customers, degree of customer concentration, profitability of customers, cost of product relative to buyer's total costs, importance to performance of customer's product, ease of backward integration, ease of switching / cost of substitutes
- Threat of new entrants: cost of know-how, new technology, cost of plant and equipment, importance of experience, economies of scale, degree of commoditisation

**PRICE dimension — competitive position:**
- Relative market share
- Rationality of competitors
- Performance relative to competition against customers' key purchase criteria (KPCs)
- Product strengths: performance, differentiation, innovation
- Relative distribution strengths: on-time delivery, distributor network, salesforce, back office, location/access, strength of customer relationships
- Brand strengths
- Costs relative to competition: cost base, proprietary technology or know-how

**VOLUME dimension — market factors:**
- Existing market share
- Market growth rate
- Stage of product life cycle
- Threat of new products
- Shifts in technology
- Cyclicality / stage of cycle
- Change of regulations

**VOLUME dimension — competitive position:**
- Segments served / not served
- New territories, applications, products

### 3.3 Customers — Key Purchase Criteria (KPCs)

The most important analytical tool in CDD. KPCs are the criteria customers use (consciously or subconsciously) to select one supplier over another.

**KPC interview output example (Howson Table 6.3):**

| KPC | Weighting | Target Score | Target Weighted | Competitor A Score | Competitor A Weighted | Competitor B Score | Competitor B Weighted |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 Price | 50% | 5 | 2.5 | 10 | 5.0 | 4 | 2.0 |
| 2 Quality | 10% | 5 | 0.5 | 5 | 0.5 | 7 | 0.7 |
| 3 Performance | 20% | 5 | 1.0 | 5 | 1.0 | 7 | 1.4 |
| 4 Design | 10% | 5 | 0.5 | 5 | 0.5 | 9 | 0.9 |
| 5 Reliability | 10% | 10 | 1.0 | 5 | 0.5 | 3 | 0.3 |
| **Total** | **100%** | | **5.5** | | **7.5** | | **5.3** |

*What this tells an investor:* The target is outperformed on price and quality but leads on reliability. Competitor A dominates on price. Any business plan claiming market leadership must show KPC scores that support the claim.

### 3.4 CDD Information Sources

**Primary sources (most valuable, in order of importance):**
1. Customers (past, present, non-customers) — KPCs and sourcing intentions
2. Competitors — market dynamics, target's weaknesses
3. Former employees — unguarded truth about operations and culture
4. Industry observers — trade journalists, academics, consultants
5. Corollary suppliers — assess input dynamics
6. Distributors — channel health and target's standing
7. Specifiers — (construction, IT) who drives specification decisions
8. Regulators — compliance status and upcoming requirements
9. Suppliers — pricing dynamics, dependency risk
10. Market leavers — companies that exited; reason reveals structural risk
11. Pressure groups — environmental and labour exposure

**Secondary sources:** Trade journals, broker reports, published market research (Keynote, Mintel, Frost & Sullivan, Datamonitor). Limitation: always out-of-date and seldom niche enough.

### 3.5 CDD Outputs

A CDD report contains three parts:
1. **Facts and analysis** — market size/growth, KPC scoring, competitive position, distribution, management assessment
2. **Opinions** — target's sales growth potential, pricing prospects
3. **Conclusions and recommendations** — the forward DCF narrative, deal-related risks

Key conclusion question: *"What does the future hold for this target company/as a result of doing this deal?"*

## 4. Financial Due Diligence (FDD)

### 4.1 Core Objectives

FDD has four goals:
1. Verify the numbers on which the offer is based
2. Identify deal breakers
3. Provide negotiating ammunition
4. Give confidence in underlying performance and future profits

### 4.2 Maintainable / Underlying Profit

**Maintainable profit** is the underlying profit the business is capable of earning — stripped of one-off, non-recurring, and distorted items. This is the concept closest to "Quality of Earnings" (QoE) in PE/DFI language.

Items that distort reported profit (must be identified and adjusted):
- Group and other management charges and expenses
- Owner expenses (personal costs run through business)
- Transfers of business / reorganisation provisions
- Transfer pricing (intra-group)
- One-off expenditure and income
- Sale of fixed assets
- Pension accounting adjustments
- Insurance claims
- Stock write-offs
- Changes in accounting practices, policies and procedures

### 4.3 P&L Manipulation — Red Flags

Howson's Table 4.3 — P&L manipulations the FDD team watches for:

| Issue | Consequences / Red Flag |
|---|---|
| Sales temporarily inflated by "channel stuffing" | Significant increase in credit notes; sales drop off rapidly post-deal; returns need to reverse |
| Sales dependent on pre-sale effort, not long-term contracts | Post-deal sales may fall without continued selling effort |
| Attempts to increase sales pre-deal have failed | Increased advertising has failed to stimulate sales; business less attractive than thought |
| Sales and cost of sales not matched | Common in construction and long-term contracts; easy to recognise sales early |
| Costs unsustainably low due to inappropriate depreciation | Fixed assets need replacing soon; depreciation will rise; profits will fall accordingly |
| Costs deferred by carrying forward in closing stock | See stock manipulation table below |
| Costs deferred by capitalisation | R&D capitalised; interest on construction borrowings capitalised — will hit P&L eventually |
| Material costs dependent on a few suppliers | Pre-sale deals; suppliers may renegotiate post-change of ownership |
| Labour costs kept artificially low | Pre-sales promises on pay rises; bonuses; NI/NSSF obligations to cease |
| Looming liabilities not provided for | Losses recognised only when realised; crystallisation could occur post-deal |

### 4.4 Balance Sheet Red Flags

**Fixed assets:**
- [ ] Are intangible assets reasonably valued?
- [ ] Is the composition of fixed assets understood?
- [ ] Are detailed registers maintained?
- [ ] Have there been recent independent valuations?
- [ ] Are depreciation policies reasonable and consistently applied?
- [ ] Does the company have clear title to its main assets?
- [ ] Has the company capitalised interest or own labour into fixed assets?
- [ ] What capital expenditure is required in the near future?

**Stock / inventory (Howson Table 4.4):**

| Issue | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Conversion costs (overhead absorption) wrong | Gross profit not sustainable post-acquisition |
| Overhead costs incorrectly allocated | Higher profits in year; will reverse |
| Year-end stock built up artificially | Deferred expenditure into next period |
| Production inefficiencies hidden in stock | Stock write-downs hidden; P&L impact deferred |
| Obsolete / slow-moving stocks | Provisions too low; inflated profits |
| Non-existent stocks | Fraud or deterioration; book position overstated |

**Debtors / receivables:**
- [ ] Is the debtor age profile acceptable?
- [ ] Is there an acceptable system for establishing and enforcing credit terms?
- [ ] Are bad debt reserves created on a reasonable basis?
- [ ] What is the past experience with bad debts?
- [ ] Is the business vulnerable to large debtors defaulting?

**Creditors and liabilities:**
- [ ] Does the company receive any special credit terms?
- [ ] Is the business under creditor pressure?
- [ ] Are there any long-term liabilities (hire purchase, finance leases)?
- [ ] Are there any loans from shareholders repayable short-term?
- [ ] Are provisions properly made and appropriately calculated?
- [ ] Is the warranty provision in line with product performance history?

### 4.5 Cash Flow Analysis

The relationship between reported profit and cash generated is a critical quality indicator.

- Heavy capex requirements versus maintenance requirements
- Sales growth may consume working capital (debtors rise faster than creditors)
- Overdraft limit breaches suggest poor cash management
- Differences between profit and cash need full explanation
- Any exceptional cash inflows or outflows?
- What are the principal uses of cash generated by operations?

### 4.6 Forecasts — The "Hockey Stick" Problem

Howson: *"The typical forecast for any business is a 'hockey stick' a couple of years out. Where a business is being sold, an even brighter picture of the future is painted."*

Questions FDD asks about forecasts:
- [ ] Have forecasts been correctly compiled on the basis of the stated assumptions?
- [ ] Are those assumptions reasonable?
- [ ] Are they clerically accurate?
- [ ] Are the accounting policies used consistent with historical policies?
- [ ] How accurate has forecasting been in the past?
- [ ] Are the forecasts consistent with trends in the management accounts?
- [ ] What are the vulnerabilities?
- [ ] What are the key sensitivities in the forecast?
- [ ] Is projected sales growth volume- or price-based?
- [ ] Are projected changes in gross margins supported by known price rises or cost reductions?
- [ ] Are projected sales levels higher than recent/current levels? If so, to what extent are they reliant on: market share gains? new products? new markets? new customers?

**The Long Form Report covers (comprehensive FDD headings):**
1. History of the business
2. Business organisation (share capital, legal structure, management structure)
3. Business strategy and activities
4. Markets and competitors
5. Sales and marketing
6. Purchasing and supplies
7. Production
8. Premises
9. Human resources
10. Information systems and accounting
11. Accounting policies and practices
12. Management information and control procedures
13. Budgets and budget process
14. Trading performance (3–6 years historical + management accounts)
15. Balance sheets
16. Cash flow statements
17. Tax position
18. Future prospects and forecasts
19. Other matters (litigation, contingent liabilities, capital commitments)

## 5. Legal Due Diligence

### 5.1 Three Objectives

1. Uncover potential liabilities (actual, future, contingent)
2. Find any legal or contractual obstacles to the transaction
3. Form the basis of the final sale and purchase agreement

### 5.2 Warranties and Indemnities

**Warranty:** A statement of fact which the seller confirms to be true. If proven wrong, the buyer can claim damages. Warranties have two functions:
1. The disclosure letter produced by the seller contains a useful information dump
2. Any breach gives the purchaser a right to retrospective price adjustment

**Indemnity:** A guaranteed remedy against a specific liability, regardless of whether the value of the target is affected. Indemnities are used where the liability is quantified but contingent — for example, tax liabilities, environmental cleanup, pending litigation.

**Other forms of protection following adverse DD findings:**
- Price adjustment
- Retention from purchase price
- Earn-out structures
- Third-party guarantees
- Insurance
- Asset sale rather than share sale
- Exclusion of certain assets/liabilities
- Rectification at seller's cost

### 5.3 Misrepresentation Warning

In the UK, under Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 Section 397, making a statement, promise or forecast which is misleading, false or deceptive in a material particular is a criminal offence. Sellers and their advisers must not say anything they cannot support. In Uganda, equivalent exposure arises under the Companies Act (Cap. 110) and general fraud provisions — overstated claims in an information memorandum can void a transaction and expose directors to liability.

## 6. Human Resources Due Diligence

### 6.1 Why HR DD Is Underestimated

KPMG research cited by Howson: employee problems are the cause of up to half of all acquisition failures. The KPMG study of pre-deal activities and their relationship to success showed that "soft keys" — selecting the management team, resolving cultural issues, and communications — have a larger positive impact on deal success than "hard keys" such as synergy identification and due diligence itself.

### 6.2 HR DD Objectives

- Identify potential deal breakers (unfunded pension liability, significant redundancy exposure)
- Uncover significant liabilities for the negotiating table
- Assess potential costs and risks from cultural differences
- Identify HR issues to be addressed during integration

### 6.3 Key HR Investigation Areas

- [ ] Organisation chart and reporting lines — documented? Current?
- [ ] Details of all directors and senior managers (experience, qualifications, duties, remuneration, service agreements, benefits, pension)
- [ ] Details of all staff (date of birth, age, date of employment commencement, length of service, salary, benefits, notice period, department, location)
- [ ] Details of dismissals, redundancies, disciplinary and grievance procedures in last 6 months
- [ ] Staff turnover rates — low or high by industry standards? What is the trend?
- [ ] Details of any union agreements and staff in each trade union
- [ ] Salary/wage payment structure, management levels and grading system
- [ ] Personnel policies, staff manuals, employee handbooks
- [ ] Holiday pay arrangements
- [ ] Any promised changes to terms and conditions including pending pay increases
- [ ] Details of training spend
- [ ] Are growth prospects threatened by skill shortages?
- [ ] Details of consultants or agents who provide regular services
- [ ] Pension scheme arrangements — is it adequately funded?

## 7. Management Due Diligence

### 7.1 The Management Premium

Howson: *"Management can add more value to an acquisition than almost anything. On the other hand, 'there is no prospect so good that incompetent management cannot screw it up.'"*

Private equity investors consistently report that their investment decision is driven more by their view of management than by any other factor. Between 50% and 75% of key managers leave of their own accord within 2–3 years of being acquired — making pre-deal management assessment essential.

### 7.2 Management Appraisal Methods

1. **Documentation analysis** — CVs, job evaluations, anything written by or about the manager
2. **Past-oriented interviews** — structured, fact-based interviews on actual past performance ("Tell me about a time when...")
3. **References** — 360-degree from customers, analysts, former colleagues; current and former colleagues are best sources
4. **Work samples** — answers to hypothetical questions ("How would you manage the integration of these two companies?")
5. **Shadowing** — observing the team in action; relationship dynamics

### 7.3 Management Competencies Assessed (Appendix B3)

Commonly assessed competencies include:
- Strategic thinking and business acumen
- Leadership and people management
- Commercial orientation and customer focus
- Financial literacy and control
- Communication and influencing
- Adaptability and change management
- Integrity and governance standards

### 7.4 Management Assessment Questions

- [ ] Who are the key people the business depends on?
- [ ] What would happen if one or more left?
- [ ] Is there a succession plan?
- [ ] Do management team meetings take place regularly? Are they minuted?
- [ ] Has management set clear strategic goals?
- [ ] Are there documented performance metrics for senior staff?
- [ ] Are management incentivised appropriately (share options, bonuses, retention schemes)?
- [ ] What is the reputation of management with customers and suppliers?

## 8. Tax Due Diligence

### 8.1 Tax DD Objectives

Tax DD is best carried out by a tax expert working closely with the FDD team. Objectives:
- Carry out a detailed review of the company's tax affairs
- Assess whether tax provisions are adequate
- Assess whether there is a risk of additional tax liabilities emerging

### 8.2 Tax DD Checklist

- [ ] Summarise tax provisions for the last 3–6 years
- [ ] Reconcile the tax charge in the accounts to prevailing statutory rates
- [ ] Describe deferred tax provisioning policy
- [ ] Are recent tax computations agreed with the Revenue authority?
- [ ] If not, what issues are outstanding or being disputed?
- [ ] Are any years still open to assessment?
- [ ] Has the company engaged in any tax avoidance schemes?
- [ ] Has tax always been paid on time? Has the company ever been penalised?
- [ ] Copy of VAT returns for current and previous years
- [ ] Copy of the VAT account including VAT bad debt account, PAYE and NIC/NSSF account
- [ ] Confirm VAT payments and returns are up to date with no default surcharge notices
- [ ] Details of the latest control visits by tax authorities
- [ ] Details of any intra-group transactions not on arm's length basis
- [ ] Are any purchases made on a non-arm's length basis?
- [ ] Details of transfer pricing policy

*Uganda-specific note:* URA conducts formal risk assessments. Outstanding EFRIS non-compliance (penalty UGX 8M/month), PAYE arrears, and withholding tax disputes are the most common adverse findings. Tax clearance certificates (TCCs) from URA will be required before DFI disbursement.

## 9. Environmental and IT Due Diligence

### 9.1 Environmental DD

Environmental DD is most critical in manufacturing, extractive, agricultural, and chemical businesses. Environmental violations are often more critical in acquiring a manufacturing operation than, for example, a department store chain.

Key areas:
- [ ] Details of past environmental assessments
- [ ] Copies of any notices of environmental claims, violations, prosecutions
- [ ] Employee claims/complaints or insurance claims related to environment
- [ ] Copies of correspondence with environmental regulators or third parties
- [ ] Are directors aware of any environmental problems?
- [ ] Site evaluations and governmental filings re: hazardous materials or toxic substances

*Uganda-specific:* NEMA environmental permits are required for Category B and C projects. DFI lenders typically require an Environmental and Social Management Plan (ESMP) for loans above USD 500,000. Failure to disclose environmental issues is a common deal disruption point.

### 9.2 IT Due Diligence

- [ ] Are management information systems producing timely and reliable information?
- [ ] Are the management information systems fully integrated?
- [ ] Is the business dependent on third parties for software/hardware maintenance?
- [ ] Have systems weaknesses been noted in auditors' management letters?
- [ ] Confirmation that the company's systems, software and technology are owned solely by the company and do not infringe any other party's rights
- [ ] What key performance indicators does the business use to monitor performance?
- [ ] Are there systems to ensure effective cash management?
- [ ] Describe main computer systems and review third-party maintenance contracts
- [ ] Report on software ownership and maintenance
- [ ] Assess back-up arrangements
- [ ] Assess adequacy of current systems for present and future needs

**EMPLOYEE INFORMATION — Directors and Senior Managers — Management, Legal, HR, Financial**

**LITIGATION — Legal**

- [ ] How frequently has the business been involved in litigation in the past?

R&D needed to improve position? degree of regulatory change? competitive forces? how cyclical? economic variable sensitivity?

**OPERATIONS / PRODUCTION — Operational, Financial**
- [ ] Brief description of production methods and the relative position to the "leading edge"
- [ ] Summary of recent production problems
- [ ] Constraints on production capacity
- [ ] Immediate capex requirements

**PROJECTIONS AND FORECASTS — Financial**
- [ ] Are regular budgets produced? Are variances monitored? Are budgets updated?
- [ ] Copy of current year's budget and estimate of trading results for the current year
- [ ] Copies of P&L and cash flow projections (1–2 years) with underlying assumptions
- [ ] Have budgets/forecasts in last 2 years been accurate? If not, why?
- [ ] Were projections prepared especially for the DD review or are they normal operating budgets?
- [ ] Do projections assume any changes in accounting policies or bases?
- [ ] Are reductions being projected in stock levels or debtor days? Justification?
- [ ] Are increases being projected in creditor days? Justification?
- [ ] Have the projections been approved by the Board?
- [ ] Are there any projected cost savings that depend upon future actions?
- [ ] Details of medium-term (3–5 year) forecasts
- [ ] Is projected sales growth volume- or price-based?
- [ ] How sensitive is the target's performance to external factors (economic cycle, fashion, proposed legislation)?
- [ ] Are projected changes in gross margins supported by known price rises, cost reductions or firmly priced orders/contracts?

**PROPERTY — Legal, Property, Financial**
- [ ] Details of premises (address, function, terms of ownership, location, size, description, dilapidation clauses, rent and rates payable, recent valuations)
- [ ] Do recent valuations differ significantly from book value?
- [ ] Sight of any recent independent or internal valuations or insurance reports
- [ ] Copies of all appraisals
- [ ] Comments on availability of any spare land
- [ ] Management's assessment of future premises requirements

**PURCHASING AND SUPPLY — Operational, Financial**
- [ ] Degree of supplier concentration
- [ ] Details of alternative arrangements for important materials currently single-sourced
- [ ] Have disruptions in supply arisen recently?
- [ ] Are subcontracting arrangements well established with alternatives in place?
- [ ] What factors affect the price of purchases? Are prices generally stable?
- [ ] Are there alternative sources of supply?
- [ ] Are there any unusual payment terms to suppliers?
- [ ] Are there any contracted forward purchase commitments?
- [ ] Are volume rebate agreements in place?
- [ ] Are any purchases made on a non-arm's length basis?

**REGULATORY — Legal, Operational, Environmental, HR**

- [ ] List of major customers with agreements; summary of those agreements
- [ ] Details of membership of trade associations or professional bodies

- [ ] Have returns, guarantee or warranty claims increased recently?
- [ ] Are there any exceptional/non-recurring revenues?
- [ ] Have unit prices increased or come under severe pressure?
- [ ] Degree to which discounting is a regular trading feature
- [ ] Has the basis of calculating discounts changed in the last 3 years?
- [ ] Are sales seasonal?
- [ ] Impact of exchange rate movements on pricing?
- [ ] Extent of sales to related parties

- [ ] Copy of any business plans in existence
- [ ] What is the essence of the company strategy?
- [ ] Who is involved in setting the strategy?
- [ ] When was the strategy last updated?
- [ ] Has the strategy changed recently?
- [ ] Have external consultants advised on strategy?

- [ ] Has the company ever been investigated by tax authorities?

- [ ] Details of latest control visits by tax authorities

**WORKING CAPITAL — Financial**

- [ ] What levels of stock loss have arisen on recent stock counts?

- [ ] Are there any unusual sundry creditors or accruals?
- [ ] Is the business under pressure to pay creditors more quickly?
- [ ] How is deferred income calculated?

## 11. Red Flags and Deal Breakers

### 11.1 Financial Red Flags

The following, identified during DD, commonly kill deals or trigger significant price reductions:

- **Overstated receivables** — aged debtors beyond 90 days not provided for; significant concentration in one debtor
- **Understated liabilities** — warranty provisions too thin, contingent liabilities undisclosed
- **Pension under-funding** — a black hole that can dwarf operating profit
- **Aggressive revenue recognition** — sales recognised before delivery, milestone-based contracts front-loaded
- **Channel stuffing** — credit notes spike post-due diligence
- **Unsustainably low depreciation** — fixed assets need replacement soon
- **Working capital "window dressing"** — year-end stock built up, creditors stretched, debtors collected early to show a clean balance sheet
- **Related-party transactions not at arm's length** — owner draws disguised as management charges, supplier contracts with connected parties
- **Tax skeletons** — open years, outstanding assessments, VAT under-declared
- **Hockey-stick forecasts without market evidence** — CDD cannot support the growth assumptions

### 11.2 Commercial Red Flags

- High customer concentration (top 3 customers > 60% of revenue) without long-term contracts
- No documented evidence of KPC leadership in any competitive dimension
- Market in structural decline masked by recent one-off events
- Customers who are "voting with their feet" — lost accounts in last 2 years not replaced
- Price discounting as primary competitive weapon (implies commodity positioning)
- Single-supplier dependency for key inputs with no alternative sourcing
- Management claims market leadership but CDD KPC interviews contradict it

### 11.3 Legal and Regulatory Red Flags

- Undisclosed litigation, even if management considers it immaterial
- IP not properly registered or owned by individuals, not the company
- Licences or consents not transferred or transferable
- Employment contracts not in writing or non-compliant with labour law
- Environmental violations with potential for regulatory action
- Data protection breaches or inadequate data governance
- Shares held by undisclosed parties or with encumbrances

### 11.4 Management Red Flags

- Key person dependency — business cannot operate without one individual
- Founder who has never developed a second tier of management
- High management turnover in the last 3 years
- Management unable to articulate a clear strategy
- Board minutes non-existent or not maintained
- Management incentive not aligned with investor returns
- Resistance to DD or information requests — a universal red flag

### 11.5 Material Adverse Change (MAC) Triggers

MAC clauses allow investors to withdraw if, between signing and closing, the target suffers:
- A significant deterioration in financial performance
- Loss of a major customer or contract
- Regulatory action or licence suspension
- Key management departure
- Material litigation commenced

In a business plan context, MAC thinking applies to risk disclosures: an investor expects you to have identified and mitigated the events that could constitute a MAC before they fund you.

## 12. Preparing FOR Due Diligence — Sell-Side / Investment Readiness

This section is the most directly applicable to a Ugandan entrepreneur presenting to a DFI (e.g., UDB, IFC, AfDB, UNCDF) or private equity investor.

### 12.1 The Core Principle

Howson frames due diligence as a tool for the buyer. For the seller or investee company, the equivalent exercise is **vendor due diligence (VDD)** or **investment readiness preparation** — conducting your own DD before the investor does, fixing what you find, and packaging the evidence.

Every question in this reference file is a question an investor *will* ask. Your job is to have a documented, honest answer ready before the meeting.

### 12.2 What Must Be Prepared Before Investor DD

**Tier 1 — Non-negotiable (must exist before first meeting):**
- [ ] 3 years audited financial statements (or audited management accounts if < 3 years old)
- [ ] Current-year management accounts (not more than 60 days old)
- [ ] Business plan with explicit, sourced assumptions for all projections
- [ ] Corporate structure documents: certificate of incorporation, articles, share register
- [ ] Certificate of compliance / good standing from the registrar (URSB in Uganda)
- [ ] Tax clearance certificate (TCC) from URA
- [ ] All licences and regulatory permits required to operate
- [ ] Proof of ownership / lease for all premises and key assets
- [ ] Written employment contracts for all key staff

**Tier 2 — Required during DD (prepare before investor engagement):**
- [ ] Key customer contracts (top 5 customers by revenue)
- [ ] Key supplier contracts
- [ ] IP registrations (URSB trademarks, patents; ARIPO where applicable)
- [ ] Bank statements for last 24 months
- [ ] Aged debtor and creditor schedules
- [ ] Fixed asset register
- [ ] Insurance schedules
- [ ] Organisation chart
- [ ] CVs for directors and all senior managers
- [ ] Management succession plan

**Tier 3 — DFI-specific additions:**
- [ ] Environmental and Social Management Plan (ESMP) for projects with environmental impact
- [ ] Board composition documentation (for loans > UGX 500M, DFIs expect an independent director or governance structure)
- [ ] Gender and social inclusion data (women and youth employment numbers, sourcing from vulnerable groups)
- [ ] Development impact narrative — SDG alignment, job creation, sector contribution
- [ ] Collateral documentation: title deeds, valuation reports, pledge agreements

### 12.3 The Management Presentation

When presenting to investors, Howson's framework suggests the management presentation must address, in order:
1. **Business overview** — what you do, for whom, and how (the business model)
2. **Market evidence** — size, growth, your position, KPC scoring vs competitors
3. **Financial performance** — historical maintainable earnings, working capital trends, cash conversion
4. **Forecast and assumptions** — the hockey stick acknowledged and justified
5. **Management team** — qualifications, track record, skin in the game
6. **Use of funds** — specific, traceable, costed deployment plan
7. **Exit / return** — for equity: expected return, exit route; for debt: DSCR, collateral cover

**Credibility tests investors apply during presentations:**
- Internal consistency: does the market growth rate support your revenue forecast?
- Assumption grounding: where did your market size figure come from?
- Honesty: have you disclosed risks, not just opportunities?
- Depth: can every member of the management team answer questions in their domain?
- Commercial awareness: do you know your competitors' KPC scores, not just your own?

### 12.4 Common Business Plan Claims That Fail DD

| Claim in Business Plan | What DD Will Find | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "The market is worth UGX X billion and growing at Y%" | No source cited; figure is aspirational | Cite UBOS, sector report, UDB data; be specific about your addressable segment |
| "We have no significant competitors" | Competitors exist and are winning customers | Document competitors honestly; explain your differentiation |
| "Our financial projections are conservative" | Gross margins are above industry benchmarks with no explanation | Reconcile margins to industry norms or explain the structural reason for premium margins |
| "We have strong customer relationships" | Top 3 customers = 80% of revenue; no long-term contracts | Produce at minimum letters of intent or framework agreements; document relationship history |
| "Our management team has 30 years combined experience" | CVs do not document relevant experience; no second-tier management | Show specific track record evidence; document succession |
| "We comply with all regulatory requirements" | TCC not obtained; NSSF contributions in arrears; unlicensed premises | Clean these up before DD; present clean compliance status |
| "The business generates strong cash flow" | Profit and cash diverge significantly | Reconcile profit to cash; explain working capital investment |

### 12.5 Vendor Due Diligence (VDD)

VDD is DD commissioned by the seller on themselves, intended to be shared with potential buyers. It solves several problems:
- Accelerates the process (buyers can rely on independent report)
- Enables the seller to manage the information flow
- Identifies and allows correction of issues before buyer sees them
- Reduces deal disruption from late-stage surprises

For Ugandan businesses seeking DFI or PE funding, a VDD-equivalent (an independent business review by a reputable local accounting firm) signals seriousness and significantly increases the probability of closing.

## 13. Investment Readiness — Practical Summary

### 13.1 The Five Things That Kill Ugandan Deals in DD

Based on the Howson framework applied to the Uganda DFI/PE context:

1. **Unaudited financials or accounts that do not reconcile to tax returns** — URA records and audited accounts must agree; discrepancies signal tax evasion risk
2. **Key person dependency with no second tier** — a business that only works while the founder is present cannot scale and cannot survive a health crisis; investors require evidence of functional management depth
3. **Customer concentration without contracts** — one or two oral relationships generating 70%+ of revenue is a dealbreaker for most DFIs; minimum requirement is evidence of repeat purchase history and written documentation of ongoing commercial relationship
4. **Missing regulatory compliance** — operating without NEMA permits, without UNBS certification where required, or with EFRIS non-compliance triggers a mandatory remediation condition; some investors will not proceed until resolved
5. **Hockey-stick forecasts detached from market evidence** — projections that show 200% year-on-year growth in Year 3 without a credible market share and competitive strategy explanation destroy credibility across the entire plan

### 13.2 Investment Readiness Scoring (Howson principles applied)

Rate each dimension 1–5:

| Dimension | 1 (Very Weak) | 3 (Adequate) | 5 (Investment-Ready) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Financial records | Unaudited, incomplete | Audited 2 years, current mgmt accounts | 3 years audited + monthly management accounts + tax reconciliation |
| Market evidence | Undocumented estimates | Industry data cited | Primary research (customer interviews, KPC scoring) + secondary sources |
| Customer quality | Oral, undocumented | Written contracts for major customers | Long-term contracts, diversified base, documented NPS/satisfaction |
| Management depth | Founder only | Founder + 1–2 functional managers | Documented team with relevant CVs, org chart, succession plan |
| Regulatory compliance | Multiple gaps | Minor gaps being resolved | Full compliance documented; TCC, all permits, NSSF current |
| Business plan assumptions | Unsourced | Partially sourced | Every assumption sourced, sensitivity analysis provided |

Score 24–30: Investment-ready — proceed to investor approach
Score 18–23: Conditional — resolve gaps before formal submission
Score below 18: Pre-investment readiness stage — 6–18 months preparation required

## 15. The Briefing Checklist — For Investment Presentations

(Adapted from Howson Appendix A3)

When briefing advisers or preparing for a formal investor presentation, confirm the following:

- [ ] Business plan and financial model are finalised and internally consistent
- [ ] All assumptions sourced and documented
- [ ] Management team briefed and rehearsed on their domain questions
- [ ] Data room assembled and indexed
- [ ] Confidentiality agreement template prepared
- [ ] Reasons for seeking investment clearly articulated (not just "we need money")
- [ ] Strategic rationale: what does this investment enable that the business cannot achieve organically?
- [ ] Post-investment plans: asset acquisition, expansion milestones, hiring plan
- [ ] To what extent will the investor be involved in governance?
- [ ] What information will you provide regularly after investment?
- [ ] Buyer/investor's existing knowledge of your sector — what do you need to explain vs what can you assume?
- [ ] Audience for DD reports: board, bankers, DFI investment committee
- [ ] Materiality limits: what threshold of issue will you disclose proactively?
- [ ] Your attitude to risk: conservative or aggressive in assumptions?

## 16. Key Concepts Summary

| Term | Howson Definition | Business Plan Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Maintainable profit | Underlying profit after removing one-off, non-recurring, and owner-distorted items | Present "normalised" earnings in financial section; show adjustments transparently |
| Quality of earnings (QoE) | How reliably reported profit converts to cash; how much is recurring | Demonstrate EBITDA-to-cash conversion; distinguish contract vs spot revenue |
| Hockey stick | Forecast pattern where future growth is dramatically higher than history | Justify every inflection point with market evidence or a specific catalyst |
| Key purchase criteria (KPCs) | The weighted factors customers use to choose between suppliers | Document your KPC advantage in the competitive analysis section |
| Customer concentration risk | Over-dependence on a small number of customers | Show customer diversification; produce written evidence of relationship durability |
| Material adverse change (MAC) | Event that substantially worsens the target's prospects post-signing | Identify and mitigate in risk analysis; show contingency plans |
| Vendor due diligence (VDD) | DD commissioned by the seller on themselves, shared with buyers | Commission an independent business review before approaching DFIs |
| Long Form Report | The primary FDD output: comprehensive written analysis of the target | The business plan is your self-authored equivalent — it must anticipate the same questions |
| Deal breaker | A finding so serious it stops the transaction entirely | Red flags list: fix these before investor approach |
| Indemnity | A guaranteed remedy against a specific, quantified liability | In business plan context: disclose known liabilities and document provisions |
