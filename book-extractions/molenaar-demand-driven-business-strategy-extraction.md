# Book Extraction: Cor Molenaar - Demand-Driven Business Strategy

**Source:** Molenaar, Cor. *Demand-Driven Business Strategy: Digital Transformation and Business Model Innovation*.

**Why this matters:** This source sharpens the engine's advice on business-model redesign. Its strongest contribution is the distinction between simple digitisation and true demand-driven transformation built on customer behaviour, data, platforms, and value networks.

---

## 1. Core Thesis

The shift that matters is not analogue to digital by itself. It is supply-driven to demand-driven.

Molenaar's key warning is especially useful:

- **digitalisation is not enough**
- moving existing processes online does not equal strategy
- the business model must be redesigned around demand, not inventory or internal convenience

## 2. Demand-Driven vs Supply-Driven

The book contrasts:

- **supply-driven** - push what the company has
- **demand-driven** - organise around what customers want, when they want it, and how they signal it

This helps the engine reject shallow digital claims such as:

- "we will build a website"
- "we will use an app"
- "we will advertise online"

without a corresponding change in how demand is understood and served.

## 3. Data as the Strategic Core

Molenaar is explicit that data becomes a central asset in demand-driven firms:

- browsing data
- behavioural data
- social data
- need detection
- predictive modelling

The implication is that plans should explain how technology improves demand sensing, not just transaction processing.

## 4. Platforms, Value Networks, and Outside-In Design

The book is particularly strong on three related ideas:

- platforms create value by matching needs and supply
- value networks matter more than isolated firm control
- demand-driven design works from **outside in**

That means the engine should ask:

- what customer signal starts the process
- how supply, fulfilment, and communication reorganise around that signal
- whether the business should remain a pipeline or become more of a coordinator, marketplace participant, subscription service, or service layer

## 5. Product-as-a-Service and Subscription Logic

One of the most practical model shifts discussed is the move from ownership to access:

- subscription
- leasing
- service wraparounds
- product-as-a-service

This is useful for the repo because many plans can improve resilience and cash-flow visibility through recurring or service-led models rather than one-off sales only.

## 6. Network Effects and Channel Power

The book highlights that competitive advantage increasingly comes from:

- network effects
- ecosystem position
- direct knowledge of customer behaviour
- selling through channels with better data, not just to channels

This is a strong corrective against plans that describe channels only as distribution endpoints.

## 7. Hardening Rules for the Repo

- distinguish digitisation from business-model transformation
- require digital strategies to explain how demand is sensed, predicted, or matched
- test whether subscription, access, or service models are more resilient than one-off sales
- assess whether platform participation or ecosystem partnership creates better economics than stand-alone buildout
- map the digital operating model from customer signal backward into fulfilment

## 8. Uganda / East Africa Application Notes

- Demand-driven logic is powerful where mobile-first behaviour, informal discovery, and rapid switching shape demand.
- Product-as-a-service can be especially relevant for equipment, mobility, solar, irrigation, logistics, software, and B2B service layers.
- Outside-in design is a good discipline for SMEs that otherwise build around owner preference rather than customer evidence.
