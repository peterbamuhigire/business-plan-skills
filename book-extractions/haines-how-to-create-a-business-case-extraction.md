# Book Extraction: Steven Haines - How to Create a Business Case

**Source:** Haines, Steven. *How to Create a Business Case* (2022).

**Why this matters:** This source strengthens the engine's ability to justify major initiatives inside a business plan, especially digitisation, new capacity, systems upgrades, channel expansion, and product launches. It pushes the suite toward clearer options, better financial logic, and post-implementation review.

---

## 1. Core Thesis

A business case is a disciplined justification for an investment, not a slogan and not a foregone conclusion.

The engine should use this logic whenever a plan recommends a material initiative such as:

- a new digital platform
- a factory expansion
- an ERP or CRM rollout
- market expansion
- a product launch
- an automation or AI programme

## 2. Start With the Problem and the Decision

Haines emphasises that every case should begin with:

- the problem or opportunity
- why it matters now
- how it aligns with strategy
- the actual funding request

This is directly useful for business plans because many weak plans jump to solutioning before proving the need.

## 3. Show Options, Including Do Nothing

One of the most portable lessons is that the status quo must be evaluated explicitly.

The repo should therefore require material initiatives to compare:

- do nothing
- low-cost / low-risk option
- preferred option
- high-ambition option when relevant

This improves credibility and reduces technology theatre.

## 4. Financial Analysis Must Be Incremental

Haines is strong on incremental economics. The business case should state:

- incremental revenue
- incremental costs
- capex
- opex
- cash flow timing
- payback
- break-even
- NPV or equivalent return logic when appropriate

This fits directly with Section 10 and Section 11 work.

## 5. Sensitivity and Risk

The source is particularly useful in two places:

- **sensitivity analysis** - change one variable at a time
- **risk analysis** - test milestone, technology, support, and timing risks

This is a useful hardening rule for any plan that depends on a major technology or expansion assumption.

## 6. Cross-Functional Ownership and Storyline

The business case is not just a spreadsheet. Haines stresses:

- cross-functional input
- a clear narrative
- early stakeholder alignment
- documented assumptions
- version control

That is useful for repo workflows because a world-class plan must read as a decision case, not a collection of disconnected sections.

## 7. Audit the Case After Implementation

One of the best ideas in the book is the post-implementation look-back:

- what was assumed
- what actually happened
- what variance emerged
- what corrective action is required

This is valuable for quarterly game plans and monitoring frameworks, especially when clients want practical strategy rather than theoretical planning.

## 8. Hardening Rules for the Repo

- require a business-case summary for major initiatives inside a plan
- force a do-nothing option and at least one credible alternative
- link use of funds to explicit incremental benefits and timing
- require assumption testing and one-variable sensitivity checks
- include post-implementation audit logic for high-capex or high-tech bets

## 9. Uganda / East Africa Application Notes

- Business-case discipline is especially important where capital is scarce and lenders demand credible use-of-funds logic.
- Incremental cash-flow timing matters in environments with delayed collections, working-capital strain, and currency risk.
- Do-nothing comparisons help founders avoid over-investing in systems or facilities before demand justifies them.
