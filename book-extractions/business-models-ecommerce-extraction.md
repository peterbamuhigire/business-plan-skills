# Book Extraction: Business Models for E-Commerce

**Source:** *Business Models for E-Commerce*.

**Why this matters:** This source strengthens the engine's treatment of digitisation as a business-model and operating-model choice, not just a marketing channel. Its most useful contribution is the structured view of e-commerce model types, value creation logic, and the cost implications of digital channels.

---

## 1. Core Thesis

E-commerce should be treated as a business model design decision with explicit value creation, revenue logic, cost effects, supporting resources, and process implications.

This is important because many plans use "online" as shorthand for modernity without describing:

- the transaction model
- the revenue model
- the process changes
- the cost reductions or new costs
- the enabling systems

## 2. Business Model Components That Matter

The most useful portable questions from the book are:

- what value proposition is being delivered online
- which customer segment the model serves
- what revenue model applies
- what cost structure changes as the model digitises
- what resources and systems are required
- what monetary and non-monetary value is created

This is useful for both new digital businesses and traditional businesses adding digital channels.

## 3. Demand-Side, Supply-Side, and Collaborative Models

The book groups e-commerce models in ways that are directly useful to the repo:

- **Demand-side models** - storefronts, online sales, customer-facing digital channels
- **Supply-side models** - e-procurement, sourcing, auction, supplier integration
- **Collaborative models** - shared planning, lifecycle coordination, information sharing

That matters because digitisation is not only a sales issue. It can improve procurement, inventory, fulfilment, and partner coordination as well.

## 4. Value Creation Through Revenue and Cost

The strongest practical lesson is that digital models create value in two directions:

- revenue enhancement
- cost reduction

Good plans should therefore spell out whether digitisation is expected to:

- increase conversion
- expand geographic reach
- improve retention
- reduce transaction cost
- reduce inventory cost
- reduce administrative cost
- shorten cycle time

## 5. Revenue Model Patterns to Keep Available

Useful recurring patterns include:

- direct online sales
- commission-based marketplaces
- subscription and service contracts
- transaction fees
- B2B ordering and procurement models
- B2B2C arrangements

These patterns should be matched deliberately to the business, rather than inserted because they sound digital.

## 6. Collaboration as an Efficiency Lever

The collaborative-commerce sections are useful for operational planning:

- product lifecycle management
- collaborative planning and replenishment
- supplier/customer information exchange
- workflow and knowledge-sharing tools

This is especially relevant where the business plan depends on distributed suppliers, quality control, design collaboration, or replenishment accuracy.

## 7. Hardening Rules for the Repo

- require plans to state the specific digital transaction model, not just "sell online"
- require both revenue and cost logic for e-commerce recommendations
- consider digitisation on both the demand side and the supply side
- encourage collaborative and systems integration thinking where supplier complexity is high

## 8. Uganda / East Africa Application Notes

- Mobile money, WhatsApp ordering, lightweight storefronts, and agent-assisted commerce often matter more than complex full-stack e-commerce.
- Supply-side digitisation can be high ROI for SMEs where procurement leakage, stock-outs, and manual reconciliation are major pain points.
- Marketplace and commission models must reflect trust, fulfilment reliability, and payment friction in the local context.
