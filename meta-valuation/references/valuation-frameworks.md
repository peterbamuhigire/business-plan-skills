# Valuation Frameworks

Use this file to keep valuation grounded in business economics rather than negotiation posture alone.

## 1. Core rule

Value comes from expected cash flows, growth, returns on invested capital, and risk. Multiples are shortcuts, not substitutes for thinking.

## 2. DCF essentials

Use DCF when the business has enough visibility on:

- revenue drivers
- margins
- reinvestment needs
- working capital
- tax
- cost of capital

Key rules:

- value the operating business first
- use free cash flow, not accounting profit
- make terminal assumptions realistic
- test sensitivity in growth, margins, and capital intensity

## 3. Relative valuation essentials

Use comparables to answer: how does the market price similar assets?

Rules:

- standardise the numerator and denominator
- use forward-looking figures where possible
- compare businesses with similar growth, margin, and risk
- avoid using a multiple just because it is common in decks

Useful checks:

- EV/Revenue for early growth businesses
- EV/EBITDA for operating businesses
- P/E only when earnings quality is strong
- P/Book for financial or asset-heavy cases

## 4. Cost of capital discipline

Do not treat the discount rate as a plug.

The cost of capital should reflect:

- business risk
- country and currency risk
- capital structure
- stage and liquidity limitations

Fix weak forecasts before inflating the discount rate to compensate.

## 5. Terminal value discipline

Terminal value often dominates the model. Treat it as a critical assumption.

Use one of:

- stable growth perpetuity with realistic long-run growth
- exit multiple consistent with the sector and maturity state

If terminal value drives nearly all value, flag the model as weakly anchored.

## 6. Scenario thinking

Good valuation uses scenarios because business outcomes are path-dependent.

Change:

- adoption speed
- pricing power
- gross margin
- capex and working capital needs
- exit conditions

Do not create fake precision.

## 7. Dilution logic

Translate value into ownership carefully:

- pre-money + new money = post-money
- investor ownership = new money / post-money

Then ask:

- does the founder still have enough incentive?
- does the investor receive enough potential upside?
- does this round leave room for future rounds?

## 8. Common errors

- valuing based on TAM rather than capture economics
- using peer multiples without adjusting for growth and quality
- ignoring reinvestment needs
- using optimistic terminal growth
- hiding uncertainty behind a single number
