## 1. The Revision Process

Writing is creative work; revision is critical work (NIBM). Budget equal time for each.

### Seven-Step Revision

1. **Immediately after drafting** — Edit for stated purpose, logical progression, smooth transitions, accurate spelling
2. **Let it cool** — Set aside for at least several hours. Fresh eyes catch what tired ones miss
3. **Second edit** — Re-read with the investor's perspective. Does each paragraph earn its place?
4. **Clean copy** — Generate a formatted version, then proofread again
5. **Check transitions** — Sentences directly before and after any correction may need adjustment
6. **Outside review** — Have someone unfamiliar with the business read it. Can they answer the six verification questions?
7. **Final polish** — Consider every suggestion. Better to catch errors now than after distribution

### Rapid Writing Technique

When stuck, use "rapid writing" (Klauser via NIBM): write without stopping for 10 minutes. No corrections, no judgement. The best ideas often come after hitting a "wall" of seeming emptiness. Edit the output later — never during.

## 2. Sentence-Level Precision

### Active Voice

Passive verbs kill prose. They drain energy, sound evasive, and obscure responsibility.

| Passive (avoid) | Active (use) |
|---|---|
| Revenue of $2.4M was generated | We generated $2.4M in revenue |
| A survey was done by the research division | The research division surveyed |
| It can be concluded that costs are rising | Costs are rising |
| The market is being disrupted by AI | AI is disrupting the market |

**How to activate passive sentences:**
- Eliminate "is," "are," "was," "were" wherever practical
- Delete suffixes (-ment, -sion, -ive, -ful, -ance) from nouns, convert to verbs: "made an acquisition" → "acquired"
- Abolish empty main clauses: "It is a fact that..." → delete and state the fact
- Begin with the logical subject, not conversational padding

### Positive Language

State what IS, not what ISN'T. Positive writing is clearer, crisper, more confident.

| Negative (avoid) | Positive (use) |
|---|---|
| did not like | disliked |
| not honest | dishonest |
| not important | trivial |
| did not remember | forgot |
| not enough | insufficient |
| did not have much confidence in | distrusted |

Use "not" only for denial or contrasting opposing qualities.

### Be Specific

Replace vague qualifiers with concrete data. A tree is an oak, a sapling, or a flowering plum — never merely "a tree" (NIBM).

| Vague (avoid) | Specific (use) |
|---|---|
| a significant market | a $4.2B addressable market |
| strong growth | 34% year-over-year growth |
| experienced team | 47 combined years in fintech |
| competitive pricing | 30% below market average |
| many customers | 2,400 active subscribers |

Effective adjectives appeal to the senses or define precise quantities. Vague adjectives ("a living wage," "good attendance," "reasonable conditions") pose more questions than they answer.

### Sentence Length

Research findings on comprehension by average sentence length (NIBM/American Press Institute):

| Avg words/sentence | Comprehension |
|---|---|
| 8 or less | Very easy |
| 11 | Easy |
| 14 | Fairly easy |
| **17** | **Standard (target)** |
| 21 | Fairly difficult |
| 25 | Difficult |
| 29 | Very difficult |

**Rule:** Target 17-20 words per sentence. If a sentence exceeds 30 words, split it. Vary length to avoid monotony.

## 3. Word Economy Rules

"Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences" — William Strunk Jr.

### What to Prune

- **Nouns used as verbs** — "She debuted as director" → "She made her debut as director"
- **Fad words and buzzwords** — parameters, ball-park figures, game-changing, synergy
- **Jargon** — Define technical terms on first use, or replace with plain language
- **Conditionals** — Remove "would," "could," "might" unless expressing genuine uncertainty
- **Abbreviations** — Spell out on first use, then use short form: "Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)"
- **Cliches** — "at the end of the day," "move the needle," "low-hanging fruit" — replace with specific language
- **Needless intensifiers** — very, quite, rather, somewhat, relatively, definitely, comparatively
- **Absolutes used carelessly** — totally unique, completely perfect, most innovative ever
- **Needless repetitions** — "past history," "future plans," "consensus of opinion"
- **Lazy adjectives** — Define with adjectives, describe with verbs: "His voracious eyes looked at the food" → "His eyes devoured the food"

## 4. Zero-Words to Eliminate

Replace pompous phrasing with concise equivalents:

| Zero-words (avoid) | Concise (use) |
|---|---|
| at the present time | now |
| in the event that | if |
| for the purpose of | for |
| in order to | to |
| with regard to | about |
| in view of the fact that | as, since |
| along the lines of | like |
| in accordance with | by |
| at an early date | soon |
| notwithstanding the fact that | although |
| on the grounds that | because |
| for the reason that | since |
| with a view to | to |
| with the result that | so that |
| in terms of | in |
| at this point in time | now |

## 5. Powerful Endings

The closing sentence can be more important than the lead — it is the last impression, the most lasting (NIBM).

### Techniques for Strong Endings

- **Echo ending** — Repeat a word or phrase from the opening with a twist that acknowledges the intervening content
- **Dynamic downbeat** — Arrange the last sentence so the final syllable is accented: "That's the only way for the team to succeed" (not "to be successful")
- **Final synthesis** — Forge a new point that ties everything together — a judgement, prediction, or recommendation

### Pitfalls to Avoid in Endings

- Never close with a cliche
- Never introduce a new topic in the closing
- Ensure the ending agrees with the lead — they should rhyme thematically
- Never trail off without a clear call to action
