## 1. The Paragraph-by-Paragraph Formula

Every business plan executive summary follows a structured paragraph sequence (Jewinski):

### Paragraph 1: Context and Hook

Sets the stage by conveying: the business name, the market opportunity, and why now.

**Function:** Answers "What is this about?" and "Why should I care?"

**Pattern:**
> [Company Name] is [what it does] serving [target market]. [Market size or problem statement that creates urgency]. [One sentence of traction or validation.]

**Example:**
> GreenHarvest Technologies is an agri-tech company deploying AI-powered crop monitoring systems for smallholder farmers across East Africa. The continent's UGX 1,036T agriculture sector loses an estimated 30% of crop yield to preventable diseases each year. Since our pilot launch in January 2026, we have deployed monitoring systems across 1,200 farms in three Kenyan counties.

### Paragraph 2: Approach and Business Model

Describes HOW the business addresses the opportunity. Provides a thumbnail sketch of the methodology so recommendations do not seem drawn from thin air (Jewinski).

**Function:** Answers "How does this work?" and "How do you make money?"

**Pattern:**
> [Solution description in 2-3 sentences]. [Business model]. [Key differentiator from existing approaches.]

### Paragraph 3: Results and Traction

Presents evidence that the approach works. This is where data from sections 04-10 is condensed.

**Function:** Answers "Is this real?" and "What have you achieved?"

**Pattern:**
> [Key metrics: revenue, users, growth rate]. [Customer validation]. [Milestones achieved]. [Financial highlights: projected revenue, margins, break-even.]

### Paragraph 4: The Ask and Call to Action

States the funding request and expected returns. This is the recommendation paragraph — it must be forceful and clear (Jewinski).

**Function:** Answers "What do you need?" and "What do I get?"

**Pattern:**
> [Funding amount and type]. [Top 3-4 use-of-funds items]. [Expected ROI or exit timeline]. [Clear call to action.]

## 2. Proposal Summary Principles

From Jewinski's analysis of effective proposal summaries:

### Focus on Essentials Only

A proposal summary confines itself to a direct statement of essential facts, omitting all complications or secondary factors:
- Schedule → Only start and end dates, not every milestone
- Costs → Total cost, not line-item breakdowns
- Team → Key leaders, not every employee
- Technology → What it does for the customer, not how it works internally

### Confirm Competitiveness

Since one proposal is almost always in competition with another, the summary should make clear which alternatives are secondary or inadequate — but through evidence, not by attacking competitors.

### The Bottom-Line Test

Every proposal summary must pass one key question: **"Does this seem worthwhile given its costs?"** The reader must never be confused about the nature of the project or the overall investment required.

### Writing Order

Always write the executive summary LAST. Only when all other sections are complete can the writer condense what they contain. Trying to write it first leads to vague, aspirational language unsupported by data.

## 3. Annotated Template

Use this structure as a starting framework. Adapt length and emphasis based on audience analysis.

```
[EXECUTIVE SUMMARY]

[HOOK: One compelling sentence — a metric, a problem statement, or a bold claim backed by evidence]

[PARAGRAPH 1 — CONTEXT: 3-5 sentences]
Company name and what it does.
The problem or market opportunity, quantified.
Why now — what has changed to make this timely.
Brief traction statement if available.

[PARAGRAPH 2 — APPROACH: 3-5 sentences]
The solution and how it works (plain language).
The business model — how revenue is generated.
Key differentiator — what makes this defensible.
AI integration or technology advantage (2026 standard).

[PARAGRAPH 3 — RESULTS: 3-5 sentences + optional bullet list]
Current traction: customers, revenue, partnerships.
Financial highlights (use bullets):
  - Year 1 projected revenue: UGX X
  - Gross margin: X%
  - Break-even: Month X
  - Year 3 projected revenue: UGX X
Key milestones achieved and upcoming.

[PARAGRAPH 4 — THE ASK: 3-4 sentences]
Funding amount being raised.
Top 3 uses of funds (single line each).
Expected investor return / exit strategy.
Clear call to action.

[CLOSING SENTENCE: Echo the opening hook with a forward-looking twist]
```

## 4. Before-and-After Examples

### Weak Opening (Before)

> "The purpose of this business plan is to present our company and its products to potential investors. We believe that our company has significant potential for growth in the marketplace and that our team is well-positioned to execute on this vision. We are seeking funding to help us achieve our goals."

**Problems:** Passive voice, vague language, no specifics, buries the point, tells the reader nothing they could not guess.

### Strong Opening (After)

> "FarmGuard's AI-powered disease detection system identified crop blight 14 days earlier than traditional scouting methods across 1,200 pilot farms in Kenya — preventing an estimated UGX 11.8B in crop losses during its first growing season. We are raising UGX 5.5B to expand to 15,000 farms across East Africa by Q4 2027."

**Strengths:** Active voice, specific numbers, concrete achievement, clear ask, creates urgency.

### Weak Financial Summary (Before)

> "Our financial projections show that the company will experience significant revenue growth over the next several years, with improving margins and a clear path to profitability. We expect to achieve a strong return on investment for our stakeholders."

### Strong Financial Summary (After)

> **Financial Highlights:**
> - Year 1 revenue: UGX 3.1B (validated by 14 signed contracts)
> - Year 2 revenue: UGX 8.9B (based on 60% customer growth rate)
> - Gross margin: 72% (SaaS delivery, minimal variable costs)
> - Break-even: Month 18
> - Year 3 projected revenue: UGX 18.9B at 68% gross margin

## 5. Self-Assessment Checklist

After completing a draft, score each criterion. All must pass before the summary is considered final.

### Content Completeness

- [ ] Business concept is clear in one paragraph
- [ ] Problem and opportunity are quantified
- [ ] Solution and value proposition are specific
- [ ] Target market is defined with TAM/SAM/SOM
- [ ] Business model is explicitly stated
- [ ] Competitive advantage is defensible and specific
- [ ] Traction evidence is included (or pre-launch validation)
- [ ] Financial highlights include specific projections
- [ ] Funding ask is a specific amount with use-of-funds
- [ ] Team credentials are relevant and concise
- [ ] Call to action is clear

### Writing Quality

- [ ] Opening sentence creates immediate interest
- [ ] Active voice throughout (no passive constructions)
- [ ] Average sentence length is 17-20 words
- [ ] No vague qualifiers ("significant," "substantial," "strong")
- [ ] No zero-words or pompous phrasing
- [ ] Technical terms are explained on first use
- [ ] Closing sentence echoes the opening or delivers a synthesis
- [ ] Total length is 800-1,200 words (2-3 pages)

### Bankability

- [ ] Every financial claim traces to section 10 projections
- [ ] Market claims trace to section 04 data
- [ ] No claims are made that lack supporting evidence
- [ ] Investor can answer all six verification questions from this document alone
- [ ] Summary passes the "two-minute test" — full understanding in 120 seconds
