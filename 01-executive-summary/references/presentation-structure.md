## 1. Know Your Audience

Before writing a single word, answer these questions (Jewinski/Bradbury):

### Audience Analysis Checklist

- **Who** will read this? (Angel investors, VCs, bank lenders, government grants, board members)
- **Seniority level** — Are they C-suite, fund managers, technical evaluators?
- **Decision maker** — Who in the audience actually signs the cheque?
- **Prior knowledge** — What do they already know about this industry?
- **Initial attitude** — Pro, neutral, or sceptical toward this type of venture?
- **Intelligence level** — Never talk down; always respect the reader
- **Time available** — How long will they spend on this? (Assume: 2 minutes maximum)
- **What they need to know** — Main points, where to find details, what it means for their decision (Jewinski's three reader needs)

### The Three Reader Needs (Jewinski)

Every reader of an executive summary needs to know:

1. **The main points** — though not every detail
2. **Where to find exact data** — though they need not memorise it
3. **What the key points mean** for their business/investment decision

### Adapting Tone to Audience Type

| Audience | Priority | Tone | Emphasis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Angel investors | Vision, team, upside | Bold, personal, passionate | Founding story, market timing |
| Venture capital | Scalability, unit economics | Data-driven, confident | Growth metrics, TAM, exit path |
| Bank/lender | Security, cash flow, collateral | Conservative, thorough | Cash flow projections, risk mitigation |
| Government grant | Social impact, job creation | Formal, policy-aligned | Community benefit, compliance |
| Board/internal | Strategic fit, feasibility | Direct, analytical | Alignment with existing strategy |

## 2. Audience Learning Styles

People process information in four distinct ways (Bradbury). An effective executive summary addresses all four:

### The Four Styles

| Style | Characteristic | What they want | How to satisfy |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tortoise** | Methodical, builds on existing knowledge | Clear logical structure, first principles | Present information step-by-step, build from foundation |
| **Philosopher** | Analytical, values accuracy | Well-researched facts, examples, evidence | Support every claim with data and anecdotal evidence |
| **Hare** | Fast, forceful, results-oriented | Bottom line first, concise delivery | Lead with conclusions, keep it brief and punchy |
| **Entrepreneur** | Big-picture, enthusiastic | The vision, the opportunity, the scale | Paint the future, show the transformative potential |

### Practical Application

A well-structured executive summary satisfies all four by:

1. Opening with the big picture and bottom line (Hare + Entrepreneur)
2. Following with logical structure and evidence (Tortoise + Philosopher)
3. Supporting claims with specific data points (Philosopher)
4. Building from context to conclusion in clear steps (Tortoise)

## 3. Structuring for Persuasion

### The Presentation Arc

Apply Bradbury's presentation structure to written executive summaries:

**Opening (10% of content)**
- Capture attention immediately
- State the core proposition
- Establish credibility in one sentence
- Preview what follows (without a dry table of contents)

**Body (80% of content)**
- Follow the four-step formula: Context → Approach → Results → Ask
- One idea per paragraph
- Use signpost transitions: "This demand creates..." / "To capture this market..." / "The evidence shows..."
- Vary paragraph length to maintain rhythm
- Place strongest arguments at the beginning and end (primacy and recency effects)

**Close (10% of content)**
- Restate the core value proposition (echo the opening)
- Summarise the ask with specific numbers
- End on a dynamic downbeat — accented final syllable
- Clear call to action

### Chunking Information

Break complex information into digestible groups (Bradbury's "chunking"):

- **Financial data** → Use a small table or bullet list, never prose paragraphs
- **Market validation** → Lead with the strongest proof point, then support
- **Team credentials** → 1-2 sentences per key person, focused on relevant achievements
- **Timeline** → Key milestones only, not granular tasks

### Persuasion Principles

- **Primacy effect** — Readers remember what comes first. Lead with your strongest point.
- **Recency effect** — Readers remember what comes last. Close with the call to action.
- **Rule of three** — Group supporting arguments in threes for maximum retention.
- **Concrete over abstract** — "$2.4M revenue in 18 months" beats "strong revenue growth potential."
- **Show, don't tell** — "We signed 14 enterprise contracts in Q1" beats "We have strong market traction."

## 4. Visual Communication Principles

Even in a written document, visual structure affects comprehension (Bradbury):

### Layout for Scanning

- **Headers and sub-headers** — Enable the reader to find any section in seconds
- **Bullet points for lists** — Financial highlights, milestones, team members
- **One small table** — Financial snapshot (Year 1-3 revenue, margins, break-even)
- **White space** — Never fill the entire page with text; breathing room aids comprehension
- **Bold key numbers** — Make critical figures visually prominent

### When to Use Bullets vs. Prose

| Use bullets for | Use prose for |
|---|---|
| Financial highlights | The business narrative and story |
| Team member snapshots | Problem/opportunity description |
| Key milestones | Competitive advantage explanation |
| Use of funds breakdown | Value proposition articulation |

## 5. Confidence and Credibility Signals

The executive summary must project confident authority without arrogance (Bradbury):

### Signals of Credibility

- **Specific numbers** over vague claims
- **Third-party validation** — Customer testimonials, industry awards, press coverage
- **Honest risk acknowledgement** — Shows maturity, not weakness
- **Track record** — Previous ventures, relevant experience, domain expertise
- **Traction evidence** — Revenue, users, LOIs, partnerships already secured

### Signals to Avoid

- Unsupported superlatives ("the best," "the first," "revolutionary")
- Defensive language ("despite challenges," "notwithstanding concerns")
- Excessive hedging ("we believe," "we hope," "we think")
- Aggression toward competitors ("unlike our incompetent competitors")
