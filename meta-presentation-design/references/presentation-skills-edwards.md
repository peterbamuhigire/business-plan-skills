# Presentation Skills — Edwards Compiled Framework

> **Note on this source.** The Edwards volume is a practitioner anthology: fifteen topic chapters assembled from multiple how-to traditions. Where other books (Duarte, Gallo, Duarte) develop a single coherent model, Edwards gives breadth — covering everything from voice physiology to room temperature. Treat it as the practitioner's encyclopaedia, not a linear methodology. All frameworks below are drawn directly from the text.

---

## 1. Core Definition — What Makes a Good Presentation

Edwards opens with four non-negotiable components (p. 4):

| Component | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Content** | Information the audience actually needs, paced for live absorption — not the same as a written report |
| **Structure** | Logical beginning, middle, and end; sequenced so the audience can follow |
| **Packaging** | Well prepared; the audience is at the mercy of the presenter — they cannot re-read |
| **Human Element** | The presenter's personality makes the content memorable in a way a report never can |

**Key insight:** Before committing to a presentation, ask whether a report would serve the audience better. If the answer is yes, write a report.

## 2. The Objectives Framework — Start Here

The single most important principle in the book (p. 35):

> "The objective of communication is not the transmission but the reception."

This means every preparation decision — structure, language, visual aids, pace — must be made with the receiver in mind, not the sender.

### Formulating Objectives (p. 36)

1. Write a single, concise statement of intent: *"The purpose of this presentation is to obtain funding approval for a UGX 450M expansion."*
2. Identify a maximum of **three objectives**. Focus on one primary objective; address others only if they do not dilute the main one.
3. If you cannot state your objective in one sentence, you are not ready to present.

### Identifying Audience Aims (p. 36)

- Research who will be in the room and what *their* objectives are.
- Frame your presentation so that achieving your objective simultaneously helps them achieve theirs.
- Example: If lenders are worried about repayment risk, lead with DSCR and collateral — not with the product story.

## 3. Structure Models

Edwards presents five distinct structural models (pp. 37–39). Select the model that fits your presentation type.

### 3.1 Sequential Argument
A series of linked statements leading to a conclusion. Each section connects explicitly to the last.
- Best for: logical reasoning, cause-and-effect cases
- Technique: frequent summary statements — "We have established X; therefore Y follows"

### 3.2 Hierarchical Decomposition
Main topic → sub-topics → smaller units. Powerful in written form; must be kept simple verbally.
- Summarise each section at its close
- Introduce each major section by stating its place in the hierarchy

### 3.3 Question-Oriented (Problem-Solution)
Introduce the problem → background → options (advantages and disadvantages of each) → preferred solution.
- Best for: seeking approval or explaining a decision
- Technique: establish the decision criteria during the presentation; this alone guides the audience toward the desired outcome

### 3.4 Pyramid
Introduce the complete story first, then add layers of detail. Each pass covers the same ground at greater depth.
- Advantages: (a) builds audience familiarity — ideas feel like their own; (b) easy to cut if time runs short, exactly like a newspaper editor cutting column inches from the bottom
- Best for: complex business cases where audience may be called away early

### 3.5 The Meaty Sandwich (Default)
Simple **beginning → middle → end**. The body contains the substance; the introduction sets up the promise; the conclusion delivers the payoff.
- Appropriate for short or single-topic presentations
- Reinforced as the structural format for all sub-sections within larger models

## 4. Preparing Content

### 4.1 Planning Questions to Answer Before Writing a Word (p. 16)
- Why am I giving this presentation? (To explain? Convince? Justify? Entertain?)
- Who is the audience? What do they already know? What technical terms need explaining?
- What questions will they ask?
- How can I keep them interested throughout?

### 4.2 Drafting and Editing Process (p. 23)
1. Write a rough draft — like a first draft of a written report
2. Review and delete irrelevant or superfluous content
3. Ensure the story flows and is consistent
4. If you cannot easily express something, leave it out — it probably reflects unclear thinking
5. **Never write the full script for delivery** — it will cause reading, not presenting

### 4.3 Cue Cards vs Script vs Notes
- **Recommended:** Cue cards (postcards size or A5) with key words, phrases, and sketches
- Number cards in case they are dropped
- Mark on each card which visual aid accompanies it
- Do not use A4 sheets — they shake visibly if your hands tremble

### 4.4 The Logic Tree (pp. 44–46)
A structured planning tool for decision-maker presentations:

```
MAIN IDEA (one thing you want the audience to remember or do)
├── Branch 1: Reason/Evidence (addresses decision-maker concern 1)
│   └── Twig: supporting data
├── Branch 2: Reason/Evidence (addresses decision-maker concern 2)
│   └── Twig: supporting data
└── Branch 3: Reason/Evidence (addresses decision-maker concern 3)
    └── Twig: supporting data
```

**The Category Trap:** Most first-time planners build a *topical outline* instead (listing by category). This results in "talking about" a topic rather than telling decision makers what they need to know. The logic tree forces focus on the audience's concerns.

**From Tree to Presentation:** Once the tree is built, add a riveting opening, a punchy closing, and relevant visuals. During delivery, if the audience signals agreement on a branch point, skip the supporting twigs and move on.

### 4.5 Structure Options for 45-Minute Talks (p. 53)
- Maximum **7 main points** in a 45-minute talk
- Structural formats to choose from:
  - **Timeline:** sequential order
  - **Climax:** points in increasing importance
  - **Problem/Solution:** problem → solution → benefits
  - **Classification:** items grouped by category
  - **Simple to Complex:** build up from fundamentals

## 5. The Beginning — Five Mandatory Elements (pp. 38–39)

Every opening must do all five:

| Element | What It Achieves | How to Do It |
|---|---|---|
| **Get attention** | Stops side-conversations; focuses the room | Startling fact, dramatic visual, strong question |
| **Establish a theme** | Activates the audience's existing knowledge and opinions | State your main objective; invoke familiar context |
| **Present a structure** | Sets expectations; provides a roadmap | Brief overview of what you will cover |
| **Create rapport** | Wins the audience in minute one; keeps them for the duration | Decide in advance how you want to appear — expert, peer, guide — and establish it immediately |
| **Administration** | Prevents distraction from logistics | Announce breaks, housekeeping, timing at the outset |

### Attention-Grabbing Openings (pp. 16–17, 29)
- A startling fact or statistic
- A pertinent, unexpected question posed to the audience
- A short, relevant personal story (under one minute; must connect to the main topic)
- A dramatic visual aid
- A timely news reference
- **Avoid:** Jokes unless you have tested them; do not open with "I'm nervous"; do not open with "Can everyone hear me at the back?"

## 6. The Ending — As Planned as the Opening (p. 39)

> "The final impression you make on the audience is the one they will remember."

- Re-capture wandering attention with a change of pace, a new visual, or a culminating idea
- **Avoid announcing a summary** — the audience immediately switches off
- Best endings come unexpectedly — leave the final vital phrase hanging in the air
- Alternatively: build to a crescendo — raise pace and voice through the final section to an inevitable conclusion
- **Ask for action** — if you want the audience to do something, the closing is your last chance to ask

### The Columbo Technique (p. 74)
Save one critical point. Seem to conclude. Then: *"Oh — just one more thing..."* and deliver the big point.
- Use once per presentation only
- Most effective mid-presentation (before a major transition) before you are fully comfortable using it at the very end

## 7. The Voice — Edwards's Vocal Framework

### 7.1 Four Vocal Parameters (pp. 4–5)
| Parameter | Definition | Practical Target |
|---|---|---|
| **Volume** | Loudness of sound | Heard without shouting; lower to draw in, raise to make a point |
| **Tone** | Character of the sound | Match emotion to content; fear in the voice frightens the audience |
| **Pitch** | High or low note | Vary to avoid monotone; use rhetorical questions to naturally raise pitch at the end |
| **Pace** | Speed and duration of sounds | Slow down for key points; vary to maintain interest |

### 7.2 Voice Self-Improvement Method (pp. 4–5)
1. **Listen to your own voice actively** — cup the right hand around the right ear and pull it forward; cup the left hand around the mouth directing sound into the ear. This is how others hear you. It is often completely different from how you think you sound.
2. Practice vocal variety by taking a single line ("This new policy is going to be exciting") and delivering it with: surprise, then irony, then grief, then anger. Over-act. The key to vocal colour is exaggeration in practice so that variation feels natural in delivery.

### 7.3 Projection vs Shouting (p. 32)
- Projection is raising the *natural* speaking voice without losing control of it
- Target the object furthest from you; deliver to the back row
- During the first minute, monitor the faces of people at the back — if they look confused, stop and ask if everyone can hear
- Practise by going into a large room and speaking at full voice to the back wall

### 7.4 Pacing Rules (p. 41)
- The safe style is slightly louder and slightly slower than a fireside conversation with a slightly deaf relative
- Adjust by watching the audience's response
- A pause at a key point **emphasises importance** — the audience reads silence as significance
- Punctuation in speech is replaced by pauses — use them where commas and full stops would appear in writing

## 8. Body Language — The Five Facets (pp. 40–42)

### 8.1 The Eyes
- Most powerful tool for conveying honesty, openness, and confidence
- Hold gaze in one direction for 5–6 seconds, then move; add a slight smile after each shift
- In large rooms, staring at a *group* convinces individuals they are personally seen
- If direct eye contact is difficult, look at foreheads or noses — the audience cannot tell the difference

### 8.2 Gestures
- Hands must not wave aimlessly, fiddle with a pen, or jingle change in pockets
- Find a neutral resting position for hands; return there after each gesture completes
- Use gestures *in unison with speech* — not before, not after
- Less is more: too many gestures dilute the power of each one
- Use gestures for active words and concepts (separation vs merger, growth vs decline)
- The most important gesture: **smile** — it signals comfort and makes the audience more comfortable

### 8.3 Posture and Stance
- Standing erect with a slight forward lean signals approachability and engagement
- Slouching signals indifference — even if you care deeply
- The stance you adopt communicates character; choose it deliberately as an actor would
- Avoid: pacing up and down (unsettles the audience), standing in front of the screen, talking to visual aids

### 8.4 Facial Expression
- Amplify natural expression by *25–30%* to compensate for distance and nerves
- If you look listless, the audience will be listless
- If you smile, they will lean in to find out why

### 8.5 Proximity and Movement (p. 6)
- Moving through the room increases interaction and creates physical closeness
- Watch for discomfort signals in the audience: rocking, leg swinging, tapping, gaze aversion
- In large rooms there is usually too much distance — move towards the audience to close it

## 9. Managing Nerves

### 9.1 The Reframe (pp. 7–8, 44)
- Do not fight nerves — welcome them
- Adrenaline from nerves is the same mechanism that improves athletic performance
- Winners use nerves for that needed edge; losers go into "flight mode" and withdraw from the audience
- Nerves are never as visible to the audience as they feel to you

### 9.2 Pre-Presentation Physical Techniques (pp. 7–8)
**Floor relaxation:** Lie flat, knees up, systematically relax toes → feet → legs → torso → shoulders → neck. Stand slowly and maintain the relaxed feeling.

**Standing shake-out:** Feet shoulder-width apart, arms loose. Shake hands, then arms, then shoulders, then torso, then legs. Rotate shoulders forward then back. Gently rotate the head clockwise and counter-clockwise.

**Breathing:** Breathe in through the nose; hold five seconds; breathe out through the mouth. Three to six cycles only — do not over-do it.

**Deep breathing timing:** Do it a half-hour before speaking, not immediately before — it takes time to take effect.

### 9.3 Mental Preparation (pp. 33–34)
- **Positive visualisation:** In the days before, lie still and picture yourself in the room, feeling calm, hearing your own voice clear and assured. Run it like a video. Do NOT rehearse failure.
- **Hot-wired opening:** Have the first four minutes completely memorised — every word and gesture. This carries you through the highest-anxiety window and builds momentum.
- **Comforting truths to hold in mind:**
  - "The audience's initial impression is made in the first three seconds — I will walk in with a smile and open posture."
  - "I look better than I feel — my pounding heart does not show on the outside."
  - "The audience wants me to succeed — they came to hear something worthwhile."
  - "A mistake will not matter much — I will move on, and so will they."

### 9.4 During the Presentation (p. 8)
- Get a drink of water; take one deep breath; consciously relax the tightest part of your body; return saying "I can do it"
- If you lose your thread: **smile, check your cue card, take your time** — the silence feels longer to you than to the audience
- Never drink alcohol to manage nerves — it affects coordination without you being aware of it

## 10. Delivery Mechanics

### 10.1 The Attention Cycle — 2–4 Minute Rule (p. 72)
The audience's attention resets approximately every 2–4 minutes. After building your presentation, go back through it and place an **attention-gaining device** at each 2–4 minute interval:
- Raise voice inflection
- Show a new visual
- Hold up a prop
- Move to a different part of the room
- Tell a story or short anecdote
- Ask a rhetorical question
- Write on the flipchart

The audience will not notice the technique — they will simply experience the time as passing quickly.

### 10.2 Making an Impression — Core Techniques (pp. 42–43)
| Technique | How to Apply |
|---|---|
| **Repeat, Repeat** | State the most important point multiple times, with different explanations and framings |
| **Draw a Sign** | Literally signal: "The most important point is..." — audiences listen when they know when to listen |
| **Draw a Picture** | Use metaphors and analogies — "We need a bigger slice of the cake before the feast" |
| **The Narrative** | Weave the message into a story; everyone stays for a story |
| **Short and Sweet** | Test your main message by imagining 30 seconds with the managing director — can you crystallise it? |

### 10.3 Transitions (pp. 72–73)
- Smooth verbal transitions ("speaking of X, now I will discuss Y") are safe but soporific
- More effective: change stage position, pause, introduce a visual, pick up a prop, sharply vary vocal volume
- One-liners, anecdotes, and rhetorical questions work well as verbal transitions
- Recap lines ("To recap this section...") are welcomed by audiences — use them

### 10.4 Delivery Checklist
- Speak clearly — judge the room acoustics; do not shout or whisper
- Do not read from notes
- Do not speak in a monotone — vary speed and pitch at every section change
- Pitch your voice to the back row
- Do not face the screen and talk to it
- Do not block the screen with your body
- Avoid filler words: "um", "ah", "you know", "like" — they irritate and signal unpreparedness
- Keep hands visible and purposeful at all times

## 11. Visual Aids

### 11.1 Design Principles (pp. 25–26, 47)
- Limit text to **maximum 10 words per slide** (some sources: 5 words per line, 5 lines per visual)
- Minimum font: 18pt for transparencies; larger is better
- Guy Kawasaki rule: divide the age of the oldest person in the room by 2 — use that as your minimum font size
- Use bullet points sparingly — images increase retention more than text bullets
- Avoid tables with many rows and columns (balance sheets do not project well)
- Keep colour scheme to 2–3 colours maximum
- Avoid orange and yellow text — poor projection visibility

### 11.2 Colour Psychology for Visuals (pp. 69–70)
| Colour | Emotional Association |
|---|---|
| Dark Blue | Stable, trustworthy, calm — ideal for financial presentations |
| Light Blue | Cool, refreshing |
| Green | Organic, healthy, growth, money |
| Grey | Integrity, neutral, mature |
| White | Pure, hopeful, clean |
| Red | Dangerous, urgent, stop — use sparingly |
| Orange/Yellow | Warm, bright — avoid for text |
| Black | Serious, heavy, profitable |
| Purple | Regal, mysterious |

**For flipcharts:** Black, blue, and green have the greatest visibility. Blue is most pleasing to the eye. Avoid purple, brown, pink, and yellow inks.

### 11.3 Handling Visual Aids (pp. 47–48)
- Keep posters and collages hidden until needed — an uncovered visual steals audience attention
- Hand out pamphlets only when needed, or at the end — ask the audience to keep them face-down until directed
- If using an OHP or projector, point to the screen rather than the foil — you will not block the image and will not dazzle yourself
- Switch off the display when not actively discussing the slide — do not leave a slide on screen for more than 5 minutes without comment
- Check every chair in the room for unobstructed line-of-sight to the screen and to you

### 11.4 Screen Size Reference (p. 71)
| Audience Size | Minimum Screen Diagonal |
|---|---|
| 3–5 people | 21 inches |
| 10–15 people | 37 inches |
| 16–35 people | 60 inches |
| 36–50 people | 72 inches |
| 51–140 people | 120 inches |
| 141–220 people | 150 inches |

## 12. Handling Questions

### 12.1 Q&A Protocol (pp. 52, 30, 52)
1. Always allow time for questions — do not rush to conclude
2. After inviting questions, pause **6 seconds** minimum before assuming there are none
3. When a question is asked, **repeat it** so the whole room hears it correctly and so you have confirmed you understood it
4. Direct your answer to the **entire audience** — not just the questioner — to keep everyone engaged
5. Relate the answer back to the main points of the presentation where possible
6. Optimal answer length: **10–40 seconds** — shorter feels abrupt; longer appears elaborate
7. If you do not know: be honest, do not waffle; take down the questioner's contact details and follow up

### 12.2 Handling Hostile or Challenging Questions (p. 52)
- Keep cool — you are a professional
- Find a way to agree with *part* of the challenging position: "Yes, I understand your concern..." or "I'm glad you raised that..."
- Never become combative — the audience will sense "us versus you" and side with the questioner

### 12.3 If No Questions Come (p. 24)
- Have one question prepared to ask the audience — this seeds the conversation: "A question I'm often asked is..."
- Avoid closing the Q&A window with an impatient tone — the audience reads terseness as "if you ask a question you weren't paying attention"

## 13. Rehearsal

### 13.1 Rehearsal Methods (pp. 31–32, 43–44)
| Method | Best For |
|---|---|
| Mirror rehearsal | Self-monitoring of posture, gesture, and facial expression — accentuate so it feels normal |
| Audio recording | Checking vocal variety, pace, filler words |
| Video recording | Most honest feedback — watching yourself forces confrontation of actual habits |
| Trusted audience rehearsal | Feedback on content clarity, timing, impact |
| Mini-rehearsal | Going over difficult sections in your head during spare moments during the day |

### 13.2 Preparation Ratio Rule (p. 36)
A minimum of **1 hour of preparation per 5 minutes of speaking time** for an average business presentation. Senior management presentations, funding pitches, and investor presentations may warrant more.

### 13.3 Rehearsal Out Loud (p. 18, 47)
- Rehearse out loud at least **four times**
- Live rehearsal takes approximately **25% longer** than mental rehearsal — account for this when timing
- Using visual aids adds time — rehearse with slides and props, not without
- It is always better to finish slightly early than to overrun

### 13.4 Breaking Habits — The Sign Method (p. 54)
To break specific distracting habits (um, ah, hands in pockets, etc.):
1. Identify the two or three worst habits through a rehearsal audience
2. Each audience member writes the habit on a large sheet of paper
3. During subsequent rehearsals, audience members hold up the sign immediately when the habit occurs
4. Practise daily for one to two weeks — the immediate feedback breaks the habit faster than any other method

## 14. Logistics and Room Management

### 14.1 Arrive Early — Always (pp. 78–80)
- Expect minor problems to be the norm — the room will never be perfectly set
- Check every item: sound system, microphone distance, projector alignment, flipchart paper and markers, chair arrangement, lighting controls, temperature
- Check for line-of-sight obstructions from every seat in the room
- Check wireless device frequency conflicts if multiple presenters are using RF equipment

### 14.2 Room Temperature (p. 81)
- Set the room slightly cooler than comfortable — body heat will bring it to the right level
- Uncomfortable people tune out; acknowledge discomfort and invite them to make adjustments

### 14.3 Seating Arrangements (pp. 80–81)
- **Semi-circular seating** is optimal for engagement and laughter — audience members can see each other's faces, which amplifies reactions
- Straight theatre-style is acceptable but reduces audience-to-audience interaction
- Keep the distance between you and the front row as small as possible — distance is a barrier to connection
- Set the room along the **long side** so the last row is as close to the speaker as possible
- Avoid rows longer than 6 seats wide (no one should cross more than 6 people to reach their seat)
- If seating cannot be changed: **you move** — animation and movement around the space compensates for fixed seating

### 14.4 Lighting (p. 26)
- Too much light near the screen: difficult to read projected content
- Completely dark room: audience falls asleep
- If using slides: know the light switches and where they are; avoid constant on-off cycling
- If you must stand in dim light to protect screen visibility, position a soft light on your face — the audience needs to see *you*

### 14.5 Sound System (pp. 81–82)
- Set audio level slightly loud during sound check — people's bodies absorb sound once the room fills
- If someone is speaking before you, go to the back of the room and assess whether the level carries
- Have a backup microphone if the event depends on amplification

## 15. Developing Skills Over Time

### 15.1 Post-Presentation Evaluation (p. 44)
After every presentation:
1. Identify the single least successful element — preparation, structure, delivery, or audience engagement
2. Write yourself a reminder note; put it in front of you at the next presentation
3. Concentrate one improvement per presentation — do not try to fix everything at once

### 15.2 Improvement Principles
- Oral communication cannot be taught passively — only pointed to; practice is the only path
- Ask colleagues periodically: "What is the one habit in my presentations I should address?" People can spot others' patterns easily; self-diagnosis is harder
- Listen to other speakers actively — analyse what works and what does not

### 15.3 The Seven Pillars of Advanced Presentation (pp. 56–57)
1. **Work out what matters** — restrict to one core message per presentation; specialise in a subject area
2. **Tell them what you want them to know** — not everything you know; present information in multiple complementary formats
3. **Throw away your notes** — notes destroy eye contact and intimacy; replace with two-minute sound bites (memorised chunks)
4. **Paint pictures** — stories and word pictures enhance the message; the quality of support material signals the value of the message
5. **Involve the audience** — involvement activities add touch and feel to aural and visual channels; 30 seconds to 15 minutes
6. **Help them laugh** — we learn best in moments of enjoyment; open with a genuine compliment; localise material
7. **Prepare, Practice, Participate** — these are not sequential steps but an integrated cycle: collect material, store it, work it into conversations, take every opportunity to speak

## 16. Storytelling in Presentations

### 16.1 Why Stories Work (pp. 43, 29, 56)
- Everyone stays for a story — it bypasses the audience's critical filter
- Stories create images; images are remembered long after words are forgotten
- The best speakers use words to create images in listeners' minds — a picture "painted" with language is as durable as a photograph

### 16.2 Story Requirements for Business Presentations
- **Short:** Under one minute for an opening story; longer for a signature story mid-presentation
- **Relevant:** Must connect directly to the main message — the story exists to carry the audience into the content, not as entertainment independent of it
- **Personal:** Stories that make the speaker a person — not a "boring public speaker" — build trust and accessibility
- **Specific:** Concrete detail is more memorable than abstraction

### 16.3 Narrative as Structural Framework (pp. 43, 53)
- The Sergeant Major's formula: "First tell 'em what you're going to tell 'em, then tell 'em, then tell 'em what you told 'em."
- This is not just a structure for the whole presentation — it is the correct structure for *each major section* within a presentation

## 17. Using Data and Evidence Persuasively

Key principles from the text (pp. 25–26, 47):

- **Never project a table with many rows and columns** — when people cannot read the characters, the data becomes useless rather than persuasive
- **Break statistics into charts or graphs** — pie charts and bar charts carry numbers more efficiently than tables
- **Extract data before displaying** — do not photocopy a page with more data than you intend to discuss; the surplus creates noise
- **Signal when evidence matters** — "The important point here is..." primes the audience to listen harder for the specific claim (the "Draw a Sign" technique)
- **Provide handouts for dense data** — if you have many statistics, present selected highlights on screen and distribute a handout with the full data
- Use the **"So What?" test** when building slides: if you cannot answer "So what?" for a slide, remove it

## 18. Recovering from Mistakes

From pp. 8, 34, 44:
- **Continue on.** If you pick up and continue after a mistake, the audience will follow. Winners continue; losers stop.
- Most mistakes go unnoticed unless the presenter draws attention to them by visibly panicking
- If you lose your thread: smile, check your cue card, take your time — the silence feels longer to you than to the audience
- Do not apologise for stumbles — apologies magnify the mistake in the audience's memory
- "Even the best presenters make mistakes. The key is to continue on after the mistake." (p. 8)

## 19. Props as a Presentation Tool (pp. 65–67)

Props are physical objects — beyond slides — used to anchor and amplify the message.

**Why use props:**
- They warm up the audience
- They substitute for notes — a speaker can anchor entire sections of a talk to a physical prop rather than a card
- They create interest and variety
- They are harder to ignore than words
- They are remembered longer — images persist in memory long after words fade
- They serve visually-oriented audience members who are under-served by verbal content alone

**Effective prop technique:**
- Keep props hidden until needed
- Ensure the prop is visible from all parts of the room
- Always speak to the audience, not the prop
- If using a surprise prop, hold it in full view for a moment before revealing its effect — do not let the audience miss the moment

**Example application for business plan presentations:**
- Use a physical product sample when presenting a product pitch
- Bring a printed one-page financial summary as a hand-held prop, not as a projected slide
- Use three physical objects (e.g., coins, boxes) to represent three scenarios in a risk analysis

## 20. Audience Analysis — Before You Prepare

Structured questions to answer before writing any content (pp. 27, 36, 53):

**About the audience:**
- What is the average age and background?
- What is their familiarity with the subject?
- What are their primary concerns and objectives in attending?
- What is their attitude toward me — neutral, friendly, sceptical, hostile?
- What do they expect to learn or decide?

**About the context:**
- What is the purpose of this gathering for the organisation?
- Who else is presenting and what will they cover?
- What decisions will be made immediately after?

**About language and formality:**
- What level of technical language is appropriate?
- What formality is expected — formal pitch, workshop, investor meeting, board presentation?

## 21. CLIENT COACHING PROTOCOL
*How to use the Edwards framework to prepare a business owner to present their own business plan*

This protocol is for a coach or consultant preparing an entrepreneur for a bank loan pitch, investor presentation, or funder meeting.

### Phase 1 — Audience and Objective Definition (Week 1)
1. Identify who will be in the room: bank credit officer, investment committee, grant panel, DFI evaluator
2. Research what that audience cares about most (repayment security? impact? returns? social outcomes?)
3. Write one sentence: "The objective of this presentation is to [specific outcome] for [specific audience]"
4. Agree on the three main points to be covered — no more

### Phase 2 — Structure and Logic Tree (Week 1–2)
1. Build a logic tree together: main message → three to four supporting branches → evidence twigs
2. Choose the structure model: for a bank loan, use Question-Oriented (problem → options → preferred solution); for an investor, consider Pyramid (big picture first, then detail)
3. Map each branch to a slide or talking point — do not allow branches without supporting evidence

### Phase 3 — Content Editing (Week 2)
1. Apply the "So What?" test to every slide and every data point
2. Convert tables to charts; reduce to 10 words per slide or fewer
3. Write the opening (attention-grabber + theme + structure preview + rapport) and closing (call to action + memorable final phrase) with care
4. Prepare 3 cue cards with key words only — no sentences

### Phase 4 — Delivery Coaching (Week 2–3)
1. Record the first full run-through on video — do not warn what will be reviewed
2. Watch back together: identify the top three delivery habits to address (filler words, eye contact, pace, hands)
3. Use the Sign Method for filler words and nervous gestures — immediate feedback per repetition
4. Practise the opening four minutes until fully memorised (hot-wired)
5. Rehearse Q&A: coach asks the 5 hardest questions the audience is likely to raise; practise 10–40 second answers

### Phase 5 — Final Rehearsal (Day Before)
1. Full run-through at actual pace with visual aids; time it
2. Practise the physical entry: walking into the room, setting up, making eye contact before speaking
3. Agree on the one habit to consciously avoid during delivery
4. Positive visualisation exercise: 10 minutes lying quietly, picturing the room, the audience, the full presentation — calm, clear voice, confident close

### Phase 6 — Debrief (Within 48 Hours)
1. What worked well? (anchor these behaviours)
2. What was the single weakest element?
3. What is the one improvement for the next presentation?

Based on the Edwards self-assessment framework (p. 19–20) and extended with coaching additions:

- [ ] I have applied the "So What?" test to every slide

- [ ] I have checked that no slide has more than 10 words
- [ ] I have rehearsed out loud at least four times
- [ ] I have timed the rehearsal — I finish comfortably within the allotted time

## 23. BEFORE / DURING / AFTER CHECKLIST

### BEFORE THE PRESENTATION

**Two weeks before:**
- Confirm the room, equipment, and audience size
- Build the logic tree / structure outline
- Write the opening and closing first
- Identify the three most difficult Q&A questions and prepare answers

**One week before:**
- Complete all slides — apply the "So What?" test
- First full recorded rehearsal (video)
- Identify top three delivery habits to address
- Begin daily mini-rehearsals of difficult sections

**Day before:**
- Final full rehearsal at full voice with slides — time it
- Prepare cue cards
- Positive visualisation exercise (10 minutes)
- Confirm logistics: room, equipment, arrival time

**Day of, before the presentation:**
- Arrive early — at least 30 minutes before the audience
- Check every seat for line-of-sight
- Complete sound check
- Set room temperature slightly cool
- Ensure handouts are ready and numbered
- Do the physical warm-up: shake-out, shoulder rotations
- Do the breathing exercise (3–6 cycles)
- Greet audience members as they arrive — this breaks the ice and reduces nerves
- Drink water; avoid caffeine and dairy in the hour before

---

### DURING THE PRESENTATION

**Opening (first 90 seconds):**
- Walk in with confident posture and a genuine smile — first impression is set in three seconds
- Make eye contact before you speak
- Deliver the attention-grabber (fact, question, story)
- State the theme
- Preview the structure
- Establish rapport

**Body:**
- Follow the logic tree; do not digress
- Every 2–4 minutes: attention-gaining device
- At each section change: pause, change pace or pitch, signal transition
- Watch audience body language — adjust if attention is dropping
- Point to the screen, not the slide or the OHP foil
- Keep slides on screen no more than 5 minutes without active discussion

**Closing:**
- Signal the finale through pace and tone — build to it
- Do not announce "and now for my summary"
- Call to action
- Final memorable phrase — then stop

---

### AFTER THE PRESENTATION

**Immediately after:**
- Answer follow-up questions individually if approached
- Note any questions you could not answer — follow up within 24 hours

**Within 48 hours:**
- Debrief (alone or with a coach):
  - What went well? What was the single weakest element?
  - What one habit to address next time?
- Write the improvement note and attach it to your preparation template

**Ongoing:**
- Seek opportunities to speak — every speaking occasion is a rehearsal
- Listen to other speakers analytically — identify specific techniques to adopt or avoid
- Review video recordings of your presentations periodically

## 24. Reference Summary Table — Edwards Principles for Business Plan Presentations

| Situation | Edwards Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Funder does not know your business | Use Pyramid structure — big picture first, details after |
| Multiple decision-makers with different priorities | Build a logic tree with one branch per stakeholder concern |
| Presenting financial projections | Convert tables to charts; limit to 10 words per slide; use the "So What?" test |
| Nervous presenter | Hot-wire the first 4 minutes; use the standing shake-out and breathing exercise; welcome the adrenaline |
| Hostile question from a sceptical banker | Repeat the question; find partial agreement; give a 10–40 second answer; remain calm |
| Presenting to a single bank credit officer | Question-Oriented structure; address their criteria explicitly; be slightly louder and slower than conversational |
| Presenting to an investor group | Semi-circular seating if possible; involve the audience; use the attention cycle every 2–4 minutes |
| Recovering from a slide error mid-presentation | Continue without apology; acknowledge and move on; audience follows the presenter's lead |
| Entrepreneur with filler word habit | Sign Method: 1–2 weeks of immediate feedback from a rehearsal group |
| Long, complex plan (many sections) | Maximum 7 main points in a 45-minute window; use hierarchical decomposition; summarise each section |
