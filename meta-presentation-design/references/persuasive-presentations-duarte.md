# Persuasive Presentations — Duarte Framework

> "Designing a presentation without an audience in mind is like writing a love letter and addressing it 'to whom it may concern.'" — Ken Haemer, Presentation Research Manager, AT&T

> "If I am to speak for ten minutes, I need a week for preparation; if fifteen minutes, three days; if half an hour, two days; if an hour, I am ready now." — Woodrow T. Wilson

---

## 1. The Sparkline — Duarte's Core Narrative Model

### What It Is

The Sparkline is Duarte's term for the fundamental structure of all persuasive presentations. After studying hundreds of great speeches and 22 years of creating presentations, she identified that the most persuasive communicators create conflict and resolution by **toggling between "what is" and "what could be"** — not once, but repeatedly throughout the presentation.

The shape on paper looks like a jagged, rising line (a sparkline) that alternates between two levels:
- **Lower level:** the present reality, the status quo, the problem — what is
- **Upper level:** the improved future, the opportunity, the solution — what could be

The presentation begins at "what is," rises to "what could be," falls back to another aspect of "what is," rises again to a new "what could be," and continues this wave pattern until it climbs to a final high point: the **New Bliss** — the compelling future state that the audience will inhabit if they adopt the presenter's ideas.

### The Three-Part Structure Within the Sparkline

**Beginning: Establish the gap**
- Describe life as the audience currently knows it. People should nod in recognition.
- Then introduce what could be — creating productive tension.
- This gap between what is and what could be is what makes the audience care.
- "If you proposed what could be without first establishing what is, you'd fail to connect with the audience before swooping in with your ideas, and your message would lose momentum."

**Middle: Build tension through contrast**
- Continue moving back and forth between what is and what could be.
- Each "what is" beat makes the status quo feel increasingly unappealing.
- Each "what could be" beat makes the future feel increasingly alluring.
- The audience starts to feel uncomfortable remaining where they are.

**Ending: The New Bliss**
- The ending does not return to "what is."
- It stays elevated — describing the heightened world the audience will inhabit after adopting your ideas.
- "Your ending should leave people with a heightened sense of what could be — and willingness to believe or do something new."
- Many presentations end with a list of action items; Duarte argues this is not inspiring enough. The last thing said should move the audience to act.

### What the Sparkline Is NOT

- It is not simply "problem → solution" (one turn).
- It is not a flat presentation of facts followed by a recommendation.
- It is not a data dump with a call to action appended.
- It requires multiple waves of contrast — typically 3–5 cycles through the beginning-middle — before arriving at the final New Bliss.

### Why It Works

The Sparkline works because:
1. **Stories feature transformation.** Audiences root for protagonists overcoming obstacles. Each wave of "what could be" invites the audience to imagine themselves in the improved state.
2. **Conflict creates engagement.** "All good presentations — like all good stories — convey and resolve some kind of conflict or imbalance. The sense of discord is what makes audiences care enough to get on board."
3. **Contrast is cognitively compelling.** "People are naturally drawn to contrast because life is filled with it: Day and night. Male and female. Love and hate."

### Worked Example: Uganda Dairy Processing Business Plan Pitch

**Context:** Presenting a UGX 450M dairy processing expansion to a commercial bank credit committee.

**Beginning — Establish the gap:**
- *What is:* "Our dairy farm currently sells raw milk at UGX 800/litre to middlemen, who process it into yoghurt and sell it back to Kampala retailers at UGX 3,200/litre. Our revenue is UGX 12M per month. We are at the mercy of middlemen's prices."
- *What could be:* "But what if we could capture that processing margin ourselves? A small pasteuriser and yoghurt line would allow us to sell directly to supermarkets at UGX 2,800/litre — tripling our revenue from the same herd."

**Middle — Three waves of contrast:**
1. *What is:* "Post-harvest losses from unsold raw milk average 15% in the wet season — we pour money down the drain." / *What could be:* "Processed yoghurt has a 21-day shelf life. We eliminate spoilage losses entirely."
2. *What is:* "We have 47 employees with no job security during the dry season when milk volumes drop." / *What could be:* "Year-round processing means year-round employment — and we can offer NSSF contributions that attract better staff."
3. *What is:* "Uganda imports UGX 180B in processed dairy annually — product we could be making here." / *What could be:* "The East African Community market is 300 million consumers. Our UNBS-certified yoghurt can cross into Kenya and Rwanda duty-free under AfCFTA."

**New Bliss (Ending):**
"In three years, with this investment, Musana Dairy becomes a UGX 85M/month business with 94 employees, certified to UNBS standards, exporting to two EAC markets. The bank's loan is fully repaid in 36 months. That is the company this investment creates."

## 2. The Big Idea — Defining Your Central Message

### What It Is

The Big Idea (Duarte's term for the "controlling idea") is the single key message the presentation must communicate. It has two components:

1. **Your point of view** — not a generalisation like "our financials" but a perspective: "Our business is being strangled by middlemen we can replace."
2. **What is at stake** — why the audience should care: "Unless we act now, we will lose our largest customer to a competitor who can supply processed product."

### How to Express It

Express the Big Idea in a **complete sentence** with a subject and a verb. The subject is often some version of "you" (to highlight the audience's role). The verb should convey action and elicit emotion.

| Weak (topic only) | Strong (Big Idea) |
|---|---|
| "Our Q3 financials" | "Your department will miss its targets unless we upgrade the workflow system this quarter." |
| "The Uganda dairy sector" | "We can triple our revenue without acquiring a single new cow — by processing our own milk." |
| "Market expansion" | "The EAC market is open and growing; we have 18 months before a better-funded competitor claims the shelf space we should own." |

> "People will move away from pain and toward pleasure. Prod them (with words like 'struggle') so they feel uncomfortable staying in their current position. Lure them toward your idea with encouragement and rewards."

## 3. Audience Analysis — The Foundation of Persuasion

### The Audience Is the Hero

> "You're not the star of the show. The audience is."

A self-focused presenter describes the initiative and explains what needs to get done. A persuasive presenter positions the **audience as the hero** and themselves as the mentor — like Yoda to Luke Skywalker. The presenter's job is to:
- Give the hero a **special gift:** Insights that improve their lives or situation
- Teach the hero to use a **magical tool:** A new skill, framework, or approach
- Help the hero get **unstuck:** A solution that removes a painful obstacle

### Audience Segmentation Framework

Before writing the presentation, segment the audience across five dimensions:

| Dimension | What to Analyse |
|---|---|
| **Politics** | Power, influence, decision-making process — who is the real decision-maker? |
| **Demographics** | Age, education, ethnicity, gender, geography |
| **Psychographics** | Personality, values, attitudes, interests, lifestyle |
| **Firmographics** | Organisation size, revenue, industry, number of locations |
| **Ethnographics** | Social and cultural needs — critical for cross-cultural pitches |

**Key rule:** Identify the single most influential person in the room and write the presentation primarily for them — while maintaining enough breadth to not alienate others.

### Deep Audience Questions (Duarte's Research Checklist)

Before every significant presentation, answer these:
- What are they like? (Describe a day in their lives — let them know you "get" them)
- Why are they here? (Willing participants or mandatory attendees?)
- What keeps them up at night? (Fear, pain point, thorn in the side)
- How can you solve their problems?
- What do you want them to do? (Specific call to action)
- How might they resist? (Logical, emotional, or practical resistance)
- How can you best reach them? (Preferred format, room setup, pre-reading?)

### The Transformation Arc

Map where the audience starts (current beliefs/behaviour) and where you want them to end (new beliefs/behaviour). This is the "transformation arc" — the whole reason the presentation exists.

**Example for bank pitch:**

| Before the pitch | After the pitch |
|---|---|
| Sceptical about the borrower's capacity to repay | Confident in the business model; ready to approve subject to conditions |
| Uncertain about collateral adequacy | Satisfied that the asset mix covers the exposure |
| Worried about management depth | Reassured that the founder has surrounded herself with experienced advisers |

### Resistance Analysis

Every audience will resist change. Anticipate resistance types and prepare for them:

**Logical resistance:** They have data or arguments against your position.
- Preparation: Research opposing viewpoints; be ready to acknowledge them and explain why your evidence outweighs them.

**Emotional resistance:** Your idea violates a bias, dogma, or moral code.
- Preparation: Do not ignore the emotional dimension. Acknowledge it directly. "I know this feels like a big risk — I felt the same way until I looked at the numbers."

**Practical resistance:** It is physically or financially difficult for them to do what you are asking.
- Preparation: Acknowledge the sacrifice. Demonstrate shared burden. Show you have thought through how to make it manageable.

> "By showing that you've considered opposing points of view, you demonstrate an open mind — and invite your audience to respond in kind."

**Advanced tactic:** Meet the toughest critics before the presentation. "Pre-sell" your idea privately. They become less likely to mount a public challenge.

## 4. Audience Resistance Map by Pitch Type

### Bank / Commercial Lender

**Primary resistance type:** Logical and practical
**Core fears:** Default risk, collateral shortfall, management weakness, over-optimistic projections

| Resistance | How to address it |
|---|---|
| "These revenue projections are too optimistic" | Show conservative, base, and optimistic scenarios. Anchor the base case to industry benchmarks (cite sources). Let the numbers speak — do not over-explain. |
| "We've seen businesses like this fail" | Pre-empt with a direct acknowledgement: "You may have seen similar businesses struggle when X happened. Here is how our model is structured differently." |
| "The collateral doesn't fully cover the loan" | Present the full collateral schedule. Show DSCR above 1.3×. Offer additional security options. |
| "Management team is too thin" | Name the advisory board. Provide CVs. Show the succession plan for key roles. |
| "Cash flow projections assume constant growth" | Present a stress test. Show the business survives a 30% revenue decline. |

**Tone:** Analytical first, emotional second. Lead with numbers, ratios, and evidence. The story supports the numbers — not the other way around.

### Equity Investor / Venture Capital

**Primary resistance type:** Logical and emotional
**Core fears:** Founder won't scale, market too small, competitive moat weak

| Resistance | How to address it |
|---|---|
| "The market is too niche" | Show the TAM/SAM/SOM clearly. Cite credible sources. Demonstrate a path to the larger market. |
| "Why can't a larger player copy this?" | Name your moat (IP, relationships, first-mover, network effects, tacit knowledge — Teece framework). |
| "I don't believe the founder can execute" | Tell the founder's story. Demonstrate grit, domain expertise, and coachability. |
| "The valuation is too high" | Have comparable deal data ready. Explain the methodology. Be willing to negotiate. |

**Tone:** Emotional and logical in balance. Investors are buying the founder as much as the business. The story matters enormously.

### DFI / Grant Funder (World Bank, AfDB, UNCDF, etc.)

**Primary resistance type:** Logical and ethical
**Core fears:** Impact not measurable, funds misused, no additionality

| Resistance | How to address it |
|---|---|
| "How do we know this creates real impact?" | Show the Theory of Change. Tie each activity to a measurable outcome indicator. Reference baseline data. |
| "What happens when the grant ends?" | Demonstrate the sustainability pathway — how the business becomes self-funding. |
| "Your beneficiary data is thin" | Show primary research: surveys, interviews, field data. Third-party validation strengthens credibility. |
| "Other organisations do similar work" | Explain additionality — what uniquely happens because of your project that would not happen otherwise. |

**Tone:** Evidence-based throughout. Use data slides with clear sourcing. Acknowledge limitations honestly — funders respect intellectual honesty.

### Client / Customer (Sales Presentation)

**Primary resistance type:** Practical and emotional
**Core fears:** Cost too high, switching too disruptive, supplier won't deliver

| Resistance | How to address it |
|---|---|
| "We already have a supplier" | Do not attack the current supplier. Show specific gaps your product/service fills. |
| "Your price is higher" | Shift from price to total cost of ownership. Show the value of quality, reliability, and after-sales support. |
| "We've been burned before" | Lead with social proof — case studies, testimonials, references. Offer a trial or pilot. |
| "Decision needs committee approval" | Equip your champion with a one-page summary they can share internally. Give them the arguments. |

**Tone:** Emotional first — they must like and trust you before they will buy. Logic closes the deal; emotion opens the door.

## 5. Story Structure for Business Presentations

### Why Stories Work

> "[Stories] are the currency of human contact." — Robert McKee

Stories work because:
1. **Stories feature transformation:** Audiences root for protagonists overcoming obstacles. It is doubly powerful to show others going through the same transformation the audience is being asked to make.
2. **Stories have clear structure:** Beginning, middle, end — the structure audiences have been conditioned for centuries to receive. It makes content easy to digest and retell.
3. **Stories elicit involuntary physical responses:** "When we hear stories, our eyes dilate, our hearts race, we feel chills. We laugh, clap, lean forward or back."

> "Rarely does someone approach a speaker weeks after a presentation to say, 'I loved your third point on leadership.' What people do say, however, is 'I still think of the story you told...'"

### The Story Template (Duarte's Emotional Impact Table)

To transform information into a story with emotional impact:

| Story element | Question to answer |
|---|---|
| **Beginning: When, who, where** | Set the scene concisely |
| **Middle: Context** | What was the situation at the time? |
| **Middle: Conflict** | What problem or tension existed? |
| **Middle: Proposed resolution** | What action was taken? |
| **Middle: Complication** | What made it hard? |
| **End: Actual resolution** | What happened? What changed? |

**Key principle:** Personal stories told with conviction are the most effective. "Audiences feel more affection for presenters who reveal their own challenges and vulnerability."

### The Founder Story for Business Plan Pitches

The opening personal narrative — the founder's story — is one of the most powerful tools in a business plan pitch. Structure it using Duarte's beginning-middle-end framework:

- **Beginning:** Describe the moment you encountered the problem. (What is — the pain you experienced)
- **Middle:** Describe your journey to find a solution — including failures and what you learned.
- **Complication:** What made it harder than expected? Where did you almost give up?
- **End:** What emerged? How did the business idea crystallise?

This story should be told in 90–120 seconds maximum. Its function is to establish authenticity, demonstrate domain knowledge, and create emotional connection before the analytical case is made.

### Using Metaphors

Dr Martin Luther King Jr.'s "I Have a Dream" speech was approximately 20% metaphorical. His "bad check" metaphor — introduced three minutes into the speech — was the first moment the audience roared and clapped.

For business presentations:
- Avoid stock visual metaphors (handshakes, bull's-eyes, racing cars).
- Develop metaphors from your own experience — they will be unique and memorable.
- Weave the metaphor throughout the presentation as a thread.

## 6. The S.T.A.R. Moment (Something They'll Always Remember)

Every presentation should contain one climactic S.T.A.R. moment — the thing the audience will chat about after the talk and share on social media. Place it at or near the peak of the final "what could be" wave. Four methods:

### 1. Shocking Statistics

Do not glide over a shocking number — amplify it. Make the magnitude tangible through concrete comparison.

**Example:** "Uganda imports UGX 180B in processed dairy products annually — that is the equivalent of every household in Kampala spending UGX 100,000 per year buying back from abroad what our farmers produce here."

**Rule:** A statistic is only as powerful as the comparison that makes it real. Abstract numbers wash over audiences.

### 2. Evocative Visuals

A single powerful image can do more than three slides of bullet points. The image must:
- Connect emotionally to the "what is" or "what could be" moment it accompanies
- Be specific and credible — not generic stock photography
- Pass the "glance test" — comprehensible within three seconds

### 3. Memorable Dramatisation

A physical demonstration, prop, or live demonstration that makes the abstract concrete. Bill Gates releasing (non-infected) mosquitoes at TED to make the point about malaria funding. A CEO holding up two glasses — one containing a chemical weed killer, one a safe herbal extract — and drinking from the latter.

For business pitches: bring a product prototype, a sample, a photograph of the site, a customer letter, a printed invoice from your largest sale. Physical objects break the slide-dominated rhythm and create a lasting memory.

### 4. Emotive Anecdote

A personal story with high stakes — told in full with sensory detail, complication, and resolution. The Symantec.cloud CEO's mountain-climbing story (pages 67–70) is a masterclass: it has physical danger, a moment of decision, a near-death outcome, a prop (the piton), and a direct metaphorical connection to business innovation.

**Structural requirement for an emotive anecdote:** It must have genuine stakes (life, livelihood, failure, loss) and a specific, unexpected complication. Generic "I worked hard and succeeded" stories are not S.T.A.R. moments.

## 7. The Call to Action

A presentation without a specific, actionable call to action is incomplete. The call to action answers the question: "What exactly do I want each person in this room to do, and when?"

Duarte classifies audience actions into four types:
- **Doers:** Take a specific action themselves
- **Suppliers:** Provide resources, funding, connections
- **Influencers:** Advocate for the idea within their networks
- **Innovators:** Co-create, adapt, or extend the idea

**Principles for an effective call to action:**
1. Be explicit — do not assume the audience will know what to do.
2. Break it into discrete, manageable tasks with deadlines.
3. Frame it in terms of benefit to the audience — not just what they must do for you.
4. Close with the "New Bliss" — remind them of the future state they are moving toward.

**Business plan pitch example:**
- "We are asking the credit committee to approve a UGX 450M term loan at the rates discussed."
- "We need a decision within 21 days to begin equipment procurement before the April planting season."
- "If approved, this investment creates 47 additional jobs and triples our tax contribution to URA within three years — that is the kind of growth story Uganda Development Bank exists to enable."

## 8. Slide Design Principles — Duarte's Visual Grammar

### The Glance Test

> "Each slide should pass what I call the glance test: People should be able to comprehend it in three seconds."

Think of slides as billboards. Drivers briefly take their eyes off the road to process billboard information — similarly, your audience should focus on what you are saying, looking only briefly at your slides when they appear.

**Why this matters mathematically:** If you use the default PowerPoint text field (approximately 80 words per slide) across 40 slides in a 40-minute presentation, your audience will spend 13 minutes (one-third of the presentation) reading instead of listening.

### Signal-to-Noise Ratio

Remove everything from a slide that does not directly support what you are saying at that moment:
- Your logo on every slide (if the audience knows who you are, remove it)
- Decorative borders and swooshes
- Bullet points that repeat exactly what you say aloud
- 3D effects on charts
- Gratuitous animations

> "Research shows that people learn more effectively from multimedia messages when they're stripped of extraneous words, graphics, animation, and sounds. The extras actually take away meaning because they become a distraction."

### Five Design Principles for Slide Arrangement

**1. Flow**
Placement governs how the eye travels across the slide. Design slides so the eye moves in one back-and-forth motion to capture the key message. Left to right, top to bottom — do not force zigzag eye paths.

**2. Contrast**
Our eyes are drawn to things that stand out. Use contrast of size, shape, colour, and proximity to direct attention to what matters most. The single most effective contrast technique for data slides: render everything in grey except the one data point you want the audience to focus on.

**3. White Space**
White space is not empty space — it is a tool. Advertisers have discovered that white space creates higher perceived value. It sharpens focus by isolating the elements that matter.
- "That doesn't necessarily mean that everything is literally 'white' — just that the design feels spacious."

**4. Hierarchy**
A clear visual hierarchy allows viewers to quickly determine which elements are most important. Large element → medium element → small element maps directly onto primary message → supporting detail → context.

**5. Unity**
Slides should look as though the same person designed them. Achieve unity through:
- Consistent typefaces (one or two maximum)
- Consistent colour palette (three complementary colours plus one or two neutral shades)
- Consistent image treatment and style throughout the deck

### Typography Rules

- Scale type as large as possible so people in the back of the room can read it.
- One typeface — two at most — for the entire deck.
- Use type size to create hierarchy: the most important text is largest.
- Never use decorative or script fonts for body text in a business presentation.

### Colour Rules

- Limit to three complementary colours plus one or two neutral shades (grey, pale blue).
- Use colour strategically to draw attention — not decoratively.
- In data slides, use a single rich colour for the data point you are highlighting; grey for everything else.
- Ensure sufficient contrast between text and background for legibility from the back of a room.

### Slide Types — When to Use Each

| Slide type | Use it when... | Common mistake |
|---|---|---|
| **Walk-in slide** | Before you begin — sets the tone | Using a corporate template that conveys nothing |
| **Title slide** | Opening — orients and focuses the audience | Making it too busy; it should be clean |
| **Navigation slide** | Moving between major sections | Overusing it — only needed for presentations over 30 minutes |
| **Bullet slide** | Clustering related ideas | Displaying all bullets at once — use builds instead |
| **Big-word slide** | One key concept or phrase to land | Making the word too small to be impactful |
| **Quote slide** | Adding credibility or emotional weight | Quoting more than 30 words; projecting multiple quotes |
| **Data slide** | Evidence, research, performance reporting | Projecting raw tables — simplify to the key insight |
| **Diagram slide** | Showing relationships between concepts | Using a diagram that is more complex than the bullet it replaced |
| **Conceptual image slide** | When showing is more powerful than telling | Using generic stock photography |
| **Video slide** | Testimonials, demonstrations, animated infographics | Poor audio quality or slow-loading files |
| **Walk-out slide** | Call to action as people exit | Ending abruptly with a "Questions?" slide |

### One Idea Per Slide

"There's no reason to crowd several ideas onto one slide. Slides are free. Make as many as you need to give each idea its own moment onstage."

**Storyboarding discipline:** Before building slides in software, sketch each slide as a small thumbnail (1.5" × 2" sticky note). If the concept does not fit in a thumbnail, it is too complex for a single slide. Duarte uses approximately 145 slides in a 40-minute talk — yet audiences consistently estimate she used 30–50.

## 9. Data Slides — Turning Numbers into Insight

### The Core Principle

> "When displaying data in a presentation, pursue clarity above all else."

Audiences read data slides in a fixed sequence: title → shapes the data makes → axes. Design for this sequence — do not fight it.

### Rules for Data Slides

**Highlight what matters, grey out the rest**
If you are showing five years of sales and discussing Q1 underperformance, render the Q1 bar in rich colour and all other bars in grey. The audience's eye goes directly to your point.

**Kill the grid lines, borders, and 3D effects**
Grid lines and borders compete visually with the data. Remove or mute them. Never use 3D effects — the depth creates false visual emphasis and distorts the perceived magnitude of values.

**Do not distort the axes**
Truncating the y-axis makes small differences look large. Stretching the y-axis makes large differences look small. Square grid proportions keep data honest.

**Find the narrative in the data**
Explain not just the "what" but the "why" and the "how." Numbers went up — what made them go up? Who was affected? What does it mean for the audience?

**Make large numbers concrete**
"Millions, billions, and trillions sound a lot alike, but they're nowhere near each other in magnitude." Help the audience grasp scale through concrete comparison: "A billion square feet — hold up a 12" × 12" carpet tile; you would need a billion of these to cover Manhattan."

**Pick the right chart for the relationship**
- Line graph: Change over time; shape of the line tells the story
- Bar chart: Comparison between categories
- Pie chart: Part-to-whole (use sparingly — rarely as effective as a bar)
- Matrix: Relationships between multiple variables
- Single large number: When one figure is the entire message

**Sometimes the best chart is no chart**
"If a number conveys your key message most clearly on its own, show just that number — huge — on the slide."

## 10. Translating Bullets into Diagrams

Bullet slides are the most common and least effective slide format. Before building any bullet slide, check whether a diagram would express the relationship more clearly.

### Diagram Taxonomy (Duarte's Five Types)

| Relationship | Diagram type | Uganda business example |
|---|---|---|
| **Network** | Hub-and-spoke | Stakeholders from various departments contributing to a factory expansion |
| **Segment** | Donut / pie | How different product lines fit within a product portfolio |
| **Join** | Hook / overlap | Supply chain partnership between farmer cooperative and processor |
| **Flow** | Parallel arrows / process flow | Two teams working in parallel toward a product launch |
| **Stack** | Vertical layers / building blocks | Annual financial targets as building blocks to profitability |

**Process for converting bullets:**
1. Look at a bullet slide
2. Circle the nouns and verbs
3. Ask: what is the relationship between these items? (Network? Flow? Stack?)
4. Replace the bullets with the appropriate diagram type

## 11. The Assertion-Evidence Slide Structure

Traditional corporate slides use a **topic headline** (e.g., "Market Overview") with bullets below containing the evidence. Duarte advocates the **assertion-evidence** structure instead:

| Traditional | Assertion-Evidence |
|---|---|
| **Title:** Market Overview | **Title (assertion):** We are neck-and-neck with an aggressive rival |
| Bullet: Market size is $10M | Visual evidence: Chart showing market share gap closing over three years |
| Bullet: We have 23% share | |
| Bullet: Competitor has 25% share | |

**The assertion is the claim the slide must prove.** The visual is the evidence. The presenter's spoken words add context and nuance.

This transforms slide titles from passive labels into active arguments — the audience can follow the logic of the presentation just by reading the titles in sequence.

> "Ask yourself, 'If people read just the titles, will they get what I'm saying?'"

### Slide Title Quality Test

| Vague, passive | Clear, active (assertion) |
|---|---|
| Market overview | We are neck-and-neck with an aggressive rival |
| Productivity gains | Production time shrank from 21 days to 8 |
| Financial projections | Revenue doubles by Year 3 even in the conservative scenario |
| Risk analysis | Three risks are manageable; one requires immediate mitigation |

## 12. Analytical vs. Emotional Appeal — Getting the Balance Right

> "No presentation should be devoid of emotional content, no matter how cerebral the topic or the audience."

Duarte identifies two classes of emotion that presentations can evoke: **pain** (discomfort, urgency, fear of missing out, concern) and **pleasure** (excitement, aspiration, pride, relief). A persuasive presentation uses both.

**For bank credit committees:** Lead with analytical content. The numbers, ratios, and evidence must be impeccable. But add emotional texture: the story of the employees whose jobs depend on this investment; the community this business serves; the vision that motivates the founder. Banks fund humans, not spreadsheets.

**For investors:** Balance roughly equally. Investors make analytical judgements but are deeply influenced by their belief in the founder.

**Technique — "Why" drilling:**
Start with any logical claim and ask "why" repeatedly until you reach the human beings whose lives are affected. That is where the emotional appeal lives.

Example: "We need cloud storage" → "to enable remote team collaboration" → "to coordinate disaster relief faster" → "to save lives." Lead with saving lives; the cloud storage is just the mechanism.

## 13. Delivery and Presence

### Authenticity

> "Transparency wins people over. Though you'll want to come across as smart and articulate, it's even more important to be open and sincere so people will trust you and your ideas."

Audiences forgive stumbles, "ums," and backtracks if they know the presenter's heart is in the right place. Authenticity is not a technique — it is the foundation on which all other delivery skills rest.

Two contrasting examples Duarte cites:
- Steve Ballmer (Microsoft): Explosive, sweaty, over-the-top passion. Nobody questioned his authenticity.
- Susan Cain (TED 2012): Quiet, measured, introverted. Her style matched her subject (introversion) and her personality. Nobody questioned her authenticity.

The lesson: **match your style to your content and to your genuine self.** Do not try to be Steve Jobs if you are a quiet analyst.

### Body Language

- **Posture:** Open posture signals confidence. Avoid folded arms, crossed legs, hands in pockets, or clasped hands.
- **Gestures:** Make them larger than in normal conversation — especially in a large room. Gestures should complement content: use expansive gestures for large numbers and successes; bring hands close together for small gaps or losses.
- **Eye contact:** Do not turn your back to the audience to read your slides. Keep eyes on people.
- **Movement:** Fill the space. Walk toward the audience when making a key point. Step back when asking a question.

### Vocal Variety

A flat delivery bores audiences. Create contrast through:
- **Volume:** Raise your voice for emphasis; lower it almost to a whisper for intimacy — audiences lean in.
- **Pace:** Slow down at key points. Speed up through supporting details.
- **Pause:** A well-placed pause after an important statement gives the audience time to absorb it. Most presenters fill pauses with "um" — replace the filler with silence.
- **Pitch:** Vary pitch to signal different types of content (questions, assertions, stories).

**Identifying verbal tics:** Record yourself presenting. Duarte discovered she said "Right?" with an annoying lilt after every key point. One viewing of the video was enough to make the tic audible — and then it was correctable.

### Managing Q&A

Q&A is an opportunity to drive the point home — not just a threat to manage.

**Preparation:** Think through every question the audience might raise, from mundane to hostile. Prepare answers. For hostile questions, know in advance exactly what you will say.

**Listening for subtext:** A question about one thing often conceals a deeper concern. If employees ask why birthday parties have been cancelled, the real question is: "Does this new management value our culture?" Answer the surface question and the deeper one.

**Honest "I don't know":** Never fake an answer. It destroys credibility instantly. "I don't have that data in front of me — I'll find out and get back to you within 24 hours" is a stronger response than a confident wrong answer.

**Handling hostile questioners:** Acknowledge the question; look at the rest of the audience when answering; do not engage in a one-on-one debate. Move on quickly.

**Ending Q&A strongly:** Do not let Q&A be the last thing the audience experiences. After the final question, deliver a brief summary that returns to the "New Bliss" — the compelling future state. End on your terms.

## 14. The "Presenting to Senior Executives" Protocol

Banks' credit committees and DFI investment committees are essentially senior executive audiences. Duarte's specific guidance:

**Get to the point immediately.** Executives make large decisions on tight schedules. Long presentations with a "big reveal at the end" do not work for them.

**Lead with the summary.** Create executive summary slides (approximately 10% of total slide count) at the front of the deck. Put everything else in an appendix. If your appendix is 50 slides, your summary section is 5 slides.

**Create a talk within a talk.** If you have 30 minutes, create your presentation within that timeframe — then mentally cut it to 5 minutes. What are the three things they absolutely must hear?

**Set expectations at the start.** "I will spend the first 5 minutes on my key findings and recommendation. The remaining 25 minutes are for your questions." Executives are patient for 5 minutes if they know they can ask questions soon.

**Rehearse with a senior adviser.** Someone who has successfully presented to executive audiences before. Ask: "Is my message coming through in the first 60 seconds? Are my summary slides skimmable?"

## 15. Business Plan Deck Structure — Applying the Sparkline

The standard 12–15 slide business plan pitch deck can be restructured using Duarte's Sparkline. The structural mapping:

### Slide Structure (Sparkline Applied)

| Slide # | Content | Sparkline moment |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Walk-in / Title slide | Sets tone before you begin |
| 2 | **The Hook — What Is** | Open with the problem/gap the audience recognises |
| 3 | **What Could Be** | The vision — what the world looks like with your solution |
| 4 | Market — What Is | Size of the problem; who suffers; current inadequate solutions |
| 5 | Market — What Could Be | Your addressable share; the opportunity |
| 6 | Product / Service | The magical tool you give the hero |
| 7 | Business Model | How value is created and captured |
| 8 | Traction / Proof | What Is already working — evidence from the field |
| 9 | **STAR Moment** | Shocking statistic, evocative image, or emotive story |
| 10 | Financials — What Is | Current performance; where you are now |
| 11 | Financials — What Could Be | Projections; the return to the investor/lender |
| 12 | Risk & Mitigation | Anticipate resistance; demonstrate that risks are managed |
| 13 | Team | The mentor's credentials — why you can deliver the New Bliss |
| 14 | The Ask | Specific, explicit call to action with amount and terms |
| 15 | **New Bliss** | The world three years from now; the return; the legacy |

### Critical Structure Rules for Business Plan Pitches

1. **Never open with the company overview or your history.** Open with the problem. Make them feel the pain of "what is" before you introduce yourself.
2. **The financial slide is NOT the climax.** Position financials as evidence mid-deck. The climax is the New Bliss — what the investment creates.
3. **One assertion per slide, not one topic.** "Revenue grows 3× in Year 2" beats "Financial Projections."
4. **The team slide belongs near the end** — after you have established the vision and the market, so the audience evaluates the team against a context they already understand.
5. **End with inspiration, not with "Questions?"** The last image and last words should be the New Bliss — the future state. Let them sit in that future for a moment before opening Q&A.

## 16. Slide Transformation Guide — Before and After

### Type 1: The Bullet-Point Dump

**Before:** A slide titled "Market Opportunity" with seven bullet points listing statistics, market segments, and trends — all in 10pt font.

**Problem:** Audience reads instead of listens. Seven points compete for attention. No single message lands.

**After — Three options:**
- **Option A (Big-Word/Assertion):** Title: "Uganda's UGX 3.2T food import bill is a gap we can fill." Visual: One large number — UGX 3.2T — with a single supporting sentence. Speaker narrates the detail.
- **Option B (Diagram):** Title: "Three underserved segments drive 80% of the opportunity." Visual: Donut diagram showing three named segments. Speaker explains each in sequence.
- **Option C (Data slide):** Title: "Kampala's middle class grew 34% in five years — our core customer." Visual: Clean bar chart, one colour for the target demographic bar, grey for all others.

### Type 2: The Raw Data Table

**Before:** A full Excel-style table with 8 rows and 6 columns pasted directly onto a slide — P&L for five years with actuals and projections.

**Problem:** Audience cannot parse this at distance in three seconds. Eyes do not know where to go. Dense text signals "no one thought about what I need to see."

**After:**
- Title becomes an assertion: "The loan is fully repaid by Month 30 — even in the stress scenario."
- Remove all rows and columns except the three that prove this point.
- Highlight the Month 30 repayment figure in a strong colour.
- Show the stress case in smaller grey text below as a footnote.
- Attach the full table as a handout or appendix.

### Type 3: The Text Wall

**Before:** A slide that is essentially a paragraph of prose — the executive summary, verbatim, pasted onto a slide.

**Problem:** The audience reads the slide and stops listening. The presenter becomes background noise.

**After:**
- Break the paragraph into its three or four distinct claims.
- Give each claim its own slide (slides are free).
- Each slide has a one-sentence assertion headline and one supporting visual or single statistic.
- Distribute the executive summary text as a handout — it works perfectly as a document; it fails as a projected slide.

### Type 4: The Generic Stock Photo

**Before:** A slide about "growth" illustrated with a stock photo of a green seedling in soil, or a slide about "teamwork" illustrated with four ethnically diverse people in business attire laughing over a laptop.

**Problem:** These images are so ubiquitous they trigger no emotional response. They signal that no one thought carefully about the visual.

**After:**
- For a Uganda business: use an actual photograph of your operation, your product, your customers, your site. Real specificity beats generic aspiration.
- For an abstract concept: replace the stock photo with a bold typographic slide or a diagram.
- If you must use stock photography, search for images that are unexpected, specific, and slightly strange — something that makes the audience look twice.

**Cliché alternatives (Duarte's examples):**

| Concept | Cliché | Replace with |
|---|---|---|
| Goal | Bull's-eye target | Maze with a single clear path; a threshold being crossed |
| Partnership | Handshake in front of globe | Reef ecosystem; Fred Astaire and Ginger Rogers |
| Security | Lock and key | A Doberman; a pepper spray canister |
| Growth | Seedling | An actual graph of your own growth — real data beats illustration |

### Type 5: The Org Chart

**Before:** A standard hierarchical org chart showing the management team, with boxes and lines and job titles — typically a slide that audiences skim and forget.

**Problem:** An org chart communicates structure, not capability. It does not answer the real question: "Can these people actually execute?"

**After:**
- Replace the org chart with individual "portrait cards" — one per key person: name, photo, two-line credential statement.
- Title the slide with an assertion: "This team has built and sold two businesses in this sector before."
- If the org chart must appear (e.g., for a DFI requirement), relegate it to the appendix.
- Use the body of the deck to tell a 30-second story about why the founder is uniquely qualified.

## 17. Practical Presentation Rules

### Length

- Plan content for 60% of your allotted time slot. The remaining 40% is for Q&A, discussion, and the unexpected.
- Human presentation tolerance: 30–40 minutes. Beyond that, build in changes of pace (guest speaker, video, exercise).
- TED's 18-minute limit is a model: "A great talk goes by quickly. A bad one — well, people can endure it if it's only 18 minutes."
- "People in your audience won't scold you for ending early, but they will for ending late."

### Preparation Rule

> "It actually takes more careful planning to distill your ideas into a few key takeaways than it does to create an hour-long presentation."

Rehearse. Record yourself. Get honest feedback from someone who will not protect your feelings. Know your material so well that you could deliver it without slides if the technology fails.

### The Two Ending Rule

Always build two natural endings:
- **False ending:** A summary of key points — could serve as the conclusion if you are running short.
- **Real ending:** An inspiring story or vivid description of the New Bliss — the one you use when timing is right.

This gives you a graceful exit if you run long without sacrificing impact.

### Slideuments vs. Slides

A slideument is a document designed in presentation software. It works as a standalone read-behind document — but not as a projected slide.

**Rule:** If your slides could be distributed and understood without a presenter, you have created a document, not a presentation. Circulate it before or after the presentation. Do not project it.

## 18. Ethos, Pathos, Logos — Applied to Business Plan Pitches

### Ethos (Credibility)

Credibility must be established before making your central claim. If the audience does not trust you, the strongest evidence in the world will not persuade them.

**Build ethos through:**
- Stating your relevant experience in the first two minutes — not your full biography, just the credential that makes you the right person to address this problem.
- Acknowledging what you do not know. Intellectual honesty signals that what you do claim is reliable.
- Citing credible sources: industry reports, government statistics, expert endorsements.
- The quality of your preparation — a well-designed, error-free presentation communicates respect for the audience's time.

### Pathos (Emotional Connection)

Pathos is not manipulation — it is connection. "Decisions are not made by facts alone." The audience needs to feel something before they will change their beliefs or behaviour.

**Pathos tools:**
- Personal story that establishes why this problem matters to you
- Statistics that connect numbers to human consequences
- Evocative language — specific, sensory, active verbs
- The S.T.A.R. moment at the presentation's emotional peak

### Logos (Logical Evidence)

Logos must underpin every claim. In a business plan pitch:
- Financial projections must have explicit assumptions
- Market size estimates must be sourced
- Competitive claims must be verifiable
- Risk claims must acknowledge uncertainty

**The "So What?" test:** For every piece of evidence, ask: "So what? Why does this matter to this specific audience?" If you cannot answer in one sentence, the evidence is not yet doing persuasive work.

## 19. Social Proof and Testimonials

Stories told by others about your business carry a different kind of credibility than your own narrative. Use:
- Customer testimonials (quoted, video, or live on stage)
- Reference letters
- Press coverage (print a relevant headline; do not show the full article)
- Third-party validation: "Our product was independently tested by [institution] and confirmed [specific result]"

In a Uganda business plan context:
- A letter from your largest customer confirming an ongoing order is more persuasive than projected sales figures.
- A recommendation from a local institution (KCCA, UNBS, Uganda Export Promotions Board) signals regulatory legitimacy.
- A quote from a satisfied employee or community member humanises the impact story.

## 20. Before, During, and After — The Extended Persuasion Timeline

Duarte argues that persuasion does not begin when you walk into the room. It begins when you commit to speak — and continues after the presentation ends.

### Before
- Send a thoughtfully written agenda with a telling subject line — explicit about what the audience will gain.
- For external audiences: post biography and talking points online; provide pre-reading.
- Meet the toughest critics privately to pre-sell your ideas.

### During
- Distribute handouts at the right moment — not all at once (people read ahead).
- If creating external buzz: post slides online with supporting videos and photos.
- Use a social media hashtag for large external presentations.

### After
- Send a personal thank-you note (handwritten when the stakes are high).
- Follow up with supplementary material that delivers additional value.
- For a bank pitch: send a brief note confirming you are available for any follow-up questions the credit committee may have.
- Book "next steps" meetings immediately to maintain momentum.

## 21. Key Duarte Principles — Quick Reference

| Principle | Summary |
|---|---|
| **Audience first** | "You're not the star of the show. The audience is." |
| **Big Idea** | One key message expressed in a complete sentence with point of view and stakes |
| **The Sparkline** | Toggle between "what is" and "what could be" throughout; end at "New Bliss" |
| **Contrast** | Create and resolve tension repeatedly — this is what keeps audiences engaged |
| **Story** | Transformation + clear structure. Personal stories with conviction are most powerful. |
| **S.T.A.R. moment** | One climactic, unforgettable moment per presentation |
| **Glance test** | Slides must be comprehensible in three seconds |
| **Assertion headline** | Every slide title is an active claim, not a passive topic label |
| **One idea per slide** | Slides are free. Never crowd two ideas onto one. |
| **Data clarity** | Highlight the one thing you want audiences to remember; grey out everything else |
| **Bullet alternatives** | Find the relationship in your bullets; replace with a diagram |
| **Authenticity** | Match your style to your content and your genuine self |
| **Rehearse** | Know your material so well that technology failure cannot derail you |
| **End strong** | Never end with "Questions?" — end with the New Bliss |
