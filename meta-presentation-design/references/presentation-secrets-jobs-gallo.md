# Presentation Secrets of Steve Jobs — Gallo Framework

> "A person can have the greatest idea in the world — completely different and novel — but if that person can't convince enough other people, it doesn't matter." — Gregory Berns

## Overview: The Three-Act Structure

Gallo organises the book — and Jobs's presentations — as a three-act play:

- **Act 1: Create the Story** — craft the narrative, headline, villain, hero, and road map before opening any slide software
- **Act 2: Deliver the Experience** — visual slide design, making numbers meaningful, demos, props, and the wow moment
- **Act 3: Refine and Rehearse** — body language, vocal delivery, rehearsal process, costume, handling failure

Jobs himself described presentation structure as "like every classic story." At a 2005 video iPod launch he explicitly divided his announcements into three acts.

---

## ACT 1: CREATE THE STORY

### Scene 1 — Plan in Analogue (Design on Paper First)

**The core principle:** Design experts who create presentations for Apple recommend spending the majority of time thinking, sketching, and scripting — *before* opening PowerPoint or Keynote.

Nancy Duarte (designer of Al Gore's *An Inconvenient Truth*) suggests up to **ninety hours** for a one-hour, thirty-slide presentation — but only one-third of that time building the slides. The first twenty-seven hours go to researching, organising ideas, and sketching the story structure.

**The three-step storyboard approach** (Cliff Atkinson, *Beyond Bullet Points*):
1. Writing (scripting the scenes)
2. Sketching (visualising slides)
3. Producing (building in software)

**Bullets kill.** "There are no bullet points in a Steve Jobs presentation." The software defaults (PowerPoint's blank format with title/subtitle + Format > Bullets) push you toward the exact opposite of what Jobs does. "Save your bullet points for grocery lists."

**The Napkin Test:** The best business ideas can be sketched on a napkin. Southwest Airlines was founded on a triangle connecting Dallas, Houston, and San Antonio — drawn on a cocktail napkin. If your idea cannot be drawn simply, it needs more work. "Slides don't tell stories; you do. Slides complement the story."

**Aristotle's Five-Point Persuasive Framework** (which Jobs follows):
1. Deliver a story or statement that arouses interest
2. Pose a problem or question to be solved
3. Offer a solution to the problem
4. Describe specific benefits for adopting the solution
5. State a call to action ("Now go out and buy one!")

**Nine elements of great presentations** (to plan before opening any software):
1. **Headline** — one big idea, 140 characters or less, subject-verb-object structure
2. **Passion statement** — why you are genuinely excited (fills the sentence: "I'm excited about this because it ___")
3. **Three key messages** — easily recalled without notes (short-term memory holds only three or four points)
4. **Metaphors and analogies** — "A computer is like a bicycle for the mind" (Jobs)
5. **Demonstrations** — show the product in action
6. **Partners** — share the stage with customers, partners, endorsers
7. **Customer evidence and third-party endorsements** — word of mouth is the number-one influencer
8. **Video clips** — embed two-to-three-minute clips; average YouTube clip viewed is 2.5 minutes
9. **Flip charts, props, and show-and-tell** — appeal to visual, auditory, and kinesthetic learners

### Scene 2 — Answer the One Question That Matters Most

**The question every audience member is silently asking:** "Why should I care?"

Jobs answers this question within the first minute. He does not open with technical specifications, company history, or agenda slides. He opens with the audience's problem and why the solution will improve their lives.

> "What am I really selling? Here's a hint: it's not the widget, but what the widget can do to improve the lives of your customers. What you're selling is the dream of a better life."

Examples of what great leaders really sell:
- Steve Jobs: "Tools to unleash human potential" (not computers)
- Howard Schultz: "A third place between work and home" (not coffee)
- Suze Orman: "The dream of financial freedom" (not mutual funds)
- John Chambers (Cisco): "Human connections that change the way we live, work, play, and learn" (not routers)

**Application to business plan presentations:** Never open with your company registration number, your organogram, or a slide listing your products. Open with the problem your customer lives with every day, stated in their language.

### Scene 3 — Develop a Messianic Sense of Purpose

**Passion is the foundation.** Jobs fell in love at age twenty-one with the vision of how personal computing would change society. That passion is contagious and comes through in every presentation. "If you are passionate about your topic, you're 80 percent closer to developing the magnetism that Jobs has."

Jobs's "Think Different" campaign — featuring Gandhi, Einstein, Picasso, Martin Luther King Jr. — reflected his belief that Apple exists to put a "dent in the universe." When he first saw the finished commercial, he cried. It was not staged.

**Director's Notes:**
- Dig deep to identify your true passion. Ask: "What am I really selling?"
- Develop a personal passion statement — one sentence that tells prospects why you are genuinely excited about working with them. "Your passion statement will be remembered long after your company's mission statement is forgotten."
- "I skate to where the puck is going to be, not where it has been." — Wayne Gretzky (Jobs's favourite quote)

### Scene 4 — Create Twitter-Like Headlines

**The one-line description:** Jobs creates a memorable, specific headline for every product — crafted in the planning stage, before the presentation, press releases, or marketing material. It is repeated consistently across all channels.

> "Welcome to Macworld 2008. There is something clearly in the air today." — Jobs's opening line, before revealing the MacBook Air headline: "The world's thinnest notebook."

**The three criteria for an effective headline:**
1. **Concise** — 140 characters or less (fits in a tweet)
2. **Specific** — not vague generalities
3. **Personal benefit** — answers "Why should I care?"

**Classic Jobs headlines:**
- "iPod. One thousand songs in your pocket." (not "a new 5 GB MP3 player")
- "Today Apple reinvents the phone!" (used five times in the iPhone keynote)
- "iPhone 3G. Twice as fast at half the price."
- "The industry's greenest notebooks."
- "MacBook Air — the world's thinnest notebook" (repeated verbatim in presentation, CNBC interview, Apple press release, website, and billboards on the same day)

**Investor test:** When Sergey Brin and Larry Page walked into Sequoia Capital, they described Google in one sentence: "Google provides access to the world's information in one click" (63 characters, 10 words). An investor said: "If you cannot describe what you do in ten words or less, I'm not investing, I'm not buying, I'm not interested. Period."

**For business plan presentations:** Write your headline before building your deck. The rest of the presentation should be built around it. Your headline is a statement that offers a vision of a better future — it's not about you, it's about your audience.

### Scene 5 — Draw a Road Map (The Rule of Three)

**The verbal road map:** Jobs always tells his audience what to expect before delivering it. "Today we are introducing three revolutionary products."

During the iPhone launch (Macworld 2007), Jobs built deliberate suspense:
- "The first one is a wide-screen iPod with touch controls."
- "The second is a revolutionary mobile phone." (audience cheers)
- "And the third is a breakthrough Internet communications device."
- "An iPod, a phone, and an Internet communicator. An iPod, a phone — are you getting it? These are not three separate devices. This is one device, and we are calling it iPhone."

**Why three works:** Short-term memory holds three or four items. George Miller's landmark 1956 paper "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two" established this; contemporary research puts the optimal number closer to three or four. The U.S. Marine Corps divides everything into threes for the same reason.

"Three is the magic number. Comedians know that three is funnier than two. Writers know that three is more dramatic than four. Jobs knows that three is more persuasive than five."

Examples of the rule of three:
- Three musketeers (not five), three bears (not four), three stooges (not two)
- "Life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness" — not simply life and liberty
- Apple's three-legged stool: Mac, iPod/iTunes, iPhone
- Every Steve Jobs keynote divided into three acts

**USA Today method:** Every great article condenses to headline, introduction, three key points, conclusion. Jobs writes his presentations the same way.

**Director's Notes:**
- Reveal your road map early: "I want to cover three things today"
- Use verbal signposts: "first," "second," "third"
- Build drama: each point should be bigger than the last

---

### Scene 6 — Introduce the Antagonist (The Villain)

**Narrative structure:** Before Jobs introduces a hero (new Apple product), he always establishes a villain — the problem, the incumbent, the status quo — so the audience shares his frustration.

> "Why do we need our own browser?" — Jobs introducing Safari, before detailing the flaws in Internet Explorer and Netscape

Jobs's most famous villain introduction: the 1984 IBM "Big Blue" speech. Before unveiling Macintosh, he spent several minutes painting IBM as a corporation "bent on world domination," positioning Apple as the only hope for freedom. The audience broke into wild cheers.

**The villain need not be a competitor.** It can be:
- A market inefficiency ("Every portable music player had bad battery life and tiny storage")
- A customer frustration ("Booking is only 5 percent of the travel process — the 95 percent before booking is where the pain is")
- A regulatory or social problem

**The four-question elevator pitch** (building the villain):
1. What do you do?
2. What problem do you solve?
3. How are you different?
4. Why should I care?

**Investor insight:** "You need to create a new space in my brain to hold the information you're about to deliver. It turns me off when entrepreneurs offer a solution without setting up the problem. They have a pot of coffee — their idea — without a cup to pour it in."

**Director's Notes:**
- Always establish the problem before revealing your solution
- Make the problem tangible — "build the pain"
- One rhetorical question is enough to introduce the antagonist: "Why do we need this?"
- Nobody cares about your product. People care about solving their problems.

---

### Scene 7 — Reveal the Conquering Hero

**The hero's mission:** Not to slay the bad guy, but to make lives better. "Jobs doesn't sell computers; he sells an experience."

The hero must:
- Be introduced *after* the villain is fully established
- Solve the problem in clear, simple, jargon-free language
- Offer specific, tangible benefits (not specs)

> "The biggest thing about iPod is that it holds a thousand songs. This is a quantum leap because for most people, it's their entire music library... your entire music library fits in your pocket. This was never possible before."

Jobs consistently avoided mind-numbing technical data. Instead of "Intel Core 2 Duo 2.4 GHz, 2 GB DDR3 SDRAM," Apple's website said: "It's gorgeous inside and out; it does what a PC does, only better; it has the world's most advanced operating system."

**"I'm a Mac. I'm a PC."** Apple's 2006 "Get a Mac" campaign ran the villain-hero arc in thirty-second vignettes: PC is stuffy, slow, and frustrated; Mac is friendly and easygoing. "There's no villain" is why Microsoft's counter-campaign ("I'm a PC") had less emotional impact.

---

### Intermission 1 — Obey the Ten-Minute Rule

**Cognitive research finding:** The brain needs a break roughly every ten minutes. Attention begins to wander after approximately ten minutes of any single activity.

Jobs structures his presentations to include a break in the action approximately every ten minutes — a demonstration, a video clip, a guest speaker, a prop. He never talks at an audience for more than ten minutes without a change of medium or format.

**Application:** In a thirty-minute investor presentation, plan three distinct segments. Between each, include something that changes the sensory experience: show a product, run a brief video testimonial, demonstrate a prototype, hand around a physical sample.

## ACT 2: DELIVER THE EXPERIENCE

### Scene 8 — Channel Their Inner Zen (Slide Design)

**The simplicity principle:** "Simplicity is the ultimate sophistication." — Leonardo da Vinci

Jobs's slides are strikingly simple. In the MacBook Air presentation, the majority of slides featured only photographs with no words, or a single phrase. He referred the audience to the Apple website for technical specifications.

**What to eliminate:**
- Bullet points ("Bullet points on presentation slides should be avoided. Pictures are superior.")
- Multiple fonts and inconsistent styling
- Text that the speaker reads aloud word for word
- More than one idea per slide

**The picture superiority effect:** Images are far more memorable than words. "Instead of giving people the benefit of your wit and wisdom (words), try painting them a picture. The more strikingly visual your presentation is, the more people will remember it." (Paul Arden)

**Comparison: ugly slide vs. Jobs slide:**
A typical competitor slide for a notebook computer contained: Display specs, size measurements, weight, storage options, battery specs, processor speed, memory — all in multiple font sizes and styles. Jobs's MacBook Air slide showed a full-screen photograph of the device and four words: "MacBook Air — The world's thinnest notebook."

**Jobs's language simplification principle:**
- MacBook Air 0.16–0.76 inch measurement → "It's the world's thinnest notebook"
- Complex OS X specs → "Mac OS X is the most technically advanced personal computer operating system ever"
- New environmentally friendly MacBook's Energy Star compliance, no BFRs, PVC-free cables, mercury-free LED → "They are the industry's greenest notebooks"

**Einstein's rule:** "If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough."

**Plain English Campaign principle:** Write so that your intended audience can read, understand, and act on it the first time they hear it. Before-and-after example:
- Before: "If there are any points on which you require explanation or further particulars we shall be glad to furnish such additional details as may be required by telephone."
- After: "If you have any questions, please call."

**Director's Notes:**
- Avoid bullet points. Always. Almost always.
- Focus on one theme per slide, complemented by a photograph or image.
- If your slide needs explanation, redesign it.

### Scene 9 — Dress Up Your Numbers

**The core problem:** Numbers are abstract. Jobs never announces a number without immediately contextualising it in a way the audience already understands.

**Classic Jobs number examples:**
- 5 GB storage → "One thousand songs in your pocket" → pulled iPod from pocket (physical demonstration of scale)
- 30 GB → "7,500 songs, 25,000 photos, or up to 75 hours of video"
- 4 million iPhones sold → "That's twenty thousand iPhones every day on average" → "iPhone captured nearly 20% of the smartphone market, matching the combined share of all remaining competitors — in the first ninety days of shipments"
- Apple's 5% market share → "Our market share is greater than BMW or Mercedes in the car industry. No one thinks BMW or Mercedes are going away."

**The SanDisk method (taught by Gallo):** When SanDisk introduced a 12 GB memory card, he coached them not to say "12 GB." Instead: "It has fifty billion transistors. Think of each transistor as an ant: if you were to put fifty billion end to end, they would circle the globe twice. What does this mean to you? Enough memory to store six hours of movies. Enough memory to listen to music while travelling to the moon... and back!"

**The Intel analogy:** When Intel released the Core i7 chip with 730 million transistors, the testing chief compared the old chip (29,000 transistors) to the city of Ithaca, New York (population 29,000) and the new chip to the continent of Europe (730 million people). "Take Europe and shrink it until it all fits in the same land mass as Ithaca."

**Three criteria for effective numbers:**
1. **Specific** — "7,500 songs" not "thousands of songs"
2. **Relevant** — connected to what the audience cares about most
3. **Contextual** — compared to something already familiar

### Scene 10 — Use "Amazingly Zippy" Words

Jobs has favourite descriptors: *unbelievable, awesome, cool, huge, screamer, revolutionary, extraordinary, insanely great.* These are not accidents — they are planned. When he called the MacBook Air processor "a screamer," it was scripted. When he called an engineering achievement "a tour de force," it was deliberate.

The language Jobs uses is direct, vivid, and jargon-free. He speaks to the broadest possible audience rather than the most technical.

**The "amazingly zippy" moment:** During the EDGE vs. 3G comparison demo, after the 3G site loaded in twenty-one seconds versus fifty-nine seconds on EDGE, Jobs said: "3G is 2.8 times faster. It's approaching Wi-Fi speeds. It's amazingly zippy!" The phrase "amazingly zippy" went viral precisely because it was unexpected and vivid.

---

### Scene 11 — Share the Stage

**Jobs as ensemble director:** "Jobs shares the spotlight with employees, partners, and products."

His methods:
- Inviting industry CEOs onstage to endorse Apple (Fox's Jim Gianopulos on the movie rental service: "When Steve came to us with the idea, it was a no-brainer. It was the most exciting, coolest thing we've ever heard.")
- Showing video testimonials from Apple employees or customers
- Reading favourable press reviews aloud while displaying them on screen ("In my view, Leopard is better and faster than Vista." — Walt Mossberg, Wall Street Journal)
- Publicly thanking his team: "I want to highlight the folks who worked on these products... I also can't leave without thanking the families."

**The best sales tool:** "A reference is good. A customer or partner physically sharing the stage is even better."

**Word of mouth is the number-one influencer** of purchasing decisions. Satisfied customers and third-party endorsements overcome buyer scepticism more effectively than any slide.

---

### Scene 12 — Stage Your Presentation with Props

**Kinesthetic learning:** Jobs transforms technical explanations into multi-sensory experiences by handing things to the audience.

At the MacBook unibody launch (October 2008): After explaining the aluminium manufacturing process, Jobs held up a sample frame, said "It's so cool, I'd like you to see it," had Apple representatives hand frames to every audience member, and stayed silent for sixty seconds while people felt the product for themselves. "You're the first to get your hands on one."

**The iPod-from-pocket moment:** When Jobs pulled the original iPod from his jeans pocket, it demonstrated in one gesture what no technical specification could: this fits in your pocket.

**Guy Kawasaki's five criteria for a great demo (from *The Macintosh Way*):**
1. **Short** — does not exhaust the audience
2. **Simple** — no more than one or two key messages; tantalise without bewildering
3. **Sweet** — shows the hottest features; differentiates from competition (imagine someone shouting "So what?" after each feature)
4. **Swift** — fast-paced; "Never do anything in a demo that lasts more than fifteen seconds"
5. **Substantial** — demonstrates a real-world problem being solved

Jobs's EDGE vs. 3G demo met all five: under two minutes, showed only two phones loading the same website, direct competitor comparison, built drama with silence, resolved a pain point everyone had experienced.

### Scene 13 — Reveal a "Holy Shit" Moment

**The planned wow:** Every Steve Jobs presentation contains at least one moment designed to make the audience audibly react.

"We're going to make some history today." — Jobs's opening line, Macworld 2007 iPhone launch

**Examples of "holy shit" moments:**
- Reaching into a manila envelope on stage and pulling out the MacBook Air — the world's thinnest notebook emerging from an ordinary envelope
- Calling a random Starbucks number on the live iPhone demo to "order 4,000 lattes to go" (scripted — it appeared in his backstage notes, but played as spontaneous)
- Announcing "Just one more thing" — the signature phrase that signals a major surprise is coming

**"One More Thing":** Jobs frequently ends presentations with a surprise addition. "Steve Jobs keeps his audience guessing." He announced his return to full-time CEO as the "one more thing" at Macworld 2000. Because audiences began expecting it, Jobs sometimes deliberately omitted it — a surprise that fails to surprise when everyone knows it's coming.

**The Schiller lesson:** When Phil Schiller filled in for Jobs at Macworld 2009, he saved the DRM-free iTunes announcement for last, knowing it would be the headline of the day. "He saved it for last. The announcement did, indeed, dominate the news coverage that followed."

**Director's Notes:**
- Plan your wow moment deliberately — it should be earned by the narrative that precedes it
- Silence *before* the reveal is as important as the reveal itself
- Your wow moment should be the single most memorable thing from the presentation

## ACT 3: REFINE AND REHEARSE

### Scene 14 — Master Stage Presence

**The 63–90 percent rule:** Body language and verbal delivery account for 63 to 90 percent of the impression left on your audience (depending on which study you cite). "Words matter, but an effective delivery makes the difference."

UCLA scientist Albert Mehrabian found that nonverbal cues carry the most impact; tone of voice is second; actual words spoken are least important of the three.

**Three body language techniques Jobs uses:**

**1. Eye contact**
- Great communicators make appreciably more eye contact than average presenters
- Jobs glances at a slide and immediately returns attention to the audience
- Eye contact is associated with honesty, trustworthiness, sincerity, and confidence
- Avoiding eye contact signals lack of confidence and leadership ability
- Jobs can maintain eye contact because he has rehearsed extensively and has visual slides that do not require reading

**2. Open posture**
- Jobs rarely crosses his arms or stands behind a lectern
- During demos, he sits parallel to the computer so nothing blocks his view of the audience
- He abandoned the lectern early in his career and never returned to it (except his 2005 Stanford commencement address)

**3. Hand gestures**
- Jobs emphasises nearly every sentence with a gesture complementing his words
- Extraordinary communicators use *more* gestures than average speakers, not fewer
- Dr. David McNeill (University of Chicago): gestures and language are intimately connected; the use of gestures actually helps speakers think more clearly; disciplined, rigorous thinkers use hand gestures that reflect the clarity of their thinking

**Counter-example — Stan Sigman (AT&T CEO at iPhone launch):** Sigman put his hands in his pockets, delivered in a low-key monotone, pulled note cards from his pocket and read from them word for word, losing all eye contact. Six minutes that felt like thirty. Blog comments: "Who's Mr. Note Card?"; "Painfully bad"; "A snoozer." "Sigman spent forty-two years at AT&T rising to run its wireless division. Yet, to many people, his appearance at Macworld will be his lasting legacy."

**Vocal delivery — four techniques:**

**Inflection:** Jobs raises or lowers his pitch to emphasise key words. "Are you getting it?" was delivered at a higher pitch. Favourite words — *unbelievable, awesome, cool, huge* — are never delivered in the same tone as surrounding sentences.

**Pauses:** "Nothing is more dramatic than a well-placed pause." Before revealing "MacBook Air," Jobs paused. Before "The world's thinnest notebook," he paused again. He lets his content breathe. He does not rush. (Most presenters script more than time allows — Jobs scripts to leave room for pauses.)

**Volume:** Jobs lowers his voice to build anticipation, then raises it to hit the big note. When introducing the iPod: raised voice for "a quantum leap in listening to music," then lowered to almost a whisper for "the coolest thing about iPod is your entire music library fits in your pocket."

**Rate:** Normal pace for demonstrations; slows considerably when delivering the headline or key message. In the iPod introduction, he lowered his voice nearly to a whisper, slowed his tempo, and built the drama to the line: "It is ultraportable."

**The iPhone vocal arc** (Table 14.2): "This is a day I've been looking forward to for two and a half years." [Pause.] … voice grows louder with each of the three products … "These are not three separate devices. This is one device" [louder still] "and we are calling it iPhone." [Loudest volume of the entire presentation.] "Today Apple is going to reinvent the phone!"

### Scene 15 — Make It Look Effortless

**Jobs's rehearsal process is legendary.**

> "Jobs unveils Apple's latest products as if he were a particularly hip and plugged-in friend showing off inventions in your living room. Truth is, the sense of informality comes only after gruelling hours of practice." — BusinessWeek

"One retail executive recalls going to a Macworld rehearsal at Jobs's behest and then waiting four hours before Jobs came off the stage." Jobs considered his keynotes "a competitive weapon." Marissa Mayer (Google) instructed up-and-coming product marketers to attend Jobs's keynotes: "Steve Jobs is the best at launching new products. They have to see how he does it."

**Former Apple employee Mike Evangelist on what it takes backstage:**
> "To a casual observer these presentations appear to be just a guy in a black shirt and blue jeans talking about some new technology products. But they are in fact an incredibly complex and sophisticated blend of sales pitch, product demonstration, and corporate cheerleading, with a dash of religious revival."

**What to rehearse:**
- Every slide transition
- Every demo step (Jobs's backstage notes for the iPhone Maps demo listed exactly five actions: Moscone West, Starbucks order 4,000 lattes, Washington Monument, Show satellite, Eiffel Tower/Colosseum — that was all)
- Every pause
- Every prop moment

**Malcolm Gladwell's 10,000-hour rule** is cited: practice isn't the thing you do once you're good — it's the thing that makes you good.

---

### Scene 16 — Wear the Appropriate Costume

Jobs's uniform — black mock turtleneck, blue jeans, New Balance running shoes — is so consistent that it became a brand identifier. "Saturday Night Live" and "30 Rock" parodied it.

**The key principle:** Your appearance signals what tribe you belong to and what your audience can expect. Jobs's casual dress signals accessibility, creativity, and anti-corporate thinking — exactly what Apple's brand represents.

**For business plan presenters:** Dress one level above your audience's standard, not two levels above. Bank presentations require formal attire. DFI presentations require professional dress. Client presentations should match or slightly exceed the client's own formality. Your appearance is part of the presentation.

---

### Scene 17 — Toss the Script

**Jobs does not read from slides.** He talks *to* the audience. He has practised enough to internalise the content.

**The five-step method for memorising without reading:**
1. Write your script in full sentences in the "notes" section of your slide software — complete ideas, no more than four or five sentences per slide
2. Highlight or underline the key word from each sentence; practice using only those words as prompts
3. Delete extraneous words, leaving only the key words; practice again
4. Memorise the one key idea per slide: "What is the one thing I want my audience to take away from this slide?" The visual becomes your prompter
5. Practice the entire presentation without notes, using slides as the only prompts. By this stage you have rehearsed each slide four times.

**Jobs's actual backstage notes** (photographed at Macworld 2007): The notes for the Maps section read:
- Moscone West
- Starbucks order 4,000 lattes to go
- Washington Monument
- Show satellite
- Eiffel Tower, Colosseum

That was it. Five bullet points to guide a six-minute live demo of a new product category.

**Using notes properly:** Notes are not inherently bad. Keep them available for peace of mind. But never read a full sentence from notes. Joel Osteen's technique: place notes on a lectern, but never stand *behind* the lectern. Walk away from it. Glance; keep moving; deliver the message directly to people.

**The visual slide as prompter:** When Jobs talked about the Intel Core 2 Duo processor in the MacBook Air, his slide showed only a photo of the processor chip. The "one thing" he wanted the audience to know was that Apple had built an ultrathin computer with no compromise in performance. The image prompted him; he never needed to read.

### Scene 18 — Have Fun

**Infotainment:** "Jobs approaches presentations as infotainment; he teaches you something new and has fun doing it."

**Classic Jobs humour:**
- OS 9 funeral: Jobs emerged from backstage as white smoke surrounded a casket with gloomy pipe-organ music. He pulled a large-scale copy of OS 9 from the casket, held it, and delivered a full eulogy. "Mac OS 9 was a friend to us all... He is in the great bit bucket in the sky... Please join me in a moment of silence as we remember our old friend." He laid a rose on the casket. The audience loved it.
- "Hell froze over" slide, announcing iTunes for Windows: "I'm here to report to you today that this has happened." Then: "iTunes for Windows is probably the best Windows app ever written!"
- Health rumours response: walked onstage, pointed to a slide reading "The reports of my death are greatly exaggerated." Said "Enough said" and continued.
- Clicker failure at Macworld 2007: told a spontaneous story about Steve Wozniak's TV jammer prank from high school, kept the audience laughing for sixty seconds, then said "OK, it looks like it's working now."

**When things go wrong — the Jobs method:**
- Acknowledge it without apology
- Smile and continue
- Never call undue attention to the problem
- If it was not obvious to the audience, do not call attention to it
- "It goes wrong only when you call attention to the issue or you let it ruin the rest of your presentation"
- After demo failure: "I need some help here, guys; this thing isn't working" [calmly, with a smile]
- After Flickr failure: "Well, I'm afraid Flickr isn't serving up photos on that one." Concluded: "All of this from your wide-screen: movies, TV shows, music, podcasts, photos from dot-Mac and — when they're serving up photos — Flickr!"

"Nobody expects perfection except you. Your audience will forgive a blooper as long as you get back on your feet."

### Encore — One More Thing

The Stanford commencement address (June 2005) is a Jobs presentation without slides. It demonstrates every technique applied to pure spoken storytelling:

- **Road map:** "Today I want to tell you three stories from my life. That's it. No big deal. Just three stories."
- **Rule of three:** "The first story is about connecting the dots... My second story is about love and loss... My third story is about death."
- **Anaphora:** "Your time is limited, so don't waste it living someone else's life. Don't be trapped by dogma. Don't let the noise of others' opinions drown out your own inner voice."
- **Headline repeated three times:** "Stay hungry, stay foolish."

"Jobs ends the speech with his headline, his key theme and advice — stay hungry, stay foolish. As we've discussed, Jobs repeated his key theme several times in a presentation. In this case, he repeats 'stay hungry, stay foolish' three times in the concluding paragraph."

## Slide Design Principles — Summary Table

| Principle | Jobs's Rule | What to Avoid |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| One idea per slide | One theme, one visual | Multiple points, cluttered layouts |
| Images over text | Photograph or strong visual | Bullet points and text-heavy slides |
| Headlines state conclusions | "The world's thinnest notebook" | Topic labels ("MacBook Air Features") |
| Typography | Simple, large, readable | Multiple fonts, inconsistent sizing |
| Colour | High contrast, limited palette | Many competing colours |
| Data | Numbers contextualised with analogies | Raw figures without comparison |
| Words | Short, vivid, plain English | Jargon, technical specifications |
| Slide count | As few as needed | More slides as a sign of thoroughness |

## Preparation Methodology — Summary

| Phase | Activity | Time investment |
|-------|----------|----------------|
| Research and collect input | Facts, market data, customer stories | ~27 hours per hour of presentation |

## Client Coaching Notes

### For a client who has never presented to investors

**Start here — the mindset shift:**
Tell your client: "You are not presenting a business plan. You are telling a story about a problem that matters, and why your solution is the only one worth backing." Everything else follows from this.

**Session 1: The headline (30 minutes)**
- Ask: "Describe your business in one sentence, as if you were texting a friend who is not in your industry."
- Keep refining until it is under fifteen words and includes a specific benefit.
- If they cannot do this, the business case is not yet clear enough to present.

**Session 2: The villain (45 minutes)**
- Ask: "Who suffers because this problem exists? What does a bad day look like for them?"
- Write this in the customer's language, not the founder's language.
- Test: Would a bank loan officer, reading this for the first time, immediately understand why the loan is needed?

**Session 3: The numbers (45 minutes)**
- List every number in the presentation.
- For each: "What familiar thing can we compare this to?" Do not allow raw figures to stand alone.
- Practice saying each number out loud with its context, until it sounds natural.

**Session 4: Physical delivery (60 minutes)**
- Record the presenter on a phone camera.
- Watch it back — specifically looking for: reading from notes, lack of eye contact, arms crossed, monotone delivery.
- Identify the one worst habit and fix it first.
- Rehearse the opening minute until it is completely natural.

**Session 5: The wow moment**
- Ask: "What is the one thing you want them to be talking about at dinner tonight, after the meeting?"
- Build that into the presentation deliberately.
- Rehearse the sixty seconds before and after the wow moment until the timing is precise.

**Five rules to give a client on the day:**
1. Know your opening three sentences by heart — no notes, no hesitation.
2. Slow down when you say the most important number. Pause after it.
3. Never apologise for your slides, your nerves, or your preparation.
4. If something goes wrong, smile, acknowledge it briefly, move on.
5. End by repeating your headline and stating your ask clearly.

**On eye contact in an East African cultural context:**
In some East African contexts, sustained eye contact with an authority figure (a senior banker, a DFI representative, a government official) can feel uncomfortable or presumptuous. Gallo's principle still applies — the *overall impression* is what matters. Maintain warm, engaged eye contact that feels natural to you. Avoiding eye contact entirely signals nervousness and low confidence; the goal is composed, respectful directness, not a staring contest. Practising in front of a mirror or with a trusted colleague calibrates this quickly.

**On the language of passion in a Ugandan business context:**
Ugandan presenters sometimes understate to appear humble. The Jobs principle inverted: understated passion reads as uncertainty to investors. You are allowed — required — to believe in your own business. "I'm excited about this because" is not boasting; it is signalling conviction. Investors fund conviction.

**On humour:**
Jobs's humour (the OS 9 funeral, the "hell froze over" slide) worked because it was carefully scripted and absolutely on-brand. Unscripted humour in a bank presentation is high-risk. Planned, warm, self-deprecating humour that shows confidence is low-risk and effective. Safe examples: acknowledging a tough market context with equanimity; a brief story about a setback and what you learned. Avoid: jokes about competitors, about the bank, or about regulators.

## The Single Most Transformative Technique for an East African Entrepreneur Presenting to a Bank or DFI

**Dressing up numbers** (Scene 9) is the single most impactful technique for a Ugandan entrepreneur in a bank or DFI presentation.

Here is why: Loan officers and DFI analysts see hundreds of business plans. They are skilled at reading financial tables. What they are *not* skilled at filtering is a mountain of raw figures presented without context. The entrepreneur who walks in and says "Our projected revenue is UGX 840 million in Year 3" sounds identical to every other applicant.

The entrepreneur who says: "Uganda loses over UGX 1.2 trillion in post-harvest maize spoilage every year — that is roughly the equivalent of building two Mulago referral hospitals and walking away. Our business captures 2% of that loss, which is our entire Year 3 revenue target" — that entrepreneur is remembered.

The technique requires:
1. Finding the vivid comparison (a building, a salary, a distance, a familiar quantity)
2. Making the number specific and contextual, not just big
3. Connecting the number to the audience's existing framework of reference (a banker understands collateral coverage ratios; a DFI understands employment multipliers; an impact investor understands beneficiary counts)

This is a learnable skill that can be coached in a single session and applied immediately. It requires no design software, no rehearsal, and no presentation experience. A founder who has never presented before can deploy it in the opening two minutes of any meeting.
