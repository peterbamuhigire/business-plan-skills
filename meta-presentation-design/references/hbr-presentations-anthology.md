# HBR Presentations — Practical Standards for Business Presenters

## Note on Book Structure

This is **not** a multi-author anthology. It is a single cohesive guide in the HBR 20-Minute Manager Series (2014, 63 pages). The content is drawn from two primary HBR sources:
- *Harvard ManageMentor* (Harvard Business Publishing, 2002)
- *Pocket Mentor: Giving Presentations* (Harvard Business School Press, 2007)

The book is organised into nine sequential chapters plus a curated further-reading section. Each chapter is treated below as a distinct section. The further-reading recommendations (articles and books cited) are documented separately at the end — they contain additional research findings that extend the book's practical content.

## Chapter 1: The Key to Presenting

**Core insight:** Effective presentation rests on three pillars — knowing your goals, knowing your audience, and knowing yourself. These three elements underpin every other principle in the guide.

**Applicable presentation types covered in this guide:**
- Status reports
- Product demonstrations
- Sales pitches
- Strategy rollouts
- Any presentation designed to inform, persuade, or sell

---

## Chapter 2: Why Give a Presentation?

**Core question before any presentation:** Is a presentation actually the right medium?

**Common presentation purposes:**
- Explaining new data
- Soliciting ideas or feedback to build consensus
- Asking people to take action
- Seeking help solving a problem
- Getting buy-in on an initiative

**Decision filter:** Write your purpose in a phrase as short as the examples above. Then ask: is a presentation the best way to achieve this?

| Situation | Better alternative to a presentation |
|---|---|
| Small number of people to reach | Face-to-face individual conversations |
| Large audience, simple message, no feedback needed | Email |
| Complex argument requiring visual data | Presentation justified |
| Buy-in from key stakeholders | Presentation plus follow-up one-on-ones |

**Business plan application:** Before pitching a business plan, confirm the investor or lender actually wants a live presentation — some funders prefer a written proposal reviewed in advance, then a shorter Q&A session.

## Chapter 3: Define Your Goal

### The Three-Layer Goal Framework

**Layer 1 — Broad objective**
Write one sentence beginning with "I want" that includes the audience and the desired change. Examples:
- "I want to inform my department about the new process for proposing product ideas."
- "I want to get other managers to sign on to recommendations I've developed for top executives."
- "I want to give people in my group the tools they need to reach their targets this quarter."

Keep it short enough that anyone can understand it without reading twice.

**Layer 2 — Desired outcomes**
Get specific about results, not content. For each objective, list 2–4 concrete outcomes. Examples for a new-system pitch:
- Audience identifies ways to use the system in daily work
- First step toward implementation taken within one week
- Obstacles identified and reported back within 10 days

Fewer outcomes are usually better — they tighten focus and avoid over-promising.

**Layer 3 — Measurement of success**
Decide in advance how you will know the goal was met:
- Ideas generated by the audience during the session
- Tasks completed by a deadline
- "Soft" data: enthusiasm, expressed buy-in
- Feedback form or brief online survey
- Handout that lists deliverables — physical takeaway that audience can carry away

**Business plan application:** For a bank loan presentation, define three layers:
1. Broad objective: "I want the credit committee to approve a UGX 150M term loan."
2. Desired outcomes: approval in principle; agreement on collateral structure; next-steps meeting booked.
3. Measurement: written term sheet issued within 14 days.

## Chapter 4: Know Your Audience

### The 9-Question Audience Analysis Framework

Before preparing content, answer all applicable questions:

1. **Size and absences** — How many people? Who will be absent? Absent stakeholders still matter; plan follow-up with them.

2. **Roles and accountabilities** — What are their responsibilities? Why does your message matter to them? How does it make their lives easier?

3. **Knowledge level** — What do they already know? What do they need to know? Avoid stating the obvious; provide enough background to track the argument.

4. **Existing assumptions** — Which assumptions are correct, which incorrect? If a misconception needs correcting, do it gently. Example: if the audience thinks a new system will take too long to learn, explain the training and support plan clearly.

5. **Relationship with presenter** — Do they know you? If not, establish rapport early. An anecdote about shared frustration with the current situation is effective.

6. **Conflicting goals among attendees** — Acknowledge conflicts upfront; explain how your proposal may help across them.

7. **Presentation style they are accustomed to** — What has worked before (data, demos, personal stories)? Speaking to number-crunchers? Open with a relatable story, then give them the numbers.

8. **Attendance status** — Are they required to be there? Anticipate apathy or hostility and plan to overcome it.

9. **Accountability after the presentation** — Who will hold attendees accountable? Align with their managers so your goals and deliverables are consistent with theirs.

**Key mindset principle:** The audience, not the presenter, is the heart of any presentation. Put yourself inside their heads. Imagine sitting in the room witnessing what you are about to say.

**Business plan / investor application:**
- Investor audience: driven by return on investment, risk mitigation, exit options — anchor every point to financial outcomes
- Bank credit committee: driven by DSCR, collateral coverage, repayment track record — lead with security and cash flow
- Grant panel: driven by social impact, alignment with programme objectives, value for money — lead with the problem and evidence of need

## Chapter 5: Craft Your Message

### The 3-Step Message Architecture

**Step 1 — Define your core message**
One memorable point the audience takes away. Test it: Does it align with your objective? Does it align with desired outcomes? If not, revise before proceeding. Every element of the presentation should advance this single core message. Anything that does not support it may weaken it.

**Step 2 — Identify relevant arguments and data**
- Back every assertion with well-chosen facts and data. Unsupported statements are opinions; opinions alone do not move audiences.
- Include *only* data that will persuade. Extraneous detail distracts from what you want people to remember.
- Do not focus only on data — connect emotionally too. Be explicit about why the audience should care. Tap into the pain or frustration of the status quo.

After gathering material, self-check:
- Can everyone follow it, or do some people need background?
- Will the supporting content actually persuade this audience?
- Are you showing people why this matters to them?
- Is the journey engaging?

**Step 3 — Organise the content**
Logical sequence is essential. Structure the argument so each point leads naturally to the next.

### The Standard 4-Part Presentation Structure

**Part 1 — Opening (hook)**
Purpose: get attention, establish rapport, define the presentation's purpose.
A strong opening does five things:
1. Hooks the audience (story, provocative question, show of hands, startling statistic)
2. Defines the purpose of the presentation
3. Highlights what is in it for the audience
4. Confirms the presenter's credibility
5. Previews the main points briefly

**Part 2 — The need or problem**
Keep this tight so that ample time remains for the solution.
An effective problem statement:
- Spells out the main challenge
- Shows how it directly affects the audience
- Has a sense of urgency
- Reflects input from others (surveys, data, field research)
- Incorporates relevant arguments and examples without distracting from the central point

**Part 3 — The solution**
Show how the problem is solved or the need met. Effective solution presentation:
- Helps the audience visualise the benefits
- Phrases the solution in terms of *their* needs, not yours
- Uses a story where possible to illustrate
- Involves the audience in developing a path forward
- Ensures the strength of the solution matches the scale of the challenge

**Part 4 — Call to action**
A strong wrap-up contains:
1. Reiteration of the challenge and the solution
2. Specific recommended action
3. Commitment or buy-in obtained
4. Clear assignments if appropriate
5. Explanation of post-presentation follow-up

### Getting Audience Input During the Presentation

- Empty questions to the room ("Is everyone following?") do not work — polite people will nod without engaging
- Address individuals directly: "Does that seem like the biggest problem, Maria, given your front-line experience?"
- When people feel their ideas are heard, they are more open to the presenter's message (the "pleasure principle")

## Chapter 6: Identify Your Resources

### Pre-Presentation Resource Audit

**Venue assessment:**
- Visit in advance if possible
- Note standing position and maximum distance to remote audience member
- Assess whether a microphone is needed
- Check seating — standing audiences leave early

**Time management:**
- Factor in audience participation when budgeting time
- Time guidelines:
  - Speak just long enough to convey the key message clearly
  - Fewer points made well beats more points made poorly
  - If a point cannot be made clear or acceptable within the available time, omit it or save it
  - Ending early is better than rushing or running over
  - Plan in advance what to cut if time is shortened

**Equipment checklist:**
- Confirm all tools needed: paper, whiteboards, projectors, laser pointer, video conferencing
- Secure anything not already in the room
- Verify network passwords and log-ins before the day
- Test all technology before the presentation starts — a delay caused by equipment failure unsettles the presenter and irritates the audience

## Chapter 7: Plan the Visuals

### Research-Backed Findings on Visual Learning

Three findings cited (source credited to underlying research on learning modalities):
- People learn 75% of what they know visually, 13% through hearing, 12% through other senses
- A picture is three times as effective at conveying information as words alone
- Words and pictures together are six times as effective as words alone

**Implication:** Visuals are not optional decoration — they are the primary learning channel. Design them to be processed quickly, because while the audience is looking at a visual they are not looking at the presenter.

### Uses of Visual Aids
- Keeping the audience engaged
- Helping people remember facts
- Clarifying ideas, relationships, and physical layouts
- Signalling transitions between topics

### Visual Failure Modes (What to Avoid)

"Death by PowerPoint" traps:
- Too many slides
- Complex, confusing visuals (flowcharts with many boxes, arrows, and feedback loops)
- Slides packed border-to-border with dense text or too many images
- Reading text from slides aloud — the audience can read faster than the presenter can speak

### Visual Design Principles (What to Do)

Slide/page design:
- Use graphics, icons, and symbols to reinforce concepts
- Use key words, not full sentences
- Maximum one concept per slide
- Maximum six lines of text per slide or page
- Maximum three to six ideas per flip chart or whiteboard display
- Use colour where possible, but not excessively
- Use pictures where feasible
- Use bullets (not numbers) for non-sequential items
- Reserve ALL CAPS for titles and acronyms only

Media selection criteria:
- Flexibility
- Cost
- Appropriateness to the presentation context
- Presenter's comfort level with the format or technology
- **Handouts: do not distribute during the presentation** — they divert attention from the speaker

## Chapter 8: Practice Your Delivery

### Rehearsal Methods (Ranked by Effectiveness)

1. **Live test audience** — best. Include people similar to the real audience in terms of roles, assumptions, and expertise. Interact as you would with the real audience.
2. **Audio or video recording** — identify flaws not apparent from notes review alone. Video is especially useful for spotting delivery problems.
3. **Aloud in front of a mirror** — useful for hearing your own voice; limited because it is hard to deliver and assess simultaneously.

### Rehearsal Checklist
- Practice with the actual equipment to be used
- Use visual aids as reinforcement, not as the speech itself
- Know content well enough not to read from notes — but do not script word-for-word (it sounds memorised)
- Rehearse the entire presentation end-to-end to test flow and timing
- Use an expressive, conversational voice throughout
- Avoid jargon and terms the audience may not know
- Rehearse responses to likely questions — focus on tone, not just content
- Ask yourself: "What is the worst that could happen?" Plan how to recover from that scenario
- If possible, rehearse in the same room where the presentation will be given

### Mental Preparation Techniques
These are applied immediately before the presentation:
- Visualise giving a successful presentation
- Repeat positive affirmations ("I am relaxed and ready")
- Use deep breathing and tension-relieving exercises
- Wear clothing that feels comfortable, confident, and professional
- Accept nervousness as natural — do not counteract it with food, caffeine, drugs, or alcohol
- Interact with audience members as they arrive in the room (reduces social anxiety and builds rapport before the formal opening)

## Chapter 9: Deliver Your Presentation

### Delivery Principles

**Voice and pace:**
- Retain the expressive, conversational tone from rehearsal
- Speak at a moderate pace — too fast sounds nervous; too slow bores
- Ensure everyone can hear; test microphone and sound system before starting
- Avoid hedging language ("kind of," "sort of") — it dilutes statements
- Take deliberate pauses — reduces filler language ("um," "er") and helps the presenter relax

**Physical presence:**
- Make eye contact with audience members
- Watch the audience for nonverbal cues
- Use facial expressions to convey interest in audience reactions

### Audience Re-engagement Techniques

For inattentive audience members (identifiable by crossed legs, fidgeting, looking around):
1. Change what you are doing — pause, alter tone, shift position
2. Survey the audience with a direct, specific question ("How many of you believe our customers are satisfied? Let's see a show of hands")
3. Add appropriate humour — audiences welcome comic relief
4. Provide analogies and vivid examples
5. Introduce personal stories
6. Return explicitly to audience relevance ("Here's what that last point means for you and your team")

### Flexibility and Adapting in the Room

- Treat unexpected developments as opportunities, not threats
- A presenter who rigidly adheres to plan despite changed circumstances is perceived as having a "tin ear" or a flawed message
- When a curveball question arises, view it as a chance to strengthen the case — share additional data or offer to research the answer
- Technical or logistical problems: handle with good humour. A joke in the face of a glitch demonstrates confidence and shows the message is more important than the medium

## Chapter 10: Manage the Response

### Q&A Timing Options

**Option A — Questions after the presentation:**
- Allows completion of the talk within the specified time
- Ensures the audience has the complete picture before questions begin
- Best practice when following:
  - Make the transition to Q&A explicit
  - State how much time remains for Q&A
  - Maintain control: repeat each question and direct the answer to the whole group, not only the questioner

**Option B — Questions throughout:**
- Keeps audience engagement higher
- Requires pre-planned "pause points" in notes
- Tell the audience explicitly why you are pausing — focuses their questions
- Keep responses brief to maintain narrative momentum

### Handling Tough Questions: 4-Step Protocol

Applicable when presenting strategic plans, proposed changes, difficult news (redundancies, cost increases, regulatory changes):

**Step 1 — Anticipate**
Before the presentation: write down every objection you can anticipate. Brainstorm responses. Run responses by a trusted colleague to ensure you are giving objections a fair hearing.

**Step 2 — Be gracious, not defensive**
Show curiosity and enthusiasm in tone and content. Responses like "That's a great question, and here's why..." signal that the audience's perspective has been considered. Be open to exploring options you had not previously considered.

**Step 3 — Know your scope**
If questions go beyond the scope of the presentation, say so. Audiences accept reasonable boundaries.

**Step 4 — Answer honestly**
- When you know the answer: state it clearly and briefly
- When you do not know: admit it; direct the questioner to another source or offer to research it
- Write down unanswered questions visibly — demonstrates commitment to follow up

### Outlining Next Steps at the End
- Specify how and to whom audience members should give feedback
- Explain how deliverables should be transmitted
- Offer support for implementing ideas
- Confirm any follow-up presentation or event
- Emphasise genuine interest in continuing the conversation
- When sharing contact information, commit to responding
- Talk to attendees as they leave — do not let one or two people monopolise time

## Chapter 11: Debrief Your Presentation

### Critiquing Content (within 1–2 hours of presenting)

Make a list of all flaws discovered during delivery:
- Small: a slide that could not be read from the back of the room
- Larger: a missing step in a process, a data gap

Incorporate corrections into master files within days, not weeks. Reconstructing problems months later yields worse results.

### Analysing Performance

Delivery problem categories:

| Category | Examples | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Minor, immediately visible | Weak opening, failed humour attempt | Specific adjustment for next time |
| Technical | Equipment failure | Learn the technology; designate an assistant |
| Chronic | General nervousness, clinging to notes | Professional coaching |

Video recording of the event is the most effective way to identify delivery problems.

### Getting Feedback from Colleagues

- Set up one-on-one meetings — do not ask "How did I do?" in a corridor (generates polite, unhelpful responses)
- Ask for frank reactions to both content and delivery
- Keep feedback meetings brief and informal but specific

## Chapter 12: Follow Up with the Audience

A presentation is not an isolated event — it is part of a longer relationship with the audience. Follow-up actions:

- Keep all promises made during the presentation (data, tables, documents promised to specific individuals)
- Correct any errors that came to light
- Follow up on explicit directives given
- Send thank-you notes to key attendees
- Email the whole audience to reinforce the takeaway message (and place your contact address in their inboxes)
- Make yourself available for post-presentation questions
- Book "next steps" meetings to drive implementation
- Give the same or similar presentation to other groups who need the message

**Principle:** If you want to persuade people for the long term, not just during the presentation itself, show them you understand the big picture they share. Audiences are more likely to act when they believe the presenter sees beyond the room.

## Research-Backed Principles (from Book and Cited Sources)

### Learning and Retention
- People learn 75% visually, 13% aurally, 12% through other senses (cited in the visual design chapter)
- Pictures are 3× more effective at conveying information than words alone
- Words + pictures are 6× more effective than words alone
- Studies cited in the further reading show audiences remember only 10%–30% of presentation content (Morgan, *Give Your Speech, Change the World*)

### Audience Engagement
- Empty questions to the whole room are ineffective — most audiences will simply nod politely
- Direct questions to named individuals are significantly more effective at generating genuine engagement
- The "pleasure principle": when audience members feel their voices and ideas are being heard, they become more open to the presenter's message
- Inattentive audience members are identifiable by physical cues: crossed legs, fidgeting, unusual amounts of looking around

### Authenticity and Body Language (from Nick Morgan, cited in further reading)
- Recent brain research shows that natural, unstudied gestures express emotions or impulses a split second before thought processes turn them into words
- Therefore, practised gestures will always be slightly mistimed — detectable by listeners' unconscious ability to read body language
- Implication: you cannot rehearse your way to authenticity through gesture practice alone
- Solution: tap into four underlying impulses — openness to the audience, connection with the audience, passion for the content, and listening to the audience's response — and rehearse with each in mind
- The body language will then take care of itself

### The Kinesthetic Connection (from Nick Morgan)
- A good speech connects aurally (dramatic stories, pacing), visually (slides, clips), and kinesthetically (physical presence, use of space)
- Most presenters achieve one or two of these but not all three
- The kinesthetic speaker feeds the audience's primal need to experience a presentation on a physical as well as intellectual level
- Technique: use "audience proxies" — individuals in the crowd who serve as representatives for the rest; make eye contact and physically orient toward them
- A presentation is the best vehicle available to executives for winning trust from large groups — employees, colleagues, or shareholders

### Pitching and Idea Selling (from Elsbach, "How to Pitch a Brilliant Idea")
- Hollywood pitch research extended to business settings (product design, marketing, venture capital)
- The "catcher" (decision-maker) evaluates the pitcher's perceived creativity as well as the idea itself
- Impression of the pitcher's ability to generate workable ideas can permanently overshadow assessment of the idea's own merit
- Catchers respond well when they believe they are participating in the idea's development
- Successful pitchers are perceived as one of three types: showrunners (smooth, professional), artists (quirky, unpolished), or neophytes (inexperienced but earnest)
- Tactic: give the catcher a chance to contribute to the idea — "pull back and project what he needs onto your idea in order to make the story whole for him" (Oliver Stone, cited in Elsbach)

### Storytelling Science (from Denning and Guber, cited in further reading)
**Denning's storytelling framework ("Telling Tales"):**
- A carefully chosen story translates abstract concepts into meaningful mandates
- For motivating action when people are not naturally inclined to act: use light-detail stories; particulars bog down listeners and divert attention from the message
- Seven story types for different organisational purposes (detailed in the HBR article)
- Form should follow function: the type of story chosen must match the purpose

**Guber's Four Truths of the Storyteller:**
1. True to the teller — embodies the speaker's deepest values and conveys them with candour
2. True to the audience — delivers on the promise that the story is worth their time; acknowledges listeners' needs and involves them in the narrative
3. True to the moment — matches the context appropriately; flexible enough for improvisation
4. True to the mission — conveys the teller's passion and enlists support for the worthy endeavour

## Financial Data Presentation: Best Practices for Investors and Boards

Drawn from the HBR guide's principles applied to financial and data-heavy contexts:

### Structuring Financial Data Slides

1. **One concept per slide** — never combine multiple financial metrics on a single slide. Do not show P&L, balance sheet, and cash flow on the same page.
2. **Headlines, not labels** — slide titles should be conclusions, not category labels. "Revenue grows 34% YoY driven by product mix" is better than "Revenue Analysis."
3. **Strip visual noise** — remove grid lines, data labels, legends, and decimal places that do not drive understanding. Your audience should grasp the chart in under five seconds.
4. **Anchor to the core message** — every financial slide should answer "so what?" explicitly. Include a one-line takeaway below each chart.
5. **Six lines maximum** — if a financial table has more than six rows of data that require active reading, split it across multiple slides or put the detail in an appendix.

### Presenting Projections
- State assumptions explicitly — undeclared assumptions undermine credibility with analytical audiences
- Distinguish clearly between historical data (solid lines, filled bars) and projected data (dotted lines, unfilled bars)
- Show the range of outcomes, not just the base case — investors expect sensitivity analysis
- Anticipate the "what if" questions: pre-prepare a back-pocket slide with stress-test scenarios

### Addressing a Mixed Audience (Analytical + Executive)
The guide's audience analysis framework applied to board/investor settings:
- Open with the narrative conclusion (for executives)
- Back it with one or two anchor charts showing the key trend (for both)
- Put detailed tables in handouts or appendices (for analysts)
- Never read the numbers aloud — point to them and interpret them

### Pre-empting Financial Questions
Apply the tough-question protocol:
1. Anticipate every likely financial objection in advance (debt serviceability, gross margin assumptions, payback period, collateral shortfall)
2. Prepare a concise, honest response for each
3. If the question goes beyond the scope of the presentation (e.g. a specific supplier contract), say so and offer to follow up with the specific information

## Presenting Bad News and Difficult Findings

The guide addresses this context directly (strategic plan rollouts, explaining redundancies):

### Principles for Difficult Presentations

1. **Lead with the need, not the conclusion** — frame the problem fully before presenting the difficult news. The audience should feel the urgency before they hear the answer.
2. **Anticipate resistance systematically** — write down every objection before the presentation; do not attempt to improvise responses to predictable pushback.
3. **Be gracious, not defensive** — defensive responses signal that the presenter has not genuinely considered the audience's perspective.
4. **Do not try to be all things to all people** — difficult presentations generate scope-expanding questions; maintain focus and explicitly acknowledge the limits of what you can address.
5. **Honesty is non-negotiable** — admit what you do not know. Write down unanswered questions visibly during the session. Follow up.
6. **Call to action still required** — even a difficult presentation needs a clear next step. Audiences experiencing uncertainty need specific direction.

### Linguistic Style and Gender (from Tannen, "The Power of Talk")
- Most managerial work happens through talk; managers are evaluated through their talk as much as their actions
- Conversational style often overrides content — it affects who gets heard, who gets credit, what gets done
- Women's linguistic styles can undermine them: making them appear less competent or confident than they are even when the content is strong
- Implication for business plan presentations: awareness of linguistic style is as important as preparation of content

## Business Plan Presentation Checklist

Synthesising the HBR guide's complete framework for a business plan pitch or investor presentation:

### Before You Begin
- [ ] Written purpose statement in one short phrase
- [ ] Confirmed that a live presentation is the right medium (vs. written proposal + Q&A)
- [ ] Broad objective written as an "I want" sentence including the audience
- [ ] 2–4 desired outcomes listed (specific, measurable)
- [ ] Success metrics defined in advance

### Audience Analysis (9 Questions)
- [ ] Group size confirmed; absent stakeholders identified for follow-up
- [ ] Audience roles and accountabilities understood
- [ ] Audience knowledge level assessed (what they know vs. what they need)
- [ ] Pre-existing assumptions and misconceptions identified
- [ ] Presenter's relationship with audience mapped; rapport strategy defined
- [ ] Conflicting goals among attendees identified
- [ ] Presentation style they are accustomed to assessed
- [ ] Whether attendance is voluntary or required considered
- [ ] Post-presentation accountability aligned with attendees' managers

### Message Architecture
- [ ] Single core message defined (what do they take away?)
- [ ] Every slide or section tested: does it advance the core message?
- [ ] Data and evidence gathered for each assertion
- [ ] Emotional connection point identified (why should they care?)
- [ ] Extraneous detail removed

### Structure (4-Part Framework)
- [ ] Opening hook selected (story, question, statistic, show of hands)
- [ ] Opening does 5 things: hook, purpose, WIIFM, credibility, preview
- [ ] Problem/need stated with urgency and audience relevance
- [ ] Solution framed in terms of audience needs, not presenter's features
- [ ] Call to action is specific (not vague), with assignments if needed

### Visual Design
- [ ] One concept per slide
- [ ] Maximum 6 lines of text per slide
- [ ] No slide reads aloud the same words the presenter will say
- [ ] Charts have conclusion headlines, not category labels
- [ ] Colour used purposefully, not excessively
- [ ] Handouts prepared separately; not distributed during presentation
- [ ] All slides readable from back of room

### Resource and Logistics
- [ ] Venue visited; seating, sight lines, and acoustics confirmed
- [ ] Time budget set; cuts planned in advance for time compression
- [ ] All equipment tested; network passwords verified
- [ ] Backup plan for technology failure

### Rehearsal
- [ ] Full run-through completed with test audience or recording
- [ ] Rehearsed with actual equipment
- [ ] Timed against allocated slot
- [ ] Visual aids used as reinforcement, not as the speech
- [ ] Tone is expressive and conversational, not read or memorised
- [ ] Jargon removed or defined
- [ ] Responses to likely tough questions rehearsed

### Day-Of Delivery
- [ ] Deep breathing and mental preparation done
- [ ] Arrived early; interacted with audience members on entry
- [ ] Sound system and equipment tested before audience is seated
- [ ] Opening hook delivered confidently without notes
- [ ] Eye contact maintained throughout
- [ ] Pace is moderate; pauses are deliberate
- [ ] Hedging language ("sort of," "kind of") avoided
- [ ] Flexibility maintained — unexpected developments treated as opportunities

### Q&A Management
- [ ] Q&A format declared at start (after presentation / throughout)
- [ ] Each question repeated to the whole room before answering
- [ ] Tough questions anticipated with prepared responses
- [ ] Scope of presentation enforced gracefully for out-of-scope questions
- [ ] Unanswered questions written down visibly
- [ ] Next steps outlined at close

### Follow-Up
- [ ] Promises made during presentation tracked and fulfilled
- [ ] Thank-you notes sent to key attendees
- [ ] Email to full audience reinforcing takeaway message
- [ ] "Next steps" meetings booked
- [ ] Debrief completed within 1–2 hours; corrections to master files within days

## Connections to Other Presentation Frameworks

### HBR Presentations and Nancy Duarte (*HBR Guide to Persuasive Presentations*, 2012)
The HBR 20-Minute Manager guide explicitly credits Duarte for the "Before/After" visual declutter example and recommends her book in the further reading. Points of alignment:
- Both emphasise the audience, not the presenter, as the centre of the presentation
- Both advocate ruthless simplicity in visual design
- Both use a structured narrative arc (opening → problem → solution → call to action) that parallels Duarte's "What Is / What Could Be" model
- Duarte's work provides the deeper theoretical and design-systems framework; the HBR guide provides the operational checklist equivalent

### HBR Presentations and Pich Anything (Klaff, 2011)
The HBR guide's findings on idea-pitching (Elsbach research) converge with Klaff's framing methodology:
- Both note that the decision-maker evaluates the pitcher, not just the pitch
- Elsbach's "catcher participation" principle parallels Klaff's concept of giving the audience a "frame" they can inhabit
- Klaff focuses on primal brain dynamics; Elsbach focuses on perceived creativity of the pitcher — complementary, not contradictory

### HBR Presentations and Carmine Gallo
Nick Morgan's "kinesthetic connection" and "four impulses to authenticity" (cited in HBR further reading) align with Gallo's emphasis on passion and physical presence in *Talk Like TED* and *The Presentation Secrets of Steve Jobs*:
- Both argue that authenticity cannot be faked through practised technique alone
- Both emphasise that the speaker's physical presence creates an emotional bond that content alone cannot achieve
- Morgan provides the neurological mechanism (gesture timing); Gallo provides the practitioner examples
