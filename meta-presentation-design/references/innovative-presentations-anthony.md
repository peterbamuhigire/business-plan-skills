# Innovative Presentations — Anthony Framework

## 1. The "Innovative" vs Conventional Presentation Distinction

### What Makes a Presentation Innovative

Anthony defines an innovative presentation as one where **creative ideas are applied with technology** to achieve results far superior to those from conventional presentations. The two primary metrics are:

- **Effectiveness** — getting the best results, reaching or exceeding goals; closing more and bigger deals, winning approvals, galvanising commitment
- **Efficiency** — communicating more, and more clearly, in minimum time; avoiding the "text slide read by a presenter" trap which is the least efficient communication method

The modern presentation problem: "Most people design and give cookie-cutter presentations using templates and formats that haven't evolved in decades." PowerPoint is described as the "Model T of presentations" — technically improved but conceptually unchanged for twenty years.

### The Five Cs — Non-Negotiable Baseline

Every presentation must pass the **Five Cs test**:
1. **Clear** — unambiguous, no jargon without definition
2. **Concise** — respects the audience's time; never pad to fill time allocated
3. **Compelling** — creates urgency and desire to act
4. **Captivating** — holds attention throughout
5. **Convincing** — drives the audience to commit

### Innovative vs Good vs Average

| Level | Descriptors |
|-------|-------------|
| Average presenter | Articulate, prepared, organised |
| Good presenter | Adaptable, confident, enthusiastic, natural |
| Innovative presenter | Audacious, captivating, daring, engaging, mesmerising, symbolic, unconventional, unexpected, visionary |

The difference is not merely better content — it is a fundamentally different **experience** for the audience.

### The Innovation Spectrum: Mild to Wild

Anthony explicitly frames a spectrum from "mild creativity" to "revolutionary/daring." He advises:
- For conservative, buttoned-up audiences (bank credit committees, regulatory bodies) — stay toward the mild end but still differentiate
- For competitive situations where conventional presentations will not win — move toward the bold end
- **The entire reason for innovation is to significantly improve results**

## 2. The Four-Step Innovation Process

### Step 1 — Set Innovative Objectives
Analyse: audience needs and priorities, competitive situation, constraints, risk appetite. Decide how innovative the presentation needs to be to reach your goals. If you stand little chance of winning with a conventional approach, you have nothing to lose by being daring.

### Step 2 — Determine Ideal Characteristics
For a sales/funding pitch against strong competition: must be captivating, compelling, and ultra-credible. For an informational briefing: clear, concise, convincing may be sufficient.

### Step 3 — Mix Creative Ideas with Technology
Brainstorm which creative ideas and technologies best serve the characteristics identified. This is where analogies, props, video, 3-D animation, demonstrations, and unconventional delivery formats are selected and combined.

### Step 4 — Design and Deliver for Desired Outcomes
Combine content, visuals, handouts, and resources to meet the goals from Step 1. Plan and execute.

## 3. Audience Analysis — The Psychological Foundation

### The Four Personality Types (Director / Thinker / Energizer / Affable)

Understanding audience personality is described as wearing "the hat of a streetwise psychologist." You need to know what motivates, influences, and persuades each type.

**Director (Choleric)**
- Fast-paced decision maker; results-oriented; direct; authoritative
- Values: efficiency, bottom-line results, brief data-backed arguments
- Presenting to Directors: be direct, get to results quickly, avoid small talk, no waffle
- Risk for presenter: being too slow or too detailed

**Thinker (Melancholic)**
- Analytical; detail-oriented; systematic; risk-averse; evidence-driven
- Values: data, proof, logic, thoroughness, precision
- Presenting to Thinkers: provide detailed evidence, cite sources, anticipate every objection with facts
- Risk for presenter: being vague or glossing over technical weaknesses
- Relevance: **bank credit officers and financial analysts are predominantly Thinker types**

**Energizer (Sanguine)**
- Enthusiastic; creative; sociable; optimistic; easily distracted
- Values: ideas, big vision, enthusiasm, relationships, stories
- Presenting to Energizers: lead with vision and story, be energetic, use vivid visuals
- Relevance: **angel investors and venture capitalists are often Energizer or Director types**

**Affable (Phlegmatic)**
- Warm; relationship-oriented; risk-averse; consensus-building
- Values: trust, collaboration, social proof, testimonials, low conflict
- Presenting to Affables: build rapport first, use testimonials, emphasise people impact
- Relevance: **DFI programme officers and NGO funders often have Affable characteristics**

### Credibility Triangle: Like + Respect + Trust
Anthony's maxim for winning over any audience: "To get a sale (or commitment), you must get the audience to like, respect, and trust you." Garner any two of the three and the third follows.

- **Trust** = consistency, openness, honesty, sincerity without hidden agendas
- **Respect** = demonstrated competence, confidence, track record, preparation
- **Like** = warmth, approachability, personal rapport

### Researching Your Audience Before Presenting
- Visit their website to understand organisational tone
- Read executive bios
- Understand their current priorities and pressures
- Know their level of familiarity with your topic before defining terms or pitching at wrong level

## 4. Planning Your Winning Strategy

### The Seven-Step Planning Process
1. Identify your desired (hypothetical) goal
2. Analyse your audience and situation — who is competition? What are constraints?
3. Confirm or modify your goal based on what you learn
4. Create a strategy — encompassing persuasion, psychology, finance
5. Develop creative presentation format to serve that strategy
6. Rehearse to perfection
7. Execute and adapt in room

### Strategy vs Tactics
Strategy is the overall plan to achieve your goal. Tactics are the individual actions. Most presenters focus only on tactics (slide design, content) and neglect strategy (how to position themselves to win given the specific audience situation).

## 5. Content Development

### Priority Order Principle
Cover topics in **priority order** — most critical and valuable information first, not saved for a dramatic ending. Rationale: decision-makers may leave early; attention is highest at the beginning; priority items deserve the most discussion time.

Structure:
1. Most critical/highest-value information for the audience
2. Supporting details they need
3. Additional valuable but secondary information

### Three-Level Content Architecture (for time-variable situations)
- **Summary Level** — core messages only (if time cut to 15 minutes)
- **Planned Level** — full intended presentation
- **Extra Level** — additional depth for extended questions or highly engaged audience

### Content Types Anthony Recommends (15 categories)
1. **Facts** — with source citable on demand
2. **Examples** — concrete illustrations of abstract points
3. **References** — credible third-party citations; publications, research
4. **Stories/Anecdotes** — most remembered long-term; humanise data
5. **Statistics** — round numbers (say "5 out of 6" not "84.7%"); always have source
6. **Expert quotations** — relevant authorities; use local/cultural figures where possible
7. **Contrasts** — "before vs after"; "old vs new"; "with vs without"
8. **Comparisons** — show options and why you recommend one
9. **Demonstrations** — FAB (Feature, Advantage, Benefit) model
10. **Definitions** — never assume shared understanding
11. **Rhetorical questions** — create anticipation and suspense
12. **Explanations** — justify and contextualise
13. **Assumptions** — state explicitly to show rigour
14. **Testimonials** — short stories; problem → decision → result format; video testimonials powerful
15. **Analogies and metaphors** — the most memorable content tool

### The FAB Demonstration Model
- **Feature**: state the technical characteristic
- **Advantage**: explain what that characteristic does better
- **Benefit**: translate to what the audience gains — productivity, profit, reduced risk

## 6. Laws of Communication Impact

Anthony identifies seven laws that drive how information is received and remembered:

### Law 1 — Primacy (Opening)
"Information first in sequence has greater impact than anything that comes later." The first two minutes determine:
- Your credibility and image
- The audience's level of engagement for the rest of the presentation
- Whether people lean in or mentally check out

**Implication for business plan presentations**: Spend disproportionate time crafting and rehearsing your opening. Consider memorising the first two sentences.

### Law 2 — Emphasis and Intensity (Body)
Key points must **stand out from supporting information**. Techniques:
- Contrast (before/after, old/new, competitor/you)
- Voice intensity changes (sudden quiet or sudden loud)
- Humorous or surprising visuals placed at key moments
- Physical demonstrations at the moment of the claim
- Coloured text or visual callouts on slides
- Props deployed at the precise statement to reinforce it

The principle: a red dot among 100 black dots draws the eye immediately. Your key message must be the red dot.

### Law 3 — Exercise and Engagement (Interaction)
"People engaged and active learn more and are impacted more." Techniques:
- Pose questions — raise hands, respond verbally
- Invite volunteers for demonstrations
- Live technology interaction (tablets, polls)
- Ask for opinions: "What are your thoughts on that, [name]?"
- Move into and among the audience where room permits

### Law 4 — Interest (Relevance)
If the audience is not interested, they disengage regardless of presentation quality. Maintain interest by:
- Continuously tying content to their specific situation and priorities
- Explicitly saying how each section addresses their needs
- Using stories from their sector or circumstances
- Calling audience members by name

### Law 5 — Effect (Consequence)
State the cause (your proposal/solution) and then vividly describe the effect (the improved outcome the audience will experience). Used most powerfully in the call-to-action section.

### Law 6 — Recency (Closing)
"The last thing people hear is the first thing they remember." The conclusion is as critical as the opening. A weak ending destroys much of what came before.

### Law 7 — Repetition (Reinforcement)
Repeat key messages across the presentation using different words, different media, and different contexts. However, avoid "wearout" — over-repetition that causes the audience to tune out. The technique of 360-degree repetition (verbal, visual, handout, prop, and demonstration all reinforcing the same message) is the ideal.

## 7. Opening Strategies — The First 90 Seconds

### Building Blocks for Introductions (combine in any order)
1. Greeting
2. Theme statement
3. Team introductions
4. Housekeeping (goals, agenda, timing, handouts)
5. Confirm findings (restate what you learned from pre-meeting research)
6. Executive summary (preview of key messages and benefits)
7. Quotation
8. Media reference
9. Remarkable/startling facts or statistics
10. Questions (rhetorical or audience-response)
11. Props
12. Demonstration
13. Humour
14. Story/anecdote
15. Transition to body

### Mild-to-Wild Opening Scenarios

**Mild — Opening with a 30-second proof video**: Two presenters skip biographical introductions and immediately show a 30-second video with superimposed real statistics from 70 customers: "30% savings, 25% productivity increase, 50% ROI, 4-month payback." Then: "Those are real numbers. Now let's discuss how your company achieves them."

**Moderate — Opening with a story**: Begin "Let me tell you a story" — recount a 5-minute narrative about three organisations facing similar challenges to the audience. Stop before the ending to create suspense. Return to the story resolution at the close.

**Bold — Confirm findings / Executive summary opener**: Demonstrate you researched the client. "You told us you need to: (1) streamline workflow; (2) improve productivity; (3) reduce error rate. Have we accurately summarised your needs?" Then immediately: "Based on those needs, our solution will deliver [specific result 1], [specific result 2], [specific result 3]."

**Wild — The blindfold opening**: CEO introduced while blindfolded, gropes for microphone, then begins: "A tsunami of data covers the world... we are drowning in data but dying of thirst for information... We wear blindfolds that prevent us from seeing the rich sea of information within our own data." Removes blindfold, shows 20/20 vision eye chart. Then at the close — gives everyone a blindfold with key messages printed on it, throws foam balls for fun exercise.

### The "Confirm Findings" Technique (critical for funding pitches)
Open by restating your pre-meeting research: "You told us you need X, Y, Z. Have we accurately summarised your priorities?" This technique:
- Demonstrates you did your homework
- Immediately shows you are presenting a solution to their real problem, not a generic pitch
- Invites correction and dialogue before you invest 30 minutes going in the wrong direction
- Builds credibility as a systematic, thorough professional

## 8. Structuring the Body for Maximum Impact

### The "Navigate, Don't Sequence" Principle
For sales and funding presentations (unlike conference talks), build your content to be **navigable not just sequential**. Executives will ask you to jump back to earlier slides or skip ahead. Prepare hyperlinks in your slides to hidden backup slides with deeper data, diagrams, or video to answer questions without disrupting the main flow.

### Keeping Audiences Engaged — Practical Techniques
- Change the nature of what you are doing every 10 minutes (slide → demonstration → prop → story → audience question)
- Move physically — present from different positions in the room
- Call audience members by name
- Transmit material to audience tablets for interactive moments
- Transition between digital slides and physical presentation board for variety

### Handling Sceptical or Analytical Audiences
Thinker-type audiences (bank credit officers, DFI analysts) require:
- More data, not less — but organised clearly
- Source citations for every key statistic
- Acknowledgement of risks and weaknesses (then mitigation)
- Precise numbers (but rounded: "1 in 6" not "16.7%")
- Logical structure — problem → evidence → solution → proof
- Anticipate their likely objections and pre-answer them in the presentation itself
- Hide backup slides with full technical detail accessible on demand

## 9. Closing Techniques That Drive Action

### The Conclusion Building Blocks
1. Theme (callback to opening theme)
2. Quotation
3. Challenge
4. Executive summary (3–5 key points maximum)
5. Humour
6. News/media reference
7. Remarkable/startling facts saved for last
8. Thought-provoking questions
9. Multimedia finale
10. Story/anecdote resolution
11. **Call to action** (always specific: "I am asking you to do X by Y date")

### The Law of Recency in Practice
"Your conclusion — the last thing they heard — is the first thing they'll remember."

Three tested conclusion types:

**Repeating a theme with a twist** (blindfold CEO example): Close by giving everyone a physical object that embeds the key message. The CEO gave branded blindfolds and had an audience activity, then distributed three gifts. Standing ovation + business.

**Leaving them smiling**: After a precise technical summary, the account manager produces a magician who performs a theatrical trick — cake multiplication — that perfectly demonstrates the product's precision capability. The final impression is professionalism + delight.

**Offering impressive incentives**: In competitive bid situations, the construction firm ended by unveiling a surprise $1 million "incentive bonus" to enhance the lakefront, physically handing out oversized replica notes. Risk-taking, memorable, won the deal.

### Signals That the End is Near
- Verbal: "In conclusion..." / "Let me leave you with..."
- Visual: callback to opening image or theme
- Energy shift: increase pace and conviction slightly
- Never: trail off, apologise for time, or repeat "one last thing" multiple times

## 10. Visual Design Principles

### The Fundamental Shift Anthony Advocates
Old model: bulleted text slides read by presenter.
New model: each idea gets its own **single striking visual** — photo, illustration, 3-D animation, or diagram. The presenter's spoken words carry the argument; the visual supports and reinforces it.

### Key Visual Design Rules
- **One idea per visual** — if you used to have 5 bullets, make 5 visuals
- **Visual metaphors** work powerfully — a labyrinth to represent a complex business problem; an open suitcase that fills with icons as services are listed; a single plant growing as revenue increases
- **Infographics** for data — study existing infographics for ideas; they communicate statistics far faster than tables
- **Before/after comparisons** — make the difference shocking and vivid
- **Colour as emphasis** — use colour to draw the eye to exactly what you want them to see; one red dot in a sea of black dots
- **Text stylisation** — choose typefaces intentionally; sans-serif (Arial, Helvetica) for clarity; 24pt minimum
- **Pointing and circling** — use your stylus or laser pointer to draw attention to specific data points in real time
- **Animated builds** — reveal information sequentially so the audience stays with you rather than reading ahead

### What to Avoid
- Slides that are read aloud by the presenter
- Complex charts that require multiple minutes to interpret
- Decorative animations that distract from the message
- Stock images that are generic or irrelevant ("cheesy stock images")
- Using slides as a crutch — know your material cold enough to speak without them

## 11. Props, Demonstrations, and Physical Media

### Why Props Work
Physical props activate multiple senses simultaneously. Anthony cites research: "The more a person's senses (sight, sound, touch, smell, taste) are involved in the evaluation process, the easier and faster they can be persuaded."

### Prop Types and Applications
- **Static props** — 3-D printed molecular model passed around a boardroom; model dump truck delivering a proposal inside it
- **Mechanical/electric props** — laser-can shooting range to demonstrate strategic targeting; remote-controlled car navigating an obstacle course
- **Product prototypes** — 3-D printed full-colour prototypes handed to audience for evaluation; makes claims tangible
- **Themed proposals in physical packaging** — proposals delivered in metal lunch boxes, paint cans, matchbooks, model vehicles (all documented with photos in the book)

### Physical Presentation Boards
Use alongside digital presentations for groups under 25 people:
- Oversized foam-core boards (5ft × 3ft) printed in high resolution
- Tri-fold boards for flexible modular content reveal
- Storyboard systems with Velcro-attached panels revealed one at a time
- Moving physically to the board breaks the presentation rhythm and re-engages attention

### Flip Charts
Still valuable for groups under 25. Uses:
- Write "important numbers" live — creates heightened attention ("what's she going to write?")
- Pre-sketch outlines in light pencil, then draw over them with marker for apparently spontaneous visual creation
- Transition tool — moving from projector to flip chart resets attention

## 12. Delivering to Specific Audiences

### Presenting to Executives and Decision Makers (Chapter 21)
Anthony's framework for C-level audiences (applicable to bank credit committees and DFI investment committees):

**What executives want**:
- Big picture only — summary statistics, trends, implications
- Clear recommendation upfront (not revealed at the end)
- Projected ROI and financial returns stated early
- Brevity: most strategic presentations should not exceed 30 minutes
- Equal time for Q&A as for presentation content: "If you have 30 minutes, present for 15 and leave 15 for questions"

**7-Step Content Structure for Executive Presentations**:
1. Review the problem or opportunity
2. Summarise your proposed solution
3. Outline goals, benefits, and strategy
4. Discuss drawbacks, risks, and limitations (acknowledge them — executives respect this)
5. Present overall cost and ROI (short- and long-term)
6. List resources and implementation requirements
7. Justify moving ahead now

**Key behaviour rules**:
- Never ramble or "wing it" — time is their most precious resource
- Expect and welcome interruptions and mid-presentation questions; answer them immediately if you can
- Know your material without referring to slides — this demonstrates true mastery
- Prepare hidden backup slides with full detail for likely deep-dive questions
- Rehearse 20–50× the length of the presentation for high-stakes presentations

**Telling stories to executives**: "Executives often trust their intuition more than sterile data. A brief, relevant story that highlights key points can be invaluable — especially with video examples, metaphors, testimonials, and embedded facts." (Quoting Tom Peters: "The best leaders at every level are master users of stories and symbols.")

### Presenting to a Sales/Funding Audience — OR&B Framework (Chapter 20)
OR&B = **Opportunities, Results, and Benefits**

Structure:
- Open with the opportunity their situation presents
- Cover results — actual outcomes achieved (your evidence)
- Frame everything as benefits to them, not features of your offering
- Refer to competitors only to show how your solution creates superior value
- "Businesses associate change with risk — you must give overwhelming evidence that value far outweighs risk"

Infomercial analysis: Anthony studies infomercials to identify what makes them effective — demonstration, before/after, testimonials in sequence, urgent call to action, risk removal. These same elements should appear in high-stakes sales/funding presentations.

### Presenting to a Mixed Personality Audience
When you cannot identify the dominant type:
- Scope the room before starting: watch posture, language, how people interact
- "Warm up the room" — casual pre-presentation conversation to build rapport
- Structure content to appeal to all four types:
  - Director: results summary and timeline upfront
  - Thinker: data and evidence in body
  - Energizer: story and vision
  - Affable: testimonials and social proof

## 13. Dealing with Questions, Resistance, and Hostile Audiences

### The Seven-Step Question-Handling Process
1. **Welcome** the question (create psychological safety for questioners)
2. **Acknowledge** — "That's an important question"
3. **Clarify** if multi-part or vague — "Is this what you're asking?"
4. **Repeat** the question for the full room
5. **Pause** to think before answering
6. **Answer** — as a structured mini-presentation with facts, examples, and evidence; use enumeration ("Three reasons: One... Two... Three...")
7. **Check acceptance** — "Does that answer your question?"

### Handling Hostile or Loaded Questions
- Take the high ground: do not respond defensively or emotionally — ever
- Use humour to defuse: "That question sounds like it should come with a bomb squad"
- Use crowd psychology: "How many others share that concern?" If few hands: "I see only two people are concerned — I'll address it briefly now and offer to go deeper with you afterwards"
- Rephrase the question to a neutral: "The more important question is..."
- For a legitimate confrontational challenge: acknowledge the concern directly, state you take it seriously, enumerate your evidence calmly

### Objection Handling
Old technique (overcome and wear down) is offensive to sophisticated audiences. Anthony's approach:
- Analyse: is this intellectual, logical, or emotional resistance?
- Provide sincere, genuine answers
- Pre-empt objections by building them into the presentation itself: "You may be wondering about X — here's the answer"
- Offer proof, not arguments

## 14. Rehearsal Methodology

### Practice vs Rehearsal — the Critical Distinction
- **Practice** = improving general speaking skills over time (join Toastmasters, take acting classes, record yourself regularly)
- **Rehearsal** = going over a specific presentation until it is word-perfect, smooth, and timed precisely

"With repeated rehearsals, you won't read from your slides — you'll use visuals primarily to help the audience understand your information."

### Anthony's Rehearsal Progression
1. Rehearse the opening more than any other section — this determines confidence for the entire talk
2. Do 5–10 full out-loud rehearsals for important presentations
3. Work on 5-minute sections first, then link them
4. Memorise the first two sentences of your opening
5. Memorise key statistics, financial numbers, and critical facts — these must not require notes
6. Time each section; most presentations run longer than expected
7. Allocate at least 15 minutes (25% of a 60-minute slot) for questions
8. Videotape after the 2nd or 3rd rehearsal; watch critically but objectively
9. For high-stakes presentations: rehearse 20–50× the presentation length in total time
10. For final run-throughs, the last two must be your best two — if not, keep going

### Team Presentation Rehearsal
- Individual members prepare their own sections first
- Team rehearsals begin after individual sections exist
- First 2–3 rehearsals will be rough — expect this
- Give structured written feedback after each run-through
- Videotape from third rehearsal onwards
- All members physically together for final 1–2 rehearsals
- Dress rehearsal is mandatory, not optional
- Plan for understudy backup: each member learns another's section

## 15. Technology and Tools

### Presentation Apps Beyond PowerPoint
Anthony reviewed multiple apps in 2014 — the principle remains: choose tools that enable **non-linear navigation** and **interactive engagement**, not just sequential slides.

- **Prezi** — zoom-based non-linear navigation; good for showing interconnections in a business ecosystem
- **Keynote** (Apple) — cleaner templates than PowerPoint; import/export compatible; good animation tools
- **iPresent** — non-linear navigation from gesture menus; enables responding to specific audience requests mid-presentation
- **9Slides** — split-screen video of presenter + slides simultaneously; excellent for recorded/remote presentations
- **Doceri** — electronic whiteboard capability; draw over slides live
- **Perspective** — turns data into animated stories; good for financial projections

### Hardware Principles
- Arrive at least 1 hour early to set up and test all equipment
- Carry your own equipment for critical presentations; never rely on venue hardware
- Have backup equipment connected and ready for critical presentations
- Use wireless (lavaliere or headset) microphone — position high on body
- Pico projectors for one-on-one; full projector (minimum 2,000 lumens) for groups
- For interactive sessions: consider wireless presentation gateways enabling audience device integration

### Remote Presenting
- Visuals must be more compelling for remote than in-person (less physical presence to compensate)
- Use "builds" — reveal information incrementally so audience stays with you
- Keep all text to minimum; large sans-serif font (24pt+); strong images dominate
- Target 20-minute maximum attention segments; insert breaks or guest speaker transitions
- Record with 9Slides or similar so presenter and slides are both visible

## 16. Proposals and Presentation Handouts (Chapter 16)

### The Innovative Proposal Concept
Anthony documents multiple examples of proposals that became objects themselves:
- Proposal delivered inside a metal lunch box with theme "We'll Help You Eat Your Competitors' Lunch" — candy bars, executive summary, branded items inside
- Proposal packaged as oversized matchbook — "Starting a Fire of Blazing Profits" — real wooden match, strike plate, tabbed sections with benefit statements on each tab
- Proposal inside a model dump truck (construction company)
- Proposal inside a paint can with artist's brush + mini palette + flash drive (communications firm)
- Race car themed proposal with 4 die-cast cars representing 4 solution characteristics

**The principle**: your proposal is the first physical impression of how you work. A creative, themed proposal signals: attention to detail, client-centricity, genuine differentiation from competitors.

**Caveat from Anthony**: "Amazing-looking proposals cannot take the place of well-written, targeted content and valuable illustrations. You need both."

### Practical Handout Design
- Use landscape horizontal format (8×14) with two-column layout for more impact
- Print in colour on quality paper stock; weight and finish change the perception
- Personalize handouts — print recipient names on their copies
- Include supplementary information that deepens key points without cluttering the slide deck
- Creative handouts tied to the theme: e.g., toilet paper roll as handout for a bathroom sanitation service company

## 17. The Elevator Pitch (Chapter 22)

### Structure of an Effective 30–60 Second Pitch
Answer these questions in sequence:
1. Who are you?
2. What do you do? (begin with a benefit or value statement, not a job title)
3. Who are your customers?
4. What specific results do you deliver?
5. What is your call to micro-action? ("May I call you to discuss...")

### Formats
**Value proposition opening**: Instead of "I make cakes" say "I sweeten people's lives." Instead of "We design rockets" say "We send people to the moon."

**Story opening**: "You know when you're trying to write something but you keep checking messages..." — create a relatable problem, then introduce your solution.

**Rhetorical question opening**: "Are you easily distracted while working on tasks requiring focus? Who isn't? My company helps people concentrate..."

**The test**: Does your pitch make the listener want to say "Tell me more" or "So what?" If the latter, rewrite.

## 18. Business Plan Presentation Innovation

### Applying the Anthony Framework to a Standard Business Plan Deck (12–15 Slides)

**The fundamental problem with most business plan pitches**: They are built for the presenter's convenience (linear, comprehensive, speaker-dependent) rather than the audience's decision-making process (non-linear, evidence-seeking, time-pressured).

#### Opening (Slides 1–2)
- Use **Confirm Findings** technique: "Our research indicates your priority is [problem]. Is that accurate?"
- Or open with a single compelling statistic visualised (not bulleted): "The [market] in Uganda is worth UGX X billion and growing at Y% annually — yet [key gap] leaves [problem] unsolved."
- Consider a 30-second proof video if you have customer evidence or a demonstration
- State your executive summary upfront: "In the next 25 minutes, I will show you that this business will achieve X, Y, Z results"

#### Problem Statement (Slide 3)
- Use a photograph or powerful image — not a bullet list
- Make the problem tangible: use a real story of a real person experiencing the problem
- Use contrast: before (current situation) vs after (with your solution)

#### Solution (Slides 4–5)
- If you have a physical product: **demonstrate it live** — even a simple demonstration beats all claims
- If you have a service: use a 60-second video testimonial from an actual customer
- Use the FAB model for each feature: Feature → Advantage → Benefit (in financial terms)

#### Market Opportunity (Slide 6)
- Use infographic-style data visualisation — avoid raw tables
- Round numbers to be memorable ("1 in 5 Ugandan households" not "21.4% of households")
- Cite your sources — Thinker-type bank analysts will ask

#### Business Model (Slide 7)
- One clear visual showing how money flows
- State unit economics on the slide: cost per unit, price per unit, margin per unit

#### Financial Projections (Slides 8–9)
- Show the key numbers (Year 1–3 revenue, break-even month, DSCR) as striking stand-alone visuals
- Prepare backup slides with full assumptions — navigate to these on demand
- Use contrast: current situation vs projected situation at Year 3
- State your funding ask clearly on one slide: amount, purpose (itemised), repayment/return

#### Management Team (Slide 10)
- Lead with track record and relevant experience — not titles
- If the team is thin, acknowledge it and explain your mitigation (advisory board, partnerships)

#### Risk and Mitigation (Slide 11)
- Proactively name the top 3 risks — this builds credibility with Thinker audiences
- Match each risk to a named mitigation

#### Call to Action (Slide 12)
- State the specific ask: "We are requesting UGX X, disbursed as [schedule], to achieve [milestones], with repayment starting [date]"
- Then state what happens when you say yes: "Within 30 days, we will [specific first action]"
- End with your strongest evidence piece — a testimonial, a result, a vivid image of future state

#### Backup Slides (unlisted)
Prepare for every likely deep-dive question:
- Full financial model with monthly breakdown
- Collateral schedule (for bank presentations)
- Regulatory compliance status
- Competitor comparison table
- Full team CVs
- Reference letters / supply agreements

## 19. Audience Type Guide — Adapting for Different Funders

### Bank Credit Officer (Thinker/Director)
- Personality: predominantly Thinker with Director urgency; high risk-aversion
- Primary interest: repayment certainty; collateral coverage; DSCR; management quality
- What they fear: losing their job by approving a bad loan; incomplete documentation
- Presentation approach:
  - Open with executive summary including key financials (revenue Y1–Y3, DSCR, LTV)
  - Confirm you understand their specific lending criteria before diving in
  - Lead with evidence (trading history, bank statements, contracts) not projections
  - Never obscure a weakness — name it and explain the mitigation
  - Data-heavy backup slides; be ready to navigate to them on demand
  - Avoid theatrical openings; mild-to-moderate creativity appropriate (professional proposal packaging, well-designed one-page summary)
  - Allocate 50% of your time to answering their questions
  - Close with a specific ask and a clear, simple repayment schedule

### Angel Investor (Energizer/Director mix)
- Personality: Energizer-dominant (vision-driven) with Director results focus
- Primary interest: size of opportunity; quality of founders; potential for outsized return
- What they fear: missing a big opportunity; backing someone who cannot execute
- Presentation approach:
  - Open with a vivid picture of the world if your solution succeeds (vision story)
  - Bold, image-driven slides; minimal text; strong visuals
  - Demonstrate the product or show a customer video early — proof-of-concept before financials
  - Lead with market size and growth; returns and exit path
  - Make the founders the heroes of the story — personal motivation, relevant experience, team chemistry
  - Use the elevator pitch principle: leave them wanting to say "tell me more" at every stage
  - Call to action: "We are raising UGX X at Y valuation — we'd love you to be part of this"

### DFI Programme Officer (Affable/Thinker)
- Personality: Affable primary (mission-driven; consensus-seeking); Thinker secondary (evidence required for committee)
- Primary interest: social/development impact; organisational capacity; sustainability; alignment with DFI mandate
- What they fear: funding projects that fail publicly; not meeting reporting requirements
- Presentation approach:
  - Open by confirming your understanding of their mandate and how your business serves it
  - Use testimonials and human stories — show real people benefiting
  - Include detailed LogFrame or Theory of Change reference (in backup slides)
  - Demonstrate financial sustainability — they want to know you won't come back asking for more
  - Acknowledge women's participation, environmental impact, community benefit explicitly
  - Reference local context (UBOS data, sector studies) to show you understand Uganda's situation
  - Mild creativity appropriate; a well-designed, thematic proposal document well-received
  - Close with a partnership framing: "We are not asking you to fund us — we are inviting you to partner with us in achieving [development outcome]"

### Potential Client / Sales Presentation
- Personality: varies; focus on the OR&B framework
- Primary interest: what problem does this solve for me? What will it cost and what will I get?
- What they fear: making the wrong decision; supplier failure; hidden costs
- Presentation approach:
  - Open with Confirm Findings: "You told us you need X. Is that accurate?"
  - Lead with results achieved for similar clients (testimonials, case studies)
  - Use the FAB model for each key element of your offer
  - Demonstrate if possible — invite them to try it themselves
  - Address their likely objections in your presentation before they ask
  - Give them a specific, simple proposal at the end with clear pricing
  - Use creative proposal packaging to differentiate from competitors

## 20. Client Coaching Notes

### The Uganda/East Africa Context

**1. Mild-to-moderate creativity is the right zone for most Ugandan business contexts.** Wild theatrical openings (robots, blindfolds) are not the target. However, in a context where most business plan presentations consist of a presenter reading PowerPoint slides with tables of numbers, even modest innovation — a striking opening statistic, a 60-second customer testimonial video, a physical product demonstration — will stand out dramatically.

**2. The Confirm Findings technique is especially powerful in East Africa.** In a high-context culture where pre-meeting relationships and information exchange matter, opening a pitch by demonstrating that you did your research on the funder's mandate and priorities communicates respect and seriousness. Bank loan officers at Stanbic, Centenary, or UDB will respond well to a presenter who begins: "We understand your SME lending criteria require a DSCR of at least 1.25 and collateral of at least 1.2× the loan. We have prepared this presentation to show you how this business meets those criteria."

**3. The physical proposal document matters more in Uganda than many Western contexts.** When most loan applications arrive as loose printouts in a clear folder, a well-bound, clearly labelled, professionally designed proposal document with tabbed sections signals organisational competence before a single number is read. Anthony's examples — themed packaging, quality paper, named sections — translate directly.

**4. Testimonials and social proof carry extra weight.** Ugandan funders and customers rely heavily on references and word-of-mouth. A 60-second video testimonial from a real customer, or a reference letter from a known business leader, can be more persuasive than three slides of projections.

**5. Storytelling is culturally resonant.** East African oral traditions value storytelling. Opening with a real, locally-grounded story of the problem you are solving — featuring someone the audience can identify with — is one of the highest-impact techniques available. Do not skip directly to numbers.

**6. The management team slide often under-delivers.** Most Ugandan business plan pitches either omit the team slide or list titles without explaining why these specific people will succeed. Anthony's framework: lead with track record and relevant experience, not credentials. "I built this same distribution network for [company] over 5 years" is more compelling than "MBA, Makerere University."

**7. Prepare for directional questions and mid-presentation interruptions.** Bank credit officers and DFI officers will interrupt. This is not hostility — it is engagement. Welcome it. Have your backup slides ready. An articulate, unruffled response to a challenging question ("Your cash flow turns positive in month 18 — what is your bridge funding plan?") does more to build credibility than any slide.

**8. The 15-minute executive version.** Always prepare a 15-minute compressed version of your presentation (Anthony's "Summary Level"). When a funder meeting is cut short, or a second decision-maker joins unexpectedly with less time, you must be able to present your core message — problem, solution, ask, key evidence — in 15 minutes without appearing to rush.

**9. Citing reference sources adds credibility with DFI audiences.** When presenting market data, citing World Bank, UBOS, or IFC sources (as per this skills suite's reference files) signals that your projections are grounded in credible data. Anthony notes: "Be prepared to give the source of your statistics if asked."

**10. Close with a specific, actionable request — every time.** Ugandan business pitches frequently end with "and we would appreciate your support" rather than a specific ask. Anthony is unequivocal: your call to action must name the exact amount, the purpose, the timeline, and what happens next. Vague calls to action produce vague results.

## 21. Ten Traits of Innovative Presenters (Anthony's Summary Chapter)

1. **Coherence** — your argument must hold together logically end to end
2. **Curiosity** — research your audience as deeply as you research your content; never assume
3. **Enthusiasm** — genuine passion is contagious; audiences mimic the presenter's energy
4. **Generosity** — give your full attention to the audience; do not split attention between notes and room
5. **Honesty** — acknowledge what you do not know; do not promise what you cannot deliver
6. **Humility** — the presentation is for them, not about you
7. **Innovativeness** — look for one way to do something differently in every presentation you give
8. **Preparedness** — never wing it; preparation is visible and its absence is equally visible
9. **Punctuality** — start and finish on time; ending early is never a failure
10. **Rehearsed** — the final presentation should look effortless because you did the work in private
