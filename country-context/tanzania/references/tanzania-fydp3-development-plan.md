# Tanzania FYDP III — National Five-Year Development Plan 2021/22–2025/26

**Theme:** Realising Competitiveness and Industrialisation for Human Development
**Period:** July 2021 – June 2026
**Issuing body:** Ministry of Finance and Planning, United Republic of Tanzania
**Significance:** Third and **final** plan under Tanzania Development Vision 2025 (TDV 2025); aims to graduate Tanzania to a semi-industrialised, middle-income competitive economy

---

## 1. Context and Background

### FYDP Sequence
| Plan | Period | Theme | GDP Growth Achieved |
|------|--------|-------|---------------------|
| FYDP I | 2011/12–2015/16 | Unleashing growth opportunities | ~7% avg |
| FYDP II | 2016/17–2020/21 | Hapa Kazi Tu (industrialisation) | 6.9% avg (2016–2019) |
| **FYDP III** | **2021/22–2025/26** | **Competitiveness & Industrialisation** | **Target: ≥8%** |

Tanzania achieved **lower-middle-income status** on 1 July 2020 (GNI per capita TZS 2,577,967 = USD 1,080, above USD 1,035 threshold). FYDP III now consolidates and deepens this.

### FYDP II Legacy (Baseline for FYDP III)
- 93.8% of macroeconomic targets met
- GDP growth averaged **6.9%** (2016–2019)
- Inflation averaged **4.4%** (below 5% target)
- Foreign reserves: USD 4,767.7 million = 5.6 months imports (Dec 2020)
- 8,477 industries established; 482,601 new jobs created
- Manufacturing GDP share: 8.5% (up from 7.8% in 2016)
- Mining sector: 100% of targets met; growth 17.7% (2019)
- Tourist arrivals: 1,527,230 (2019); earnings USD 2.6 billion
- Tourism target USD 3.6B missed due to COVID-19

## 13. SWOC Analysis (Forward-Looking for FYDP III)

### Strengths
1. Political will, peace, stable political atmosphere
2. Abundance of natural resources — land, wildlife, minerals, forestry
3. Legal and institutional investment framework in place
4. High economic growth averaging 7.0% over last five years
5. Lucrative investment potentials: oil and gas, electricity generation, distribution
6. Agribusiness opportunities in agricultural sector
7. Expanding infrastructure: aviation, roads, railways, ports, real estate, ICT
8. **Strategic geographical location** — gateway to 6 landlocked countries; EAC + SADC member (600M+ combined market, USD 1T+ GDP)
9. Long coastline with blue economy potential
10. High, growing population offering labour force and growing middle class as domestic market

### Weaknesses
1. Low human capital development; low STI capacity; insufficient specialised skills in extractive industries
2. Inadequate disaggregated data for planning and monitoring
3. Weak monitoring and evaluation systems
4. **Low private sector development and engagement**
5. **Low per capita incomes**

### Opportunities
1. Improving enabling environment for small firms and agricultural transformation
2. Formalisation of informal sector — emerging taxpayer base
3. Macroeconomic stability and economic diplomacy to attract FDI
4. Regional and LGA investment guides available
5. Irrigable fertile land available
6. Improved market infrastructure for agriculture
7. Blueprint initiative for cross-cutting reforms
8. SEZ/EPZ in border regions for regional trade
9. **Global and regional markets (SADC, EAC, AfCFTA)**

### Challenges
1. Exogenous shocks — regional instability
2. **Climate change** (EUDR, deforestation, drought)
3. Natural calamities
4. Pandemics
5. Global/regional geo-political instabilities (China trade tensions, regional conflicts)

## 14. Private Sector Context

**Tanzania's private sector reform history:** Since mid-1980s. SOE divestiture in 1990s. Now growing rapidly.

**Industrial development during FYDP II:**
- 8,477 new industries established (2016–2020)
  - Large-scale: 201
  - Medium-scale: 460
  - Small: 3,406
  - Micro: 4,410
- 482,601 jobs created (up from 254,687 in 2015)
- Industries grew: 52,633 (2015) → 61,110 (2019)

**Industrial credit constraint:** Industrial credit = 10.5% of total credit; personal loans = 27.4%; trade = 20.5%.

**Start-up context (Box 1):** 75% of Tanzania's population is under 35. ~1 million young people enter labour market/year; ~200,000 find immediate employment; 800,000 rely on entrepreneurship. Government will create legal and operational systems for youth-owned, women-led, disability-inclusive and start-up businesses. Master Plan for Financial Development 2020/21–2029/30 supports this.

## 15. Key Business Opportunities Identified by FYDP III

By sector and sub-sector:
1. **Agriculture value chain** — cold chain, storage, processing, irrigation, certified seed, extension services, cooperative financing
2. **Livestock processing** — milk processing (5% → 14%), meat processing, leather (hides/skin processing = near-zero currently)
3. **Fisheries** — aquaculture, deep-sea fishing, processing, fish feed (464 → 2,500 tonnes target)
4. **Manufacturing** — medium-tech and high-tech import substitution; domestic input processing
5. **Construction** — local contractors (35% → 60% market share target); road, railway, building
6. **Tourism services** — target 5M tourists vs 1.5M baseline; lodge/hotel, adventure, cultural tourism
