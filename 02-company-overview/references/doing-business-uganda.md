# Doing Business in Uganda — Regulatory and Setup Reference

*Sources: RSM Eastern Africa, "Doing Business in Uganda 2025/26" (RSM International, 2026); Baker Tilly Hem LLP, "Business in Uganda" (Baker Tilly, undated, c.2024).*

This reference file covers business registration, legal structures, licensing, employment law, investment framework, accounting requirements, and economic context for Uganda business plans. For tax rates and computation methodology, see `10-financial-projections/references/uganda-tax-framework.md`.

---

## SECTION A: Legal Structures and Business Entity Types

### A.1 Principal Business Structures in Uganda

All business entities in Uganda are registered through the **Uganda Registration Services Bureau (URSB)** via the Online Business Registration System (OBRS) at https://obrs.ursb.go.ug.

| Structure | Shareholders/Members | Liability | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Private Limited Company (Ltd)** | Minimum 2, maximum 50 | Limited to shares held | Cannot invite public to subscribe; share transfer restricted; most common SME structure |
| **Single Member Company (SMC)** | One | Limited to shares held | One member, 1–7 directors; requires Nominee and Alternate Director; does not need a company secretary |
| **Public Limited Company (PLC)** | Minimum 7, no maximum | Limited to shares held | Can offer shares to public; subject to USE listing rules if listed; minimum UGX 1B share capital for USE MIMS listing |
| **Partnership (General)** | 2–50 partners | Joint and several (unlimited) | Partners share profits/losses equally unless deed states otherwise; partners taxed individually |
| **Limited Liability Partnership (LLP)** | 2–20 | Limited partners protected | General partners remain fully liable; limited partners liable only to capital contributed; retains tax transparency |
| **Branch of Foreign Company** | N/A | Parent company liable | Must register with URSB; must appoint a local representative; registration takes 3 working hours after submission |
| **NGO** | Minimum 2 founding members | Depends on constitution | Registered with NGO Bureau (under Ministry of Internal Affairs) under NGO Act 2016; requires separate company incorporation and NGO Bureau registration |

**Bank lending preference:** Commercial banks and development finance institutions (Uganda Development Bank, EFC Uganda) generally prefer to lend to **private limited companies** as they provide cleaner security structures, separate legal personality, and auditable financial statements. Sole traders and informal partnerships face significantly higher scrutiny.

### A.2 Beneficial Owners Register (Mandatory from 2022/23)

The Companies (Amendment) Act 2022 and Partnership (Amendment) Act 2022 require every company and LLP to maintain a register of **beneficial owners** — natural persons who exercise ultimate ownership or control (directly or indirectly).

- Notice of register creation must be given to the URSB Registrar of Companies within **14 days** of company incorporation.
- A URSB circular of 11 January 2023 required all existing companies to file beneficial owners' information within **30 days**.
- The register must disclose: personal information of beneficial owners, nature of ownership or control, and date of becoming/ceasing to be a beneficial owner.
- Format and content: Companies (Beneficial Owner) Regulations 2023.

## SECTION B: Company Registration Process

### B.1 OBRS Platform — Account Creation

1. Go to https://obrs.ursb.go.ug and click "Create Account"
2. Ugandan nationals: enter National Identification Number (NIN) — system validates and returns name/DOB/gender
3. Non-citizens: provide Passport or Alien ID number, name, gender, contact details, and attach scanned ID copy
4. Verify email and phone number via link sent to email

**NIN/BRN rule (effective 2025):** The Taxpayer Identification Number (TIN) for an individual is now linked to the NIN issued by the National Identification and Registration Authority (NIRA). For companies, the TIN is derived from the Business Registration Number (BRN) issued by URSB. Every director must have a TIN before a company TIN can be assigned.

### B.2 Name Reservation

1. Log in → "Start" → "Reserve Name"
2. Select entity type (Business Name / Company / Building Society)
3. Enter preferred name and 2–3 alternative names
4. Select "Nature of Business"
5. Generate Payment Reference Number (PRN) and pay reservation fee
6. Reservation confirmed; reserved name is available for incorporation

### B.3 Private Limited Company Registration (Step-by-Step)

After name reservation, log in and click "Incorporate Company":

1. Identify reserved name from pop-up window → "Register"
2. Select optional forms to accompany mandatory forms
3. Fill **Form S18**: company physical address, proposed share capital, subscribers
4. Fill **Form A1**: total shares, share classification, value per share
5. Complete Memorandum and Articles of Association
6. Enter Beneficial Owners particulars
7. Fill **Form 20**: director particulars (name, address, nationality, TIN)
8. Enter official company contact details (email, phone)
9. Preview all captured details
10. Download, sign, and upload all forms
11. Attach required documents (scanned IDs, etc.)
12. Pay registration fee and stamp duty via OBRS → submit
13. **Certificate of Incorporation issued within 2 working days** via official email

**Post-incorporation (within 14 days):**
- File **Form 18**: Notice of Registered Office and Postal Address

### B.4 Foreign Company Branch Registration

Documents required:
- Certified copies of charter/memorandum and articles/constitution from country of origin
- Form 24: list of directors and secretary; company name, number, date of incorporation, address
- Form 25: local representative names and postal address
- Form 26: full registered office address
- Form 13: statement of charges

**Timeline:** Registration certificate issued **within 3 working hours** of complete submission and payment.

### B.5 Post-Registration Requirements

| Step | Agency | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **TIN registration** | Uganda Revenue Authority (URA) — www.ura.go.ug | Free; requires: Certificate of Incorporation, Form 7, company email, director TINs, bank account details |
| **Trading licence** | KCCA (Kampala) or District Council (upcountry) | See Section C below |
| **NSSF registration** | National Social Security Fund | Required before engaging employees; takes 1–7 days |
| **Investment licence** | Uganda Investment Authority (UIA) — www.ugandainvest.go.ug | Foreign investors mandatory; local investors optional (but required for certain incentives) |
| **Company seal** | Any seal maker | Mandatory under Companies Act 2012, s.117(b) — embossed metal die with company name engraved |
| **Auditor appointment** | ICPAU-registered firm | Required at first AGM (Companies Act 2012, s.167) |
| **Company secretary** | Advocate/CPA/Chartered Secretary | Required for all companies except single member companies; public company secretary must hold professional qualification |

### B.6 TIN Requirements

For an **Individual TIN:**
- Two copies of ID (passport, national ID, voter's card, driving permit, work permit)
- Working email address
- Mother's name and phone contacts
- A referee who already holds an active TIN

For a **Non-Individual (Company) TIN:**
- Certificate of Incorporation from URSB
- Company Form 7
- Contact person details
- Company email and phone
- Bank account details
- Each director's individual TIN

**Cost:** Free through URA directly; third-party agents charge separately.

## SECTION C: Regulatory and Licensing Landscape

### C.1 Trading Licences

**Kampala (KCCA):**
1. Apply at the Municipal Authority/Council in the division where premises are located
2. Download and complete standard Form A via the **City Operator Identification Number (COIN)** system
3. Submit with: Memorandum and Articles, director names, Certificate of Incorporation, lease agreement
4. Receive premises inspection by licensing officer → assessment form issued
5. Pay assessed fee at bank → trading licence issued
6. Fees vary by location and business type; regulated by Trade Licensing (Amendment of Schedule) Instrument, 2017

**Upcountry (districts outside Kampala):**
- Apply at the District Council or Municipal Authority covering the business location
- Same process but administered locally; fees generally lower than Kampala
- Seven new cities created from July 2020 now operate their own licensing regimes (Arua, Gulu, Lira, Mbale, Mbarara, Masaka, Jinja)

**URSB e-licensing portal:** URSB maintains a central repository of all required business licences across all sectors at its e-licensing portal — a single point of access to determine which licences apply to any business in any sector. This is the first reference point before approaching individual regulatory agencies.

### C.2 Sector-Specific Licences

| Sector | Regulatory Body | Licence/Approval Required |
|---|---|---|
| Environmental impact | National Environment Management Authority (NEMA) | Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) certificate; Environmental Compliance Certificate; Resettlement Action Plan (RAP) where applicable |
| Food/drugs/pharmaceuticals | National Drug Authority (NDA) | Product registration, premises licence |
| Standards compliance | Uganda National Bureau of Standards (UNBS) | Product certification against Uganda Standards (US); mandatory for manufactured goods |
| Tourism and hospitality | Uganda Tourism Board (UTB) | Tourist enterprise licence; classification for hotels, lodges, tour operators |
| Banking and financial services | Bank of Uganda (BoU) | Banking licence; microfinance licence; forex bureau licence |
| Microfinance and savings | Uganda Microfinance Regulatory Authority (UMRA) | Tier IV (SACCOs, MFIs) licence |
| Mining and minerals | Ministry of Energy and Mineral Development | Prospecting Licence, Exploration Licence, Mining Lease, Location Licence, Mineral Dealers Licence (5 types under Mining Act 2003) |
| Oil and gas | Petroleum Exploration and Production Dept. | Petroleum licence |
| Air transport | Civil Aviation Authority | Air operator certificate |
| Energy generation | Electricity Regulatory Authority (ERA) | Generation and distribution licences |
| Insurance | Insurance Regulatory Authority (IRA) | Insurer/broker licence |
| Capital markets | Capital Markets Authority (CMA) | Broker/dealer, investment adviser, collective investment scheme licences |
| Telecoms | Uganda Communications Commission (UCC) | Operator licence |
| Education | Ministry of Education | School/institution registration |
| Health facilities | Ministry of Health | Health facility licence |

**Sequencing rule:** Sector-specific regulatory approvals/permits must generally be obtained **before** applying for the UIA Investment Licence. The investment licence is the culminating approval, not the starting point.

### C.3 One-Stop Centre (OSC) at UIA

The Uganda Investment Authority operates a physical One-Stop Centre housing 14 government agencies including:
- Kampala Capital City Authority
- National Environment Management Authority
- National Water and Sewerage Corporation
- Uganda Registration Services Bureau
- Directorate of Immigration and Citizenship
- Uganda National Bureau of Standards
- Ministry of Lands, Housing and Urban Development
- Uganda Revenue Authority
- Uganda Free Zones Authority

The eBiz electronic OSC (https://ubiz.go.ug) provides online access to most of these services.

## SECTION D: Investment Licensing

### D.1 Uganda Investment Authority (UIA)

UIA was established under the Investment Code 1991 (amended by Investment Code Act 2019) as the primary investment promotion and facilitation agency.

**Investment Licence requirements by investor type:**

| Investor Type | Minimum Capital | Licence Required? |
|---|---|---|
| Foreign investor | USD 100,000 planned investment | **Mandatory** — legalises the investment in Uganda |
| Local investor | USD 50,000 planned investment | Optional (but recommended for incentive access) |
| Foreign trader (retail/wholesale) | USD 100,000 operating capital | Required before trading/entry permits issued |
| Local investor (smaller) | Below USD 50,000 | Not required from UIA; proceed to KCCA/district licencing directly |

**Documents for Investment Licence:**
1. Certificate of Incorporation and Memorandum and Articles of Association (from URSB)
2. Brief business proposal (not more than 5 pages)
3. Evidence of availability of funds (bank statement for company or directors)
4. Land status (title deed or tenancy agreement)
5. Passport photocopies of foreign directors
6. Completed UIA Application Form

**Processing:** Licence issued **free of charge within 2 working days** of complete document submission (Baker Tilly).

### D.2 Tax-Related Incentives for Investors

**10-year income tax exemption** (s.21(1)(ae), Income Tax Act): Income of operators in industrial parks or free zones — or outside those zones meeting all conditions — is **exempt from income tax for 10 years** provided:

1. **Investment threshold:** Foreign investors: minimum USD 10M; Citizens: minimum USD 300,000; Citizens investing up-country: minimum USD 150,000
2. **Local sourcing:** Minimum 70% of raw materials locally sourced (subject to availability)
3. **Employment:** Minimum 70% of employees must be citizens earning at least 70% of total wage bill
4. **Qualifying activities:** agro-processing, pharmaceuticals, building materials, automobile assembly, household appliances, furniture/pulp/paper, vocational institutes, logistics/warehousing, IT, commercial farming, tyres/footwear/mattresses/toothpaste, chemicals, textiles, glassware, leather, industrial machinery, electrical equipment, sanitary products, electric vehicles/batteries

**VAT exemption** (s.19, VAT Act; Schedule 3(1)(ae)): Qualifying investors (same thresholds as above) are exempt from VAT on:
- Feasibility study and design services
- Locally produced construction materials for factory or warehouse
- Locally produced raw materials, inputs, machinery, and equipment

For **imported machinery and equipment**: VAT deferment (not exemption) where deferred VAT amount is at least USD 4,000. Deferred VAT is written off once machinery is installed and in use.

**Stamp duty NIL** for qualifying investors on: debentures, mortgages, further charges, land leases, share capital increases, land transfers — provided investment meets the above thresholds and activities.

**Excise duty NIL** on construction materials, locally produced raw materials, and inputs for qualifying industrial park/free zone operators.

**Other tax incentives available (per URA website):** Initial allowance on new industrial buildings, accelerated depreciation, loss carry-forward provisions. Full list at www.ura.go.ug.

### D.3 Foreign Investment Protections

Uganda is signatory to:
- Multi-lateral Investment Guarantee Agency (MIGA)
- International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes (ICSID)
- Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards (CREFAA)
- Overseas Private Investment Corporation (OPIC) of USA
- Agreement on Trade Related Investment Measures (TRIMS)

**Foreign exchange:** Uganda has a **totally liberalised foreign exchange regime** — no exchange controls on profit repatriation. Investors may freely repatriate dividends, capital gains, loan repayments, and management fees.

**Land ownership:** Foreign companies may own property through a leasehold arrangement; freehold ownership by non-citizens is restricted.

**Double Taxation Agreements:** Uganda has DTAs with Denmark, India, Italy, Mauritius, the Netherlands, Norway, South Africa, United Kingdom, China, and Zambia. EAC and Egypt treaties awaiting parliamentary ratification.

## SECTION E: Employment and Labour Law

### E.1 Governing Legislation

- **Employment Act 2006, Cap 226** — primary employment law governing contracts, hours, leave, termination, discrimination
- **National Social Security Fund Act Cap 222** — mandatory savings scheme
- **Workers' Compensation Act** — compensation for work-related injuries

### E.2 Employment Contract Requirements

Written contracts must be provided within **12 weeks of commencement** and must include:
1. Full names and addresses of both parties
2. Date employment commenced
3. Job title
4. Workplace/duty station
5. Wages, payment intervals, and authorised deductions
6. Rate of overtime pay
7. Normal hours of work, days of week, shift arrangements
8. Number of days annual leave and pay during leave
9. Length of notice period
10. Sick leave terms

Any amendment to the contract requires written notice and employee consent.

### E.3 Working Hours and Overtime

- Maximum: **8 hours per day, 6 days per week = 48 hours per week**
- Shift workers: may work up to 10 hours/day or 48 hours/week, where the 3-week average does not exceed 10 hours/day or 56 hours/week (s.53(5), Employment Act)
- Work on a public holiday: must be paid at **not less than double the normal daily rate**

### E.4 Leave Entitlements

| Leave Type | Entitlement | Conditions |
|---|---|---|
| **Annual leave** | 21 working days per year (7 days per 4 months of continuous service) | Employee must complete at least 6 months of service; applies to employees working 16+ hours/week |
| **Maternity leave** | 60 working days, fully paid | At least 4 weeks post-birth; written notice 7 days before; employer finances leave pay |
| **Paternity leave** | 4 working days, fully paid | On birth or miscarriage of wife |
| **Sick leave** | Full wages and benefits per contract | After 1 month continuous service; employee must notify employer and provide medical certificate; employer may terminate after 2nd month if illness continues |

**On termination:** Employee is entitled to payment for unused annual leave proportional to length of service.

### E.5 Public Holidays (14 days)

New Year's Day (Jan 1), NRM Day (Jan 26), Janani Luwum Day (Feb 16), Women's Day (Mar 8), Good Friday, Easter Monday, Labour Day (May 1), Martyrs' Day (Jun 3), National Heroes Day (Jun 9), Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, Independence Day (Oct 9), Christmas Day (Dec 25), Boxing Day (Dec 26).

### E.6 NSSF — Statutory Contributions

| Contribution | Rate | Paid By |
|---|---|---|
| Employer contribution | 10% of gross monthly wage | Employer (operating expense) |
| Employee contribution | 5% of gross monthly wage | Employee (deducted from salary) |
| **Total NSSF** | **15% of gross wage** | Remitted to NSSF by 15th of following month |

**Applicable to:** Private sector employees aged 16–55; NGOs; government parastatals not covered by government pension scheme. **Threshold for mandatory compliance:** employers with 5 or more employees (National Social Security Fund Act).

### E.7 Foreign Employees — Work Permit Process

A non-citizen working in Uganda requires an **Entry Permit** (work permit) under the Uganda Citizenship and Immigration Control Act. Eight classes of permits exist (A–H); employees fall under **Class G2**.

**Process:**
1. Employer obtains Immigration Organisation Code from National Citizenship and Immigration Control (NCIC)
2. Expatriate applies for **Special Pass** (valid 3 months, extendable while work permit is pending)
3. Simultaneously apply for work permit (Class G2) with:
   - Employer cover letter to NCIC
   - Worker CV and academic documents (in English)
   - 2 passport photos and passport copy
   - Police clearance/letter of good conduct from home country
   - Appointment letter
   - Proof employer could not find a qualified Ugandan
   - Training plan for Ugandans
   - Company documents: Certificate of Incorporation, Memorandum and Articles, Trading Licence, bank statements (3–6 months), TIN registration, income tax clearance, investment letter
4. Pay official work permit fees plus refundable security bond
5. Renewed on expiry; employers must proactively track expiry dates

**Note:** Section 35 of the Employment Act requires vacancies to be notified to the district labour officer before hiring — this provision is not currently being enforced in practice.

## SECTION F: Accounting and Audit Requirements

### F.1 Governing Framework

| Law | Scope |
|---|---|
| **Companies Act 2012** | All registered companies — financial reporting, AGM, audit, company secretary |
| **Accountants Act 2013** | Professional accountancy regulation via ICPAU |
| **Insurance Act** | Insurance companies — specialised reporting |
| **Financial Institutions Act** | Banks — specialised reporting |
| **Retirement Benefits Schemes** | Pension schemes — specialised reporting |

**Accounting standard:** International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS) are the required standard for financial reporting in Uganda. Books must be kept in English.

### F.2 Mandatory Accounting Records

- Companies must keep proper books of account in English covering all business transactions
- Records must be retained for at least **5 years** after the end of the year of income (income tax purposes) and **6 years** for VAT purposes

### F.3 Audit Requirements

Every company must:
1. Appoint an auditor at each Annual General Meeting (AGM) — first auditor may be appointed by directors before first AGM
2. Auditors hold office from one AGM to the next
3. Auditors must be registered with ICPAU

**ICPAU (Institute of Certified Public Accountants of Uganda):** Established by Parliament in 1992; regulates the accountancy profession in Uganda; over 4,000 members. An accountant practising in Uganda must be a registered member of ICPAU.

### F.4 Annual Statutory Filing (with URSB)

Companies with share capital must file an **Annual Return** at least once per year containing:
- Registered office details
- Registers of members and debenture holders
- Share capital and debenture information
- Details of past and present members, directors, and secretary

**Other mandatory filings (within stipulated time, nominal fee):** Changes in directors, changes in registered office, changes in share capital.

### F.5 Financial Year

- Default tax year ends **30 June** (Income Tax Act)
- Companies may apply for a **substituted year of income** ending on a different date (e.g., 31 December)
- A transitional year (less than 12 months) arises when changing accounting dates

**Tax filing deadlines for companies:**
- Provisional tax return and payment: last day of **6th and 12th months** of the year
- Final income tax return: last day of the **6th month after year-end**
- Annual Return to URSB: annually (no specific statutory date stated, but failure attracts penalties)

## SECTION H: Import, Export and Customs

### H.1 Customs Framework

Uganda applies the **EAC Common External Tariff (CET)**:

| Category | Rate Range | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Capital goods, raw materials | 0% | Production machinery (HS Chapters 84–85), agricultural inputs |
| Intermediate goods | 10% | Components, semi-finished materials |
| Finished consumer goods | 25% | Most imported finished products |
| Maximum rate | Up to 100% (RSM); up to 60% (Baker Tilly) | Specific sensitive products |
| Average weighted rate | ~25% | — |

**VAT on imports:** 18% VAT is levied on the sum of CIF value + duty + other applicable taxes (i.e., VAT is charged on the duty-paid value, not just the CIF price).

**EAC and COMESA preferences:** Imports from EAC member states and COMESA region enjoy special reduced or zero customs duty rates.

### H.2 Import Procedures

- Administered by Uganda Revenue Authority Customs Department
- Declaration filed via Uganda Customs Management System
- Pre-shipment inspection may be required for certain goods
- Customs clearance withholding tax: 6% of value withheld by customs for non-resident contractors/professionals clearing goods

### H.3 Export Documentation

- Uganda Export Promotion Board supports exporters
- Key exports: coffee, fish, gold, tea, tobacco, sugar, cut flowers, sesame
- AfCFTA provides framework for preferential intra-African trade
- Value-addition in coffee, dairy, fish, and pharmaceuticals is a government priority under NDP IV

## SECTION I: Banking and Financial Services

### I.1 Commercial Banking Landscape

Uganda has a competitive commercial banking sector regulated by the **Bank of Uganda (BoU)**. Listed on the Uganda Securities Exchange, Stanbic Bank and DFCU are among the largest. Key banks include:

- **Stanbic Bank Uganda** (largest by assets; SME lending; trade finance)
- **DFCU Bank** (mortgages, SME, agri-lending)
- **Bank of Baroda Uganda** (Indian trade corridor; SME)
- **Centenary Bank** (rural banking; microfinance-oriented; agri-loans)
- **Equity Bank Uganda** (EAC network; SME; mobile banking)
- **PostBank Uganda** (government-backed; upcountry reach; SME)
- **Pride Microfinance** (Tier III MFI; microloans)

**Central Bank Rate (CBR):** 9.75% (2025). Commercial bank SME lending rates typically run at **CBR + 8–12 percentage points**, resulting in effective rates of approximately **18%–22% per annum** for collateralised SME loans. Unsecured or microfinance lending rates can be 30%–50%+ annually.

**Collateral requirements:** Commercial banks typically require collateral of **130%–150% of loan value**. Acceptable collateral: title deeds (land), motor vehicles, fixed/floating charge over business assets, personal guarantees. First-charge mortgage on titled land is the preferred security instrument.

### I.2 Development Finance Institutions

**Uganda Development Bank (UDB):**
- Government-owned development finance institution
- Lending rates: typically **12%–15% per annum** (concessional vs. commercial banks)
- Focus sectors: manufacturing, agro-processing, tourism, health, education
- Minimum loan sizes generally UGX 50M+
- Requires: business plan, financial projections, audited accounts (where applicable), collateral
- Agricultural Credit Facility (ACF): government-funded; BoU-administered; provides liquidity to commercial banks for on-lending to agri-businesses at reduced rates

**EFC Uganda (Entrepreneur Financial Centre):**
- SME-focused MFI; smaller loan sizes; faster processing

### I.3 Microfinance and SACCOs

**Uganda Microfinance Regulatory Authority (UMRA)** regulates Tier IV institutions:
- **SACCOs (Savings and Credit Cooperatives):** Member-owned; lower interest rates than banks; often the first port of call for small businesses; deposits mobilised from members used for member lending; regulated under UMRA
- **MDIs (Microfinance Deposit-Taking Institutions):** Tier III; regulated by BoU (Pride Microfinance, UGAFODE, etc.)
- **MFIs (Tier IV):** Village savings and loan associations, community microfinance groups; regulated by UMRA

### I.4 Mobile Money and Digital Financial Services

Mobile money is embedded in Uganda's financial ecosystem:
- **MTN Mobile Money** (dominant market share)
- **Airtel Money**

Mobile money services offered: P2P transfers, bill payments, merchant payments, savings products, micro-loans (MTN MoMo Pay loans, Airtel Wewole). Both networks integrate with banks via USSD and mobile apps. **Mobile money tax (OTT/excise on transactions)** applies.

## SECTION J: Intellectual Property

### J.1 Patent Registration

- Filed with URSB or ARIPO (African Regional Intellectual Property Organisation) for regional coverage
- Duration: **20 years**
- Requirements: novelty, inventive step, industrial applicability
- Governed by the Industrial Property Act 2014

**Fees:**
| Stage | Fee (UGX) |
|---|---|
| Application | 180,000 |
| Grant | 300,000 |
| Maintenance (1st annuity) | 48,000 |
| Each subsequent year | Additional 12,000 |
| Conversion to utility model | 12,000 |

### J.2 Trademark Registration

- Filed with URSB
- Initial validity: **7 years from filing date**; renewable for **10-year periods indefinitely**
- Process: name search → file application → examination → gazette publication (60 days) → certificate of registration
- Foreign companies must appoint a local agent (Advocate of the High Court)
- Governing law: Trademarks Act Cap 217

### J.3 Copyright

- Automatic protection from creation (no registration required for protection)
- Optional registration: UGX 50,000 application fee to Registrar of Copyright; published in Uganda Gazette for 60 days
- Duration: **life of creator + 50 years** (natural persons); **50 years from first publication** (corporations)
- Governed by Copyright and Neighbouring Rights Act 2006

## SECTION K: Capital Markets and Securities Exchange

### K.1 Uganda Securities Exchange (USE)

Established 1997; licensed 1998 by Capital Markets Authority. Three market segments:

1. **Main Investment Market Segment (MIMS):** Large companies; minimum share capital UGX 1 billion; minimum net assets UGX 2 billion; 16 listed equities (9 domestic, 7 cross-border)
2. **Fixed Income Securities Market Segment:** Treasury bonds, corporate bonds, preference shares, commercial papers; currently 6 corporate bonds and 39 government treasury bonds
3. **Growth Enterprise Market Segment (GEMS):** Designed for SMEs; less stringent criteria; lower barriers to equity capital raising

**Domestic listings (selected):** Uganda Clays, BAT Uganda, Bank of Baroda (U), DFCU, New Vision, Stanbic Bank Uganda, National Insurance Corporation, UMEME, Cipla Quality Chemicals, MTN Uganda.

**Cross-border listings (selected):** EABL, Kenya Airways, Jubilee Holdings, Equity Bank, KCB, Nation Media Group, Centum.

### K.2 Capital Markets Authority (CMA)

Semi-autonomous body; established 1996. Objectives: investor protection, market efficiency, market confidence, transparency. Issues licences to broker/dealers, investment advisers, and collective investment schemes.

## SECTION L: Business Plan Application Notes

### L.1 Company Overview Section

When writing the company overview section of a business plan:
- State the legal structure and registration status (or planned structure if pre-registration)
- Cite the URSB registration number and Certificate of Incorporation number once obtained
- Include TIN and, where applicable, VAT registration number
- State the trading licence authority (KCCA/district) and licence class
- Reference sector-specific licences (UNBS, NEMA, NDA, UTB etc.) relevant to the industry

### L.2 Risk Section — Regulatory Compliance Risks

Key compliance risks to flag in the risk register:
- **EFRIS non-compliance:** Penalty = double the tax due on affected transactions; mandatory for many sectors from July 2025
- **Beneficial owners register:** Failure to file with URSB within 14 days of incorporation; criminal liability possible
- **Work permit expiry:** Automatic illegal working status for expatriates; deportation risk for the employee; penalty for employer
- **NSSF arrears:** NSSF Act provides for interest charges and prosecution for non-remittance
- **Sector licence non-compliance:** Operating without required NEMA/UNBS/NDA approval — product seizure, premises closure, criminal liability

### L.3 Financial Projections — Compliance Cost Budget

Include in start-up costs:
- URSB registration fees (name reservation + incorporation + stamp duty)
- KCCA/district trading licence fee (annual, recurring)
- NEMA EIA or compliance certificate (project-specific; UGX 500K–5M+ depending on project scale)
- UNBS product certification (varies by product; UGX 200K–2M)
- EFRIS hardware and software setup: approximately UGX 500,000–2,000,000
- Company seal: approximately UGX 50,000–200,000
- Auditor fees: approximately UGX 1M–5M+ per year depending on company size

Include in recurring operating costs:
- Annual trading licence renewal (KCCA/district)
- Annual URSB return filing fee
- NSSF monthly contributions (10% employer on all gross wages)
- PAYE monthly remittances (by 15th of following month)
- Auditor/accountant fees (annual audit and tax compliance)
