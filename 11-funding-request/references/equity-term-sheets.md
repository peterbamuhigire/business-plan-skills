# Equity Term Sheets: A Founder's Reference Guide

## 1. The Two Key Concepts: Economics and Control

Every venture capital term sheet — and indeed every equity financing — reduces to two things that actually matter:

**Economics** — the return the investors will ultimately receive in a liquidity event (company sale, wind-down, or IPO), and all terms that directly affect that return. This covers: valuation, liquidation preference, participation, anti-dilution, dividends, and the option pool.

**Control** — the mechanisms that allow investors to affirmatively direct the business or veto certain decisions. This covers: board composition, protective provisions, drag-along rights, and conversion terms.

> Any term that affects neither economics nor control is largely irrelevant. An investor who spends significant negotiation time on other terms is either inexperienced or using distraction tactics. A competent lawyer will keep you focused on what matters.

Founders receive **common stock**. Investors almost always receive **preferred stock**, which carries special rights. A preferred holder may convert to common at any time, but conversion is one-way — preferred cannot revert once converted.

## 2. Valuation: Pre-Money vs. Post-Money

### Definitions

| Term | Definition | Formula |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Pre-money valuation | What the investor values the company at *before* the investment | Negotiated |
| Post-money valuation | Value of the company *after* the investment | Pre-money + investment amount |
| Investor's ownership % | What the investor buys | Investment ÷ Post-money valuation |

### Worked Example

- Investment: $2 million
- Pre-money valuation: $6 million
- Post-money valuation: $8 million
- Investor ownership: $2M ÷ $8M = **25%**

### The Valuation Trap

When a VC says "I'll invest $5 million at a valuation of $20 million," they almost always mean **post-money** — meaning the pre-money they are offering is $15 million. An entrepreneur who hears "$20 million pre-money" expects the post-money to be $25 million. The gap is real. Always confirm: "I assume you mean $X pre-money."

### How VCs Determine Valuation

Valuation is not an exact science. Key factors:

- **Stage of company** — early stage valuations lean on team quality and market size; later stage valuations are driven by financial performance
- **Competition for the deal** — more VCs bidding drives price up; the best leverage a founder has is multiple term sheets
- **Founder/team experience** — more experienced teams attract higher valuations
- **Market size and trendiness** — large or fashionable sectors attract higher multiples
- **Current economic climate** — macro conditions affect risk appetite and valuations
- **Financial numbers** — revenue, EBITDA, burn rate, growth rate all feed into price at later stages

Valuation at early stage is a guess influenced by many factors. Focus on what you can control and attract multiple interested investors.

## 3. The Option Pool Shuffle

The employee option pool is inextricably linked to valuation and is a common trap. The size of the unissued pool is taken into account in the pre-money calculation — a larger pool demanded by the VC effectively lowers your real pre-money valuation.

### How it Works

Assume:
- Investment: $5 million at $20 million pre-money
- Existing unissued pool: 10%
- VC demands: 20% post-financing unissued pool

The extra 10% comes out of the pre-money, making the effective pre-money **$18 million**, not $20 million. The VC pays the same; founders and existing shareholders are diluted.

### Negotiation Approaches

1. **Fight the pool size** — argue for 15% rather than 20% with an option budget showing actual hire plans
2. **Accept larger pool, negotiate pre-money up** — ask for $22 million pre-money to compensate
3. **Request post-money pool addition** — same pre-money, higher post-money

Come armed with an option budget listing every planned hire and approximate grant size to the next financing date. This is your strongest argument.

## 4. Liquidation Preference

The liquidation preference determines how proceeds are shared when the company is sold (or wound down). It is critical and applies in both good and bad outcomes — a "liquidation event" in VC parlance includes acquisitions and mergers, not just bankruptcy.

### The Two Components

1. **The preference** — how much preferred investors receive *before* common shareholders get anything (expressed as a multiple, e.g., 1×, 2×)
2. **Participation** — whether preferred investors also share in remaining proceeds after receiving their preference

### Types of Participation

| Type | What it means |
|------|--------------|
| **Non-participating** (simple preferred) | Investor takes preference *or* converts to common — not both. Converts at high exits; takes preference at low exits. |
| **Fully participating** | Takes preference *and* shares pro-rata as if converted — never converts. The "have your cake and eat it" structure. |
| **Capped participation** | Participates until a total return cap (e.g., 3×), then stops. Best compromise for founders at mid-range exits. |

### Worked Examples

Assumptions: Series A — $5 million invested at $10 million pre-money. Post-money: $15 million. Investors own 33%, founders own 67%.

**Acquisition at $15 million:**

| Case | Structure | Investor receives | Founders receive |
|------|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| 1 | 1× preference, non-participating | $5M (takes preference; conversion also = $5M) | $10M |
| 2 | 2× preference, non-participating | $10M (takes preference > conversion) | $5M |
| 3 | 1× preference, fully participating | $5M + 33% of $10M = **$8.3M** | $6.7M |
| 4 | 1× preference, 3× cap participating | Same as Case 3 (cap not reached) | $6.7M |

**Acquisition at $30 million:**

| Case | Structure | Investor receives | Founders receive |
|------|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| 1 | 1× preference, non-participating | 33% = $10M (converts) | $20M |
| 2 | 2× preference, non-participating | 33% = $10M (converts — same) | $20M |
| 3 | 1× preference, fully participating | $5M + 33% of $25M = **$13.3M** | $16.7M |
| 4 | 1× preference, 3× cap participating | Same as Case 3 (cap still not reached) | $16.7M |

**Acquisition at $100 million:**

| Case | Structure | Investor receives | Founders receive |
|------|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| 1 | 1× preference, non-participating | 33% = $33M (converts) | $67M |
| 2 | 2× preference, non-participating | 33% = $33M (converts) | $67M |
| 3 | 1× preference, fully participating | $5M + 33% of $95M = **$36.4M** | $63.6M |
| 4 | 1× preference, 3× cap participating | 3× cap = $15M; converts to 33% = $33M | $67M |

### Key Insight

Participation matters most at **mid-range exits** (between 1× and 3× return). At high exits, investors are better off converting to common. At low exits below the preference, common shareholders receive nothing regardless.

**Stacked vs. blended preferences** (multiple rounds): stacked = Series B gets preference before Series A; blended (pari passu) = all series share pro-rata by invested capital. Push for 1× non-participating. Avoid 2× or higher multiples — a red flag about investor character.

## 5. Anti-Dilution Provisions

Anti-dilution protects investors when the company raises a future round at a lower valuation than the previous round (a "down round"). It adjusts the conversion price of earlier preferred stock, effectively increasing the number of common shares the investor receives on conversion.

### Two Types

| Type | How it works | Founder impact |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| **Full ratchet** | Earlier round price drops to match the new lower price, regardless of volume issued | Extremely dilutive; rare except in distressed financings |
| **Broad-based weighted average** | New price accounts for magnitude of down-round issuance | Less dilutive; the standard |

### Weighted Average Formula

`NCP = OCP × (CSO + CSP) ÷ (CSO + CSAP)`

Where: NCP = new conversion price; OCP = old conversion price; CSO = common stock outstanding (fully diluted); CSP = shares purchasable at old price with consideration received; CSAP = shares actually purchased in new issuance.

**Broad-based** includes all convertible securities (options, warrants) in CSO — less punishing than narrow-based, which counts only issued shares.

**Standard carve-outs** (anti-dilution does not apply): employee option pool shares; non-cash consideration shares in acquisitions; equipment/bank financing shares; shares where majority of preferred waive the right.

**Negotiation advice**: Focus on building value so a down round never occurs. Accept broad-based weighted average; resist full ratchet.

## 6. Cap Table Construction

The capitalisation table (cap table) shows who owns what percentage of the company before and after a financing.

### Basic Cap Table Example

Assumptions:
- Founders hold 2,000,000 shares (pre-financing)
- Pre-money valuation: $10 million
- Investment: $5 million
- VC requires 20% unissued option pool (post-financing)

| Class | Shares | % | Notes |
|-------|--------|---|-------|
| Founders | 2,000,000 | 46.67% | Known input |
| Unissued option pool | 857,081 | 20.00% | Calculated |
| Series A investors | 1,428,326 | 33.33% | Calculated |
| **Total** | **4,285,408** | **100%** | |

**Price per share calculation:**
- Total shares = 2,000,000 ÷ 0.4667 = 4,285,408
- VC shares = 4,285,408 × 0.3333 = 1,428,326
- Price per share = $5,000,000 ÷ 1,428,326 = **$3.50/share**

**Verification:** (Founders + option pool) × price = pre-money
(2,000,000 + 857,081) × $3.50 = $10,000,000 ✓

### Dilution Across Rounds

Each subsequent round dilutes all existing shareholders pro-rata. Unissued options that are never granted revert to all shareholders proportionally on exit ("reverse dilution"). Founders should model post-investment ownership before accepting any term sheet.

### Converting Notes in the Cap Table

Three methods exist for calculating conversion of outstanding notes (with different dilution outcomes):

| Method | Who bears dilution | Pre-money fixed? |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Pre-money** (most common) | Founders and Series A share pro-rata | Yes |
| **Percentage-ownership** | Founders bear all dilution; investor % fixed | No |
| **Dollars-invested** (compromise) | Post-money = pre-money + new investment + note principal | No |

Assume pre-money method unless the term sheet specifies otherwise.

## 7. Founder Vesting

### Standard Schedule

**4-year vesting, 1-year cliff:**
- Nothing vests if founder leaves before 12 months
- 25% vests at the 12-month mark (the "cliff")
- Remaining 75% vests monthly over the following 36 months

If a founder departs at 18 months: 18 ÷ 48 = **37.5%** of shares vested.

VCs will impose vesting on founders who don't already have it. Founders starting the company more than a year before the VC financing can typically negotiate credit for time already served.

### Acceleration on Acquisition

| Type | Trigger | What happens |
|------|---------|-------------|
| **Single trigger** | Acquisition alone | All unvested shares vest immediately |
| **Double trigger** | Acquisition *plus* termination without cause | Unvested shares vest on termination |

Double trigger is the standard in VC-backed companies. Single trigger is resisted because acquirers want founders to stay post-acquisition and will build management retention packages around unvested equity. Double trigger with one year of acceleration on termination is a balanced compromise.

### Why Vesting Matters Beyond VCs

Vesting protects all founders from a co-founder departing with a large equity stake. It aligns incentives. View it as alignment architecture for the whole team, not just a VC control mechanism.

## 8. Key Shareholder Rights: Pro-Rata, Drag-Along, Tag-Along

### Pro-Rata Rights (Right of First Refusal on New Shares)

Investors have the right to purchase their proportional share of any future equity issuances to maintain their ownership percentage. Also called **right of first refusal** or **pre-emption rights**.

- Standard and expected — don't fight it
- Watch for "super pro-rata" rights (investor can buy *more* than their pro-rata share) — resist this
- Define what constitutes a "major investor" to limit who receives this right (avoids giving it to very small shareholders)

### Drag-Along Rights

Gives a majority shareholder class (usually preferred) the right to compel all other shareholders to vote in favour of a sale, even those who object.

Two flavours:
1. **Preferred drags common** — if majority of preferred agrees to sell, common must follow
2. **Departed founder drag** — once a founder leaves, their shares are voted proportionally with all other shares (prevents holdout by disgruntled ex-founders)

**Negotiation position**: Push for drag-along to follow majority of *common* stock, not preferred. This means you can only be dragged if a majority of people with equal rights to you also agree.

### Tag-Along Rights (Co-Sale Rights)

If a founder wants to sell shares to a third party, investors have the right to **participate in the sale on a pro-rata basis** under the same terms.

Effectively: founders cannot sell without offering investors the same deal.

Standard language:
> *"The Founders may not sell, transfer, or exchange their stock unless each Investor has an opportunity to participate in the sale on a pro rata basis."*

This clause is nearly impossible to remove from a financing. Negotiate for a floor — small sales (e.g., to buy a house) should be exempt below a certain threshold.

## 9. Convertible Notes and SAFEs

### How Convertible Notes Work

A convertible note is **debt** that converts into equity at the next priced equity round. It avoids the need to negotiate a valuation at the time of the seed investment.

**Key terms:**

| Term | Typical range | What it means |
|------|--------------|--------------|
| **Discount rate** | 10–30% (20% most common) | Note converts at this % below the next round price |
| **Interest rate** | 5–12% (8% typical) | Annual interest on the loan principal |
| **Valuation cap** | Negotiated | Maximum effective valuation at which the note converts |
| **Maturity date** | 12–24 months typical | Date by which conversion or repayment must occur |
| **Qualified financing threshold** | e.g., $1 million minimum raise | Minimum round size that triggers automatic conversion |

### Discount Mechanics Example

- Convertible note: $500,000 with 20% discount
- Next round: $1.00 per share (Series A)
- Note conversion price: $0.80 per share ($1.00 × 80%)
- Note investors receive: $500,000 ÷ $0.80 = **625,000 shares**
- Series A investors receive: $1,000,000 ÷ $1.00 = **1,000,000 shares**

### Valuation Cap Mechanics Example

- Note: $100,000 with 20% discount and $4 million cap
- Next round: $20 million pre-money valuation
- Without cap: effective conversion valuation = $20M × 80% = $16M — unattractive for early investor
- With $4M cap: investor converts at $4M effective valuation regardless of actual round price

VCs may treat the valuation cap as a ceiling for their own price negotiation. Keep note terms confidential until a new VC has agreed to a price.

### Arguments For and Against

| Convertible Note | Equity Round |
|-----------------|-------------|
| No valuation negotiation up front | Valuation set immediately |
| Faster and cheaper to document | More thorough — preferred stock rights established |
| Defers price — may result in higher effective price than equity | Clear ownership picture from day one |
| Maturity date creates pressure if no priced round follows | No maturity risk |
| Debt rights can give investors excessive leverage if things go wrong | Investors only have equity rights |

### SAFEs (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)

Developed by Y Combinator. A SAFE is a warrant-like instrument — it is **not debt** and carries no interest or maturity date.

| Feature | Convertible Note | SAFE |
|---------|-----------------|------|
| Interest | Yes (5–12%) | No |
| Maturity date | Yes | No |
| Explicit pro-rata right | Usually included | May not be included |
| Balance sheet treatment | Liability | Equity instrument |
| Investor leverage if company struggles | High (debt) | Lower |

SAFEs can have caps and discounts like notes. A **Most Favoured Nation (MFN)** clause ensures that if better terms are given to future SAFE investors, those terms flow back to earlier SAFE holders.

**For Uganda/East Africa context**: Most angel networks (e.g., East Africa Business Angels Network, Village Capital cohorts) and impact investors (Acumen, Novastar) use equity rounds rather than SAFEs, as SAFE documentation is less familiar to local legal counsel. Convertible notes are used in accelerator contexts (e.g., Seedstars, Grindstone). Confirm your lawyers have experience with whichever instrument you use.

## 10. Board Composition and Control

### Board Structure

The board of directors has the power to fire the CEO, approve budgets, option plans, financing rounds, and major expenditures.

**Typical early stage board (3-person):**
1. Founder/CEO (elected by common stock holders)
2. VC (elected by preferred stock holders)
3. Outside independent director (elected by mutual consent)

**Typical early stage board (5-person):**
1. Founder
2. CEO
3. VC investor 1
4. VC investor 2
5. Outside independent director

The outside director is the critical swing vote and mediator. Aim for someone with relevant industry experience who can mentor the executive team. Outside board members typically receive options for 0.25–0.5% of the company, vesting over 2–4 years.

### Board Observers

VCs sometimes request a board observer seat (non-voting). Be cautious: observers can influence discussion without accountability. Limit observers where possible, especially in early stage companies where board meetings should be working sessions.

### Protective Provisions (Investor Veto Rights)

Protective provisions give preferred shareholders veto rights over specific company actions. Standard provisions require investor consent to:

- Change the rights of preferred stock
- Increase or decrease authorised shares
- Create new share classes senior to or equal to preferred
- Buy back common stock
- Sell the company or effect a change of control
- Amend articles of incorporation or bylaws
- Change the size of the board
- Pay or declare dividends
- Borrow money above a threshold (e.g., $100,000)
- Declare bankruptcy
- License away intellectual property

**Negotiation notes:**
- Increasing the debt threshold is the most commonly accepted change
- Push for a single class vote across all series of preferred (Series A, B, C voting together) rather than separate class vetoes for each series
- Avoid consent percentages above 50.1% — a 90% threshold lets a 10.1% investor block the whole company

## 11. What to Negotiate Hard vs. What to Concede

### Terms That Matter — Fight Hard

| Term | Why it matters | Target position |
|------|---------------|----------------|
| Pre-money valuation | Determines your dilution | As high as justified with market data |
| Option pool size | Larger pool = lower effective pre-money | Match to realistic hire budget; no more than needed to next round |
| Liquidation preference structure | Determines founder payout in most exits | 1× non-participating; resist full participation |
| Participation cap | Limits preferred double-dipping | 3× cap if participation cannot be avoided |
| Board composition | Controls the company's destiny | Balanced board; not VC-controlled |
| Protective provisions | Effective veto rights | Single class vote; no separate series vetoes |
| Vesting — founder credit | Prevents clawback on prior work | Negotiate credit for time already served |
| Anti-dilution type | Down-round protection mechanism | Broad-based weighted average only; never full ratchet |

### Terms That Matter Less — Concede Gracefully

| Term | Why you can concede | Notes |
|------|--------------------|----|
| Registration rights | Only relevant at IPO; bankers will override anyway | Accept standard terms |
| Right of first refusal on future rounds | Expected by all investors; generally fine | Limit to major investors |
| Co-sale / tag-along rights | Near-impossible to remove | Negotiate a small-sale floor threshold |
| Dividends (non-cumulative) | Rarely declared; board-controlled | Ensure board majority required to declare |
| Information rights | Transparency is good practice anyway | Accept standard quarterly/annual reporting |
| Drag-along | Can protect you in a sale | Push for common majority trigger, not preferred |

### The Only Three Things That Matter in a Negotiation

1. Achieving a good and fair result
2. Not damaging the personal relationship in the process
3. Fully understanding the deal you are striking

Focus on economics (valuation, option pool, liquidation preference) and control (board, protective provisions). Time spent elsewhere is generally wasted and signals inexperience.

## 12. The VC Investment Process

Understanding how VCs make decisions helps founders manage the fundraising process more effectively.

### How VCs Evaluate Deals

1. **Introduction channel** — Warm referrals from trusted sources outperform cold outreach. Many VCs will not fund without a prior connection.
2. **Seniority of your contact** — Associates source deals but cannot get them done. Ensure a general partner or managing director is actively engaged.
3. **Due diligence** — VCs review deck, financial model, team bios, customer pipeline, and competitive analysis. A VC demanding five-year projections for a pre-revenue company is not an early stage investor.
4. **Partnership approval** — Most decisions need a full partnership vote. Always confirm the term sheet has been partnership-approved before entering exclusivity.
5. **Term sheet to close** — A signed term sheet is not cash in the bank. Legal documentation follows. Most close unless diligence uncovers material problems.

### How VC Funds Work (Implications for Founders)

- Fund life: 10 years; new investments mainly in first 5 years
- Management fee: ~2% per year (pays salaries); carried interest: 20% of profits above returned capital (this is the real VC incentive)
- VCs target 10× returns on individual investments — most portfolio companies will fail or return less than invested
- A VC in year 8 of a 10-year fund faces pressure to generate liquidity; this shapes their behaviour and may push for earlier exits

### What VCs Look for Beyond the Numbers

- Team quality (especially first-time founders with no track record)
- Market size sufficient to produce a 10× return on their investment
- Business model defensibility
- Early traction (paying customers, growth rate)
- Founder-market fit — deep domain knowledge

To generate leverage: run a competitive process, time investor processes to converge, and never disclose other investors' names until competing term sheets arrive.

## Uganda and East Africa Context

### Investor Landscape

The East African equity ecosystem is less developed than Silicon Valley. Key investor types relevant to Uganda-based businesses:

| Investor type | Examples | Typical instrument | Typical cheque |
|---------------|----------|-------------------|---------------|
| Angel networks | EABN, VC4A, Viktoria Business Angels | Equity or convertible note | $10K–$100K |
| Accelerator funds | Seedstars, Grindstone, Founders Factory Africa | Equity (5–10%) | $50K–$250K |
| Impact investors | Acumen, Novastar, Goodwell | Preferred equity | $250K–$2M |
| Development finance institutions | IFC, BIO, FMO, aBi Finance | Equity or quasi-equity | $500K–$10M+ |
| Uganda Development Bank | UDB | Debt (some equity windows) | UGX 100M–5B |
| Diaspora investors | Via VC4A, EAVCA networks | Equity or SAFE | $25K–$500K |

### Key Localisation Notes

- Most East African rounds use **equity**, not SAFEs — confirm legal counsel is experienced with preferred share structures under Ugandan company law (Companies Act, Cap.110)
- Impact investors (Acumen, Novastar) target **2–3× exit multiples** over 5–7 years, not 10× VC returns; terms will be lighter than US-style VC deals
- DFI equity investments require audited accounts, ESMP compliance, board composition policies, and quarterly reporting
- Uganda's **thin exit market** (no SME stock exchange, limited M&A activity) means liquidation preferences matter even more — preferred investors will focus on acquisition scenarios as the exit path

## Founder Quick Reference: 10 Things to Know Before Signing a Term Sheet

1. **Pre-money vs. post-money** — Always confirm which one the VC means when they quote a valuation. The difference is the investment amount, not a rounding error.

2. **The option pool shuffle** — A large option pool demanded by the VC comes out of your pre-money, not theirs. Know your actual effective pre-money after accounting for pool expansion.

3. **Liquidation preference is your number one economic risk** — 1× non-participating is fair. Full participation means the VC gets paid twice — first their preference, then their pro-rata share. Resist it.

4. **Participation compounds across rounds** — Whatever liquidation preference structure you accept in your seed or Series A round sets a precedent for all future rounds. Start clean.

5. **Anti-dilution means broad-based weighted average** — Full ratchet is toxic. If a VC insists on full ratchet, treat it as a serious red flag.

6. **The board controls your destiny** — The board can fire you. Aim for a balanced board (equal founder and investor seats) with a truly independent outside director. Do not let investors hold a majority of board seats.

7. **Vesting protects you too** — It prevents a co-founder from walking away with a large equity stake after contributing little. Negotiate credit for time already served; accept double-trigger acceleration.

8. **Convertible notes defer problems, not eliminate them** — The valuation cap sets a ceiling that future VCs will treat as your max price. Disclose note terms only after price is agreed with new investors.

9. **Multiple term sheets are your best negotiating tool** — Never name other VCs you're talking to. Time your investor processes to converge. The moment you have two competing term sheets, your economics improve dramatically.

10. **The term sheet is the blueprint** — What's in the term sheet usually ends up in the final agreements. Negotiate it seriously, not as a preliminary document. Once signed, you are morally (if not legally) committed to the deal.
