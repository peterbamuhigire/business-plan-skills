# Credit Assessment Frameworks: 5 Cs and CAMPARI

Reference file for `11-funding-request` skill. Provides the two principal credit evaluation frameworks
used by Ugandan commercial banks and development finance institutions, with Uganda-specific context,
worked examples, and guidance on structuring a funding request to satisfy each criterion.

---

## 1. The 5 Cs of Credit (Traditional Framework)

The 5 Cs framework is the foundational model taught in banking and applied universally across
commercial lenders. Each C represents a dimension of credit risk.

### 1.1 Character

**Definition:** The borrower's integrity, reputation, and demonstrated willingness to repay obligations.

Character is the most subjective C, yet lenders consistently rank it first. A technically strong
financial case can be overridden by weak character signals.

**Evidence a lender looks for:**
- Credit Bureau of Uganda (CBU) report — clean history or explained blemishes
- Director CVs showing relevant career progression
- NIN (National Identification Number) confirmed and matching company records
- CRB clearance certificate (for formal bank applications)
- Character references from LC1, religious leaders, employer, or established business partners
- Company registration history — consistent ownership, no pattern of abandoned entities
- Court/litigation search — no outstanding judgements
- Tax compliance certificate from URA

**How to strengthen this section in your plan:**
Present director profiles that emphasise track record in the sector, prior repayment of loans
(including SACCOs and microfinance), and community standing. Attach a CRB clearance certificate
as an appendix.

---

### 1.2 Capacity

**Definition:** The business's demonstrable ability to generate sufficient cash flow to service the
proposed debt without distress.

**Key metric: Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)**

```
DSCR = EBITDA ÷ Annual Debt Service

Where:
  EBITDA     = Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation, and Amortisation
  Annual Debt Service = Annual Principal Repayments + Annual Interest Payments
```

**Bank minimum: DSCR ≥ 1.25x.** A ratio of 1.25x means the business generates 25 shillings of
operating profit for every 20 shillings of debt service — a 20% buffer. UDB and concessional
lenders may accept 1.20x; commercial banks in Uganda typically require 1.25x–1.40x.

**Evidence a lender looks for:**
- Three years of audited or management accounts (for existing businesses)
- 36-month cash flow projection with stated assumptions
- Income statement showing progressive revenue growth
- Schedule of existing debt obligations (EMIs, lease payments, overdraft)
- Sensitivity analysis showing DSCR under a stress scenario (e.g., 20% revenue reduction)

---

### 1.3 Capital

**Definition:** The owner's own financial commitment to the project — "skin in the game."

Lenders do not finance 100% of any project. Owner equity demonstrates confidence in the venture
and provides a cushion absorbing initial losses.

**Minimum expectation:**
- Commercial banks (Uganda): 20–30% of total project cost as owner equity
- UDB Development Finance: 30% minimum own equity
- ACF (Agricultural Credit Facility): 30% own equity

**What counts as equity:**
- Cash already invested or held in business accounts
- Retained earnings (shown on balance sheet)
- Director loans converted to equity
- Existing assets contributed to the business (land, equipment, stock) — valued by approved valuer
- Grant funds already received and deployed

**What does NOT count:**
- The loan itself
- "Sweat equity" alone (may count partially for grant proposals, not bank loans)
- Projected future profits

---

### 1.4 Conditions

**Definition:** The purpose of the loan and the broader economic and market conditions affecting
repayment probability.

**Evidence a lender looks for:**
- Clearly stated, productive loan purpose (not vague "working capital")
- Itemised use-of-funds schedule matching the loan amount
- Evidence that market conditions support projected revenues
- Regulatory environment stable (e.g., no pending policy reversal affecting the sector)
- Industry benchmarks showing the sector is viable

**Uganda-specific conditions the lender will consider:**
- Sector exposure — are they already over-exposed to this sector?
- Exchange rate risk — does the business import inputs paid in USD?
- Seasonality — does cash flow dip in certain months and can loan servicing still be met?
- GoU economic policy — does the business depend on a subsidised input or price control?

---

### 1.5 Collateral

**Definition:** Assets pledged as security against default. Secondary repayment source if the
primary source (business cash flow) fails.

**Minimum coverage:** 125% of loan value. Most Ugandan commercial banks require 130–150%.

**Uganda collateral types (most to least preferred):**

| Collateral Type | Notes |
|---|---|
| Freehold land title (Kampala/peri-urban) | Best. Must be registered in Torrens system. |
| Mailo land title (Buganda) | Accepted, but check kibanja/customary complications |
| Leasehold title (≥15 years remaining) | Accepted; shorter leases may be discounted heavily |
| Vehicle logbook (CAN-verified) | Up to 60% of forced-sale value; depreciates fast |
| Debenture over business assets | Plant, equipment, stock — lender appoints receiver if default |
| Personal guarantee from director | Strengthens application; requires personal NIN + assets |
| UCGS (Uganda Credit Guarantee Scheme) | GoU-backed guarantee covering 50–80% of loan; accessed via participating banks |
| Life insurance policy | Sometimes accepted as additional security |

**Important:** Ugandan courts process collateral realisation slowly. Lenders therefore require
over-collateralisation as a practical risk buffer.

## 2. CAMPARI Framework (Uganda Commercial Bank Standard)

CAMPARI is the preferred mnemonic used by Ugandan commercial bank credit committees. It maps
closely to the 5 Cs but adds granularity on Ability, Purpose, Amount, and Insurance.

### C — Character

Same as the 5 Cs Character dimension. See §1.1 above.

Additional CAMPARI emphasis: lenders explicitly assess whether the borrower has given false
information in the past, has CCB/CRB listings, or has been a director of a company that was
wound up leaving debts unpaid.

---

### A — Ability (Management Capability)

**Definition:** Does the management team have the sector-specific expertise and operational
competence to execute the business plan?

**Evidence a lender looks for:**
- Director CVs with at least 2–3 years of relevant sector experience
- Professional qualifications relevant to the sector:
  - Finance/accounting roles: ICPAU (Institute of Certified Public Accountants of Uganda)
  - Legal matters: ULS (Uganda Law Society)
  - Engineering: ERB (Engineers Registration Board)
  - Medical/health: AHPC (Allied Health Professionals Council)
  - Management consultants: UMC (Uganda Management Centre)
- Operations plan showing the team understands production processes, regulatory requirements,
  and supply chains
- Evidence of prior management of a comparable-scale operation

**In the business plan:** The management team section (skill `09-management-team`) feeds directly
into CAMPARI Ability. Ensure CVs are attached as appendices.

---

### M — Means (Financial Capacity)

**Definition:** Does the business have the financial means — equity, assets, and projected cash
flow — to support the loan?

**DSCR formula (repeated for CAMPARI context):**

```
DSCR = EBITDA ÷ Annual Debt Service  ≥ 1.25x (bank minimum)
```

**Additional means indicators:**
- Owner equity ≥ 20% of project cost (30% for UDB/ACF)
- Net worth positive (total assets > total liabilities on pro-forma balance sheet)
- Cash flow positive within 12 months of loan drawdown
- Working capital ratio ≥ 1.5x (current assets ÷ current liabilities)

---

### P — Purpose (Loan Purpose)

**Definition:** The specific, legitimate, and productive use of the requested funds.

**What lenders reject:**
- Vague applications: "general business expenses" or "to grow the business"
- Personal expenses disguised as business purposes
- Refinancing existing debt without compelling justification
- Speculative purposes (stock market, forex trading)

**What lenders accept:**
- Capital expenditure: equipment, machinery, leasehold improvements, vehicles
- Working capital: stock purchase, pre-funded orders, seasonal inventory build
- Construction/renovation: with approved plans and BOQ
- Trade finance: LCs, invoice discounting, supply chain finance

**Best practice:** Provide an itemised use-of-funds table (each line item, cost, and supplier
if known). Tie each item to a specific phase in the implementation timeline.

---

### A — Amount (Loan Sizing)

**Definition:** Is the loan amount correct — neither underfunded nor over-borrowed?

**Sizing rules of thumb for Ugandan SMEs:**
- Total loan ≤ 3× annual revenue (for SMEs without substantial asset base)
- Loan should equal the sum of specific, costed line items — not a round number
- Loan structure should match purpose:
  - Capital expenditure → term loan (3–7 years)
  - Seasonal working capital → revolving credit facility or overdraft (12 months)
  - Construction → drawdown facility (stage payments tied to construction milestones)

**Red flags that trigger lender concern:**
- Loan amount is exactly a round number with no itemised justification
- Loan significantly exceeds the business's asset base
- Multiple loans from different lenders (total exposure question)

---

### R — Repayment (Repayment Schedule)

**Definition:** Identification of the specific revenue streams that will service the debt, with a
realistic repayment schedule.

**Best practice in the business plan:**
- Show a month-by-month loan repayment schedule for at least Year 1 and Year 2
- Identify the primary repayment source (e.g., "monthly revenue from catering contracts averaging
  UGX 18M will service the UGX 4.8M monthly EMI")
- Confirm the break-even month occurs before the first repayment date
- Stress-test DSCR: show that even at 80% of projected revenue, DSCR ≥ 1.25x
- Include a grace period request (if needed) with justification — e.g., 3–6 months for
  construction completion before revenue commences

---

### I — Insurance (Collateral and Risk Mitigation)

**Definition:** What security does the lender hold if the primary and secondary repayment sources
both fail?

**Insurance in CAMPARI covers both:**
1. **Collateral** — physical assets pledged (see 5 Cs Collateral, §1.5 above)
2. **Insurance policies** — asset insurance, life/key-person insurance, crop insurance

**Uganda-specific insurance requirements:**
- Immovable collateral: fire insurance at minimum; lender named as beneficiary
- Vehicles pledged: comprehensive motor insurance; lender noted on policy
- Agricultural assets: crop/livestock insurance (via APA Insurance or Jubilee; ACF requires this)
- Key-person life insurance: often required for director-dependent SMEs; lender as co-beneficiary

**Compensating factors when collateral is weak:**
- UCGS guarantee coverage (50–80%) reduces collateral shortfall
- Strong DSCR (≥ 1.50x) may allow some collateral concession
- Personal guarantee from a creditworthy director or sponsor
- Assignment of a confirmed purchase order or offtake agreement as additional security

## 3. Uganda-Specific Credit Context

### 3.1 Interest Rates and Lending Environment

Uganda maintains relatively high SME lending rates driven by the Central Bank Rate (CBR) and
structural credit risk premiums:

- **CBR (Bank of Uganda base rate):** 9.75% (Q1 2025/26)
- **Commercial bank SME rates:** 19–22% per annum (CBR + 8–12 percentage point margin)
- **Microfinance lending rates:** 24–36% per annum
- **UDB concessional rates:** 12–15% per annum (sector-priority loans)
- **ACF (Agricultural Credit Facility):** 10% fixed per annum

Source: (RSM/Baker Tilly, 2025 — Doing Business in Uganda 2025/26)

### 3.2 Collateral Requirements

- Commercial banks: 130–150% of loan value (loan-to-value ratio 67–77%)
- UDB Development Finance: 110–125% of loan value
- Microfinance/Pride: 100–120% (group guarantee accepted in lieu of physical collateral for some)

### 3.3 SME Credit Access Gap

Only 6% of Ugandan SMEs have access to bank loans, compared with 44.1% in Kenya (IFC CPSD, 2022).
This structural gap is not primarily a creditworthiness issue — it reflects collateral constraints,
documentation barriers, and lender risk aversion. A well-prepared business plan with strong CAMPARI
compliance materially improves approval odds.

**Practical implication for plan writers:** Do not assume the business will qualify for commercial
bank credit simply because the DSCR looks adequate. Explicitly address collateral availability and
consider whether UCGS or UDB is a more realistic first-round lender.

### 3.4 Credit Approval Timelines

Following the Financial Institutions Act amendment (May 2024) requiring full board-level approval
for certain credit categories, commercial bank credit approval time has lengthened to approximately
**6 weeks** for standard SME loans. UDB applications typically take **8–12 weeks**. Plan the
implementation timeline accordingly — do not assume funds arrive at month zero.

## 4. Credit Guarantee Schemes

### 4.1 UCGS — Uganda Credit Guarantee Scheme

- **Administrator:** Ministry of Finance, Planning and Economic Development (MoFPED)
- **Coverage:** 50–80% guarantee on approved SME loans
- **Effect:** Reduces collateral required from the borrower; lender's net exposure is 20–50% of loan
- **Access:** Through participating commercial banks (Stanbic, ABSA, Equity, Centenary, dfcu, others)
- **Eligibility:** Registered SME, bankable plan, viable purpose, borrower contributes own equity

### 4.2 ACF — Agricultural Credit Facility

- **Administrator:** Bank of Uganda (BoU)
- **Interest rate:** 10% fixed per annum
- **Eligibility:** Agribusiness only; farmers, processors, cooperatives, agro-dealers
- **Own equity required:** 30% minimum
- **Accessed through:** 21 participating financial institutions
- **Tenor:** Up to 8 years for long-gestation agribusiness investments

Source: (BoU CBR data, 2025)

### 4.3 WEF — Women's Entrepreneurship Fund

- **Administrator:** Ministry of Trade, Industry and Cooperatives (MTIC)
- **Target:** Women-owned or women-led businesses (≥51% female ownership)
- **Terms:** Concessional interest; revolving fund model
- **Documentation required:** Business registration with female directors named

### 4.4 aBi Finance — Agribusiness Initiative

- **Focus:** Agribusiness SMEs in production, processing, and trade
- **Mechanism:** Works through financial intermediaries (MFIs, SACCOs, commercial banks)
- **Products:** Term loans, value chain finance, agricultural leasing
- **Target clients:** Cooperatives, farmer organisations, agro-processors

## 5. DSCR Worked Example

The following illustrates how to calculate DSCR and diagnose shortfalls.

```
ILLUSTRATIVE DSCR CALCULATION

Business: Small restaurant, Kampala
Loan requested: UGX 80,000,000 at 19% p.a., 4-year term

Annual EBITDA (from Year 2 projections):    UGX 36,000,000

Annual debt service:
  Annual principal repayment (80M ÷ 4):     UGX 20,000,000
  Annual interest (approx. on declining):   UGX 15,200,000
  Total annual debt service:                UGX 35,200,000

DSCR = 36,000,000 ÷ 35,200,000 = 1.02x

⚠ Below 1.25x bank minimum. Loan is unlikely to be approved as structured.

REMEDIAL OPTIONS:

Option 1: Reduce loan to UGX 60,000,000 (increase owner equity by UGX 20M)
  Annual principal repayment (60M ÷ 4):     UGX 15,000,000
  Annual interest (approx.):                UGX 11,400,000
  Total annual debt service:                UGX 26,400,000
  DSCR = 36,000,000 ÷ 26,400,000 = 1.36x ✓

Option 2: Extend term to 6 years (same loan amount of UGX 80M)
  Annual principal repayment (80M ÷ 6):     UGX 13,333,000
  Annual interest (approx. on declining):   UGX 11,400,000
  Total annual debt service:                UGX 24,733,000
  DSCR = 36,000,000 ÷ 24,733,000 = 1.46x ✓

Option 3: Improve EBITDA through efficiency measures
  If EBITDA can be raised to UGX 44,000,000:
  DSCR = 44,000,000 ÷ 35,200,000 = 1.25x ✓ (borderline)

RECOMMENDATION: Combine Options 1 and 2 for a comfortable margin and lower risk
to the borrower — reduced loan at extended term maximises DSCR buffer.
```

## 6. Presenting Credit Compliance in the Business Plan

When writing the funding request section, structure the narrative to address CAMPARI explicitly —
even if the headings are not labelled as such. Lenders recognise the pattern.

**Checklist for a CAMPARI-compliant funding request:**

- [ ] Director CVs attached (Character, Ability)
- [ ] CRB clearance certificate obtained (Character)
- [ ] NIN confirmed for all directors (Character)
- [ ] DSCR calculated and shown to be ≥ 1.25x (Means)
- [ ] Owner equity contribution confirmed as ≥ 20–30% (Means)
- [ ] Loan purpose itemised line by line (Purpose)
- [ ] Loan amount equals sum of line items — no unexplained rounding (Amount)
- [ ] Repayment schedule included month-by-month (Repayment)
- [ ] Break-even month identified and precedes first repayment (Repayment)
- [ ] Collateral listed with estimated values and documentation status (Insurance)
- [ ] Insurance policies on collateral assets confirmed or planned (Insurance)
- [ ] UCGS or other guarantee scheme considered if collateral is insufficient (Insurance)
