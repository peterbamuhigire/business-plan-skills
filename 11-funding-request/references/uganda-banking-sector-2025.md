# Uganda Banking Sector Reference — 2023/2025

*Primary source: Uganda Bankers' Association, Banking Sector Report for the Year 2023. Supplementary source: Norfund / DFCU Bank — Increasing Access to Finance in Uganda (case study).*

---

## 1. Sector Overview

### Regulatory Structure

Bank of Uganda (BoU) supervises all Supervised Financial Institutions (SFIs) in tiers:

| Tier | Institution Type | Examples |
|------|-----------------|---------|
| Tier 1 | Commercial Banks (full-service) | Stanbic, Absa, DFCU, Centenary, Equity, KCB |
| Tier 2 | Credit Institutions (limited services) | Pride Microfinance, UGAFODE |
| Tier 3 | Microfinance Deposit-Taking Institutions (MDIs) | Yako Microfinance |
| Tier 4 | Non-deposit SACCOs and money lenders | Various, not BOU-supervised |

As of end-2023, there were **32 Supervised Financial Institutions** (SFIs) in total; 28 of 32 reported profits for the year. One MDI — EFC Uganda Limited — ceased operations in January 2024.

### Sector Size (end-December 2023)

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total commercial bank assets | UGX 49.5 trillion |
| Year-on-year asset growth | 8.9% |
| Net loans and advances | UGX 20.5 trillion |
| Customer deposits | UGX 34.1 trillion |
| Total liabilities | UGX 40.1 trillion |
| Total equity | UGX 9.4 trillion |
| Cumulative net profit (2023) | UGX 1.4 trillion (+20% YoY) |

Tier 2 and 3 institutions held an additional UGX 1.3 trillion in total assets, with UGX 649 billion in customer deposits.

### Credit to Private Sector

- Private sector credit (PSC) from commercial banks: **UGX 20.5 trillion** (December 2023)
- PSC grew **8% YoY** in 2023 (up from 6% in 2022)
- Historical context: In 2004 the ratio of credit to GDP was only **8%**, far below the Sub-Saharan Africa average of 53% at that time
- PSC grew **55% over the six years from 2018 to 2023**, driven particularly by personal/household loans (+97%), manufacturing (+65%), and real estate/construction (+56%)

### Sector Composition of Credit (December 2023)

| Sector | Share of Total PSC |
|--------|-------------------|
| Personal and household loans | 23.1% |
| Building, mortgage, construction, real estate | 20.6% |
| Trade | 16.3% |
| Manufacturing | 13.9% |
| Agriculture | ~6% |
| Other (electricity, transport, other) | ~20% |

These top four sectors have consistently accounted for over 70% of private sector credit for the past decade.

### DFCU Bank — Detailed Profile

DFCU began commercial banking operations in **2000**, established originally by the Ugandan government and CDC (UK Development Finance Institution). It listed on the Uganda Securities Exchange in 2004. Arise B.V. (a vehicle of Norfund, FMO, and Rabo Development) holds the majority stake.

**Growth trajectory:**
- 2003: USD 28M loan book; 7 branches; 217 staff
- 2017: USD 360M loan book; 67 branches; 756 staff (48% women employees)
- DFCU acquired the assets of Crane Bank in 2017 (via an Arise-funded bridging facility), becoming one of Uganda's top three banks

**Loan book profile (2017 data, most recent detailed breakdown):**
- SME segment: **89% of total loan book**
- Long-term loans (5+ year tenor): **47% of loan book** (significantly above market average)
- Agriculture: **16% of loan book**
- 54% of loans disbursed to sectors defined as primary growth sectors by the Government

**Key products:**
- Business growth loans (working capital, trade, stock purchase)
- Trade financing (letters of credit, invoice financing)
- Leasing (plant, machinery, commercial vehicles)
- Agriculture-specific products using group guarantees and deposits (not just land collateral)
- Mortgage finance

**Special programmes:**
- **Women in Business (WiB)** — established 2007; preferential terms, business training, mentorship, financial advisory (one-stop Women Advisory Centre); ~19,000 registered women; >3,000 benefited from WiB loans; three segments: professionals, traders, and women in agribusiness
- **Financial Literacy Programme** — rolled out 2014; benefited over 84,000 investment clubs
- **Agri-business Development Centre** — trains smallholder farmers to improve bankability
- **Women Business Advisory Council** — established 2012; first such initiative in Uganda

## 3. Lending Rates and Conditions

### Central Bank Rate (CBR) — 2023 Trajectory

| Period | CBR |
|--------|-----|
| January–July 2023 | 10.0% |
| August–December 2023 | 9.5% |
| Rediscount rate (from August 2023) | 12.5% |
| Bank rate (from August 2023) | 13.5% |

**Outlook (2024–2025):** The BoU raised the CBR to **10%** in an unscheduled March 2024 meeting (50 bps increase) in response to renewed inflationary and exchange rate pressures. Gradual rate cuts are expected from early 2025 as inflation eases, with a target of approximately **8.5% by end-2025**.

### Lending Rates — 2023 Data

| Metric | Rate |
|--------|------|
| Weighted average UGX lending rate (Dec 2023) | 17.5% |
| Weighted average UGX lending rate (peak, Jun 2023) | 19.27% |
| Average prime lending rate (commercial banks) | 20.5–20.6% |
| Average lending rate on USD-denominated loans | 8.8–9.2% |
| Commercial bank lending rate (April 2024) | 17.7% |

**Long-run averages (2011–2023):**
- CBR average: 11.4%
- UGX weighted lending rate average: 21.1%
- USD lending rate average: 8.4%

The trend is downward over 13 years, but rates remain significantly above regional peers. Uganda's spread between deposit rate and lending rate (net interest margin) averaged **10.76%** in 2023 — one of the highest in East Africa.

### Collateral and Loan-to-Value

- Most commercial banks require **land and property** as primary collateral
- Typical loan-to-value (LTV): **60–80%** of forced sale value of collateral
- SME loans frequently require additional security: personal guarantees, debentures over business assets, or assignment of insurance
- Agriculture loans at DFCU and specialist lenders use **group deposits and guarantees** as alternative collateral (particularly for smallholder groups)
- Women-owned enterprises face a systemic barrier: customary land tenure (approximately 84% of land in Uganda is customarily held) limits ability to provide titled collateral

### Loan Tenors

- Working capital / trade finance: 3–12 months
- Term loans (equipment, vehicles): 2–5 years
- Mortgage / long-term investment: 5–15 years (limited availability; DFCU deliberately lengthened its book with Norfund support — 47% long-term by 2016)
- The sector's Loan-to-Deposit ratio averaged **60%** in 2023, indicating significant unused lending capacity

| Channel | % of Adults |
|---------|------------|

| Pensions | 4% |

| Insurance | 2% |

### The Access Gap — Key Facts

- **Only 14% of adults use bank services** despite significant mobile money penetration
- Urban adults use banks at twice the rural rate: **24% urban vs 10% rural**
- Women are disproportionately excluded: only 10% of female adults use banks vs ~18% of male adults
- Total number of bank accounts (December 2023): **23.6 million**, but **94% hold balances below UGX 1 million** — the majority are low-value transactional accounts
- **93% of total deposit volume** is concentrated in accounts with balances above UGX 12 million — extreme top-heavy concentration
- Only **10% of firms** in Uganda have a bank loan or line of credit (World Bank Enterprise Surveys, cited in Norfund source) — less than half the Sub-Saharan Africa average
- Large foreign-owned banks historically focus on corporates and high-net-worth individuals; SMEs face a structural financing gap
- Funding to SMEs is mostly **short-term**, creating rollover and interest rate risk and discouraging long-term investment
- An estimated **5.2 million bankable Ugandans** lack access to banking services partly due to financial illiteracy (DFCU data)
- **60% of MSMEs** use commercial banks at all (FSDU data, circa 2016–17)

### Access Infrastructure (2023)

| Access Point | Count / Metric |
|--------------|---------------|
| Total ATMs (banking sector) | 984 |
| Bank agents (December 2023) | 22,217 (76% active) |
| Agent banking transactions (2023) | 8.1 million transactions worth UGX 15.1 trillion |
| Mobile money registered customers | 43.4 million |
| Mobile money transactions (2023) | 6.4 billion transactions worth UGX 227.5 trillion |
| Internet subscribers | 27 million (59 per 100 Ugandans) |
| Mobile phone ownership (adults) | 72% (17.6 million) |

Agent banking has been the fastest-growing access channel, expanding at an annual average of **44%** over the past decade, while branches and ATMs have been broadly flat.

## 5. NPL Ratios and Sector Health

### Non-Performing Loans (2023)

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Commercial bank NPLs (December 2023) | UGX 1.22 trillion |
| NPL growth (2023 YoY) | +2% (minimal) |
| NPL ratio to total gross loans (sector average) | 5.9% |
| NPL coverage ratio (provisions vs NPLs) | 91–92% |
| Tier 2/3 NPLs | UGX 37.3 billion (+9% YoY) |

An NPL ratio of **2–5%** is generally considered indicative of good asset quality; the sector's 5.9% sits at the upper boundary of acceptable.

**Historical context:** The sector peak NPL ratio was reached during and after the COVID-19 period (35% spike in NPL growth between 2019 and 2020). Recovery has been sustained since 2021. Bad debts written off in 2023 totalled UGX 529 billion; over the five years 2019–2023 the sector wrote off UGX 1.7 trillion.

| Core (Tier 1) capital to RWA | 24.69% | 10.5% |

The sector is **well-capitalised**, consistently exceeding minimum requirements by a wide margin.

## 6. Sector Trends 2023–2025

### Digital Banking and Mobile Money

- Mobile money transactions grew **126% in volume** and **211% in value** over five years to 2023
- Mobile banking active users grew **24.9%** in Q4 2023 alone (1.9M → 2.4M users)
- Debit card transactions: UGX 581 billion per quarter by end-2023
- RTGS transactions grew 23% in volume and 30% in value (YoY 2023)
- Banks are investing heavily in digital channels; online/mobile has become as important as branches for retail SFIs
- **13-percentage-point urban-rural gap** in digital transfer usage, but this gap has halved since 2018
- Barriers to e-payment adoption: security and trust concerns dominate non-user hesitation

### Agent Banking Expansion

- 22,217 agents as at December 2023 (44% compound annual growth rate over 10 years)
- Agents predominantly handle cash (cash deposits = 73% of all agent transactions by volume)
- Agents are now **more accessible** than ATMs or branches for most Ugandans
- Over half of adults live within 1 km of a mobile money agent

### Interest Rate Environment (2024–2025 Outlook)

- CBR held at **10%** (March 2024 unscheduled hike); expected to ease toward 8.5% by end-2025
- Commercial lending rates fell to **17.7%** by April 2024 (down from 19.3% April 2023)
- Continued compression expected as BoU eases policy; businesses should model rates in the 16–18% range for UGX loans in 2025/26 projections
- USD loan rates remain in the 8–10% range; accessible only for forex-earning businesses (exporters, tourism, etc.)

### Macroeconomic Drivers for Banks

- GDP growth projected at **6% (FY 2023/24)**, **7.2% annually 2025–2026**; oil sector development is the primary tailwind
- Exchange rate: averaged **UGX 3,771/$1** over the 12 months to April 2024; continued mild depreciation expected through 2026 due to capital imports for EACOP
- Inflation returned to low levels (3.2% average for 12 months to May 2024); modest uptick expected from mid-2024
- Government borrowing through T-bills and bonds is expanding; financial institutions will be active buyers, potentially crowding out some private sector lending

## 7. Implications for Business Plans

### What Lenders Look For — Uganda Banking Context

Uganda's commercial banks apply a variant of the **CAMPARI** framework (Character, Ability, Means, Purpose, Amount, Repayment, Insurance) in credit assessment. Key practical tests:

| Factor | What Banks Actually Check |
|--------|--------------------------|
| Character | CRB credit history; NIN/BRN registration; references; director background |
| Ability | Management track record; audited financials (≥2 years preferred) |
| Means | Collateral: land title or property at 60–80% LTV; debenture over assets |
| Purpose | Specific use of funds; alignment with lender's sector focus |
| Amount | Whether requested amount is proportionate to business size and collateral |
| Repayment | DSCR ≥ 1.25× minimum; source of repayment must be explicit |
| Insurance | Asset insurance required; key-man insurance for sole-director SMEs |

### Matching Business Type to Lender

| Business Type | Recommended Lenders | Rationale |
|--------------|--------------------|-----------|
| Small agribusiness / farmer groups | DFCU (agribusiness unit), Centenary Bank, Pride Microfinance | Specialised products; group guarantee accepted; seasonal repayment structures |
| Women-owned SME | DFCU (WiB programme), Finance Trust Bank, Centenary | Preferential terms; training support; alternative collateral considered |
| Urban retail / trade SME | Equity Bank, KCB, DFCU, Centenary | SME-oriented; competitive working capital products |
| Manufacturing / equipment finance | Stanbic, Absa, DFCU (leasing) | Leasing structures available; larger loan sizes |
| Mortgage / real estate | Housing Finance Bank, Stanbic, DFCU | Specialist products; longer tenors |
| Corporate / forex-earning | Stanbic, Standard Chartered, Absa | Trade finance; USD loan options; treasury services |
| Export / agriculture value chain | Stanbic, Absa, Equity (AfDB-backed lines) | Export finance, warehouse receipts |

### Positioning a Loan Application

1. **Formalise the business first**: NIN, BRN, TIN, EFRIS registration, trading licence — banks will not process applications without these. NIN/BRN gates were tightened significantly in 2025.
2. **Prepare 2–3 years of financials**: Audited is ideal; CPA-certified management accounts are the minimum. Mobile money statements can substitute for rural/informal businesses at some lenders (see `informal-business-records.md`).
3. **Identify collateral early**: Land titles must be free of encumbrances; obtain a forced sale valuation from an approved valuer. If no land, explore group guarantees (DFCU/Centenary), leasing structures, or DFI-backed guarantee schemes (aBi Finance, USAID-DCA).
4. **Show DSCR ≥ 1.25×**: The business plan's financial projections must demonstrate that annual net operating cash flow covers annual loan repayment by at least 1.25 times. See `credit-assessment-frameworks.md`.
5. **Match loan purpose to lender focus**: A request to Centenary Bank for a city-centre commercial property development is a mismatch; take it to Housing Finance Bank or Stanbic instead.
6. **Use the Women in Business angle where applicable**: DFCU's WiB programme offers genuinely preferential terms and business support — not just marketing. Qualifying women-owned businesses should apply through this channel.
7. **Consider a tiered approach for large amounts**: Amounts above UGX 500 million may require board-level governance documentation and enhanced due diligence. See `meta-due-diligence/SKILL.md`.
