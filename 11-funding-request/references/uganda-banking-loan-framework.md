# Uganda Banking Loan Framework

## 1. Overview of Commercial Bank Lending in Uganda

Uganda's banking sector comprises 26 licensed commercial banks (Tier I), 4 credit institutions (Tier II), and 3 microfinance deposit-taking institutions (Tier III), all regulated by the Bank of Uganda under the Financial Institutions Act, 2004.

Total industry loans grew from UGX 3.4 trillion (2006) to UGX 9.4 trillion (2014), reflecting rapid credit expansion. However, this growth has been accompanied by persistent non-performing loan (NPL) challenges driven by both borrower and institutional factors.

A key structural feature: the top 5 borrowers per bank represent approximately 40% of all loans, creating significant concentration risk (IMF, 2003). Interest rate spreads have historically ranged 15–20%, reflecting high perceived credit risk and limited competition.

## 2. Lending Practices — Rates and Terms

### Interest Rate Environment

| Category | Indicative Rate Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Lending rate spread over deposit | 15–20 percentage points | Uganda historical norm since 1994 |
| Real lending rates | 10–25% p.a. | Varied since 1996 |
| BoU Central Bank Rate (CBR) | 9.75–10.5% (2025) | Base reference rate |
| Commercial bank lending | 20–28% p.a. | Typical SME/individual rate |
| Default / penalty rate | Higher than contract rate | Determined at bank discretion |
| Early settlement fee (Stanbic) | 10% of total loan amount | Charged after cooling-off period |

### Loan Types Available

- **Fixed-term loan** — Set repayment schedule over agreed term; no drawdown after disbursement until fully repaid.
- **Revolving term loan** — Can be re-drawn after repaying at least 25% of original amount; minimum 6-month seasoning.
- **Revolving line of credit** — Short-cycle revolving; 1-month minimum before redraw.
- **Business instalment loan (BIL)** — Used specifically for SME and development finance (UDB terminology).
- **Agricultural loan** — Centenary Bank specialty; repayment may be deferred during drought/flood; linked to harvest cycles.
- **Group loan** — Members share repayment responsibility; high recovery rate (≈98% under Grameen-type structures).

### Collateral Requirements

Banks commonly require:
- Land titles (freehold or leasehold) — most accepted form in Uganda
- Vehicles and moveable assets (with log books)
- Insurance assignments (life and asset)
- Personal guarantees and director guarantees
- Group guarantees (for microfinance products)
- Compulsory savings deposits (as a condition of MDI/Tier IV lending)

Poor or rural borrowers who cannot provide conventional collateral may access micro loans through alternative mechanisms: group pressure, compulsory savings, character references, and salary domicile.

## 3. Loan Application Process — Stanbic Bank Personal Loan

Stanbic Bank Uganda (a Tier I commercial bank) provides a 12-page structured personal loan application. The following summarises the key requirements and process.

### Documentation Checklist

| Item | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Identity | National ID, passport, or employer financial card (biometric) |
| Employment | Employment letter, contract details, occupancy status |
| Income | Most recent payslip; gross and net monthly salary |
| Address | Most recent utility statement |
| Banking history | 6 months' account statements (new customers) |
| Other facilities | Declaration of existing loans, guarantees, and credit facilities |
| Insolvency history | Declare if declared insolvent in last 10 years |

### Application Fields Required

- Personal information (name, date of birth, gender, nationality)
- Employment details (employer name, industry, sector, seniority, employment date, contract expiry)
- Financial information (rent/mortgage, vehicle finance, other income, monthly commitments)
- Loan details (amount, type, repayment method, term, interest structure)
- Security offered (value and type of collateral)
- Debit authorisation (standing instruction to debit repayments from current account)

### Salary Domicile Requirement

Borrowers must maintain an active current/transactional account with Stanbic into which monthly salary is deposited. Failure to maintain salary domicile gives the bank the right to recall the entire outstanding amount immediately. Repayments are debited automatically via salary deduction, debit order, or stop order.

### Repayment Mechanics

- Principal, interest, fees, and charges all rank equal; payments credited: (1) interest first, (2) fees second, (3) principal third.
- Early repayments permitted at any time (Clause 1.5); no change to instalment schedule unless formally rescheduled in writing.
- Early settlement after cooling-off period: 10% fee on total loan amount.
- Revolving eligibility: 25% repaid + 6 months elapsed + satisfactory account conduct.

### Loan Protection Insurance

Stanbic requires credit life insurance (through Liberty Life Insurance Uganda or borrower's own insurer). Cover settles outstanding balance on death, accidental disability, and critical illness; provides up to 6 months' instalments on retrenchment.

### Default Triggers

The bank may convert the loan to demand and/or increase interest upon:
- Missed instalment payment
- Material deterioration in financial position
- Misleading information in application
- Bankruptcy/insolvency proceedings
- Guarantor default or termination of guarantee
- Outstanding balance exceeding approved limit

## 4. Uganda Development Bank — Development Finance Lending

Uganda Development Bank Limited (UDBL) is a government-owned development finance institution (DFI) established in 1972. It finances enterprises in key growth sectors: agriculture, infrastructure, industrialisation, services, real estate. It supports SMEs and large-scale development projects.

### UDBL Loan Cycle (Value Chain)

`Initiation → Assessment → Disbursement → Maintenance → Collection → Termination`

This 6-stage process is managed at different organisational levels: Business Development Officers, Credit Management staff, Monitoring and Recovery staff, and Strategy staff.

### UDBL NPL History

| Year | NPL Indicator |
|---|---|
| Pre-2012 | NPL-to-gross-loans ratio in excess of **37%** |
| 2011 | Net asset portfolio (after suspended interest and provisions) = 37% of gross loans |
| 2013 | Restructuring completed; credit policies revised |

Key causes at UDBL: staff-related factors (inadequate skills, fraud risk, community relationships), outdated credit policies, weak MIS, and macroeconomic shocks.

### Key Risk Factors Identified at DFIs

1. **Staff competence** — poor credit scoring, inadequate collateral appraisal, weak monitoring
2. **Policy gaps** — outdated credit standards; policies copied from other banks without local adaptation
3. **Insider lending** — officers lending to related parties; community connections compromising objectivity
4. **MIS weaknesses** — non-integrated systems; inability to track total client exposure across the banking group
5. **Macroeconomic shocks** — inflation, exchange rate changes, commodity price volatility affect repayment

## 5. Centenary Bank — Retail and Agricultural Lending

Centenary Bank (formerly Centenary Rural Development Bank) was established in 1983 and registered as a full commercial bank. It serves over 650,000 deposit customers and 94,000 borrowers, primarily in rural Uganda (83% of loans to rural inhabitants; 77% of deposits from rural areas).

### Key Lending Features

- **Focus sectors**: Agriculture, micro-enterprise, small business, personal development
- **Loan purpose (respondent survey)**: 72% business improvement; 22% construction/asset purchase; 2% consumption
- **Group lending**: Introduced group pressure mechanisms — high recovery rate (≈98% in successful schemes)
- **Agricultural adjustment**: Loan repayment periods extended (without interest penalty) during drought or flood

### Default Causes Identified at Centenary Bank (Lira Branch)

| Cause | % of Defaults |
|---|---|
| Bad habit / wilful refusal to repay | 32% |
| Natural calamities (drought, floods) | 26% |
| Misappropriation of loan funds | 24% |
| High interest rates making repayment unaffordable | 16% |

The default rate in Lira District was recorded at approximately **25% of the loan fund per season** — though the bank treated this data confidentially.

### Interest Rate Perception (Borrower Survey, 2011)

| Perception | % of Respondents |
|---|---|
| Very high interest rate | 62% |
| High interest rate | 30% |
| Fair | 6% |
| Low | 2% |

High interest rates were identified as a direct cause of default (borrowers unable to repay principal + accumulated interest; some defaulters went into hiding).

## 6. Loan Recovery Mechanisms

### Methods: Aggressive vs Non-Aggressive

| Approach | Method | Recovery Rate |
|---|---|---|
| **Aggressive** | Group loan denial; group pressure on defaulters | ≈98% (Grameen-type schemes) |
| **Aggressive** | Penalty interest rates on overdue amounts | Raises average interest payable proportional to delay |
| **Aggressive** | Asset seizure and sale | High short-term recovery; damages community trust |
| **Aggressive** | Legal action / court proceedings | Used by Centenary Bank after 2004; effective but expensive |
| **Non-aggressive** | Field follow-up by credit officer | Varies; 60–67% in rural Uganda programmes |
| **Non-aggressive** | Loan rescheduling | Used for agricultural borrowers post-natural disaster |
| **Non-aggressive** | Loan write-off | Last resort; leads to scheme collapse if overused |

### Legal Recovery Channels

- BOU aligned NPL classification to international standards in October 2001; circulars issued April and July 2002.
- Commercial banks have the right to pursue debtors through court, attach and sell assets, and appoint receivers.
- Stanbic Bank T&Cs explicitly allow set-off across all accounts held by the borrower; writ of execution attachment within 30 days.
- UDBL pursued legal recovery actions on non-performing large loans.

### Industry Collapse Precedents

Poor loan assessment led to the closure of 4 large Ugandan banks in 1998/99, including ICB, Greenland, and the Cooperative Bank. Trans Africa was subsequently taken over by Orient Bank. UCBL was privatised and merged with Stanbic. These events triggered tighter BOU supervision standards.

## 7. Non-Performing Loan (NPL) Framework

### BOU Classification Standards (post-October 2001)

A loan is classified as non-performing when:
- Principal and/or interest is **90 days or more** past due (Alton & Hazen, 2001)
- Under older Ugandan standards: default classification at **180 days** (Nsereko, 1995)
- No longer accruing interest (interest suspended on NPLs)

Portfolio at Risk (PAR) = balance of all loans with one or more payments past due / total outstanding portfolio. This is the standard delinquency measure.

## 8. Practical Guidance for Business Plan Authors

### What Lenders Assess (Bank-Specific Factors)

1. **Collateral quality and value** — land titles preferred; vehicles accepted; group guarantees for micro loans
2. **Repayment capacity** — salary or business cash flow sufficient to cover instalments; DSCR ≥ 1.25× preferred
3. **Character and credit history** — Credit Reference Bureau check (CompuScan CRB); payment history, defaults, court orders
4. **Business purpose** — 72% of Centenary Bank borrowers borrow for business improvement; clear purpose improves approval
5. **Loan product match** — match loan type to cash flow pattern (fixed term for capital assets; revolving for working capital)

### Red Flags That Increase NPL Risk

- Borrowing for consumption rather than income generation
- Operating in sectors exposed to weather risk without insurance
- No prior banking relationship or salary domicile
- Existing undisclosed debts or guarantees
- Weak or informal financial records

### Strengthening a Loan Application

- Provide 2–3 years of audited or management accounts
- Include a cash flow projection showing DSCR ≥ 1.25× throughout the loan term
- Identify specific collateral (include independent valuation if available)
- Demonstrate prior repayment history (SACCO, mobile money records, prior loans)
- Open a salary/business account with the target bank before applying
- Consider credit life insurance to address lender risk concerns

### UDBL vs Commercial Bank — Key Differences

| Feature | UDBL (DFI) | Commercial Bank |
|---|---|---|
| Target borrower | SMEs, large projects, growth sectors | All segments |
| Loan size | Medium to large (infrastructure/agri) | Small to large |
| Terms | Development finance; longer tenors | Shorter tenors |
| Interest rates | Lower (development mandate) | Market rates (20–28%) |
| NPL tolerance | Historically higher (restructuring history) | Lower; strict provisioning |
| Collateral | Land, assets; government guarantee | Land, salary, assets |
