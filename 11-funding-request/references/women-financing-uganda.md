# Women-Specific Financing in Uganda

*Sources: World Bank/IFC, "Unlocking the Potential of Women Entrepreneurs in Uganda" (2022); IFC/World Bank, "Creating Markets in Uganda" CPSD (2022). Cite as (World Bank, 2022) or (IFC/World Bank, 2022) as appropriate.*

---

## 1. The Financing Gap for Women Entrepreneurs

### Why Women Face a Different Financing Environment

Women-owned enterprises in Uganda face structural financing constraints distinct from general SME credit access challenges:

| Constraint | Evidence |
|---|---|
| Capital deployed | Women have 50% less capital than male-owned peer enterprises |
| Collateral access | 84% of rural land parcels held under unregistered customary tenure — women hold secondary use rights, not ownership |
| Gender discrimination | No laws prohibiting gender-based discrimination by financial institutions in Uganda |
| Loan size mismatch | Microfinance too small; commercial banks require too large — "missing middle" |
| Household financial pressure | Unequal bargaining power means business revenues diverted to household use |

### The "Missing Middle" Problem
Growth-oriented women-owned enterprises face a financing gap between:
- **Microfinance** (typically UGX 500,000–5,000,000 / US$135–1,350): too small to fund meaningful capital investment
- **Commercial bank SME loans** (typically requiring collateral, audited accounts, formal registration): too complex and asset-heavy to access

This missing middle is the primary financing constraint for women with genuine growth potential.

### Evidence on Impact of Larger Finance
- Ethiopia Women Entrepreneurship Development Project: larger individual liability loans → **+40% profit** after 3 years + **+55% employment growth**
- Nigeria business plan competition (avg US$50,000 grant): increased likelihood of enterprise operation, boosted employment, sales, and profits
- Comparison: Standard microfinance has "poor track record for transformational business growth"

## 2. Funding Sources Available to Women Entrepreneurs in Uganda

### 2.1 Government Programmes

| Programme | Administrator | Type | Target |
|---|---|---|---|
| Women's Enterprise Fund (WEF) | MFPED / Ministry of Gender | Grants + soft loans | Women entrepreneurs at district level |
| Uganda Women Entrepreneurs Association (UWEAL) | UWEAL | Training, advocacy, networks | Kampala and major towns |
| Operation Wealth Creation (OWC) | Government (UPDF-administered) | Agricultural input subsidies | Farmers (warning: marred by quality and timing issues) |

*Note: OWC inputs are sometimes procured at 20–50% above market prices and distribution is unreliable — not a dependable financing source.*

### 2.2 Development Finance Institutions

| Institution | Product | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Uganda Development Bank (UDB) | Long-term loans (5–15 years) at concessional rates | Explicit youth and women lending windows; rates typically below commercial |
| aBi Finance (formerly aBi Trust) | Agricultural value chain finance | Targets smallholder farmers and agri-SMEs including women |
| UNCDF Uganda | Digital financial services, micro-investments | Focus on rural and peri-urban women |
| Pride Microfinance (largest MFI) | Group and individual microloans | Widely accessible; graduated loan products |
| Post Bank Uganda | Savings and loans | Government-owned; some rural branches |

### 2.3 International DFI and Donor Windows

| Institution | Relevance |
|---|---|
| IFC (International Finance Corporation) | Supports financial intermediaries serving women; agribusiness investment |
| World Bank Group | Programme lending and TA through government; housing finance via Uganda Mortgage Refinancing Corporation |
| USAID | Enterprise finance programs; agriculture value chains |
| GIZ / KfW | German development finance; various sector lending |
| IFAD | Rural agricultural finance; women farmer programmes |

### 2.4 SACCO and Community Finance

SACCOs and community-based savings are often the most accessible financing for women:
- SACCOs can lend 3× member savings (group guarantee replaces collateral)
- Mobile savings + training has compounding positive effect on enterprise outcomes
- Evidence: combining business registration + bank account → 28% increase in enterprise sales, 20% increase in profits (Malawi study)

**Uganda SACCO landscape:**
- Over 20,000 SACCOs registered with Ministry of Finance
- Key regulator: Uganda Microfinance Regulatory Authority (UMRA)
- NSSF does not provide retail lending, but SACCO linkage through NSSF-registered employers exists

### 2.5 Innovative Financing Mechanisms

| Mechanism | How It Works | Relevance for Women |
|---|---|---|
| Psychometric credit scoring | Replace/complement collateral with character/intent assessment | Enables women without land titles to access loans; 7× repayment correlation |
| Movable assets as collateral | Law enacted in Uganda | Allows equipment, inventory, receivables as security |
| Mobile money savings | Separate business from household income; privacy for women | M-Pesa model lifted 194,000 households out of poverty in Kenya |
| Group lending (VSLA/MFI) | Guarantee pool replaces individual collateral | Well-suited to women with social capital but limited assets |
| Business plan competitions | Grants for strong business plans | High-impact but highly competitive |

## 3. Finance Strategy for a Women-Owned Business Plan

### Structuring the Funding Request

When the business owner is a woman, the funding section should:

1. **Acknowledge and address collateral constraints explicitly:**
   - State what assets are available as security (equipment, stock, vehicle, land if any)
   - Reference the movable assets as collateral law if applicable
   - Identify any group guarantee or co-guarantee arrangement

2. **Layer the funding sources:**
   - Stage 1 (start-up): Personal savings + SACCO loan + possible WEF grant
   - Stage 2 (growth): UDB concessional loan or aBi Finance agri-loan
   - Stage 3 (scale): Commercial bank with track record / DFI facility

3. **Show financial separation:**
   - Business bank account (distinct from personal/household)
   - Mobile money business wallet
   - Bookkeeping system demonstrating business vs household cash separation

4. **Match loan size to growth stage:**
   - Avoid microfinance if growth capital requirement exceeds UGX 10M — the "missing middle" trap
   - If gap exists, make the case for a larger loan with business plan evidence and personal initiative narrative

5. **Frame the Social Return:**
   - Women-owned enterprises employ 75% women — document projected female employment
   - Gender-responsive investors and DFIs explicitly value this multiplier
   - NSSF-linked employment reporting is an additional benefit of formalisation

### Sensitivity Analysis for Financing Costs
- Commercial bank lending rates: 19–24% p.a. — model high-rate scenario
- UDB concessional rate: approximately 12–15% p.a. (verify current rate)
- SACCO rates: typically 12–18% p.a. declining balance
- Microfinance: often 24–36% p.a. effective — only use for short-term, high-turnover working capital

## 4. Risk Considerations for Women-Owned Business Plans

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| No collateral for loan | Movable assets law; SACCO group guarantee; psychometric scoring |
| Household income drain | Separate bank account; documented transfer policy; household budget planning |
| Time constraints (care responsibilities) | Staffing plan; flexible operations model; delegation framework |
| Gender discrimination by lender | Pre-identify DFI lenders with women mandates; use UDB or aBi Finance first |
| Sector wage gap | Explicit plan to operate in or adjacent to higher-value sector; pricing strategy |
| Credit history gap | Start with SACCO to build credit record before approaching commercial bank |

## 5. Sector-Specific Finance Notes

### Agribusiness / Farming (Women)
- Only 6% of small-scale agribusinesses have access to a loan or line of credit (vs 44.1% in Kenya)
- 10% of farm households had credit access in past five years
- aBi Finance, IFAD, and USAID programmes specifically target women in agricultural value chains
- Temperature-controlled logistics and cold chain infrastructure: PPP opportunities available (IFC CPSD recommendation)

### Housing / Real Estate
- NSSF (US$3 billion+ in assets) theoretically deployable into housing investment vehicles
- Uganda Mortgage Refinancing Corporation being established to deepen mortgage market
- Real estate investment trust framework being piloted — relevant for longer-term housing portfolio investors
- Current formal mortgage: accessible only to top 11.5% of households

### Manufacturing / Light Industry
- Uganda Development Corporation (UDC) equity and debt into target pioneer industries
- Industrial parks (Buikwe, Namanve, Luzira, etc.) offer subsidised infrastructure for manufacturing SMEs
- Local content preferences in Investment Code benefit Ugandan-owned manufacturers
