# Uganda Intellectual Property Framework for Business Plans

*Sources: Uganda National IP Policy, Ministry of Justice and Constitutional Affairs / WIPO, May 2019; URSB Intellectual Property Examination Guidelines, 1st Edition, 2025.*

This reference file provides IP registration processes, protection durations, costs, and practical guidance for embedding IP analysis into Uganda business plans. IP sections are relevant to: company overview (§02), products and services (§03), competitive analysis (§06), risk analysis (§12), and appendices (§15).

---

## 1. Why IP Matters for Uganda Business Plans

The National IP Policy (2019) formally recognises IP as a driver of socioeconomic development. For a business plan, IP matters in three ways:

**Competitive moat:** A registered trademark, patent, or trade secret protects the business from competitors copying its brand, product, or process. Lenders and investors expect to see that protectable assets are actually protected.

**Asset on the balance sheet:** Registered IP has assessed commercial value. Brand equity (trademark) and patented technology can appear as intangible assets, supporting a stronger net asset position in financing applications.

**Risk mitigation:** Unregistered brands are vulnerable to third-party registration. Unprotected recipes and processes are exposed to employee leakage. The risk register should identify IP risks and their mitigations.

**Institutional framework:**

| Institution | IP Role |
|-------------|---------|
| URSB (Uganda Registration Services Bureau) | National IP Office — registers all IP types; maintains registers; enforcement coordination |
| ARIPO | Africa Regional IP Organization — regional patent/design filing across 19 member states |
| URSB IP Enforcement Unit (est. 2016) | With Uganda Police Force officers attached |
| URA Customs and Excise | Border seizure of counterfeit/infringing goods |
| UNBS | Product certification; market surveillance against IP-infringing goods |
| CMOs (UPRS, URRO) | Collective Management Organisations for copyright |
| Uganda Judiciary (High Court) | IP litigation — High Court Commercial Division preferred for complex matters |

## 3. Trademark Registration — Full Process

Governed by the **Trademarks Act Cap. 225** and **Trademarks Regulations 2023**, administered by URSB.

### 3.1 System Characteristics

- **Single-class system:** Each class applied for is a separate application with separate fees. A food manufacturer (Class 30) and restaurant (Class 43) must file two applications.
- **Nice Classification 11th Edition:** 45 classes — Classes 1–34 for goods, Classes 35–45 for services.
- **Online filing:** URSB Online Trademark Application System (electronic filing mandatory; paper only if system unavailable).
- **Who can apply:** Any "person" — natural persons (individuals) or legal persons (incorporated companies). Partnerships must file through individual partners. Business names are not legal entities — owner applies "trading as [name]." Refugees treated as local applicants (Refugees Act s.29(2)).
- **Foreign applicants:** Must file through a registered trademark agent (an Advocate of the High Court registered per Regulation 11 of the Trademarks Regulations 2023). Local applicants may self-file.

### 3.2 Step-by-Step Process

**Step 1 — Trademark Search**
- Submit search application to URSB
- Receive search report: existing marks, classes, applicant names, filing dates, registration status
- Optional: request Preliminary Advice from Registrar (informal opinion on registrability — not legally binding)

**Step 2 — File Application (electronically)**
- Application number and filing date assigned on receipt (critical for establishing priority)
- Examiner target: initial formality response within 48 working hours

**Step 3 — Formality Examination**
Examiner verifies:
- Correct form used; prescribed fees paid (local vs. foreign rate, correct number of classes)
- Applicant details complete; mark representation clear (JPEG/PDF/PNG, acceptable resolution)
- Correct Nice Classification class; goods/services described in Nice terms
- Accompanying documents present (Form of Authorisation / Power of Attorney if agent)
- Applicant has 30 days to remedy formality deficiencies; failure = application abandoned

**Step 4 — Substantive Examination**
Two grounds assessed:

*Absolute grounds (inherent registrability):*
- Mark must be inherently distinctive — capable of identifying commercial source
- Not registrable: descriptive terms, generic names, laudatory words ("Premium," "Super," "Ultimate"), purely geographical names, surnames (without acquired distinctiveness), marks contrary to morality or public order, national flags/emblems, internationally protected symbols (Olympics, Red Cross)

*Relative grounds (conflicts):*
- Visual, phonetic, and conceptual similarity to earlier marks assessed
- "Likelihood of confusion" test — would the average Ugandan consumer be confused?
- Examiner searches the register (IPAS database) for conflicting earlier marks

**Step 5 — Examination Report (if objections)**
- Written report issued; applicant has **30 days to respond** (extendable on written request)
- Acceptable responses: written arguments, mark amendments, disclaimers, evidence of acquired distinctiveness (advertising records, market surveys, sales figures), consent agreements
- Appeals: to the High Court against any Registrar decision

**Step 6 — Publication in Uganda Gazette**
- Applicant arranges and pays for gazette publication
- **Opposition period: 60 days** from publication date
- Any person may file opposition on valid grounds
- Uncontested: proceeds to registration

**Step 7 — Registration Certificate**
- Applicant applies for registration (with gazette copy)
- Certificate issued; mark entered in Register with registration date

**Step 8 — Renewal**
- Initial protection: **7 years** from filing date
- Renewals: **every 10 years** thereafter

### 3.3 Grounds for Refusal — Quick Reference

| Ground | Examples |
|--------|---------|
| Descriptive | "Fresh Coffee" for coffee; "Fast Delivery" for courier services |
| Generic | "Matoke" for matoke; "Boda" for motorcycle taxis |
| Laudatory | "Superior," "Premium," "No.1," "Ultra" |
| Purely geographical | "Kampala Coffee," "Masaka Rice" (without acquired distinctiveness) |
| Surname | "Ssemakula Bakery" (surname element not registrable without proof of distinctiveness) |
| Scandalous/immoral | Vulgar, sexually explicit, or offensive marks |
| Deceptive | Implies false connection to origin, quality, or affiliation |
| Protected symbols | Flags, Red Cross, Olympic rings, national emblems |

### 3.5 Collective Marks and Certification Marks

Cooperatives, SACCOs, producer groups, and associations can register **certification marks** or **collective marks**:
- Certification mark: owned by a competent authority/body; used to certify standards (quality, geographic origin, mode of manufacture). Examples: UNBS Q-mark, BUBU (Buy Uganda Build Uganda) mark.
- The certifying body (owner) must not itself trade in the goods being certified.
- Application must include rules governing use (eligibility, testing, supervision, penalties for misuse).

Collective marks: distinguish goods/services of members of an association from non-members.

## 4. Patent Registration

Governed by the **Industrial Property Act Cap. 224** and **Industrial Property Regulations 2017**.

### 4.1 Eligibility Criteria

Three cumulative requirements:

1. **Novelty:** Invention must not have been disclosed anywhere in the world before the filing or priority date. Any prior publication, public demonstration, or use destroys novelty.

2. **Inventive Step (Non-Obviousness):** Invention must not be obvious to a person skilled in the relevant technical field, given the prior art at the filing date.

3. **Industrial Applicability:** Invention must be capable of being made or used in any kind of industry (including agriculture), producing a reproducible result.

**Not patentable:**
- Discoveries, scientific theories, mathematical methods
- Aesthetic creations, literary or artistic works
- Business methods, mental act methods, game rules
- Diagnostic, therapeutic, and surgical methods for humans or animals
- Plant and animal varieties; essentially biological production processes (exception: microbiological processes and their products)
- Inventions contrary to public order, morality, public health, or environmental safety

### 4.2 Utility Model — Simpler Alternative

- Protects new technical solutions modifying existing products/processes
- Examined for **novelty** and **industrial applicability** only (no inventive step requirement)
- Duration: **10 years** (vs. 20 for a full patent)
- Lower threshold — more accessible for informal sector innovators and local product adaptors

### 4.3 Application Requirements

1. Title (technical description of the invention)
2. Description — sufficient for a skilled person to reproduce the invention without undue experimentation
3. **Claims** — define the legal scope of protection; the most important element
4. Drawings (usually essential for mechanical/process inventions)
5. Abstract (brief summary for search databases)
6. Inventor named (mandatory; inventor may waive right to be named on the granted patent)
7. Statement justifying applicant's rights if applicant differs from inventor
8. Prescribed fees (different for local vs. foreign applicants)
9. Foreign applicants: must file through a local agent; power of attorney required

### 4.4 Examination Process

1. **Formality Examination** — checks all documents present, fees paid, inventor named, applicant identified
2. **IPC Classification** — International Patent Classification symbols assigned to organise prior art search
3. **Prior Art Search** — WIPO PATENTSCOPE, Google Patents, Espacenet, national collections; identifies documents that may affect novelty or inventive step
4. **Novelty Assessment** — feature-by-feature comparison with prior art using a novelty/feature table
5. **Inventive Step Assessment** — would the solution have been obvious to a skilled person given the prior art?
6. **Industrial Applicability** — at least one practical use stated; reproducible result
7. **Examination Report** — if objections raised, applicant responds; application may be amended

### 4.5 Publication and Grant

- Applications published after **18 months** from filing or priority date (or earlier on request)
- Certificate of grant issued on satisfying all requirements
- **Duration:** Patent = **20 years** from filing date; Utility Model = **10 years** from filing date
- Annual maintenance fees payable to keep patent in force

### 4.6 Compulsory Licensing

The Industrial Property Act provides for compulsory licensing where:
- The patent owner fails to work (use/manufacture) the invention in Uganda within a prescribed period
- National emergency or public interest demands access (e.g. essential medicines)
- Anti-competitive practices are being employed through the patent
- Compulsory licensee pays royalties to the patent owner

## 5. Industrial Design Registration

### 5.1 Definition

An industrial design protects the **ornamental or aesthetic aspect** of a product — its shape, configuration, pattern, colour combination, or ornamentation — as applied to an article of industry or handicraft. It does **not** protect the technical function (that requires a patent).

### 5.2 Process

1. Application form, drawings/photographs (all views: front, back, sides, top, bottom, perspective), fees, applicant and creator details, power of attorney if agent
2. **Formality Examination**: completeness check
3. **Substantive Examination**: novelty (no prior disclosure anywhere globally); originality (independent creative effort); industrial applicability; exclusions (functional-only designs, immoral designs, official symbols)
4. **Classification**: Locarno Classification system
5. **Publication in Uganda Gazette**: any person may oppose within **90 days**; counterstatement within 30 days; affidavit exchange; hearing; appeal to High Court within 90 days
6. **Registration Certificate** issued

### 5.3 Duration

- **5 years** initial protection from filing date
- Renewable for **two further 5-year periods** → maximum **15 years total**

### 5.4 Practical Notes

- **Deferred publication:** Applicant may request deferral for up to **12 months** (s.73(5) IPA) — useful if the product is not yet ready for market
- **Priority claims:** 6-month priority window from first filing in a Paris Convention country; certified copy of earlier application required within 3 months of Uganda filing

## 6. Copyright

### 6.1 Automatic Protection

Copyright arises **automatically upon creation and fixation** — no registration is required. Protected works include:
- Literary works (books, articles, training manuals, business reports, software code)
- Musical works and sound recordings
- Artistic works (photographs, drawings, logos, packaging designs)
- Audiovisual works (videos, films, presentations)
- Broadcasts
- Compilations and databases

The © symbol and author/year notice are recommended on all materials but are not a legal requirement for protection.

### 6.2 Optional URSB Registration

Voluntary registration provides:
- A public record of the work and its author
- **Evidentiary value** in infringement disputes — registered owner is presumed to be the rights holder, shifting the burden of proof to the defendant

Documents for registration: application form, copy of the work, author identification, prescribed fee.

### 6.3 Duration

| Work Type | Duration |
|-----------|---------|
| Individual author (literary, artistic, musical) | Life of author + **50 years** |
| Anonymous/pseudonymous works | 50 years from publication |
| Corporate-authored/commissioned works | 50 years from creation or first publication |
| Photographs | 50 years from creation |

### 6.4 Moral Rights

Authors of literary, artistic, and musical works retain moral rights (right to be identified as author; right to object to derogatory treatment) even after assignment of copyright. Moral rights cannot be assigned but may be waived.

## 7. Trade Secrets

### 7.1 Protection Mechanism

Uganda has no specific Trade Secrets Act. Protection derives from:
- **Contract law:** Confidentiality and Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDAs)
- **Employment law:** Implied duty of confidentiality in employment relationships
- **Common law:** Equitable doctrine of breach of confidence

### 7.2 What Qualifies

"A formula, practice, process, design, instrument, pattern, or compilation of information which is not generally known or reasonably ascertainable, by which a business can obtain an economic advantage over competitors or customers." (Uganda National IP Policy 2019)

Examples: unique food recipes, proprietary sourcing networks, customer databases and pricing models, manufacturing processes not published in patents.

### 7.3 Best Practice for SMEs

- Execute NDAs before sharing sensitive information with suppliers, distributors, investors, or employees
- Include trade secret and non-compete clauses in employment contracts
- Document what information is classified as confidential
- Limit access to trade secrets to employees who genuinely need it (access control)

### 7.4 Enforcement Options

- Civil claim for breach of confidence in the High Court
- Injunction (interim and permanent) to stop disclosure or use
- Damages or account of profits
- Computer Misuse Act prosecution if misappropriation involved digital systems

## 8. Geographical Indications (GI)

### 8.1 Definition

A GI identifies goods originating in a particular country, region, or locality where a given quality, reputation, or other characteristic is essentially attributable to that geographical origin. Governed by the **Geographical Indications Act Cap. 223** and GI Regulations 2018.

### 8.2 Categories of Goods Eligible

- Natural products (minerals, water)
- Agricultural products (coffee, tea, fruits, crops)
- Animal products (dairy, honey, meat, leather)
- Handicraft and industry products (crafts, textiles, artisanal goods)

### 8.3 Who Can Apply

- Legal entity of producers/farmers/artisans in the geographical area
- Group of representatives of producers
- Competent authority for national-character GIs (e.g. local government, MAAIF)

### 8.4 Key Process Steps

1. Search the GI register
2. File application with: applicant details, geographical area map, goods description, quality/reputation/characteristics (soil, climate, history, production methods, causal link to place)
3. Formality examination
4. Substantive examination: geographical origin verified, link between quality and place confirmed
5. Publication in Gazette; objections within **60 days**; counterstatement within 30 days
6. Registration

### 8.5 Duration and Restrictions

- **5 years**, renewable for two further 5-year periods
- GI rights **cannot be licensed or assigned** independently — they transfer only with the relevant part of the business

### 8.6 Uganda GI Examples and Opportunities

- **Bugisu/Mt. Elgon Arabica coffee** — strong regional reputation, distinct altitude/soil characteristics; robust GI candidate
- **West Nile tobacco**, **Buganda** crafts, **Rwenzori** products — potential GI opportunities
- Rwenzori water cited in URSB Guidelines as a mark with geographical significance that acquired trademark distinctiveness through use

## 9. ARIPO — Pan-African IP Registration

### 9.1 What ARIPO Covers

ARIPO (Africa Regional Intellectual Property Organization) provides regional registration across member states — Uganda is a full member.

- **Harare Protocol** (patents, utility models, industrial designs) — Uganda party
- **Banjul Protocol** (trademarks) — Uganda's accession status under development

Member states (Harare Protocol): Botswana, Eswatini, Gambia, Ghana, Kenya, Lesotho, Liberia, Malawi, Mozambique, Namibia, Rwanda, São Tomé and Príncipe, Sierra Leone, Somalia, Sudan, Tanzania, Uganda, Zambia, Zimbabwe (19 states).

### 9.2 When to Use ARIPO

| Scenario | Recommendation |
|----------|---------------|
| Business operating only in Uganda | National URSB filing only |
| Exporting to 2–3 EAC markets | Consider separate national filings in Kenya, Tanzania |
| Building a regional brand across 5+ ARIPO states | ARIPO filing is cost-effective |
| Seeking patent protection across multiple African markets | ARIPO Harare Protocol filing |

### 9.3 PCT (Patent Cooperation Treaty)

Uganda acceded to PCT 9 February 1995. A single PCT application can designate multiple countries, providing **18 months** to decide which national/regional patent systems to pursue before paying national fees. Useful for inventions with international commercial potential.

## 10. IP Strategy by Business Type

| Business Type | Priority IP Action | Timeline |
|---------------|-------------------|---------|
| Food/beverage brand | Trademark registration (Class 29, 30, 32, or 33 as applicable) | Before launch |
| Restaurant/hospitality | Trademark (Classes 43, 35) | Before launch |
| Technology/software | Copyright (automatic); assess patent for novel processes | Ongoing |
| Agriculture — unique product | Geographical Indication (if regional quality link) | Medium-term |
| Manufacturing — unique product design | Industrial design registration | Before launch |
| Manufacturing — novel process | Patent or utility model | Before launch |
| Consulting/services | Trademark (service mark); trade secret for methodology | Before launch |
| Creative (media, design) | Copyright (automatic); optional registration | Ongoing |
| Cooperative/SACCO group product | Certification mark or Collective mark | Medium-term |

### 10.1 Minimum IP Checklist for Any Business Plan

- [ ] Brand name and logo: has a trademark search been conducted? Is registration planned?
- [ ] Trade secrets: are there NDAs in place for employees and key partners?
- [ ] Copyright: are © notices on all marketing materials, website, and documentation?
- [ ] Products: are any unique designs or inventions protectable?
- [ ] Import/export: does the business use any third-party IP under licence? Is that licence documented?
- [ ] Risk: is IP infringement by competitors identified in the risk register?

## 11. IP Enforcement in Uganda

### 11.1 Available Remedies

**Civil (High Court):**
- Injunction (interim and permanent) — stop infringement immediately
- Delivery up of infringing goods for destruction
- Damages (compensatory) or account of profits
- Legal costs

**Criminal:**
- Prosecution under Trademarks Act (counterfeiting)
- Prosecution under Copyright and Neighbouring Rights Act 2006 (piracy)
- Computer Misuse Act (digital IP theft)

### 11.2 Enforcement Agencies

- URSB IP Enforcement Unit (with Uganda Police Force officers)
- URA Customs and Excise — trained by URSB; border seizure of counterfeit imports
- UNBS — market surveillance; seizes non-compliant/counterfeit goods

### 11.3 Court Appeals Timeline

| Decision | Appeal Forum | Time Limit |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| Trademark refusal/condition | High Court | Per Trademarks Act ss.7(5), 12(5) |
| Patent refusal | Registrar General → High Court | Within prescribed time |
| Industrial design refusal | High Court | 90 days (Regulation 49, IP Regulations) |
| GI refusal | High Court | Per GI Act regulations |

### 11.4 Practical Realities

- Most IP cases currently heard in Magistrates Courts which "do not possess the requisite competences to properly handle IP-related litigation" (National IP Policy 2019) — complex cases should be taken to the High Court Commercial Division
- Enforcement is improving but still requires active investment of time and legal costs by the rights holder
- The most practical first line of defence is prevention: register, document, and use proper NDAs before infringement occurs
