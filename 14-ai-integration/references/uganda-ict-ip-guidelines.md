# Uganda ICT Intellectual Property Guidelines (January 2025)

**Source:** Ministry of ICT and National Guidance (MoICT&NG), *Uganda ICT IP Guidelines: Guidelines for Managing Intellectual Property Rights of Locally Developed IT Systems, Applications, and Innovations*, Version 1, January 2025. Funded in partnership with JICA.

---

## SECTION A: Overview of the ICT IP Framework in Uganda

### Scope

These guidelines apply to all individuals and entities that develop innovations and applications for the Government of Uganda and the public, either to solve a specific need or following financial support from MoICT&NG. They cover all stages of the Intellectual Property (IP) Value Chain: generation, protection, utilisation, and enforcement.

The guidelines specifically address:
- IP arising from government-commissioned software and IT systems
- IP generated through contractor/supplier relationships with MoICT&NG
- Innovations developed with government funding or grants
- Technology transfer, commercialisation, and open-source strategies for publicly funded ICT

### Relevant Laws and Regulations

Uganda has established a multi-layered IP legal framework relevant to the ICT sector:

1. The Trademarks Act, Act 17 of 2010
2. The Copyrights and Neighboring Rights Act 2006
3. The Trade Secrets Protection Act 2009
4. The Industrial Property Act 2014
5. The Plant Variety Protection Act 2014
6. The Geographical Indications Act 2013
7. The Data Protection and Privacy Act 2019
8. The Uganda Computer Misuse Act 2011 (amended)
9. Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights (TRIPS, 1994) — Uganda is a WTO member
10. The Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works (1886)
11. The Paris Convention for the Protection of Industrial Property (1900)
12. Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT, 1970)
13. The Kampala Protocol (2021) — African Regional Intellectual Property Organization (ARIPO) voluntary copyright registration and regional database

| WIPO | International IP — Uganda is a member; provides global forum and PCT filing |
| ARIPO | Regional IP — Uganda is a member; Kampala Protocol for copyright; streamlines registration across member states |

### Uganda's ICT IP Policy Objectives

The government's four strategic objectives for ICT IP (Figure 1 in the document):

1. **Facilitation of High-Level ICT Innovations** — Harmonise and promote development of innovative ICT solutions, including adaptations from external sources, to enhance technological advancement
2. **Improvement of ICT Standards Compliance** — Foster adherence to best practices and standards for ICT solutions, focusing on domestically created and adapted technologies
3. **Establishment of Shared IP Infrastructure** — Enable the creation of shared infrastructure and services to regulate innovations encompassing both domestic and transferred technologies
4. **Promotion of IP Rights and Commercialisation** — Safeguard IP rights for innovators and inventors while facilitating the commercialisation of innovations to generate marketplace income

**Policy context:** Uganda's National Intellectual Property Policy was approved by Cabinet in 2019. This is supplemented by the Intellectual Property Management Toolkit 2024. The government views IP as a critical driver of industrialisation under NDP III and Vision 2040.

## SECTION B: Software and Digital IP Protection

### Forms of IP Applicable to ICT Products

**Table 1 — Common Forms of IP in the ICT Sector:**

| Form | Scope of Protection | Formal Registration | Term of Protection |
|---|---|---|---|
| Copyright | Written documents, books, broadcasts, music, computer programs, databases, etc. | Not required; encouraged to register as it provides proof of authorship | Varies: 50 years for computer programs; lifetime + 50 years for individuals; 50 years after first publication for anonymous works |
| Trademarks | Word, name, number, aspect of packaging — used to distinguish goods or services of a trader | Required | Valid for 7 years from application filing date; renewable indefinitely for **10 years** with prescribed renewal fee |
| Patents | Device, substance, method or process that is new, inventive, and industrially applicable | Required | **20 years** (standard patent); annual maintenance fee required to keep in force |
| Utility Model | New technical solution, incremental improvement to an existing product or process | Required | **10 years** |
| Trade Secrets | Technical information (formulas, manufacturing processes, software algorithms, business information, customer lists, negotiation tactics, etc.) | Not required | Not applicable — protection lasts as long as secrecy is maintained |
| Industrial Designs | Three-dimensional features (shape, surface of article) or two-dimensional features (patterns, colours) | Required | **5 years** (renewable for two more consecutive 5-year terms) |

### Copyright Protection for Software

**What is protected:** Source code is protected as a literary work under the Copyright and Neighboring Rights Act 2006. This prevents people from copying source code without permission from the creator. Computer programs (software, applications, systems) receive copyright protection automatically upon creation — no registration required.

**What is included:**
- Source code (all programming languages: JavaScript, PHP, Python, etc.)
- Object code / compiled code
- Databases
- User interface designs (to the extent they constitute original creative works)
- Software documentation, user manuals, specifications
- Website content and digital content

**Duration:** Copyright for computer programs lasts **50 years**. For works by individual authors, it is the author's lifetime plus 50 years. Anonymous works and first publications are protected for 50 years after becoming available to the public.

**Registration:** Although copyright is automatic, it is strongly advised that owners of copyrights or neighbouring rights apply to utilise and safeguard original content — especially crucial for software, databases, and digital assets. Registration with URSB (or under the Kampala Protocol through ARIPO) provides:
- Formal proof of authorship and date of creation
- Legal standing in infringement disputes
- Access to enforcement mechanisms
- To obtain copyright protection in other countries, you must register in the national IP office of the relevant country

### Software Patents in Uganda

Uganda operates under the **Industrial Property Act 2014**. A patent requires a novel, inventive, and industrially applicable invention. In Uganda, a patent can only be granted for a novel invention with an inventive step, and it must be industrially applicable.

**Utility Models** are specifically relevant for software-adjacent innovations: they protect new technical solutions or incremental improvements to an existing product or process. Utility models are suitable for protecting extra functionality features in ICT products with short life cycles, as they are quicker and easier to register than patents. Term: 10 years.

The document does not explicitly exclude software patents, but the standard inventive step and industrial applicability requirements apply. Software algorithms embedded in hardware or process inventions may qualify.

### Trade Secrets for Software

Trade Secrets are confidential information a business uses to gain an economic advantage over competitors or customers. They are a helpful protection mechanism for non-patentable inventions (i.e., those that do not meet the minimum requirements of a patent or do not qualify for protection), such as:
- Prototypes
- Manufacturing processes
- **Software source code or algorithms**
- Business information (customer lists, pricing models, go-to-market strategies)

Trade secret protection does not require registration and lasts indefinitely as long as secrecy is maintained.

### Database Protection

Databases are included in the scope of copyright protection as literary works under Ugandan law. The Copyright and Neighboring Rights Act 2006 covers databases.

### Open Source Software Considerations

**Free and Open Source Software (FOSS):** FOSS licenses allow users to use, study, and (re)distribute the software in object or source code without further permission from the owner.

**Benefits for government contractors:**
- Avoids vendor lock-in
- Enables re-use and adaptation by other government agencies and private sector developers
- Avoids high costs associated with tailormade solutions

**Creative Commons:** The Creative Commons licensing umbrella allows contractors to sign agreements allowing revenue generation from solutions emerging from open innovation. Agreements are signed giving contractor access to re-use and use the modified open-source code for commercial or socio-economic gains. These agreements are signed for a period of **3 to 5 years**.

**Open Innovation definition:** An innovation can be the product of open-source production if: (a) actors in the innovation process voluntarily disclose the knowledge (technical or not) they produce; (b) engage in collaborations to enrich the open knowledge base; (c) knowledge disclosure is continuous and not the work of one single individual at a single point in time. Therefore, an innovation which emerges from the use of open-source software will qualify as an innovation if it meets this criterion.

**Proprietary vs. open source strategy:** When commissioning software development, the Contractor shall consider whether to release the software to the public for re-use under an open-source licence. If doing so, it must either retain the copyright in the software (ensuring an open-source licence is obtained) or ensure that there are contractual clauses in the contract that require the service provider to release the software under a specified open-source licence.

## SECTION C: Domain Names and Online Brand Protection

The document references the Uganda Communications Commission (UCC) as the telecommunications regulator and the body responsible for the `.ug` domain space. However, detailed domain dispute resolution procedures are not covered in depth in this document.

**Brand protection online:** Trademarks protect word, name, number, and aspect of packaging used to distinguish goods or services. A trademark registration grants the owner exclusive rights to use the registered mark on the goods or services. Registration allows businesses to differentiate themselves from competitors and prevents others from exploiting an enterprise's marketing efforts and goodwill. Trademark protection is territorial — apply on a country-by-country basis before commercialising products and services.

**Trademark term:** Valid for 7 years from application filing date; renewable indefinitely for **10 years** with a prescribed renewal fee in Uganda. The owner can sue for infringement if someone uses the same or similar mark.

## SECTION D: Data Protection

### Data Protection and Privacy Act 2019

The Data Protection and Privacy Act 2019 governs data protection and privacy in Uganda. A software system developed or being developed shall involve any processing, collection and storage of personal data.

**Contractor/developer obligations under the Act:**
- The contract requires the developer to design the software in such a way as to adhere to the laws on data protection and privacy
- The software shall have robust cyber security safeguards
- The software shall respect the data subject's rights under the Data Protection and Privacy Act
- The software shall be designed in such a way as to implement the principles of data protection as provided for under the Data Protection and Privacy Act 2019

**Registration requirement:** Developers are encouraged to register with the Personal Data Protection Office given that their activities involve obtaining, accessing, processing and storage of personal data both at the prototype stage and system deployment and usage.

### Data, Data Collector, Data Controller, Data Processor (Glossary Definitions)

- **Data:** Information collected, processed or stored to file a system or form part of a filing system
- **Data Collector:** An individual acting for themselves or on behalf of a legal entity to collect any form of data
- **Data Controller:** A person who, individually or jointly with other persons or in familiarity with other persons or as a statutory duty, determines the purpose for and the way personal data is collected or is to be collected, processed or is to be processed. This definition does not alter the definition of a data controller as defined under the Data Protection and Privacy Act 2019
- **Data Processor:** In relation to personal data, a person other than an employee of the data controller who processes or accesses the data on behalf of the data controller

### Confidentiality

Confidentiality protects any trade secrets or confidential information shared during the licensing process. The contractor shall obtain written acknowledgement of NDA obligations from innovators and members within the disclosure team (see Annex B template).

## SECTION E: Digital Content and Creative Economy IP

### Copyright for Digital Content

Copyright protects original literary, artistic, musical or artistic works, giving the owner the exclusive legal right to control the use and distribution of their creations. Copyrights can be used exclusively to protect creative designs, text, images and graphics that may be essential to the software system.

**Automatic protection:** Copyright is automatically obtained by creating the original work — unlike patents, there is no need to go through an application process.

**Scope:** Written documents, books, broadcasts, music, paintings and films, computer programs, databases, website content, social media content, videos, graphics, photographs.

### Uganda Performing Rights Society (UPRS)

Referenced in the abbreviations section — UPRS is Uganda's performing rights body, relevant to music and performing arts content used in digital products.

### Industrial Design for Digital Products

Industrial design protects the aesthetic aspect of a product, encompassing three-dimensional features (shape, surface of article) or two-dimensional features (patterns, colours). Applied to electronic devices, packaging, containers, lighting equipment, graphic symbols. Industrial design rights are granted for **5 years**, renewable for **two more consecutive 5-year terms**. However, industrial design protection does not protect the product's technical features.

### IP Ownership Default Position (Section 3.1)

The default position is that contractors shall own the IP rights of the software and other related rights in commissioned software developed for the purpose of a contract. The contractors shall have the right to use, exploit, disclose and reproduce the software.

**Ownership variations:**
- Where the contractor retains the software's copyright and related rights, they may grant the developer or service provider a licence to allow them to commercialise the software
- On a non-exclusive basis, the contractor can grant licences to third parties
- The developer/service provider can own the copyright and related rights in the commissioned software, but only with a written agreement. The default position under such arrangements is a perpetual, royalty-free, fully paid-up non-exclusive licence, including the right to sub-licence the software
- Alternatively, where circumstances require, the developer/service provider may be necessary to assign the copyright and related rights in the software to the contractor for a specified period
- The contractor can elect to jointly own the IP with the service provider, but this is generally not recommended due to complex management issues

### Terms and Licence Types

- **Exclusive licence:** Grants the licensee the sole right to use the IP; in contrast, a non-exclusive licence allows the licensor (owner) to use it themselves and potentially licence it to others
- **FRAND (Fair, Reasonable, and Non-Discriminatory):** Key for licensing IP crucial to a technical standard. Ensures fair terms for both parties: reasonable fees, no unfair conditions, and similar treatment for all licensees
- **Royalties and Fees:** Specifies the compensation the licensor receives. Can be based on a percentage of sales, a fixed fee, or a combination
- **Scope of Use:** Defines what the IP can be used for, the specific products, services, or territories
- **Quality Control Provisions:** The licensor may set standards for how the licensee uses the IP to ensure it meets quality expectations and does not damage the reputation of the IP
- **Minimum Use Requirements:** In some cases, the licensor may require the licensee to use the IP to a certain extent to maintain the licence
- **Term and Termination:** Duration of the licence and conditions under which either party can terminate it

## SECTION G: Registration Process for ICT/Digital IP

### Copyright Registration with URSB

Although copyright is automatic upon creation, registration is strongly advised and provides formal proof of authorship and legal standing.

**Process (Section 2.4 — Procedure of Identifying and Recording IP):**

1. **Recognition of assets upon receipt or commissioning:** Through its online and physical platforms, the registration office shall receive several applications daily. The office sends a letter of receipt via email or hardcopy to the applicant. The applicant must include the date, location and details of a contact person for the application. The response to the application shall be within 24 hours after the application is received or at such time as the registration office shall comply but at the earliest time possible.

2. **Verification of the existence of the asset:** When an application has been received and recognised by the registration office, the office shall do verification done by experts who understand both local and international standards of patents, copyrights and trademarks. From then on, the verification team will officially certify and accredit the tool as suitable and applicable within its relevant field. A certificate shall be issued to the accredited tool and thus be recognised as registered.

3. **Complete and accurate records of registration with URSB and maintenance status:** An accredited tool shall be further forwarded to URSB to register and maintain its registration record.

**Challenging an assessment:** Any dissatisfied applicant may challenge the assessment or valuation by the registration office, and this may be done by writing a letter to the registration office or the Permanent Secretary of the Ministry of ICT and National Guidance. Where a challenge has been addressed, a response shall be within **twenty-one (21) working days** upon receipt of the letter.

### IP Asset Register (Section 2.5)

Agencies and businesses are encouraged to establish an IP Asset Register. Relevant information to include:

1. Identity of the creator(s) or inventor(s)
2. Description of the IP
3. IP registration details
4. The start and end date of the IP protection
5. Essential details of relevant procurement and funding agreements
6. Ownership and licensing details

### Invention Disclosure Form (Annex A)

Key fields in the Invention Disclosure Form:
- Name, Employer (if any), Company Registration Number
- Physical address, Contact person, Contact email, Information sharing email, Telephone, Company telephone, Website
- Estimated IP invention contribution
- Description of the problem being solved
- Describe stage of development
- Description of innovation in detail
- Date of creation of the innovation/invention
- Description of experimental data used
- Competitive advantage
- Where the application can be applied
- What other research should be further added

### IP Valuation (Section 2.6)

Government agencies assess and value the IP in their possession as well as that which will come from contractors/suppliers. The valuation should be recorded. This helps innovators and developers leverage the commercial viability of their creations.

**Assessment and Valuation (Section 2.6.1):** The registration office shall be mandated to assess and value intellectual property through legal assessors who should be well-versed in all the legal parameters set in the laws of Uganda and these guidelines. The value shall then be modified or maintained as the valuers deem necessary.

### International Protection

- **WIPO membership:** Uganda is a WIPO member — access to the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) for multi-country patent filing, and the Madrid System for international trademark registration
- **ARIPO membership:** Uganda is an ARIPO member — the Kampala Protocol (2021) focuses on voluntary copyright registration and related rights registration; aims to create a regional database and streamline enforcement for creators in member states
- **Berne Convention:** Uganda is a Berne Convention signatory — automatic copyright recognition in all Berne Convention countries (168+ nations) without need for registration in each country
- **TRIPS Agreement:** Uganda is a WTO/TRIPS member — sets minimum standards for IP protection and enforcement internationally

## SECTION H: Enforcement of Digital IP in Uganda

### URSB's Enforcement Role (Section 2.3)

URSB serves as the primary entity in Uganda responsible for **registering and protecting** all forms of IP. URSB:
- Collaborates closely with law enforcement agencies to **enforce intellectual property rights** and protect the interests of creators and inventors in cases of IP infringement
- In collaboration with the Uganda Police, has established an Enforcement Unit to facilitate enforcement against infringement, counterfeiting and piracy in Uganda
- Provides mechanisms for **resolving disputes** related to IP; this approach helps settle conflicts amicably and efficiently
- Conducts **public awareness campaigns** and programmes to educate the public on the significance of IP protection
- Is a signatory to **international agreements** and conventions related to IP

### IP Risk Management (Section 2.7)

IP risks to be considered and managed as part of an agency's risk management framework:
- Contracts not addressing pre-existing IP
- Infringing on a third party's IP
- Not appropriately protecting the Agency's IP
- Failure to adequately protect and keep confidential IP

### IP Risk Assessment Gauge (Figure 6)

A four-level risk assessment framework for software development contracts:

| Risk Level | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **Low Risk** | Minimal IP disputes; limited use of third-party IP; minor emphasis on IP rights and deliverables; strong emphasis on quality and timeliness |
| **Moderate Risk** | Potential for IP disputes for moderate use of third-party IP; may involve modification of existing products; potential for confidential information; working in a tight competitive market |
| **High Risk** | Significant IP risk of disputes; may involve infringement of existing patents; potential for conflicting IP within the development of software; significant relevance with the development team |
| **Critical Risk** | Very high risk of disputes; working with unlinked authorised third-party software; infringing on existing patents; confidential information at risk; working with unlinked unauthorised software; confidential safeguards compromised |

**Source Code Escrow Agreements** mitigate risk by depositing source code with a third-party escrow agent. This safeguards the continuity of operations dependent on that software. The escrow agent acts in case of any dispute or legal action.

Terms of Source Code Escrow Agreements specify:
1. The software developer is required to deposit the source code
2. The Contractor (software licensee) may call for the release of the source code
3. The escrow agent must act in case of any dispute or legal action

**Code Repository:** A code repository should be used where the Contractor commissions a developer or service provider to develop software for it and owns the source code created therein. Contractor will have access to the code repository where the source code is kept.

### Uganda Computer Misuse Act (Section 3.6)

The Uganda Computer Misuse Act, enacted in 2001, is a pivotal piece of legislation that addresses the complexities introduced by the rapid development of digital technology and the internet. Its objectives include:

1. Enhancing provisions against unauthorised access to information — crucial for safeguarding both personal and intellectual property data
2. Prohibiting the sharing of information relating to minors without parental consent — protecting vulnerable individuals
3. Banning the dissemination of hate speech and malicious content, which can infringe on both personal rights and the ethical use of information
4. Addressing the circulation of false information, which can have detrimental effects on public trust and the integrity of intellectual property rights
5. Imposing restrictions on individuals convicted under the Computer Misuse Act from holding public office for a decade — emphasising the importance of accountability in the realm of ICT/IP protection

**The right to privacy** is enshrined in the Constitution of Uganda, which protects individuals from unwanted interference regarding their communications. The dissemination of false and malicious information not only poses serious challenges to personal privacy but also to the integrity of intellectual property, as misinformation can distort public perception and damage reputation. The potential for unauthorised access and data breaches can undermine personal privacy and intellectual property rights.

## SECTION I: AI and Emerging Technology IP

The document does not contain a dedicated AI IP section. However, the following provisions are relevant to AI-generated content and emerging technology:

### Software and Algorithm Protection

Trade Secrets protection explicitly covers **software source code or algorithms** as examples of confidential technical information that qualifies for trade secret protection. This is relevant for AI models, training algorithms, and proprietary AI system architectures.

### Innovation Definitions and Utility Models

**Innovation (OECD definition as cited):** Implementing a new or significantly improved product (good or service), process, a new marketing method, or a new organisational method in business practices, workplace organisation or external relations. Innovation involves the application of information, imagination, and initiative to derive more excellent or differentiated value from resources. It includes all processes for generating and translating new ideas into valuable products.

**Types of innovation recognised:**
1. Product innovation — development of a new product
2. Process innovation — improvement of an existing product; changes in the equipment and technology used in manufacturing, improvements in techniques, improvements in software used in product design, development and supply chain and delivery systems
3. Business model innovation — changes in how products get to the market

**Utility Model** (relevant for AI products): A government-granted exclusive right for a new product or process offering a new technical solution to a problem. Protects for **10 years**; making it easy and fast to register. This system allows the holder to exclude others from exploiting the protected innovation, making it particularly relevant for SMEs and particularly for protecting extra functionality features in ICT products with short life cycles.

**WIPO Copyright Treaty (1996):** This treaty updates the Berne Convention to address the challenges of the digital age — directly relevant to AI-generated digital content questions under international IP law.

### Government Ownership of AI-Generated Output

Under Section 3.1, where the government (through MoICT&NG) is the funding body:
- Any unique product innovations, including software or new emerging hardware developed under the support of Contractors' funds like microchips, radioactive technologies, Virtual Reality (VR) gear, etc., should be registered for IP rights with the Uganda local regulator (URSB)
- The government takes the position that IP generated from government-funded innovation will immediately apply within Uganda; however, intellectual property rights should not be used solely to prevent further innovation and development by others

## SECTION J: Practical Guidance for ICT Startups

### IP Checklist for Digital Businesses at Launch (from Section 2.4 — Figure 4)

The document's four-step IP management cycle for innovators:

1. **List your intellectual property** — Assess existing IP; identify what is unique to your product or service
2. **Make sure your idea is unique** — Check to see if it can be directly registered for IP for your product or service; conduct prior art search
3. **Create an intellectual property portfolio** — Keep a log of activities that records the development of your innovation/invention
4. **Contract your intellectual property** — Ensure that all contracts/consultancy agreements clearly address management of any intellectual property developed

### Benefits of IP Protection (Section 2.2 — Figure 3)

Six core benefits the document identifies:

1. **Strategic IP Use** — Align IP with legislation, government policy, and national/SDG objectives
2. **Compliance and Accountability** — Ensure compliance and meet record-keeping and accountability obligations
3. **Resource Efficiency** — Ensure appropriate and effective use of resources necessary for IP
4. **Facilitated IP Access** — Enable those with facilitated rights to use or distribute in promoting access
5. **Reduced Duplication** — Avoid redundant IP acquisition or creation efforts
6. **Enhanced IP Protection** — Improve IP rights protection while minimising infringement risks

### Open Source vs. Proprietary Strategy

**When to use open source (contractor perspective):**
- Saves agencies time and money
- Enables re-use by other government agencies and developers in the private sector
- Avoids high costs associated with tailormade solutions

**Two options available under an open source arrangement:**
1. Attains a licence for the software that shall be used for government and private-facing applications, depending on what it shall be used for (may be exclusive or non-exclusive)
2. The Contractor signs a deed of assignment with the software vendor

**When to retain proprietary ownership:**
- When the software represents a core business asset
- When the technology provides competitive advantage that would be lost by open sourcing
- When the IP is linked to future commercialisation revenue

**Deed of Assignment:** A duly registered legal document that transfers legal rights and benefits in the IP that can be used when a party to an agreement wishes to assign its rights and benefits under that agreement to another person. No payments are required for a deed of assignment, and duration is usually indicated to indicate the time frame for ownership of rights.

### Commercialisation and Revenue (Section 5.0)

**Full Ownership (Section 5.1):** Innovators with exclusive IP rights will freely distribute and sell their solutions and products commercially. The Government of Uganda is focused on promoting local talent, growing entrepreneurship within the ICT sector, increasing job opportunities and ensuring value for money.

**Revenue Sharing with MoICT&NG (Section 5.2):** For applications, software and innovations directly supporting core Government business functions, there will be a revenue share where applicable, agreed upon by both parties: a **25 per cent share** of revenue obtained from IP from these innovations. The 25 per cent is expected to cover the recurrent costs of development and commercialisation of the IP and finance more innovations.

**Registration by Government of Uganda (Section 5.3):** Where the government owns the IP rights through MoICT&NG, it must register with URSB.

**Registration by Innovators (Section 5.4):** For innovations developed (fully or partially) before obtaining funding from Contractor, the innovators/innovation hubs shall register their IP rights with URSB. For innovations developed from scratch with funding from Contractor and whose IP is owned by the innovator, the innovators/innovation hubs will be expected to register their IP rights with URSB. For government-funded programmes, MoICT&NG will conduct a 3-year hand-holding process before giving clearance for the IP rights to be attained, ensuring the specific innovations meet expected technical standards, operational requirements and can market/sell their innovations sufficiently.

### Non-Disclosure Agreements (Section 5.5)

Non-disclosure is the release of information on an invention to the public before the patent application is filed or the release of an innovation after it has been acquired, but disclosing information that either party should not disclose as per their NDA. Disclosure may include abstracts, poster sessions, shelved theses, or even specific talks describing an innovation to an open audience, even if given by someone who is eventually judged not to be the inventor. Such unauthorised disclosure may disqualify an invention from patentability.

Intellectual property disclosures are usually considered confidential. The contractor shall obtain written acknowledgement of NDA obligations from the innovators and members within the disclosure team. A template NDA is provided in Annex B (see below).

### MoICT&NG's Six Roles (Section 5.6)

1. **IP Rights Protection and Guidance** — Provide innovators with clear guidelines on ownership, registration, commercialisation, and disclosure of IP; assist in applying for IP protection for copyrights, patents, designs or trademarks under relevant laws
2. **Support and Incentivisation** — Offer assistance to innovators in fulfilling their IP obligations; reward and encourage participation in subsequent exploitation of innovations
3. **IP Commercialisation Strategies** — Advise creators on available options for commercialising IP or making it freely available; includes licensing, technology transfer, and other suitable commercialisation pathways
4. **IP Monitoring and Evaluation** — Conduct regular reviews of IP development and associated commercial activities and outcomes from MoICT&NG-funded innovations; identify areas for improvement
5. **IP Landscape Analysis and Guidance** — Guide innovators in assessing existing IPs in the field that may impact their innovations; conduct prior art searches and provide guidance on avoiding IP infringement
6. **IP Awareness and Capacity Building** — Raise awareness through nationwide workshops; advocate for inclusion of dedicated IP protection course units in institutions of higher learning

**Making IP protection affordable:** MoICT&NG will seek to make IP protection more affordable for Ugandan innovators by:
- Mobilising funds to support costs associated with IP protection
- Advocating for government grants or subsidies to help innovators cover IP protection expenses
- Partnering with private companies or organisations to provide discounted rates for IP protection services

### Common IP Mistakes to Avoid

The document identifies the following IP risk scenarios that constitute mistakes/failures:
- Contracts not addressing pre-existing IP — entering a contract without clarifying who owns background IP
- Infringing on a third party's IP — launching a product that uses someone else's protected technology
- Not appropriately protecting the Agency's/company's IP — failing to register or secure key IP assets
- Failure to adequately protect and keep confidential IP — allowing trade secrets to be disclosed without NDAs

**For government contracts specifically:**
- Failure to include IP identification and disclosure in procurement documentation
- Failing to register IP with URSB within the MoICT&NG's 3-year hand-holding period for funded innovations
- Not signing NDAs with team members who have access to confidential IP
- Abandoning IP (Section 2.9) — disposing of IP when it has reached the end of its usefulness, when a third party can better exploit it, or when the government attains ownership under a funding agreement

### Annex B — Non-Disclosure Agreement Template

### Annex D — Sample Revenue and Equity Sharing Agreement
Template for use between the Trust (government), the Organisation (innovator/contractor), and a Technology Transfer Group (if applicable). Covers: revenue sharing table (tiered by cumulative income: X%, Y%, Z%), equity sharing, accounting and payments, monitoring of Trust-funded IP, exploitation safeguards.

**Revenue sharing structure:** Tiered based on cumulative income:
- Income up to threshold X: X% to Organisation/Tech Transfer Group + X% to Trust
- Income up to threshold Y: Y% (proportional scale)
- Income above Z: Z% (proportional scale)

**Payment terms:** Late payments subject to interest; all payments exclusive of VAT payable in Uganda; financial statements required within 3 months of annual accounting date; exploitation reports required annually.

## Quick Reference: IP Terms and Duration Summary

| IP Type | Term | Registration | Best For (ICT) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Copyright | 50 years (software); life + 50 years (individual) | Optional but recommended | Source code, databases, UI, content, documentation |
| Trademark | 7 years (renewable indefinitely in 10-year blocks) | Required | App name, logo, brand, domain brand |
| Patent | 20 years | Required | Novel hardware inventions, patentable processes |
| Utility Model | 10 years | Required | Software-adjacent incremental innovations, app features |
| Trade Secret | Indefinite (while secret maintained) | None | Algorithms, proprietary AI models, business logic |
| Industrial Design | 5 years (+ two 5-year renewals) | Required | UI/UX visual design, hardware aesthetics |
