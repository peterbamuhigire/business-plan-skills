## 1. The 6D Model for Modern Strategy

The 6D model is a cyclical strategy framework built around six sequenced steps (Digital Business Strategy, 2024, Ch.4–7). It replaces linear strategic planning with iterative "stradaptability" — strategic adaptability in a rapidly changing environment.

**The six steps:**

1. **Discover** — Understand the market, how the business is positioned, the social and cultural environment it operates in, and internal performance and ambitions
2. **Detect** — Look further out to future-proof thinking: identify impending or possible external trends and internal signals that need to be considered
3. **Diagnose** — Assess knowledge gathered in steps 1–2 and extract critical themes for consideration, including how and where to create value
4. **Direct** — Prioritise strategic themes for development; nail down required drivers, desired objectives, and target outcomes
5. **Deliver** — Translate chosen strategic themes into actionable delivery plans supported by governance
6. **Dexterity** — Ongoing internal and external evaluation and recalibration using the Discover-to-Deliver cycle continuously

**"Stradaptability"** — the combination of strategic stability (fixed purpose) with operational adaptability (iterative external scanning and recalibration). The model is cyclical: once Deliver is complete, Discover begins again.

**Application to business plans:** The 6D model gives investors confidence that the founding team has a systematic approach to strategy, not just a static plan. In the AI integration section, describe which step in the 6D cycle AI tools enhance most (typically: Detect = trend monitoring; Diagnose = data analytics; Deliver = automated execution; Dexterity = performance dashboards).

| 6D Step | AI Tool Application | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Discover** | Market data aggregation; sentiment analysis | Scraping sector reports; Google Trends monitoring |
| **Detect** | Anomaly detection; news monitoring | Price spike alerts; competitor activity feeds |
| **Diagnose** | Data analytics; pattern recognition | Sales funnel analysis; unit economics modelling |
| **Direct** | Scenario planning tools; decision support | Financial stress-test models; SWOT automation |
| **Deliver** | Project management automation; CRM | Trello/Asana automations; automated invoicing |
| **Dexterity** | KPI dashboards; early warning systems | Power BI / Google Data Studio; alert thresholds |

## 2. The Strategy Nucleus (Purpose-Led Strategy)

The Strategy Nucleus is the nested architecture of business intent (Digital Business Strategy, 2024, Ch.5, Figure 12):

```
Purpose (centre — fixed, permanent)
  ↑
Vision (long-range aspirational state)
  ↑
Mission (what the business does and for whom)
  ↑
Strategy (how the business creates and captures value)
  ↑
Values (principles that govern how decisions are made)
```

**Purpose is the fixed anchor** — unchanged as the outer layers iterate. Vision, Mission, and Strategy evolve as markets shift, but Purpose remains constant.

**Purpose-performance data:**
- 62% of consumers want companies to take a stand on current relevant issues (Accenture, 30,000 respondents)
- 47% said they would walk away if disappointed by a company's social actions; 17% said "never come back"
- 62% of employees in purpose-driven companies describe their companies as "focused on innovation and continuous transformation" vs. 26% in companies without a clear purpose (Ernst & Young / Harvard Business Review Analytics)

**Connection to Golden Circle (Sinek):** See `02-company-overview/references/mission-vision-why.md` — Purpose = Why; Strategy = How; Products/Services = What. The Strategy Nucleus formalises this into five nested layers.

**Nucleus integrity test:** Ask of each strategic decision — does this serve the Purpose? A move that increases short-term profit but undermines Purpose fails the test. This discipline is particularly relevant when integrating AI: automation that saves cost but degrades customer relationships may violate Purpose even if it improves margins.

**Application to business plans:** In Section 02, anchor the company narrative in Purpose first. In Section 14 (AI Integration), describe how AI tools serve the Purpose — not just automate tasks, but create the data visibility and speed that enable the company to deliver its purpose at scale.

## 3. Three Horizons and Multimodal Innovation

McKinsey's Three Horizons, updated with Deloitte's multimodal framing (Digital Business Strategy, 2024, Ch.12):

The original Horizons model was misinterpreted as sequential — first master H1, then move to H2, then H3. The current understanding is simultaneous operation:

| Horizon | Mode | Strategic Intent | Culture Required | Return Currency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **H1 — Exploitative** | Sustaining innovation | Cost and profit — sweat the core | Lean, low risk, incremental | Margins, ROI, productivity |
| **H2 — Extending** | Developmental innovation | Diversification and growth — stretch the core | Assertive, risk-accepting, flexible | Acquisition, market share, payback |
| **H3 — Exploratory** | Disruptive innovation | Experimentation — probe for new cores | Rapid, risk-taking, curious | Learning, venture building, brand |

**Multimodalism principle:** Organisations that pursue all three simultaneously ("multispeed innovation") consistently outperform those that sequence them. This requires organisational "ambidexterity" — deploying multiple practices at once. Leadership discipline and organisational tooling are prerequisites.

**Resource allocation guide (for SMEs):**
- H1: 70–80% of management time and budget
- H2: 15–20% — at least one growth initiative with dedicated ownership
- H3: 5–10% — at least one exploratory experiment with a clear learning objective and kill criteria

**Horizon misalignment risk:** The most common strategic error is letting H1 urgency crowd out H2/H3 entirely. When this happens, the business is highly efficient at the present but has no preparation for the future. AI tools can help here: dashboards that automate H1 monitoring free management attention for H2/H3 work.

**Application to business plans:** For most Ugandan SMEs and early-stage businesses, H1 dominates (sweating existing operations). A credible AI integration section shows how AI tools extend H1 efficiency while creating the data foundation for H2 development. H3 experiments (new business models, new markets) should be described as small, time-boxed pilots, not full commitments.

## 4. Doblin's 10 Types of Innovation

Doblin's framework categorises innovation across three domains (Digital Business Strategy, 2024, Ch.12):

**Configuration domain** (inside the business):
1. **Profit model** — How the business makes money: pricing innovation, revenue model changes, tiered access
2. **Network** — How the business develops partnerships and open innovation to drive value
3. **Structure** — How intangible or tangible assets are utilised and configured
4. **Process** — How operational processes are optimised or made distinctive

**Offering domain** (core product/service):
5. **Product performance** — Value of the product itself: features, quality, reliability
6. **Product system** — Complementary products bundled together (e.g., smartphone + SIM)

**Experience domain** (customer interaction):
7. **Service** — How customers are treated and supported
8. **Channel** — Which platforms and pathways customers use to access the offering
9. **Brand** — Distinct message, values clarity, customer identity signal
10. **Customer engagement** — Innovative interactions and marketing techniques

**Strategic use:** Map current competitive advantages against Doblin's 10 types. Most SMEs compete on Product Performance (type 5) alone. Businesses with advantages in 3+ types have more defensible positions. The framework also identifies where AI creates new innovation opportunities — especially Process (type 4), Service (type 7), Channel (type 8), and Customer Engagement (type 10).

**Doblin × AI overlay:**

| Doblin Type | AI Application |
|---|---|
| Profit model (1) | Dynamic pricing algorithms; subscription model analytics |
| Network (2) | Platform matching; supplier scoring models |
| Process (4) | Automated quality control; inventory optimisation |
| Service (7) | Chatbots; automated follow-up sequences |
| Channel (8) | Personalised recommendations; mobile-first delivery |
| Customer engagement (10) | Behavioural segmentation; loyalty trigger modelling |

**Innovation audit:** Use Doblin's 10 types to audit where the business currently innovates. If all innovation is in type 5 (Product Performance), the business is vulnerable to commodity competition. Types 1–4 (Configuration) and 7–10 (Experience) are often underinvested and represent the highest-impact AI application opportunities for SMEs.

## 5. Blue Ocean Strategy in Five Steps

Blue Ocean Strategy creates uncontested market space by simultaneously differentiating and reducing costs (Digital Business Strategy, 2024, Ch.12):

**The strategy map** plots competitive attributes on a curve. The goal is to create a "blue ocean" — clear water between your strategy curve and competitors' curves.

**Five-step process:**
1. **Map** — Map key competitive attributes against closest/most challenging competitors on a strategy curve
2. **Identify** — Through ideation (including Design Thinking), identify features or services that could create competitive differentiation and uniqueness
3. **Isolate** — Isolate features that are in high demand or would enable step-change growth
4. **Deprioritise** — Select features to be de-emphasised: low-demand items, features the market is not yet ready for, or ones the business cannot execute
5. **Eliminate** — Retire features that are obsolete, out of fashion, or commercially unviable. Simplification creates focus.

**Gaming console example:** Oculus/Meta Quest chose to differentiate on VR access and avatars while deprioritising graphics quality and crowd-gaming (features where established competitors dominated). This created a distinct competitive position without competing head-on.

**Four Actions Framework (ERRC grid):**

| Action | Question | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Eliminate** | Which factors the industry takes for granted should be eliminated? | Reduces cost |
| **Reduce** | Which factors should be reduced well below the industry standard? | Reduces cost |
| **Raise** | Which factors should be raised well above the industry standard? | Increases value |
| **Create** | Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered? | Increases value |

The ERRC grid forces simultaneous cost reduction and value creation — the defining characteristic of Blue Ocean thinking.

**Connection to competitive analysis:** Blue Ocean Strategy provides the operational technique for "creating clear blue water" between the business and alternatives — one of the quality criteria in Section 06 (Competitive Analysis).

## 6. VRIN Analysis

VRIN evaluates whether a competitive resource or capability provides a genuine, sustained competitive advantage (Digital Business Strategy, 2024):

| Criterion | Question | If Yes |
|---|---|---|
| **Valuable** | Does this resource help the business meet customer needs better than alternatives? | Basis for competitive advantage |
| **Rare** | Do few or no competitors possess this resource? | Prevents easy imitation |
| **Inimitable** | Is it difficult for competitors to replicate or substitute? | Sustains the advantage over time |
| **Non-substitutable** | Can competitors meet the same customer need a different way without needing this resource? | Determines durability |

**VRIN in practice:** Apply VRIN to each claimed competitive advantage in the business plan. A resource that is Valuable but not Rare provides no sustainable advantage (it can be copied). A resource that is Rare and Inimitable but not Valuable does not improve competitive position. The business plan should identify 1–2 resources or capabilities that pass all four tests.

**Common VRIN failures in SME business plans:**
- Claiming "quality products" as a competitive advantage — Valuable, but not Rare or Inimitable
- Claiming "experienced team" — Valuable and possibly Rare, but substitutable (competitors can hire experience)
- Claiming "low prices" — Not Rare; erodes margins; invites price war

**AI as VRIN asset:** Proprietary training data collected from operations can be Valuable, Rare, Inimitable (data is specific to this business), and Non-substitutable — making it one of the strongest moats in a digital business. The AI integration section should identify what data the business collects that competitors cannot easily replicate.

**VRIN assessment template:**

| Resource / Capability | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Non-substitutable? | VRIN Score |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| [Claim 1] | Y/N | Y/N | Y/N | Y/N | 0–4 |
| [Claim 2] | Y/N | Y/N | Y/N | Y/N | 0–4 |
| Proprietary customer data | Y | Y | Y | Y | 4/4 |

Only resources scoring 4/4 provide sustained competitive advantage. Resources scoring 2–3 provide temporary advantage. Resources scoring 0–1 do not belong in the competitive analysis.

## 7. Design Thinking (Five Steps)

Design Thinking is a structured creative process for developing user-centred solutions (Digital Business Strategy, 2024, Ch.12):

1. **Discover** — Empathise with users: understand their actual behaviour, frustrations, and unmet needs through observation and interviews
2. **Define** — Synthesise insights into a clear problem statement: "How might we...?" framing
3. **Ideate** — Generate multiple possible solutions without early evaluation; volume before quality
4. **Prototype** — Build low-fidelity representations of the most promising ideas quickly and cheaply
5. **Test** — Put prototypes in front of real users; observe behaviour rather than asking opinions; iterate

**Key principle — observation over opinion:** In the Test step, watch what users do, not what they say they will do. Users consistently report preferences that do not match their actual behaviour. Behavioural observation is more reliable evidence than survey responses.

**Design Thinking × AI product development:**

| Design Thinking Step | AI Development Application |
|---|---|
| Discover | Interview target users about current pain points; map their existing (non-AI) workflows |
| Define | Write a "How might we automate/improve X for [user type]?" problem statement |
| Ideate | Generate 10+ possible AI feature concepts; no filtering yet |
| Prototype | Build a mock-up, wizard-of-oz simulation, or simple spreadsheet model before writing code |
| Test | Observe real users interacting with the prototype; capture confusion points and workarounds |

**Connection to MVP development:** See `03-products-services/references/` — Design Thinking produces the user insight that drives MVP scope decisions. The Prototype step in Design Thinking maps to the low-fidelity MVP concept.

**Application:** Use Design Thinking when assessing what AI features to build for customers vs. internal operations. Customer-facing AI (chatbots, recommendations) requires Design Thinking to ensure the experience matches real user behaviour, not assumed behaviour.

## 8. The Transform Loop (Palfreyman, 2020)

The Transform Loop is an agile transformation engine for businesses implementing digital change:

**Six steps:**
1. **Scan** — Continuously monitor internal performance and external trends for transformation opportunities
2. **Prioritise** — Score opportunities on a Transformation Backlog (0–9 scale for strategic value and implementation difficulty)
3. **Learn** — Before building, run learning experiments to validate assumptions
4. **Experiment** — Time-boxed tests of highest-priority backlog items
5. **Plan** — Convert validated experiments into implementation plans
6. **Build** — Execute against the plan using agile delivery sprints

**The Transformation Backlog:** A ranked list of digital improvement items scored on two axes:
- **Strategic value (0–9):** Does this create meaningful value for customers or the business?
- **Implementation difficulty (0–9):** How hard is it to build and deploy?

Prioritise high-value, moderate-difficulty items first. Avoid high-difficulty, low-value items entirely.

**Backlog priority matrix:**

| | Low Difficulty (0–3) | Medium Difficulty (4–6) | High Difficulty (7–9) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **High Value (7–9)** | Do first | Do second | Do third (with caution) |
| **Medium Value (4–6)** | Do if resources allow | Backlog | Avoid |
| **Low Value (0–3)** | Backlog | Avoid | Eliminate |

**Strategy first principle (Rumelt, cited in Palfreyman):** Three strategy questions that must be answered before transformation begins:
1. Where do we compete?
2. What unique value do we deliver?
3. How do we deliver it?

Transformation without strategy answers produces activity without direction. Many digital transformation projects fail because technology implementation begins before strategic clarity is established.

**Five faces of agile (Palfreyman):**
1. **Strategic agility** — the ability to recalibrate strategy as signals emerge
2. **Transform loop** — the iterative scan-learn-build engine
3. **Experiment-mode agile** — hypothesis-driven sprints for unproven ideas
4. **Architectural flexibility** — modular systems that can be changed without full rebuilds
5. **Cultural agility** — the ability to change how people work, not just what systems they use

**Common transformation failure modes (Palfreyman):**
- Starting with technology before defining the strategic problem
- Treating transformation as a one-off project rather than an ongoing capability
- Underestimating the cultural change required (faces 5)
- Building monolithic systems that cannot adapt (face 4)
- Skipping the Learn step and moving directly from Prioritise to Build

## 9. Industry 4.0 and the AI Integration Journey

Three stages of digital maturity (Palfreyman, 2020):

| Stage | Definition | Strategic Value |
|---|---|---|
| **Digitisation** | Converting analogue processes to digital records | Low — data is now capturable but not yet used strategically |
| **Digitalisation** | Using digital data to improve existing processes | Medium — efficiency gains and operational visibility |
| **Digital transformation** | Reinventing the business model using digital capabilities | High — structural competitive advantage |

Most Ugandan SMEs are at the Digitisation stage (mobile money records, basic accounting software). The AI integration section should be honest about where the business currently sits and what the realistic next step is — not a leapfrog claim to full transformation.

**Digital maturity self-assessment:**

| Indicator | Stage 1: Digitisation | Stage 2: Digitalisation | Stage 3: Transformation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Financial records | Paper / mobile money history | Accounting software (QuickBooks, Wave) | Real-time dashboards with alerts |
| Customer data | WhatsApp contact list | CRM with purchase history | Predictive churn / upsell models |
| Inventory | Manual counts | Spreadsheet tracking | Automated reorder triggers |
| Marketing | Word of mouth | Social media presence | Data-driven campaign optimisation |
| Operations | Owner-dependent | Documented processes | AI-assisted process automation |

**Realistic AI integration pathway for SMEs:**

*Year 1 (Digitisation → Digitalisation):*
- Implement mobile money transaction exports → cash-flow spreadsheet
- Set up WhatsApp Business with auto-replies and product catalogue
- Begin recording customer purchase data in a structured format
- Adopt basic accounting software (Wave is free; QuickBooks Lite for UGX ~100,000/month)

*Year 2 (Digitalisation — deepening):*
- Build simple demand forecasting model in Google Sheets using historical sales data
- Use social media analytics to identify highest-performing content and channels
- Implement basic CRM (HubSpot free tier or Zoho CRM)
- Automate invoice generation and payment reminders

*Year 3+ (Toward Transformation):*
- Explore AI-assisted pricing using demand and competitor data
- Build customer segmentation model from accumulated purchase history
- Evaluate sector-specific AI tools (agri-tech, fintech, health-tech platforms operating in East Africa)
- Assess whether proprietary data has reached sufficient scale to create VRIN advantage

## 10. Uganda/East Africa Application

| Framework | Uganda/EA Application |
|---|---|
| **6D Model** | Ugandan businesses typically have strong Discover (market knowledge) but weak Detect (no systematic trend-scanning). Recommend: set up a quarterly "Detect" review using UBOS data, Bank of Uganda bulletins, and sector association reports. |
| **Strategy Nucleus** | Many Ugandan SMEs have a mission statement but no Purpose — a deeper "why" that would differentiate them to both customers and impact investors. Purpose-led positioning is increasingly required by DFIs and NGO funders. |
| **Three Horizons** | H1 is where cash is. H2 is where growth is. H3 is where competitive advantage is built. For a Ugandan agri-processing company: H1 = current product line; H2 = export market entry; H3 = data-driven crop forecasting service. All three can run simultaneously with modest resource allocation to H2/H3. |
| **Doblin's 10 Types** | Ugandan businesses typically compete on Product Performance (type 5) alone. The highest-impact innovation opportunities are usually Process (type 4 — lower-cost delivery) and Channel (type 8 — mobile-first distribution). Both are low-capital innovations. |
| **Blue Ocean** | In fragmented Ugandan markets, the Blue Ocean approach often reveals that the competitive attributes everyone is fighting over (price, quantity) matter less than the attributes no one has claimed (trust, speed, after-sale support). The strategy map forces this clarity. |
| **VRIN** | For Ugandan businesses, rare and inimitable resources often include: established relationships with specific suppliers or government bodies, certifications (UNBS, organic), geographic access to inputs, and community trust. These are real VRIN advantages that belong in the competitive analysis. |
| **Design Thinking** | In markets with low digital literacy, Design Thinking's observation-over-opinion principle is especially important. Ugandan end-users frequently agree with features in interviews that they will not use in practice. Prototype testing with real users before investment is essential. |
| **Transform Loop** | For Ugandan SMEs beginning digital transformation, the Transformation Backlog prevents overcommitment. Start with: (1) mobile money integration for payment (high value, low difficulty); (2) WhatsApp Business for customer service (high value, very low difficulty); (3) basic inventory tracking in Google Sheets or similar (medium value, low difficulty). |
| **AI in Uganda** | Practical AI tools at low cost: M-Pesa/MTN Mobile Money data exports for cash-flow forecasting; WhatsApp Business API for automated customer responses; Google Sheets with basic formulas for demand forecasting; Kenya-based Zola Electric and Farmbetter as regional AI-adjacent examples. |

**East African AI ecosystem reference points:**
- **Farmbetter (Kenya/Uganda)** — AI-driven crop advisory delivered via SMS and USSD; demonstrates that AI can reach rural users without smartphones
- **Apollo Agriculture (Kenya)** — machine learning for smallholder credit scoring using satellite imagery and farm data
- **Umba (Kenya/Nigeria)** — alternative credit scoring for micro-borrowers; relevant model for Ugandan fintech
- **JUMO (pan-Africa)** — financial inclusion platform using transaction data for credit; UGX-denominated products available through MTN Uganda
- **M-KOPA (Uganda)** — IoT + data platform for asset finance; demonstrates AI-enabled customer lifecycle management at scale in Uganda

**Regulatory context for AI in Uganda:**
- Uganda's National Information Technology Authority (NITA-U) oversees digital infrastructure but has no AI-specific regulation as of 2025
- Data protection: Uganda Data Protection and Privacy Act 2019 applies to personal data used in AI systems — businesses collecting customer data for AI training must register with the Personal Data Protection Office (PDPO)
- Financial AI: Bank of Uganda sandbox framework exists for fintech innovation; AI-driven credit scoring products require BoU approval if classified as financial services
- UNBS has no AI product certification standard as of 2025; international standards (ISO/IEC 42001 for AI management systems) may apply for export markets

## 12. Quick Reference

**6D Cycle:** Discover → Detect → Diagnose → Direct → Deliver → Dexterity → (repeat)

**Strategy Nucleus layers (inner to outer):** Purpose → Vision → Mission → Strategy → Values

**Three Horizons:** H1 = exploit core (70–80%); H2 = extend core (15–20%); H3 = explore new cores (5–10%)

**Doblin's highest-AI-impact types:** Process (4), Service (7), Channel (8), Customer Engagement (10)

**Blue Ocean ERRC:** Eliminate, Reduce, Raise, Create

**VRIN threshold:** Must score 4/4 for sustained competitive advantage

**Design Thinking sequence:** Discover → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test

**Transform Loop:** Scan → Prioritise → Learn → Experiment → Plan → Build

**Palfreyman's five agile faces:** Strategic agility, Transform loop, Experiment-mode, Architectural flexibility, Cultural agility

**Digital maturity stages:** Digitisation → Digitalisation → Digital Transformation
