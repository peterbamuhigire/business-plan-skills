# Algorithmic AI Transformation Reference

Use this reference to keep the AI section practical. The two useful ideas from these books are:

- AI should follow **process redesign**, not replace it.
- AI should improve the **customer and employee experience**, not just cut cost.

## 1. The five-step algorithm for AI-enabled redesign

From Jon McNeill's *The Algorithm*:

1. **Question every requirement**
2. **Delete every possible step in a process**
3. **Simplify and optimise**
4. **Accelerate cycle time**
5. **Automate last**

### Planning implication

Do not recommend AI against a broken process. First ask:

- Which approval, report, handoff, or data request exists only because "we have always done it"?
- Which steps can be removed entirely?
- Which steps can be standardised before software touches them?
- Where is the real delay: people, handoffs, waiting time, or system friction?

Only after those questions are answered should the plan recommend workflow automation, copilots, or AI agents.

## 2. Expand the product to the entire customer experience

McNeill's cultural insight matters for business plans: the product is not just the thing sold. It is the **whole customer experience** around discovery, purchase, onboarding, support, and renewal.

### Planning implication

When proposing AI, map it to the full experience:

- demand generation and enquiry handling
- buying or application friction
- onboarding and first-value delivery
- support quality and response time
- repeat purchase, retention, and referral

If AI improves internal cost but worsens trust, clarity, or response quality, it is strategically weak.

## 3. Customer-experience-led AI

Francis Goh's contribution is useful here: AI initiatives should sit inside a wider CX and innovation roadmap.

Core prompts for any AI initiative:

- Which customer or employee pain point is being improved?
- How does the AI initiative support the company's broader vision and CX ambition?
- What adoption, governance, and literacy work is required for it to stick?
- What ROI metric will prove it is actually working?

### Planning implication

A strong AI section should not be a tool list. It should connect:

- business strategy
- CX strategy
- process redesign
- adoption and literacy
- ROI measurement

## 4. AI literacy and adoption

AI rollout fails when the business treats tools as the whole transformation.

Minimum adoption elements:

- owner or sponsor for each AI use case
- staff training on safe and effective use
- clear boundaries for human review
- a pilot period with success metrics
- a feedback loop to refine or stop the use case

## 5. Metrics that matter

Measure AI by operational and commercial outcomes, not by novelty.

Useful metrics:

- cycle time reduction
- first-response time
- error reduction
- throughput increase
- labour hours saved
- conversion uplift
- retention or repeat-purchase improvement
- customer satisfaction or complaint reduction
- payback period

## 6. Transformation culture

The books converge on three cultural needs:

- **urgency and accountability**: someone owns each initiative, deadlines are explicit, and reviews are regular
- **learning posture**: treat pilots as evidence generation, not as PR
- **dogfooding**: use the system internally before making it customer-critical

## Uganda / East Africa application

- Most SMEs should begin with a narrow AI pilot tied to one painful workflow, not a full transformation claim.
- WhatsApp, CRM follow-up, quoting, customer support, payment reminders, and simple forecasting are often stronger first use cases than advanced custom models.
- AI literacy is especially important where teams are new to structured digital tools; training and supervision belong in the plan.
- Customer trust matters. Any customer-facing AI should state when a human takes over, especially in credit, health, or high-value B2B relationships.
