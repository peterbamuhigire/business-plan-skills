# High-Value Proposal Strategy Reference

Use this reference for proposals where the client is buying transformation, growth, or operating improvement rather than a simple commodity service.

## 1. Lead with a transformation thesis

A winning high-value proposal should answer:

- what changes for the client
- why change is urgent now
- why your team is credible to deliver it
- how the client will measure value

Do not lead with "about us". Lead with the client's growth, efficiency, risk, or market problem.

## 2. Use the eight pillars as capability proof

From Brendan Keegan's transformation framing, proposals should show capability across these pillars:

1. Leadership
2. Culture
3. People
4. Systems
5. IQ
6. EQ
7. Flexibility
8. Fearlessness

### Proposal implication

When the assignment is strategic, operational, or organisational, do not describe only activities. Show how your approach strengthens the client's ability to execute across these pillars.

## 3. Frame value by lifecycle

From Rose Jia: growth strategy should match lifecycle stage.

### Proposal implication

If the client is:

- launching something new: emphasise validation, early traction, rapid learning
- scaling: emphasise repeatability, operating discipline, conversion, retention
- defending a mature business: emphasise resilience, optimisation, adjacencies, profitability

This prevents a generic methodology section.

## 4. Make the economics visible

High-value proposals should make commercial value legible:

- revenue growth potential
- cost reduction or productivity gain
- cycle-time reduction
- risk reduction
- customer-retention improvement
- payback period or ROI logic

The proposal does not need a full financial model in every case, but it should signal how value will be measured.

## 5. Structure the message around confidence

Useful positioning principles from the books:

- clarity beats breadth
- quality beats volume
- profitable growth matters more than busy activity
- the buyer should feel that you understand both the business case and the execution risk

Use a disciplined narrative:

1. client situation
2. root problem
3. transformation thesis
4. approach and workstreams
5. team and proof
6. commercial value
7. low-friction next step

## 6. Include a 90-day mobilisation plan

For serious proposals, especially six- or seven-figure engagements, include the first 90 days.

Minimum mobilisation structure:

- days 1-30: diagnose, align, baseline
- days 31-60: design, prioritise, quick wins
- days 61-90: implement initial changes, governance, reporting

This reduces perceived execution risk and shows control from day one.

## 7. What to avoid

- generic claims like "we have extensive experience"
- feature-heavy methodology with no commercial logic
- a budget disconnected from outcomes
- no ownership model for delivery
- no risk-reduction plan for the client's stakeholders

## Uganda / East Africa application

- Many buyers care about transformation but still need confidence on execution discipline and financial realism.
- In proposal environments with high scepticism, visible early wins, practical governance, and a clear first-90-days plan are stronger than abstract innovation language.
- Where buyers are cost-sensitive, position price against ROI, risk reduction, and payback, not against effort alone.
