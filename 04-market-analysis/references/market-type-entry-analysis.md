# Market Type & Entry Analysis

> Based on Blank, S. & Dorf, B. (2012). *The Startup Owner's Manual*; Cooper, B. & Vlaskovits, P. (2010). *The Entrepreneur's Guide to Customer Development*; Alam, M.M. *Transforming an Idea Into a Business with Design Thinking*.

## Market Type Framework

Every market entry strategy must begin by identifying the market type. Each type requires fundamentally different approaches to timing, spending, and competitive strategy (Blank & Dorf, 2012).

### Four Market Types

| Type | Definition | Key Challenge | Time to Scale | Spending Pattern |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Existing market** | Known customers, known competitors | Competing on product attributes | 1-3 years | Front-loaded (sales/marketing) |
| **New market** | No existing customers or category | Educating customers that a problem exists | 3-7 years | Back-loaded (wait for adoption) |
| **Re-segmented market** | Underserved niche or low-cost position in existing market | Finding the right segment to target | 2-4 years | Moderate, targeted |
| **Clone market** | Copying successful model from another country/region | Local adaptation and execution | 1-3 years | Moderate |

### Revenue Growth Curves by Market Type

- **Existing market** — relatively predictable growth based on market share capture from competitors
- **New market** — slow initial adoption (the "hockey stick" is real but takes 3-7 years; don't project it in year 1)
- **Re-segmented market** — similar to new market curve initially; assess the switchable customer subset
- **Clone market** — depends on local market dynamics; reference the original market's growth pattern

### Market Type Determines Competitive Strategy

| Market Type | Compete On | Don't Compete On |
|---|---|---|
| Existing | Product performance, features, speed | Price (unless you have structural cost advantage) |
| New | Vision, education, evangelism | Features (no one understands the category yet) |
| Re-segmented (niche) | Specialised fit for underserved segment | Breadth (you're the specialist) |
| Re-segmented (low-cost) | Price and "good enough" quality | Premium features |
| Clone | Local execution, relationships, regulation | Innovation (the model is proven elsewhere) |

## Cost-of-Entry Analysis

From military operations research, applied to market entry (Blank & Dorf, 2012):

### Market Share Thresholds

| Market Structure | Share Held | Implication for New Entrant |
|---|---|---|
| Effective monopoly | 74%+ by single company | Unassailable — do not enter head-on |
| Duopoly | Top 2 combined >74%, within 1.7× of each other | Impervious to frontal attack |
| Market leader | 41%+ with 1.7× lead over #2 | Very difficult entry; consider re-segmentation |
| Unstable market | 26%+ but no clear dominance | Possible entry; competition on execution |
| Fragmented market | No player >26% | Easiest entry; win through superior execution |

### Attack Cost Rules

- **Head-on attack** against a dominant player requires **3× their sales/marketing budget**
- **Multi-competitor attack** requires **1.7× the target competitor's budget**
- **Re-segmentation** (niche or low-cost) is the most capital-efficient entry strategy for resource-constrained ventures

### Implications for Ugandan Market Entry

In Uganda's context, many markets are fragmented (<26% share by any player), making direct entry feasible. However:
- Some sectors have dominant players (e.g., telecoms: MTN/Airtel duopoly; banking: Stanbic/DFCU concentration)
- Import-dependent sectors may have entrenched distributors acting as gatekeepers
- Informal market share is hard to measure but real — include informal competitors in analysis

## Bottom-Up vs. Top-Down Market Estimation

### Top-Down (less reliable for startups)
Start with industry reports → apply percentage for your segment → estimate capture rate.
Problem: each percentage is a guess; errors compound multiplicatively.

### Bottom-Up (more reliable)
Start with specific customer counts → multiply by realistic purchase frequency and price → aggregate.
Better because each assumption is individually testable.

### Fred Wilson's 30/10/10 Law (web/mobile)
For digital products: 30% of registered users are monthly active, 10% are daily active, 10% of daily active are concurrent at any moment. Use to reality-check digital market projections.

## Market Segmentation Criteria

A true market segment has three characteristics (Cooper & Vlaskovits, 2010):
1. **Shared need** — members have the same problem
2. **Access to each other** — members communicate and influence each other
3. **Mutual referencing** — members reference each other when making decisions

**Benefits of single-segment focus:**
- Faster learning (one customer type)
- Unoccupied niche (less competition)
- Earlier market leadership (be the big fish in a small pond)
- "Bowling pin" sequential conquest (dominate one segment, then expand to adjacent ones)
- Capital efficiency (focused spending)

## Business Context Map

Six-sector environmental scanning tool, each split into near-term (<3 years) and long-term (>3 years) (Alam):

1. **Technology trends** — mobile money penetration, internet access expansion, solar/off-grid technology
2. **Business model trends** — platform economics, as-a-service models, data-driven pricing
3. **Competition** — broadly defined as "anything that replaces the need for your solution" (not just direct substitutes)
4. **Industry trends** — structural shifts, consolidation, regulation changes
5. **Macroeconomic trends** — GDP growth, urbanisation rate, demographic dividend, inflation
6. **Regulatory/Other** — URA requirements, KCCA/district licensing, UNBS standards, NEMA permits

## Practical Application in Business Plans

When writing section 04 (Market Analysis), explicitly:
1. **Name the market type** — existing, new, re-segmented, or clone
2. **Show the cost-of-entry analysis** — what are the market share thresholds?
3. **Use bottom-up estimation** for market sizing (top-down as cross-check only)
4. **Define the segment** using the three criteria (shared need, access, referencing)
5. **Map the business context** across all six sectors with time horizons
6. **Match the revenue curve** to the market type — don't project existing-market growth for a new-market venture
