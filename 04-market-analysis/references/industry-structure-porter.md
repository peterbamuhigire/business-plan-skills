# Industry Structure Analysis — Porter (1980)

## Overview

This reference covers the structural analysis of industries as a dynamic system: how to map the competitive landscape within an industry using **Strategic Groups**, how to predict how that landscape will change using **Industry Evolution** analysis, and how to identify which structural forces will matter most over time.

These frameworks complement the Five Forces analysis in `competitive-strategy-porter.md`. While Five Forces explains *why* an industry is attractive or unattractive in aggregate, Strategic Groups explain *why performance differs among firms within the same industry*, and Industry Evolution explains *how the competitive rules change over time*.

---

## 1. Strategic Dimensions — Mapping How Firms Compete

*(Chapter 7: Structural Analysis Within Industries)*

Before identifying strategic groups, each firm in the industry must be characterised along the key strategic dimensions that capture meaningful differences in competitive posture.

### The Strategic Dimensions

Porter identifies the following dimensions as typically capturing the relevant strategic differences among firms in most industries:

| Dimension | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Specialisation** | Degree of focus in terms of product line width, target segments, and geographic markets |
| **Brand identification** | Extent of brand-building vs. competing primarily on price |
| **Push vs. pull** | Whether the firm builds demand through end-consumer advertising (pull) or works through channel partners (push) |
| **Channel selection** | Choice of distribution from company-owned to specialty to broad-line outlets |
| **Product quality** | Level of quality in terms of materials, specifications, features |
| **Technological leadership** | Leader, fast follower, or imitator in technology |
| **Vertical integration** | Extent of value added in-house — backward integration into inputs, forward into distribution |
| **Cost position** | Whether the firm seeks the low-cost position through investment in scale and efficiency |
| **Service** | Degree of ancillary service provided — engineering, in-house service network, credit |
| **Price policy** | Relative price position in the market |
| **Leverage** | Amount of financial and operating leverage |
| **Relationship with parent** | Whether the business unit has a corporate parent that provides resources, imposes objectives, or requires shared operations |

Not every dimension matters in every industry. In a commodity industry like ammonium fertiliser, brand identification is near-zero, but vertical integration, service, and cost position vary enormously among firms.

## 2. Strategic Groups

A **strategic group** is the group of firms in an industry that are following the same or a similar strategy along the strategic dimensions. Strategic groups are the intermediate unit of analysis between the industry as a whole and the individual firm.

### Why Strategic Groups Matter

"Within-group rivalry is typically most intense — firms in the same group compete most directly." (Porter, 1980)

- **Within a group:** Firms are direct competitors — serving the same customers in the same way. Competitive rivalry is most intense within groups.
- **Between groups:** Firms compete less directly. Cross-group competition is moderated by mobility barriers.
- **Profitability varies by group:** Some strategic positions are structurally superior — they face weaker Five Forces than others.

### How to Construct a Strategic Group Map

1. Select the two or three most important strategic dimensions that differentiate firms in the industry.
2. Plot each significant competitor on a two-dimensional map (e.g., X axis = price position; Y axis = product breadth or service level).
3. Draw circles around clusters of firms with similar positions — these are strategic groups.
4. Size the circles proportional to group revenue or market share.

**Example: Major Appliance Industry (Porter)**
- **Group A (GE prototype):** Broad product lines, heavy national advertising, extensive integration, captive distribution and service. Competing on brand and scale.
- **Group B (Maytag):** Specialist producers focusing on high-quality, high-price segment with selective distribution. Competing on reputation and premium positioning.
- **Group C (Roper, Design & Manufacturing):** Unadvertised products for private label. Competing on cost.

Each group faces different competitive dynamics. Group A worries about new technology and import cost; Group B worries about quality dilution; Group C worries about volume pricing pressure from retailers.

### Strategic Group Analysis Questions

- Which strategic group does the business belong to?
- Who are the direct competitors within that group?
- What does the competitive rivalry look like within the group (pricing behaviour, product launches, distribution battles)?
- Are there positions on the map with no incumbents — and why? Is it an exploitable gap or an unviable position?
- Is the group we are targeting structurally more or less attractive than others?

## 3. Mobility Barriers

Mobility barriers are the factors that **prevent firms from moving between strategic groups**. They are analogous to barriers to entry (which prevent firms from entering the industry at all), but they operate within the industry.

### Why Mobility Barriers Sustain Performance Differences

If there were no mobility barriers, any firm that saw a more profitable strategic position would immediately move there. Mobility barriers explain why performance differences among groups persist over time. They are the structural explanation for why some firms consistently outperform others in the same industry.

### Sources of Mobility Barriers

| Barrier | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Economies of scale** | Group position requires scale not achievable without long investment period |
| **Product differentiation** | Group has established brand identities, customer loyalties, or reputations that take years and large spending to replicate |
| **Capital requirements** | Moving to the group requires large investment in facilities, R&D, advertising, or distribution |
| **Switching costs** | Customers of firms in the target group have high switching costs that prevent them from easily moving to a new entrant |
| **Access to distribution** | Target group's channels are tied up by incumbents through relationships, exclusive arrangements, or service networks |
| **Proprietary technology** | Technical capabilities that define the group's position cannot be acquired quickly |
| **Vertical integration** | Target group is integrated in ways that require the entrant to enter multiple stages simultaneously |
| **Experience and learning** | Group has accumulated operational efficiency through learning that cannot be replicated quickly |

### Strategic Implication

A firm that occupies a strategic group with **high mobility barriers** on all sides is in a protected position. A firm in a group with low mobility barriers on the entry side is vulnerable to rapid competitive imitation.

When assessing competitive position in a business plan, identify:
1. What group does this business belong to?
2. What mobility barriers protect that group from invasion by competitors from other groups?
3. Is the business trying to move between groups — and if so, what barriers must it overcome?

## 4. Industry Evolution

*(Chapter 8: Industry Evolution)*

Industries are not static. The competitive forces, the strategic groups, and the attractiveness of the industry change over time. A firm that understands the direction of change can position for the future before competitors do.

### Porter's Critique of the Product Life Cycle

The conventional product life cycle model (introduction → growth → maturity → decline) is a useful starting point but has serious limitations as a strategic tool:

1. **Duration varies wildly:** Some industries take decades to reach maturity; others skip stages or revitalise after apparent decline (bicycles, radio).
2. **Competitive patterns differ by industry:** Some industries start concentrated and stay concentrated; others fragment; others consolidate. The standard life cycle predictions do not hold uniformly.
3. **Companies can reshape the curve:** Through product innovation and repositioning, firms can extend growth phases or restart maturity industries.
4. **The concept describes but does not explain:** It tells you what *might* happen, but not *why* or *when* — and not when it won't happen.

**The life cycle is useful as a rough orientation, not as a predictive framework.** Use it to establish a baseline expectation, then apply the Driving Forces framework to predict *how and why* the industry will diverge from that baseline.

### Life Cycle Stage Reference Table (Porter, 1980: Figure 8-2)

| Dimension | Introduction | Growth | Maturity | Decline |
|-----------|-------------|--------|----------|---------|
| **Buyers** | High-income, inertia-heavy; must be convinced | Widening buyer group; accepts uneven quality | Mass market; repeat buying; brand choice | Sophisticated; switching; reduced loyalty |
| **Products** | Poor quality; many variants; no standards; frequent design changes | Technical differentiation; reliability key; quality improving | Superior quality; standardisation; less differentiation; annual model changes | Little differentiation; spotty quality |
| **Marketing** | Very high advertising/sales ratio; high prices (creaming strategy) | High advertising but lower % of sales; distribution key | Market segmentation; extend life cycle attempts; broader line; more service and deals | Low advertising; minimal marketing expenditure |
| **Manufacturing** | Overcapacity; short runs; high skilled labour; specialised channels | Undercapacity; shift to mass production; mass channels | Optimum capacity; stable processes; lower labour skills; long runs | Substantial overcapacity; mass production; specialty channels |
| **Competition** | Few competitors | Many entrants; mergers and casualties | Price competition; shakeout; fewer rivals; private labels rise | Exits; few survivors |
| **Profitability** | High prices, low profits | Highest profits | Falling prices; lower profits | Low prices and margins; possible late-decline recovery |
| **Strategy focus** | R&D and engineering; increase market share | Marketing is key; cost management | Cost control; competitive cost position critical; market share stability | Cost reduction is key; manage for cash |

## 5. Driving Forces of Industry Evolution

Rather than mechanically applying life cycle stages, Porter recommends identifying the **driving forces** that will fundamentally change industry structure. The test: does this force alter the strength of one or more of the Five Forces?

### Key Driving Forces

| Driving Force | How It Changes Industry Structure |
|--------------|----------------------------------|
| **Long-run changes in growth rate** | Growth exhaustion shifts the industry from share expansion to share competition — intensifying rivalry |
| **Changes in buyer segments** | New buyer groups enter; existing groups' characteristics change (income, sophistication, needs) |
| **Product innovation** | New features redefine buyer expectations; substitutes emerge; entry barriers shift |
| **Process innovation** | Changes cost structure; may favour new entrants with purpose-built facilities over incumbents with legacy operations |
| **Marketing innovation** | New distribution channels bypass existing networks; new media changes competitive dynamics |
| **Entry or exit of major firms** | Reshuffles competitive structure; can import capabilities from other industries |
| **Changes in input costs** | Shifts relative cost positions among competitors; may favour local producers vs. importers |
| **Regulatory change** | Creates or removes entry barriers; changes cost structure; opens new market segments |
| **Reduction in uncertainty** | As information about the industry increases, risk-averse firms may enter; pioneers' advantages erode |
| **Adoption of complementary products** | Products that complement the industry's product becoming widespread changes demand patterns |

### Applying Driving Forces Analysis

For any industry analysis in a business plan, ask:
1. Which two or three of these driving forces are most active in this industry right now?
2. What structural change will each force produce? (Which Five Force will it strengthen or weaken?)
3. What is the expected time horizon for each force to take effect?
4. Which firms in the industry are best positioned to benefit from each force — and which are threatened?

## 6. Industry Segmentation

A complete industry analysis must also segment the industry — different segments can have very different structural characteristics even within the same broad industry definition.

**Segmentation dimensions to consider:**
- **Product variety:** Different product configurations may face different buyer types, substitutes, and competitive dynamics
- **Buyer type:** Industrial vs. consumer; large vs. small; price-sensitive vs. quality-seeking
- **Geographic market:** Local, regional, national, or international — each may have different competitive structures
- **Distribution channel:** Serving through wholesalers vs. direct vs. retail vs. e-commerce changes buyer and supplier power

**Strategic implication:** A firm may occupy an unattractive position in one industry segment while an adjacent segment within the same industry is structurally much more attractive. Focused strategies often exploit precisely this — finding the segment with the most favourable structure.

## Uganda/East Africa Application

### Strategic Group Mapping in Low-Data EA Markets

In Uganda and Kenya, formal strategic group data is scarce. Use these proxy indicators:

| Strategic Dimension | How to Observe It in Uganda/EA |
|--------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Price position** | Physical price surveys; market stall audits; buyer interviews |
| **Product quality / certification** | UNBS marks, ISO certification, export licences (EAC CoO) |
| **Distribution** | Physical coverage (urban vs. rural); supermarket presence vs. market stalls; e-commerce presence |
| **Vertical integration** | Do competitors own their own transport? Own a farm/factory? Brand their own output? |
| **Brand** | Advertising spend observable through radio/billboard presence; consumer awareness surveys |
| **Service** | Warranty, after-sale support, credit terms offered to customers |

**Typical Uganda strategic groups (example: dairy industry):**
- **Group A — Branded processors:** Brookside, Pearl Dairy — pasteurised, packaged, supermarket-distributed, high price
- **Group B — Cooperative processors:** Bukoola, KDFA — semi-processed, wholesale and institution buyers, medium price
- **Group C — Informal traders:** Raw milk, farm-gate sales, urban hawkers, lowest price, no brand

Each group faces fundamentally different competitive dynamics. A new entrant must choose which group to join — or whether to create a new group.

### Industry Evolution in East Africa

Uganda and EA markets often exhibit compressed evolution — they may move through introduction and growth phases faster than western markets (mobile money, M-Pesa) or stall in transition to maturity due to infrastructure constraints.

**Key driving forces active in EA markets 2025–26:**

| Driving Force | EA Manifestation |
|--------------|-----------------|
| **Urbanisation** | Uganda's urban population growing ~5.5%/year — consumer product markets urbanising rapidly |
| **Mobile penetration** | Mobile money (MTN MoMo, Airtel Money) reshaping payment, credit, and distribution dynamics |
| **AfCFTA** | Gradual tariff reductions opening East African markets to wider competition — accelerating shakeout |
| **Input cost changes** | Uganda shilling depreciation, fuel price volatility, and global commodity prices shifting cost positions |
| **Regulatory change** | EFRIS/digital invoicing, NIN-BRN gateway, EAC standards harmonisation — restructuring compliance costs |
| **Youth demographics** | 50%+ under-18 population creating expanding consumer base with different preferences from previous generation |

### Strategic Group Analysis — Questions for the Business Plan

In the competitive analysis section (§06), use strategic group analysis to:
1. Map the 3–5 most significant competitors into groups based on price, quality, and coverage
2. Identify which group this business occupies or intends to join
3. Assess the mobility barriers protecting each group
4. Identify any empty space on the map — an unoccupied strategic position — and explain whether it represents an opportunity or a structural impossibility
