## 1. Africa Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA)

AfCFTA is the world's largest free trade area by number of participating countries:

- **54 member countries**, 1.3 billion people, combined GDP of **$3.4 trillion**
- Uganda ratified AfCFTA in **2021**; preferential tariff schedules are under phased implementation
- UNCTAD projects intra-African trade could rise from **15% to 52%** of total African trade once fully implemented
- AfCFTA Annex on Trade in Services will progressively open professional services, financial services, tourism, and digital services across all 54 member states

**Pan-African Payment and Settlement System (PAPSS):**
PAPSS enables cross-border payments in local currencies, removing the need for USD conversion in intra-Africa transactions. Uganda participated in the pilot phase. For Ugandan SMEs trading with other African markets, PAPSS reduces:
- Currency conversion costs (typically 2–5% of transaction value)
- Settlement delays (from days to hours)
- USD liquidity dependency

**Business implication:** As AfCFTA matures, the total addressable market for Ugandan businesses extends to 54 countries without prohibitive tariff barriers. PAPSS makes cross-border receipts and payments operationally simpler for SMEs.

## 2. East African Community (EAC)

### 2.1 Membership and Scope

The EAC has **8 member states**: Uganda, Kenya, Tanzania, Rwanda, Burundi, South Sudan, DRC, and Somalia.

The EAC Common Market provides for free movement of goods, services, capital, and persons — the most advanced regional integration framework in Africa. Goods cleared at the **first port of entry** (e.g., Mombasa for landlocked Uganda) are not re-inspected at subsequent EAC borders under the Single Customs Territory.

### 2.2 Common External Tariff (CET)

The EAC CET uses a **3-band structure**:

| Category | Tariff Rate | Examples |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Raw materials | 0% | Industrial inputs, unprocessed commodities |
| Semi-processed goods | 10% | Intermediate manufactured goods |
| Finished goods | 25% | Consumer goods competing with local production |

**Zero-tariff within EAC:** Goods with **≥35% East African value-added content** qualify for intra-EAC zero tariff under the Rules of Origin. Businesses exporting manufactured or processed goods to EAC markets must document and maintain this threshold.

### 2.3 EAC Tax Harmonisation

| Country | VAT Rate | Corporate Income Tax |
|---------|----------|----------------------|
| Uganda | 18% | 30% |
| Kenya | 16% | 30% |
| Tanzania | 18% | 30% |
| Rwanda | 18% | 30% |
| Burundi | 18% | 30% |

The near-uniform tax structure simplifies cross-border pricing calculations for business plans. The main differentiator is Kenya's lower VAT (16%), which can affect cost competitiveness for goods sold into the Kenyan market.

## 3. Uganda's Trade Agreements

Uganda participates in six major preferential trade frameworks:

| Agreement | Markets Covered | Key Benefit |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------|
| **EAC** | 7 partner states | Zero tariff on qualifying goods (≥35% local value-added) |
| **COMESA** | 20 member states | Preferential or zero tariffs across Eastern and Southern Africa |
| **AfCFTA** | 54 African countries | Phased tariff reduction; services access from 2026 |
| **EBA (Everything But Arms)** | EU (27 countries) | Duty-free, quota-free access for LDC exporters; Uganda qualifies |
| **AGOA (Africa Growth and Opportunity Act)** | USA | Duty-free access for >6,000 products; Uganda qualifies |
| **UK DCTS (Developing Countries Trading Scheme)** | United Kingdom | Post-Brexit Enhanced Preferences; Uganda qualifies |

Uganda also maintains **bilateral trade agreements** with China and India, its two largest import sources.

## 5. Uganda Export Growth Targets (2040 Vision)

From World Business Journal 2025 (WBJAC International):

- Raise exports as a share of GDP from **15% (FY 2022/23) to 50% by 2040**
- Increase manufactured goods' share of exports from **13% to 50%**
- Triple coffee export earnings to **$5 billion by 2030**
- Grow the economy from **$50B to $500B by 2040** (10-fold growth)

These targets create long-run tailwinds for agri-processing, manufacturing, and export-oriented services businesses.

## 6. EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) — Market Access Condition

The **EU Deforestation Regulation (2023/1115)** took effect **31 December 2025** and is now a live market access requirement, not a future risk.

**Products affected:** coffee, cocoa, soy, palm oil, cattle, wood, rubber, and all derived products.

**Compliance requirement:** Operators must demonstrate that commodities were not produced on land deforested after 31 December 2020. Required documentation includes:
- GPS coordinates of the farm or origin plot
- Satellite imagery confirming forest cover status
- Full supply chain traceability documentation

**Uganda exposure:**
- The EU accounts for **67% of Uganda's coffee export market** (~$660M/year)
- Non-compliant exporters and producers lose EU market access entirely
- EUDR-compliant producers can command **premium pricing** from buyers seeking verified supply chains

**Business plan implication:** Any business plan involving coffee, cocoa, timber, or palm oil production or processing must include EUDR compliance as a baseline operational requirement — not an optional investment. Traceability systems (GPS tagging, farm registration) should appear in the Operations Plan (§08) and the cost base.

## 7. Practical Implications for Business Plan Authors

### Addressing the EAC Market (§04)

When sizing the total addressable market, business plans can legitimately claim the EAC as the relevant geographic market — provided the business has a credible distribution or export strategy. Use the EAC's 300 million+ population as the outer bound; size the serviceable market based on logistics reach and regulatory clearance.

### Rules of Origin (§03 and §08)

Manufacturers planning to export to EAC markets at zero tariff must document that ≥35% of product value originates within the EAC. This affects input sourcing decisions: locally sourced raw materials count towards the threshold; fully imported inputs do not.

### PAPSS and Cross-Border Payments (§10)

For businesses with cross-border revenue in KES, TZS, or RWF, PAPSS settlement reduces forex conversion costs. Financial projections can model this as a reduction in transaction costs compared to USD-intermediated payments.

### AGOA and EBA (§07 and §04)

Exporters of garments, horticulture, fish, and processed coffee can access US and EU markets duty-free. The marketing strategy section should reference the specific tariff preference by name when positioning export market opportunity, as this is a bankability-relevant competitive advantage.

### AfCFTA Services (§03 and §07)

Professional services firms (legal, accounting, IT, engineering, health) should note that AfCFTA's Annex on Trade in Services, once operational, removes many barriers to cross-border service delivery. This is particularly relevant for digital services that can be delivered remotely across borders.
