## 1. The Marketing Research Process

The American Marketing Association defines marketing research as "the function that links an organisation to its market through the gathering of information." It facilitates identification and definition of market-driven opportunities and problems, development and evaluation of marketing actions, and monitoring of marketing performance.

### The Four-Phase Research Process

**Phase 1: Determine the Research Problem**
Three interrelated activities:
1. Identify and clarify information needs
2. Define the research questions
3. Specify research objectives and confirm information requirements

The "Iceberg Principle" warns that obvious, measurable symptoms (e.g., declining sales) may hide deeper root problems (e.g., low-quality products, inappropriate delivery system, unethical treatment of customers). Researchers must separate symptoms from root causes.

**Phase 2: Select the Research Design**
- Determine if existing data answers the question (secondary data first)
- Choose between exploratory, descriptive, predictive, or causal designs
- Select data collection methods (surveys, observation, experiments)
- Develop sampling plan and determine sample size

**Phase 3: Execute the Research Design**
- Collect and prepare data
- Assign numerical codes to response categories
- Analyse using appropriate statistical techniques
- Determine if data is valid, reliable, and generalisable

**Phase 4: Communicate the Results**
- Prepare research report with findings
- Provide interpretations and recommendations
- Present to decision makers

### When Research is NOT Necessary

Research may not be needed when:
- The information already exists and is readily available
- The decision has already been made and research won't change it
- The cost of research exceeds the potential benefit
- The decision must be made immediately (no time for research)
- Research would tip off competitors to strategy

### Key Question Before Starting

> "Can the decision-making problem be solved based on past experience and managerial judgment?" If no, research should be considered.

### Application to Business Plans

When writing a market analysis section, follow this process:
1. Define what you need to prove (market exists, is growing, is large enough)
2. Search secondary data first (cheaper, faster)
3. Design primary research only if secondary data is insufficient
4. Analyse findings using appropriate techniques
5. Present findings with credibility and supporting data

## 2. Secondary Data Sources and Evaluation Criteria

Secondary data is existing data that was collected for a purpose other than the current research problem. It should always be the starting point because it is faster and cheaper than primary data collection.

### Five Criteria for Evaluating Secondary Data Quality

| Criterion | Questions to Ask |
|---|---|
| **1. Purpose** | Why was this data originally collected? Is the purpose compatible with our needs? |
| **2. Accuracy** | Was the methodology sound? What was the margin of error? Was it peer-reviewed? |
| **3. Consistency** | Do multiple sources corroborate the data? Are there contradictions to explain? |
| **4. Credibility** | Who collected it? What is their reputation, technical competence, and expertise? |
| **5. Methodology** | How was the data gathered? What was the sample size and design? Were there flaws? |

### Internal Secondary Data Sources

| Source | Key Variables |
|---|---|
| **Sales invoices** | Customer name, address, product class, price per unit, salesperson, terms, shipment point |
| **Accounts receivable** | Customer name, products, total unit/dollar sales, customer as % of sales, profit margin, credit rating |
| **Accounting/cost data** | Cost of goods, operating expenses, profit margins, overhead allocation |
| **CRM systems** | Customer interactions, purchase behaviour, touchpoints, lifecycle stage |

### External Secondary Data Sources

**Government Sources:**
- Census data (population, housing, retail trade, service industries, manufacturing, wholesale trade, agriculture, construction, transportation)
- Voter and election data
- Economic indicators (GDP, employment, inflation)
- Bureau of Labor Statistics
- Federal Reserve data

**Syndicated Data Sources:**
- Consumer panels (purchase tracking over time)
- Media panels (media consumption habits measurement, e.g., Nielsen)
- Store audits (retail-level product/brand movement)
- Scanner-based data (point-of-purchase optical scanning)

**Scholarly Sources:**
- Google Scholar for peer-reviewed research
- Academic journals (Journal of Marketing, Journal of Consumer Research)
- Literature reviews identify scales, methodologies, and established relationships

**Popular/Trade Sources:**
- Industry publications (Ad Age, Wired, trade journals)
- Marketing blogs and industry analyst reports
- News sources (often secondhand reporting -- verify methodology)

**Online/Digital Sources:**
- Web analytics (visits, duration, page views, cart abandonment)
- Search analytics (keyword data from search engine marketing)
- Social media data (engagement, sentiment, demographics)
- Behavioural targeting data (browsing patterns across sites)

### Conducting a Literature Review

Purpose: Identify existing knowledge, proven relationships, measurement scales, and methodologies before designing primary research.

Steps:
1. Brainstorm relevant keywords (include synonyms and related terms)
2. Search Google Scholar for peer-reviewed articles
3. Search popular/trade sources for current industry information
4. Evaluate each source using the five criteria above
5. Identify gaps that require primary research
6. Use findings to develop hypotheses about market relationships

### Application to Business Plans

For market analysis sections, secondary data provides:
- Market size estimates from industry reports
- Growth rates from government and syndicated data
- Competitive landscape from trade publications
- Customer demographics from census and panel data
- Industry trends from analyst reports and scholarly research

**Always cite sources.** Flag estimates clearly as "estimated" when hard data is unavailable.

## 3. Research Design Types

### Three Core Research Designs

#### Exploratory Research
**Purpose:** Gain initial insights, clarify ambiguous problems, discover new ideas.

**When to use:**
- At the start of a research project when the problem is not well defined
- When little is known about the market or customer behaviour
- When developing hypotheses for later testing
- When a complex web of consumer culture or psychological motivations requires in-depth understanding

**Methods:**
- Focus groups (qualitative, 6-12 participants discussing a topic)
- In-depth interviews (one-on-one, probing conversation)
- Ethnography/netnography (extended observation of consumers in context)
- Online communities (private or public, for ongoing consumer insights)
- Case studies

**Limitations:** Results are not statistically generalisable; used for direction, not conclusions.

**Business plan application:** Use exploratory research to validate initial market assumptions, identify customer pain points, and discover unmet needs before committing to a full market analysis.

#### Descriptive Research
**Purpose:** Describe characteristics of a market, population, or phenomenon; answer "who, what, when, where, how" questions.

**When to use:**
- When the problem is well defined and you need to quantify it
- To describe customer profiles, market segments, or competitive positioning
- To track changes over time (longitudinal studies)
- To identify relationships between variables

**Methods:**
- Cross-sectional surveys (snapshot at one point in time)
- Longitudinal surveys (same population measured over time)
- Observation studies
- Content analysis

**Key advantages:** Large sample sizes, generalisable results, ability to distinguish small differences between groups, statistical hypothesis testing.

**Disadvantages:** Difficulty developing accurate survey instruments, inaccuracy in construct definition and scale measurement, limited depth.

**Business plan application:** Primary method for market sizing, customer segmentation, competitive analysis, and industry benchmarking. Survey-based descriptive research provides the quantitative evidence investors expect.

#### Predictive and Causal Research
**Purpose:** Predict future outcomes or establish cause-and-effect relationships.

**Predictive research** uses historical data and statistical models to forecast:
- Sales forecasts at various price levels
- Demand sensitivity to price changes
- Impact of advertising spend on sales volume
- Market growth trajectories

**Causal research** uses experiments to determine if one variable causes a change in another:
- A/B testing (comparing two variations)
- Test marketing (controlled field experiments in real market conditions)
- Laboratory experiments (controlled artificial settings)

**Key variables in experimental design:**
- **Independent variable** — The variable manipulated by the researcher
- **Dependent variable** — The outcome variable being measured
- **Extraneous variables** — Other factors that may influence the outcome
- **Control variables** — Variables held constant to isolate the effect

**Validity considerations:**
- **Internal validity** — Degree to which the experiment accurately identifies causal relationships (control)
- **External validity** — Degree to which results are generalisable to real-world settings (realism)
- Trade-off: Laboratory experiments have high internal validity but low external validity; field experiments have the opposite

**Business plan application:** Use predictive models for financial projections and sales forecasts. Reference A/B testing or test marketing results to demonstrate evidence-based decision making.

### Survey Method Types

| Type | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Person-administered** | Face-to-face interviewing (in-home, mall-intercept) | Complex questions, stimulus-heavy research |
| **Telephone-administered** | Phone interviews, CATI systems | Quick data collection, moderate complexity |
| **Self-administered** | Mail surveys, drop-off surveys | Low cost, respondent control, sensitive topics |
| **Online surveys** | Web-based questionnaires (Qualtrics, SurveyMonkey) | Speed, cost-effectiveness, large samples (60%+ of all data collection) |
| **Mobile surveys** | Smartphone-based surveys, geofencing | In-the-moment feedback, younger demographics |

### Three Factors Affecting Survey Method Choice

1. **Situational characteristics** — Budget, time frame, data quality requirements
2. **Task characteristics** — Complexity of questions, stimuli needed, information volume, topic sensitivity
3. **Respondent characteristics** — Diversity, incidence rate, willingness and ability to participate

## 4. Sampling Methods and Sample Size Determination

### Key Sampling Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Population** | The identifiable group of elements of interest to the researcher |
| **Defined target population** | The complete group of elements identified for investigation, defined by elements, sampling units, and time frames |
| **Sampling unit** | Target population elements actually available for selection |
| **Sampling frame** | The list of all eligible sampling units |
| **Census** | Data collected from every member of the target population |
| **Sampling** | Selecting a small number of elements from a larger group, expecting the sample is representative |

### Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

The CLT is the theoretical backbone of survey research:
- The sampling distribution of the mean from a simple random sample will be approximately normally distributed when n >= 30
- A single random sample's mean will closely approximate the true population mean as sample size increases
- Enables: drawing representative samples, obtaining accurate estimates, reducing data collection costs, assessing reliability and validity, and statistical analysis

### Probability Sampling Designs

| Design | How It Works | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Simple Random Sampling (SRS)** | Every element has equal chance of selection; use random number generator | Small, homogeneous populations with available sampling frames |
| **Systematic Random Sampling** | Select every kth element from a list (k = population size / sample size) | Large populations with ordered lists |
| **Stratified Random Sampling** | Divide population into strata (subgroups), then random sample within each | Ensuring representation of all important subgroups |
| **Cluster Sampling** | Divide into clusters (often geographic), then sample all or randomly within selected clusters | Cost-effective for geographically dispersed populations |
| **Area Sampling** | Clusters formed by geographic designations (cities, blocks, MSAs) | When no list of individuals exists but geographic boundaries are available |

**Stratified sampling variants:**
- **Proportionate** — Sample from each stratum proportional to its size in the population
- **Disproportionate** — Sample sizes based on economic importance or research priority, not proportional to stratum size
- **Optimal allocation** — Considers both relative size and variability within each stratum

### Nonprobability Sampling Designs

| Design | How It Works | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Convenience sampling** | Select whoever is readily available | Early-stage research, pretesting questionnaires |
| **Judgment (purposive) sampling** | Researcher selects participants believed to meet study requirements | Expert opinions, key account insights |
| **Quota sampling** | Pre-specified quotas for demographic or behavioural categories | Ensuring representation of subgroups without a sampling frame |
| **Snowball (referral) sampling** | Initial respondents refer others with similar characteristics | Small, hard-to-reach, uniquely defined populations |

**Limitation of all nonprobability methods:** Results cannot be statistically generalised to the population; sampling error cannot be calculated.

### Sample Size Determination

#### Formula for Estimating a Population Mean

```
n = (Z_CL)^2 x (sigma^2 / e^2)

Where:
  Z_CL  = Standardised z-value for confidence level
          (1.645 for 90%, 1.96 for 95%, 2.576 for 99%)
  sigma = Estimated population standard deviation
  e     = Acceptable error tolerance (precision)
```

#### Formula for Estimating a Population Proportion

```
n = (Z_CL)^2 x (P x Q / e^2)

Where:
  Z_CL = Standardised z-value for confidence level
  P    = Estimated proportion with the characteristic
  Q    = 1 - P (proportion without the characteristic)
  e    = Acceptable error tolerance
```

**When P is unknown, use P = 0.50 (maximum variability, most conservative estimate).**

#### Small Population Correction Factor

When the calculated sample size exceeds 5% of the population:

```
Adjusted n = n x [N / (N + n - 1)]

Where:
  N = Population size
  n = Originally calculated sample size
```

#### Practical Guidelines

- For populations of 500 or fewer, consider a census rather than a sample
- Minimum of 384 sampling units needed for +/- 5% confidence at +/- 5% sampling error
- Minimum subgroup sample size: 30-100 per subgroup (30 minimum, 100 preferred)
- Rule of thumb: 5 respondents per questionnaire question (25 questions = 125 minimum)
- B2B studies with populations of 200-300: aim for 30%+ response rate, examine respondent profile for representativeness

### Seven Steps in Developing a Sampling Plan

1. **Define the target population** — Who should be investigated (elements, sampling units, time frames)
2. **Select the data collection method** — Personal, telephone, self-administered, online, observation
3. **Identify the sampling frame(s) needed** — Lists of eligible sampling units
4. **Select the appropriate sampling method** — Probability or nonprobability, based on seven factors
5. **Determine necessary sample sizes** — Using formulas or practical guidelines
6. **Create an operating plan** — Procedures for selecting sampling units
7. **Execute the operational plan** — Carry out data collection

### Seven Factors for Selecting Sampling Design

| Factor | Key Question |
|---|---|
| Research objectives | Qualitative or quantitative design needed? |
| Degree of accuracy | Making predictions/inferences or only preliminary insights? |
| Resources | Budget and human resource constraints? |
| Time frame | How quickly must the project be completed? |
| Knowledge of target population | Are complete lists available? How easy is it to generate a sampling frame? |
| Scope of the research | International, national, regional, or local? |
| Statistical analysis needs | How accurate must statistical projections and hypothesis testing be? |

### Application to Business Plans

For market analysis, sampling methodology matters when:
- Conducting primary research surveys of potential customers
- Validating market size estimates through customer interviews
- Testing pricing sensitivity or product-market fit
- Supporting claims about customer preferences or behaviour

State the sampling method used and sample size to establish credibility. If using nonprobability samples (common for startups), acknowledge the limitation honestly.

## 5. Key Statistical Analysis Techniques for Market Analysis

### Descriptive Statistics

| Technique | Purpose | Business Plan Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Frequencies** | Count/percentage of responses in each category | Market segmentation, customer profile distribution |
| **Mean** | Average value of a variable | Average spend, satisfaction scores, pricing benchmarks |
| **Standard deviation** | Spread/variability around the mean | Consistency of preferences, risk assessment |
| **Cross-tabulations** | Relationship between two categorical variables | Segment differences, demographic breakdowns |

### Hypothesis Testing

**Null hypothesis (H0):** No relationship exists between variables (the hypothesis always tested).
**Alternative hypothesis (H1):** A relationship does exist.

| Test | When to Use | Business Plan Application |
|---|---|---|
| **t-test** | Compare means of two groups | Male vs. female preferences, before/after comparisons |
| **ANOVA** | Compare means of three or more groups | Segment comparisons, regional differences |
| **Chi-square** | Test association between categorical variables | Demographic segments vs. purchase behaviour |

### Correlation Analysis

**Pearson correlation coefficient:** Measures linear association between two interval/ratio variables.
- Range: -1.0 to +1.0
- Sign indicates direction (positive or negative relationship)
- Absolute value indicates strength

**Spearman rank order correlation:** Used when one or more variables are ordinal.

**Critical distinction:** Correlation does not imply causation. Two variables may correlate due to a third variable or statistical coincidence.

### Regression Analysis

**Bivariate regression:** One independent variable predicting one dependent variable.

```
Y = a + bX

Where:
  Y = Dependent variable (outcome)
  a = Y-intercept (value of Y when X = 0)
  b = Regression coefficient (change in Y for each unit change in X)
  X = Independent variable (predictor)
```

**Multiple regression:** Multiple independent variables predicting one dependent variable.

```
Y = a + b1X1 + b2X2 + ... + bnXn
```

**Key regression metrics:**
- **R-squared (r2)** — Proportion of variance in dependent variable explained by independent variables (0 to 1.0; higher = stronger model)
- **F statistic** — Tests overall significance of the regression model
- **t-tests for coefficients** — Test whether each individual predictor is significant
- **Beta coefficients** — Standardised regression coefficients; allow comparison of relative importance of predictors
- **Multicollinearity** — When independent variables are highly correlated with each other, distorting individual coefficient estimates

**Business plan application:**
- Predict sales based on marketing spend, pricing, and market size
- Identify which factors most strongly drive customer satisfaction or purchase intent
- Support financial projections with statistical evidence

### Perceptual Mapping

A technique used to picture the relative position of products or brands on two or more dimensions important to consumer purchase decisions.

**Process:**
1. Identify relevant product/brand attributes important to consumers
2. Survey consumers on how they perceive brands on those attributes
3. Plot brands on a two-dimensional map (e.g., price vs. quality)
4. Identify gaps in the market (unoccupied positions)
5. Determine positioning strategy

**Business plan application:** Use perceptual maps to visually demonstrate competitive positioning and identify market gaps your business will fill. Very effective for investor presentations.

### Structural Equation Modelling (PLS)

Partial Least Squares (PLS) enables analysis of complex models with:
- Three or more stages of relationships
- Constructs measured by multiple questions
- Non-normal data distributions
- Smaller sample sizes than traditional SEM

**Business plan application:** Advanced technique for demonstrating that your understanding of customer behaviour is supported by statistical evidence linking multiple factors.

## 6. Communicating Research Findings in a Business Plan

### Four Objectives of a Research Report

1. **Effectively communicate findings** of the research project
2. **Provide interpretations** in the form of sound, logical recommendations
3. **Establish credibility** of the research methods and conclusions
4. **Serve as a reference document** for future strategic and tactical decisions

### Standard Report Format

| Section | Content | Business Plan Equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Title page** | Subject, recipient, author, date | Cover page |
| **2. Table of contents** | Topics in sequential order with page numbers | Table of contents |
| **3. Executive summary** | Research objectives, concise method, key findings, conclusions and recommendations | Executive summary |
| **4. Introduction** | Background, definitions, scope, objectives, hypotheses | Market analysis introduction |
| **5. Research methods** | Design type, data sources, collection procedure, sampling process | Methodology note (often in appendix) |
| **6. Data analysis and findings** | Results with tables, figures, graphs; logical arrangement by research objective | Market size, trends, competitive data |
| **7. Conclusions and recommendations** | Descriptive statements generalising results; action-oriented recommendations | Market opportunity summary |
| **8. Limitations** | Sampling bias, constraints, measurement error | Assumptions and limitations |
| **9. Appendixes** | Questionnaires, statistical calculations, detailed data | Supporting data |

### Three Dimensions of Credibility

1. **Accuracy** — All data, statistics, calculations, and interpretations must be correct; no errors in grammar or terminology
2. **Believability** — Clear and logical thinking, precise expression, accurate presentation; fuzzy logic undermines reader confidence
3. **Professional organisation** — Quality layout that conveys professionalism of the research effort

### Three Levels of Reader

Write reports to serve all three:
1. **Executive readers** — Read only the executive summary
2. **Engaged readers** — Read the summary and browse the body of findings
3. **Technical readers** — Read the entire report and appendix for detailed methodology

### Executive Summary Best Practices

The executive summary is "the soul of the report." It must:
- Stand alone as a complete document
- Be written last (after all analyses are done)
- Contain: research objectives, concise method statement, summary of key findings, conclusions and recommendations
- Not exceed approximately one page
- Present findings that agree with the detailed findings section

### Data Visualisation Guidelines

**General principles (from Edward Tufte):**
- "Good design is clear thinking made visible"
- The goal is to explain, not to impress
- Avoid "chart junk" (unnecessary decorative elements)
- The right metaphor for presentation is teaching, not theatre

**Specific presentation techniques:**

| Data Type | Recommended Visualisation | Tips |
|---|---|---|
| **Frequencies** | Bar charts, pie charts, tables | Remove unnecessary decimal places; round percentages to whole numbers |
| **Means comparison** | Bar charts, tables with means and standard deviations | Order from highest to lowest; show maximum scale value; one decimal place for means |
| **Group comparisons** | Side-by-side bar charts, comparison tables | Mark significant differences with asterisks; state significance level |
| **Correlations** | Correlation tables, ordered by strength | Arrange from largest to smallest; mark significant correlations |
| **Regression results** | Conceptual model diagrams with arrows | Show beta coefficients on arrows; include R-squared; show significance in parentheses |
| **Dashboard** | Combined visualisations on one page/slide | Present related but different pieces of information together |

**Tools mentioned:** Microsoft Power BI, Tableau, SPSS, PSPP (free), Excel, PowerPoint

### Five Common Report Problems to Avoid

1. **Lack of data interpretation** — Don't just show tables; always interpret what the numbers mean
2. **Unnecessary complex statistics** — Use the simplest technique that answers the question; Chi-square is often sufficient
3. **Emphasis on packaging over quality** — Professional graphics matter, but substance comes first
4. **Lack of relevance** — Every finding must connect to a research objective; don't pad the report
5. **Over-reliance on few statistics** — Base conclusions on the weight of evidence, not a single significant result

### Critical Thinking in Reporting

Avoid these biases when writing market analysis:

- **Confirmation bias** — Interpreting evidence to fit preexisting beliefs about the market
- **Over-generalising from samples** — Small or non-random samples require cautious claims
- **False causal claims** — Correlation between market factors does not prove causation
- **Wrong construct labelling** — Ensure what you measured is actually what you claim to have measured
- **Methodological biases** — Survey length, question wording, order effects, and method choice all influence results

### Oral Presentation Best Practices

1. Keep visual presentations simple; avoid distracting transitions and animations
2. Don't read slides verbatim; use them as support
3. Focus on explaining findings, not impressing with technique
4. Anticipate questions about surprising findings
5. Have clear explanations for all methodology choices
6. Present technical information in non-technical language

### Application to Business Plans

A business plan market analysis is essentially a research report for investors. Apply these principles:

- **Lead with the executive summary** — State the market opportunity clearly and concisely
- **Use both top-down and bottom-up data** — Show methodology, not just numbers
- **Visualise key data** — Use charts and graphs for market size, growth, segmentation
- **Cite every claim** — Build credibility through sourcing
- **Acknowledge limitations** — Honest about data gaps; flag estimates
- **Keep it relevant** — Every data point should support the investment thesis
- **Write for multiple audiences** — Summary for time-pressed readers; appendix for the diligent

## 7. Practical Frameworks and Checklists

### Market Research Decision Checklist

Before conducting market research for a business plan, answer:

```
[ ] Is the information available from existing secondary sources?
[ ] Is the cost of research justified by the decision at stake?
[ ] Is there enough time to conduct meaningful research?
[ ] Will the research provide actionable insights?
[ ] Could the research reveal our strategy to competitors?
[ ] Do we have the expertise to design and execute the research properly?
```

### Secondary Data Evaluation Checklist

For each secondary data source used in the market analysis:

```
[ ] Purpose: Was the data collected for a compatible purpose?
[ ] Accuracy: Is the methodology sound? What is the margin of error?
[ ] Consistency: Do multiple sources corroborate this data?
[ ] Credibility: Is the source reputable and technically competent?
[ ] Methodology: Is the sample size adequate? Was the design appropriate?
[ ] Currency: Is the data recent enough to be relevant?
```

### Market Analysis Research Design Selection Guide

| What You Need to Know | Recommended Design | Methods |
|---|---|---|
| "Is there a market for this?" | Exploratory | Interviews, focus groups, online communities |
| "How big is the market?" | Descriptive | Secondary data analysis, surveys |
| "Who are the customers?" | Descriptive | Surveys, segmentation studies, panel data |
| "What do customers prefer?" | Descriptive | Surveys, conjoint analysis |
| "Will customers pay this price?" | Causal/Predictive | Pricing experiments, surveys, regression |
| "What drives purchase decisions?" | Descriptive/Causal | Surveys, regression, structural modelling |
| "How will the market grow?" | Predictive | Time series analysis, regression, expert forecasts |

### Hypothesis Development Framework

For market analysis, structure hypotheses as testable statements:

```
Independent variable --> Dependent variable

Examples for business plan market analysis:
- "Higher income consumers are more likely to adopt [product category]"
- "Digital marketing spend is positively related to customer acquisition"
- "Price sensitivity differs between age segments"
- "Customer satisfaction predicts repeat purchase intention"
```

**Null hypothesis (H0):** No relationship exists (always the hypothesis tested).
**Alternative hypothesis (H1):** A relationship does exist.

A conceptual model visually represents the hypothesised relationships:
- Constructs flow left to right (independent to dependent)
- Arrows indicate predicted relationships
- Signs (+/-) indicate predicted direction

### Sampling Quick-Reference for Business Plans

| Scenario | Recommended Approach | Minimum Sample Size |
|---|---|---|
| Startup validating market | Convenience + quota sampling | 30-100 interviews |
| Market sizing survey | Stratified random sampling | 384 (for +/- 5% at 95% confidence) |
| B2B market research | Census or judgment sampling | 30%+ of known population |
| Customer satisfaction study | Random sampling from CRM | 100+ per segment analysed |
| Price sensitivity testing | Experimental design | 30+ per experimental condition |

### Market Analysis Credibility Framework

To make your market analysis credible to investors:

| Element | How to Establish Credibility |
|---|---|
| **Market size claims** | Cite sources; show both top-down and bottom-up; reconcile within 30% |
| **Growth projections** | Reference historical CAGR; cite analyst forecasts; state assumptions |
| **Customer insights** | Describe methodology (sample size, design); acknowledge limitations |
| **Competitive data** | Use multiple sources; distinguish fact from estimate |
| **Trend analysis** | Cite data-backed sources; rate confidence level for each trend |
| **Financial projections** | Link to market data; show sensitivity analysis; state assumptions |

### Perceptual Mapping Template for Competitive Positioning

```
Step 1: Identify 2-3 key dimensions customers use to evaluate options
        (e.g., Price vs. Quality, Convenience vs. Personalisation)

Step 2: Rate each competitor and your business on each dimension
        (survey data or expert judgment)

Step 3: Plot on two-dimensional map

Step 4: Identify gaps (unoccupied positions = opportunities)

Step 5: Define your positioning strategy relative to the map
```

### Report Section Mapping: Research Report to Business Plan

| Research Report Section | Business Plan Equivalent | What to Include |
|---|---|---|
| Executive summary | Executive summary | Market opportunity in 2-3 sentences |
| Introduction | Market analysis introduction | Industry context, why this market matters |
| Literature review | Industry overview | Published research, analyst reports, trends |
| Secondary data analysis | Market size and growth | TAM/SAM/SOM with sources |
| Primary research findings | Customer validation | Survey results, interview insights |
| Statistical analysis | Supporting evidence | Key metrics, charts, segmentation data |
| Conclusions | Market opportunity summary | Why the market supports this business |
| Recommendations | Business model justification | How findings inform strategy |
| Limitations | Assumptions and risks | Data gaps, estimate confidence levels |
| Appendix | Appendix | Detailed methodology, raw data, survey instruments |
