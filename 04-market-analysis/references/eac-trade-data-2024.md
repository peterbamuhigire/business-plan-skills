## 1. EAC Trade Performance Overview (2018–2024)

### 1.1 EAC Trade Volumes

EAC total exports to the world grew by **76% between 2018 and 2022**, from US$ 14 billion to US$ 25 billion (Export Trade Potential of the EAC, 2024).

| Year | EAC Exports to World | EAC Exports to Africa | Intra-EAC Exports |
|------|---------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| 2018 | $14.2B | $5.9B | $4.0B |
| 2019 | $18.7B | $6.8B | $4.1B |
| 2020 | $19.5B | $6.9B | $4.3B |
| 2021 | $21.7B | $8.3B | $5.6B |
| 2022 | $25.0B | $9.7B | $6.3B |
| % change 2021–22 | +15.4% | +16.0% | +12.9% |

**Key structural finding:** The EAC maintains a **trade surplus with Africa** (US$ 1.8 billion surplus in 2022) but a **trade deficit with the world** (US$ 29 billion deficit in 2022). This pattern strongly favours intra-African trade strategies.

### 1.2 EAC Trade Partners (2018–2022 average)

**Top export destinations — world:** UAE ($4.0B in 2022), India ($1.4B), China ($1.3B)

**Top export destinations — Africa:** DRC ($1.7B in 2022), South Africa ($1.0B), Uganda ($1.1B)

**Top import sources — world:** China ($11.0B in 2022), India ($5.9B), UAE ($8.1B)

**Top import sources — Africa:** Kenya ($2.0B), South Africa ($1.4B), Tanzania ($1.4B)

### 1.3 EAC Intra-Regional Trade Trends

Historical data (2001–2021) shows:
- Average intra-EAC exports as share of total EAC world exports: **13.9%**
- Share of intra-EAC exports in EAC exports to Africa: **50.9%** on average; rising to 52.7% in 2019–21

By **2024, total EAC trade rose to over US$ 11 billion, up 22% on 2023** (Eastern Africa Trade Performance, 2025). Key manufactured exports within the region include textiles, chemicals, edible oils, cement, iron and steel, cosmetics, and pharmaceuticals.

The broader intra-African picture: **in 2024, intra-African trade grew by 8.5%** compared with just 0.4% growth in exports outside the continent (Eastern Africa Trade Performance, 2025). Intra-African trade has consistently outpaced extra-African trade for two consecutive years.

## 2. Uganda Trade Performance (2024–2025)

### 2.1 Uganda Export Growth

Uganda achieved **22% export growth in 2024** — one of the strongest in the region alongside Ethiopia (30%) and Rwanda (30%) (Eastern Africa Trade Performance, 2025).

In **January–May 2025**, Uganda was among the region's largest exporters, with **50% growth** versus January–May 2024 (UNECA, 2025).

Uganda's **exports to the United States rose 121.2%** in April–July 2025 versus the same period in 2024 (from $41.3M to $91.3M), reflecting trade diversion effects as US tariffs disadvantaged Asian competitors (Eastern Africa Trade Performance, 2025).

### 2.2 Uganda Export Drivers

Uganda's non-gold exports were boosted by higher receipts from **coffee, tea, fish, and flowers** (Eastern Africa Trade Performance, 2025). Gold price appreciation (up 60%+ from January 2024 to July 2025) also supported Uganda's headline export earnings.

**Uganda's AGOA status**: Uganda lost AGOA eligibility effective **1 January 2024**. Uganda had been less reliant on AGOA (only about 11% of US-bound exports). It responded by diversifying toward regional blocs, the EU, and dairy exports, which **more than doubled in value** (Eastern Africa Trade Performance, 2025).

### 2.3 Uganda's Intra-EAC Trade Share

Uganda's exports to Africa (2019–2022):

| Year | To World | To Africa | To EAC | EAC share of Africa exports |
|------|----------|-----------|--------|----------------------------|
| 2019 | $3.6B | $1.3B | $1.2B | 89.8% |
| 2020 | $4.1B | $1.4B | $1.2B | 87.8% |
| 2021 | $4.0B | $1.8B | $1.5B | 86.2% |
| 2022 | $3.6B | $2.2B | $1.9B | 87.2% |

**Uganda exports approximately 87–90% of its African exports within the EAC region** — a higher concentration than Kenya (70%), Tanzania (48%), or Burundi (74%). This reflects both Uganda's landlocked position and strong EAC market integration.

### 2.4 Uganda's Top Export Products to Africa (2016–2022 average)

1. Coffee, tea, mate and spices — 16%
2. Cereals — 8%
3. Iron and steel — 6%
4. Dairy produce — 6%
5. Edible vegetables — 6%

Top-5 products accounted for approximately 61% of Uganda's exports to Africa.

### 2.5 Uganda Infrastructure Initiatives (2024–2025)

- Feasibility studies completed for **Kampala–Jinja Expressway**; construction planned for 2025 to ease cargo movement toward Mombasa port
- **Export Development Fund** launched 2024 to support agro-processing and reduce raw commodity dependence
- Uganda and Kenya signed agreement in **August 2025 to remove tariff and non-tariff trade barriers** (Eastern Africa Trade Performance, 2025)

### 3.5 Export Diversification Opportunities for Uganda

Uganda's best options for new export product diversification in Africa (based on product space analysis):
- **Semi-milled rice** — accessible primarily through EAC countries; non-EAC markets include South Africa, Sudan, Morocco, Zambia, Egypt
- **Crude palm oil** — EAC countries; expanding to South Africa, Morocco, Algeria
- **Superphosphates** — EAC and South Africa, Sudan, Morocco, Zambia, Egypt, Mozambique, Ethiopia
- **Tubes of iron/steel** — EAC and regional markets
- **Flavour mixtures for food/drink** — EAC and adjacent markets

### 3.6 Trade Barriers Affecting Uganda's Export Potential

**Uganda's identified challenges (public sector, 2024):**
- High cost of production, especially capital
- Poor storage facilities for fish and fresh food products
- Poor road network to other countries
- Non-tariff barriers (NTBs), especially for dairy products
- Limited access to finance for traders
- Low product diversity; over-reliance on agricultural products

**Uganda's identified challenges (private sector, 2024):**
- Low awareness of AfCFTA and its instruments
- High electricity costs
- NTBs from trading partners
- Costly credit/finance facilities
- Poor infrastructure
- Limited technology to transform commodities along value chains
- Climate change impact on agricultural commodities

**Dairy export barriers:** Milk has very high export potential but faces classification as a **sensitive item** in key target markets including Zambia, Malawi, and SACU countries. Uganda should explore markets that treat dairy as non-sensitive, including ECOWAS member states.

**Uganda export opportunities identified by stakeholders:**
- Exploitation of natural land endowment for agricultural expansion
- Value-addition in coffee, dairy, and timber
- Processed food, fish, and leather-based value chains

## 5. Supply Chain Integration in EAC

### 5.1 EAC Global Value Chain (GVC) Participation

Data from Borin & Mancini (2019) covering 1990–2015 (de Melo & Twum, 2020):

**EAC Regional Value Chain (RVC) participation: only 1.7% of total gross exports** are related to regional value chains within EAC. This compares poorly with:
- ASEAN: 17.2%
- MERCOSUR: 4.6%
- SADC: 3%

**What this means practically:** Less than 2 in every 100 dollars of EAC exports involve production chains that cross multiple EAC borders. EAC is primarily integrated into *global* (not regional) value chains, and mainly as an exporter of raw materials used by other countries to make finished goods.

### 5.2 GVC Participation Benchmarks (2015)

| Region | Overall GVC rate | Backward (imports for exports) | Forward (exports used by others) |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| ASEAN | ~40% | 36% | 18% |
| EAC | ~38% | 19% | 19% |
| COMESA | ~41% | 17% | 24% |
| SADC | ~40% | 16% | 24% |
| ECOWAS | ~35% | 7% | 28% |
| MERCOSUR | ~30% | 12% | 18% |

Note: EAC's overall GVC participation rate is comparable to peers, but almost entirely through non-regional (global) value chains, not EAC-internal ones.

### 5.3 Uganda's Supply Chain Performance

Uganda has **underperformed** relative to peer economies on supply chain integration (de Melo & Twum, 2020):
- Uganda remained in the **"high commodities"** GVC category throughout 1990–2015 (primary goods ≥40% of total domestic value-added in exports)
- Uganda is classified lower than Rwanda (comparable GDP per capita) and even Burundi (lower GDP per capita) in GVC diversification
- Uganda showed a **reduction in GVC participation in 3 out of 4 strategic sectors** (agriculture, food & beverages, wood & paper, textiles) over 1990–2015
- Only textiles showed no change; no sector showed improvement

**Kenya and Tanzania** by contrast moved into "limited manufacturing" GVC category by 2015, with higher manufacturing shares and greater backward integration (importing intermediates for processing and export).

### 5.4 Uganda's Services Trade Restrictiveness

Uganda's Trade Restrictiveness Index for services (de Melo & Twum, 2020):

| Sector | Uganda Score | EAC Average |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Financial | 27.7 | 26.5 |
| Professional | 38.0 | 46.9 |
| Retail | 50.0 | 15.0 |
| Telecommunications | 25.0 | 30.0 |
| Transportation | 21.1 | 25.7 |

Higher scores indicate greater restrictions on services trade. Uganda's retail sector score (50.0) is significantly above the EAC average (15.0), creating barriers for cross-border commerce. Professional services restrictions (38.0) are below EAC average, which is a relative advantage.

### 5.5 Three Key Obstacles to EAC Supply Chain Development

Research identifies three policy-amenable obstacles (de Melo & Twum, 2020):

**Obstacle 1: High tariffs on intermediate goods**
- Average tariff on intermediates across African countries: ~10%
- This is approximately twice the average rate in other developing regions
- Tariffs compound along supply chains: a 5% tariff repeated across 5 production stages creates a 10.5% effective cost increase; across 10 stages, 25.8%
- EAC's proposed CET revision (from 3-band to 4-band structure) risks increasing intermediate tariffs further

**Obstacle 2: Restrictive Rules of Origin (RoO)**
- EAC's 35% value-added threshold for zero-tariff qualification is administratively burdensome
- The number of non-recognition cases of Certificates of Origin remained constant at 13 per year across EAC (2014 and 2016)
- AfCFTA negotiations on RoO are contested: West Africa prefers general rules; South Africa and Egypt push for product-specific rules that may disadvantage smaller partners

**Obstacle 3: High barriers to trade in services and digital connectivity**
- EAC roaming costs are significantly higher than ECOWAS, COMESA, and SADC — almost double in some categories
- The **One Network Area (ONA)** initiative (2015) has shown strong results where implemented: inbound roaming calls Kenya-from-Rwanda increased 950%; retail roaming rates in Uganda fell 8-fold
- East Africa Single Digital Market (SDM) modelling: successful development could add 0.57%–1.6% to GDP growth and create 1.6–4.5 million new jobs; existing internet users would capture US$ 1.2–4 billion in consumer surplus from falling broadband prices (World Bank, 2018)

### 5.6 Strategic EAC Sectors for Regional Value Chains

EAC's industrialisation strategy targets these sectors for RVC development:
- **Cotton, textiles and apparel**
- **Leather and footwear**
- **Agro-processing: food and beverages**
- **Wood and wood products**
- **Iron and steel**
- **Automobiles**

Within EAC, the roles by sub-sector:
- **Leather**: Rwanda is the major EAC supplier
- **Cotton**: Uganda and Tanzania are major suppliers
- **Iron and steel**: Kenya is the major EAC exporter

Trade in all four textile/agro sectors within EAC is dominated by exports of **raw materials and intermediate products** — processing and value-addition remain limited.

## 6. EAC Trade Facilitation

### 6.1 One-Stop Border Posts (OSBPs)

The EAC passed the OSBP Bill in 2012, enacted as law in 2016. Status as at 2024:
- **13 of 15 earmarked OSBPs are complete and operational**
- 10 additional borders identified for future OSBP development

**Results (as at 2024):**
- Border crossing clearance time reduced by **79%** (from 14 hours to 1 hour 18 minutes) since OSBP implementation
- Taveta-Holili (Kenya-Tanzania): reduced by 20 hours
- Mutukula (Tanzania-Uganda): reduced by 17 hours
- Busia (Kenya-Uganda): reduced by 6 hours 40 minutes
- Direct cost reductions of **0.3%–14%** across different cargo routes in East Africa (Export Trade Potential of the EAC, 2024)

### 6.2 Non-Tariff Barrier (NTB) Trends

The EAC reported that the number of NTBs **increased from 10 to 48 between November 2024 and May 2025** — a significant deterioration despite progress on formal tariff liberalisation (Eastern Africa Trade Performance, 2025).

The **EAC Single Customs Bond** was introduced in 2024 as a regional trade facilitation instrument to simplify movement of goods across borders.

The **Uganda–Kenya bilateral NTB elimination agreement** (August 2025) is a significant bilateral step.

### 7.3 Commodity Price Drivers

Two commodity price surges drove EAC export earnings in 2024–2025 (Eastern Africa Trade Performance, 2025):

**Gold:** Up 60%+ from January 2024 to July 2025. Benefits Uganda and Tanzania significantly.

**Coffee:** Prices **doubled** by early 2025 from January 2024 levels. Benefits Uganda (major Robusta producer), Kenya (premium Arabica), and Rwanda.

**Tea:** Prices moderated in 2024, but Kenya reached record tea export earnings of **US$ 1.7 billion in 2024** (up from $1.4B in 2023), making Kenya the world's third-largest tea producer.

### 7.4 Structural Concern: Declining Manufacturing Share

Eastern Africa's mineral dependence has increased (2009–2024):
- EA-14 region: mineral share rose from 20% to 53% of exports
- EA-5 (key mineral producers): mineral share rose from 38% to 41%

Manufacturing as a share of total Eastern African exports:
- **EAC-5**: declined from 28% to 18% (2008–2024)
- **EA-14**: declined from 25% to 16%

This structural shift — more minerals, less manufacturing — creates long-term vulnerability as the region becomes more exposed to commodity price cycles and less able to build industrial employment (Eastern Africa Trade Performance, 2025).

### 7.5 Impact of US Tariffs on Eastern Africa (2025)

Eastern Africa exported approximately **$2.04 billion to the US in 2024** — only **3% of total regional exports**. Imports from the US were $3.79 billion — also 3% of regional imports.

The April 2025 US "Liberation Day" tariffs imposed:
- 10% baseline tariff on nearly all imports
- 20%–30% on major Asian textile exporters (China, Vietnam, Bangladesh, India)

**Effect on East Africa:** Countries still eligible under AGOA benefited from relative competitive advantage. Uganda's exports to the US rose 121% in April–July 2025.

AGOA eligibility status as of 2025:
- **Eligible:** Kenya, Tanzania, Djibouti, DRC, Madagascar, Comoros
- **Uganda:** Lost eligibility 1 January 2024 (LDC graduation concerns)
- **Ethiopia:** Suspended January 2022 (human rights concerns)
- **Rwanda:** Partially eligible (suspended from apparel in 2018)

**Business implication for Uganda:** Uganda cannot rely on AGOA for US market access. The EU (via EBA/Everything But Arms) remains the preferred developed-country preferential access route for Ugandan SME exporters.

## 8. Implications for Business Plan Market Analysis (§04)

### Sizing the EAC Addressable Market

When claiming the EAC as a total addressable market, note:
- EAC-6 (pre-Somalia and DRC expansion) had combined GDP of approximately **US$ 228B** in 2022
- EAC population: 303 million (21% of Africa's population)
- EAC GDP growth averaged **5.5% in 2022** — above African average of 2.7%
- EAC maintains a **trade surplus with Africa**, signalling strong intra-regional demand

For Uganda businesses, the primary EAC export market is approximately **$9.7 billion in EAC exports to Africa in 2022**, with Uganda accounting for roughly $2.2 billion of exports to the continent.

### Uganda's Priority Export Sectors for Business Plans

For businesses building export-oriented plans, Uganda's **highest-potential** AfCFTA sectors (with most unrealised upside) are:
1. **Dairy** — $91M unrealised potential; primary target markets: Egypt, Zambia, Mozambique
2. **Sugar** — $44M unrealised; South Africa, Sudan, Morocco
3. **Ferrous metals/steel products** — $64M+ unrealised; Sudan, Zambia, South Africa
4. **Wood products (laminated, MDF)** — $43M unrealised; Morocco, Egypt, Zambia
5. **Fish and shellfish** — $26M unrealised; Zambia, South Africa, Morocco
6. **Coffee** — $40M unrealised; Morocco, Tunisia, South Africa
7. **Low-fat milk powder** — $43M unrealised; Egypt, Zambia, South Africa

### Trade Facilitation Progress to Cite

OSBP impact (for logistics cost sections):
- Border crossing time reduced 79% (from 14 hrs to 1 hr 18 min)
- Direct cost savings of 0.3%–14% on regional cargo routes
- Mombasa–Kampala corridor cleared significantly faster post-Busia OSBP

### Contextualising NTBs in Risk Analysis (§12)

The increase in EAC NTBs from 10 to 48 between November 2024 and May 2025 is a live risk for any export-oriented business plan. Key NTB categories affecting Uganda exporters:
- Customs and administrative procedures
- Technical Barriers to Trade (TBT) — standards and certification
- Sanitary and phytosanitary (SPS) measures — especially for food, dairy, fish
- Specific country limitations (e.g., dairy classified as "sensitive" in SACU)

### Supply Chain Positioning

Uganda currently sits in the **"high commodities" GVC category** — exporting primarily raw materials and semi-processed goods. Business plans for agro-processing, food manufacturing, or wood products should explicitly frame the venture as contributing to Uganda's **transition toward "limited manufacturing"** GVC participation — a policy priority recognised in EAC's Industrial Development Strategy and AfCFTA Implementation Plan.

**Value chain framing for bankable plans:**
- Input sourcing: local sourcing counts toward the 35% EAC Rules of Origin threshold for zero-tariff export
- Processing: any washing, grading, packaging, or transformation adds value and improves RVC participation
- Export markets: identify which markets treat your product category as "Category A" (non-sensitive) under AfCFTA tariff schedules
