# Uganda Private Sector Markets — IFC Country Diagnostic

*Source: IFC/World Bank, "Creating Markets in Uganda: Growth through the Private Sector and Trade" (Country Private Sector Diagnostic, February 2022). Cite as (IFC/World Bank, 2022) in business plans.*

---

## 1. Private Sector Structure Overview

### Economy-Wide Characteristics

| Indicator | Value |
|---|---|
| Population (2022 reference) | 44.7 million |
| GDP per capita (constant 2010) | US$11,057 |
| Middle-income status projection | 2040 |
| New labour force entrants per year | 600,000+ (pre-2030); 1 million/year by 2040 |
| Jobs in microbusinesses (2010) | 7 out of 10 |
| SOE portfolio | 33 (down from 139 in 1990s) — one of the smallest in region |
| Public debt | 41% of GDP — low distress risk |

### GDP Sectoral Composition
- **Agriculture:** 23% of GDP (down from 50%+ in 1970s–80s); employs >70% of workforce
- **Manufacturing/Industry:** 16% of GDP; 7% of employment
- **Services:** 43% of GDP; 21% of employment
- Food processing alone: 40% of manufacturing output (led by sugar, coffee, tea)
- Construction materials, chemicals: other major manufacturing subsectors

### Trade Performance
- Uganda exports 19.5% of GDP (2018) — above East African average, remarkable for a landlocked country
- Agricultural exports (including agro-processing): >50% of total merchandise export value
- Services exports: 37% of total export value
- Tourism was largest foreign exchange earner pre-COVID: US$1.2 billion (2018)
- Manufacturing goods: 16% of total merchandise exports

## 2. Investment Climate

### Foreign Direct Investment
- FDI reached US$1.3 billion in 2018 — exceeding peer countries
- 161 new greenfield investments (2015–2019); 30% from Kenya, others from India, South Africa, USA, UAE
- Efficiency-seeking FDI: 21% (Uganda) vs Kenya 28%, Rwanda 10%, Tanzania 0%
- Agribusiness FDI tripled 2010–2019, reaching US$230 million
- Tech-leveraging companies present: SafeBoda (ride-hailing), Fenix International (solar pay-as-you-go), Andela (software), Jumia (e-commerce)

### Investment Climate Strengths
- Liberal, pro-private sector policy environment
- Smallest SOE portfolio in region — minimal distorting state competition
- Early AfCFTA ratifier; COMESA member since 2012; EAC co-founder (1992)
- Effective applied weighted average tariff: 8.05% (2019) — lower than Kenya (11.48%)
- Mobile money inclusion: 56% of adult Ugandans (2018); total financial inclusion 58%

### Investment Climate Weaknesses — Ranked by Private Sector Impact
1. **Corruption:** 41% of respondents paid a bribe (2017 East Africa Bribery Index) — up sharply from 2.6% in 2013
2. **Weak public financial management:** Efficiency estimated below 40%; US$300M/year lost to inefficiencies
3. **Infrastructure deficit:** US$1.4 billion/year gap; road quality second-lowest in peer group
4. **Weak competition:** Ranked 116th/141 in Global Competitiveness Report competition indicators — no competition law
5. **Land titling:** Customary tenure covers >70% of land; mortgage processing previously 28 days (now 3 days)
6. **High interest rates:** Commercial lending 19.26% avg (FY2019/20); Central Bank rate 9.3%

## 3. Financial Sector & Access to Finance

### Banking and Credit
| Indicator | Uganda | Kenya (for comparison) |
|---|---|---|
| Domestic credit to private sector (% GDP, 2020) | **14.2%** | 32% |
| Stock market capitalisation | 3.4% of GDP | 20.6% GDP |
| Non-performing loans (end 2020) | 5.2% (of total gross loans) | — |
| Commercial bank lending rate | 19.26% (FY2019/20) | — |
| Mobile money penetration (2018) | 56% of adults | — |

### SME Credit Access
- Only **10–25%** of bank lending goes to SMEs
- Banks rely on short-term deposits (90% of funding base) — constrains long-term lending
- Only **10% of farm households** had access to credit in past five years
- Only **6%** of small-scale agribusinesses had access to a loan or line of credit (vs 44.1% in Kenya)
- **Agricultural finance:** Banks perceive high risk; agribusiness consistently generates second-highest NPL rate
- Legal uncertainty over property rights and collateral recovery weighs on credit risk assessment
- Two credit bureaus operational; law allowing movable assets as collateral enacted

### Implications for Business Plans
- Debt financing at 19–24% p.a. materially increases project cost of capital
- Agribusiness loan applications face heightened scrutiny — strong financial projections and collateral are essential
- Development finance institutions (DFIs) and Uganda Development Bank (UDB) offer concessional alternatives
- SACCOs and microfinance remain primary credit channel for informal/small operators

## 4. Sector Opportunity Assessment

### 4.1 Agribusiness — Priority Value Chains

The IFC CPSD identifies 10 NDP III priority agricultural value chains: coffee, tea, fish, cocoa, cotton, vegetable oil, beef, maize, dairy, and cassava. Detailed assessment of three:

#### Fish / Fisheries
| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Uganda's global market share: fresh/chilled/frozen Nile perch fillets | ~33% (world's largest exporter) |
| Fish exports share of total (2015–2019 avg) | ~5% by value |
| ITC untapped export potential (5 fish products) | >US$150 million |
| Wild-catch processing factories operational (pre-COVID) | 12 |
| Aquaculture potential yield increase | Up to 20× with proper inputs |

**Key opportunities:** Aquaculture expansion, secondary processing (fish oils, fish meal, silage), temperature-controlled cold chain, domestic feed industry.

**Key risks:** Overfishing/depleting wild stocks; IUU fishing estimated at >10% of informal exports; biosecurity gaps; Kenya import ban on Ugandan fish.

#### Dairy
| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Cattle herd | 13–15 million (Sub-Saharan Africa's 7th largest) |
| Dairy cattle share | ~33% of herd |
| Daily production (pre-COVID) | 2.7 million litres/day |
| ITC untapped export potential | >US$90 million (milk powder, low-fat milk) |
| Current productivity | 1–4 litres/cow/day (potential: 20–25 litres) |
| Largest export market | Kenya (>90% of formal exports — now subject to import ban) |

**Key opportunities:** Powder milk, yogurt, kefir, condensed milk, butter; new corridors (Eastern, Albertine) opening remote dairy regions; CGIAR projects demand to triple over coming decades.

**Key risks:** Extreme dependency on Kenya market (import ban 2019); 60% EAC Common External Tariff protects regional producers from Uganda's exports.

#### Maize
- Overall yields barely increased: 2,505 kg/ha (2014) → 2,621 kg/ha (2018)
- Current yields for maize, millet, rice, sorghum: only 20–33% of rainfed potential
- Fertiliser use: 1.2 kg/ha — one of the lowest in Sub-Saharan Africa; <10% of smallholders apply fertiliser
- High informality in exports; quality levels often below acceptable export standards
- AfCFTA modelling: agricultural output could grow >US$1.6 billion by 2035

### 4.2 Energy Sector
| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Installed generation capacity (2020) | 1,252 MW (quadrupled since 2002) |
| On-grid electricity access | 24% of population |
| Off-grid electricity access (SHS etc.) | 27% |
| Solar home system units in use (2019) | 428,100 (from 284,328 in 2018) |
| SHS market players (Dec 2019) | 210+ solar companies |
| Transmission network | 2,890 km (66kV, 132kV, 220kV) |
| Funding gap for 5-year transmission expansion | ~US$2.5 billion |
| Cost of inaction on transmission/distribution | US$950 million/year |

**Productive use of electricity:** Manufacturing and industrial sectors account for 78% of all electricity sales; 44% of enterprises turned customers away due to unreliable power; 30% of enterprises and 50% of manufacturers lack electricity access.

**Off-grid opportunity:** National Electrification Strategy targets 5.9 million new SHS connections (valued at US$409 million) and 342 mini-grid sites (US$213 million) by 2030.

### 4.3 Housing and Construction
| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Housing shortfall (2022) | 2 million units (growing 200,000/year) |
| Projected shortfall by 2030 | 3 million units |
| Annual new housing needed (to keep pace) | 180,000 units/year |
| Formal housing units built/year | <5,000 registered units (all >US$50,000) |
| Formal housing market access | 12% of households only |
| Urban households in slums/informal settlements | >60% |
| Urban rental vs ownership | 44% rent, 46% own (2018) |
| Households that can afford mortgage-financed housing | 11.5% |
| Mortgage available housing threshold | >US$37,000 |
| Intermediate input contribution to GDP (2018) | 4.8% |
| Construction non-construction-related costs | 57% of total development cost |

**Housing affordability submarkets (urban households):**
- High income (4.0%): >US$3,000/month → property value >US$55,000
- Middle-upper (7.5%): US$2,000–3,000/month → US$37,000–55,000
- Middle-lower (19.9%): US$1,000–2,000/month → US$18,000–37,000
- Low income (76.2%): <US$1,000/month → demand-based (informal market)

**Kampala specifically:** 3.3 million people (2020) → 5.5 million by 2025; over 50% of Kampala households rent; less than 30% of road network paved (lowest of any East African capital).

**NSSF assets:** Over US$3 billion / UGX 10 trillion — largely not deployed into housing; major institutional capital pool.

## 5. Cross-Cutting Constraints for Business Planning

### Infrastructure Gaps
| Constraint | Business Impact |
|---|---|
| Power: 44% of enterprises turned away customers (unreliable supply) | Factor into generator/solar backup costs |
| Internet penetration: 14% (among lowest of 10 African peer countries) | Digital business models require factoring connectivity costs |
| Roads: 90%+ of traffic by road; quality second-lowest in peer group | Cold chain, logistics, and distribution cost assumptions |
| Cold chain: significant cost for temperature-sensitive products | Fish, dairy, horticulture businesses must budget cold storage |

### Trade and Logistics
- Uganda landlocked: goods transit Mombasa (Kenya) or Dar es Salaam (Tanzania)
- Non-tariff barriers (NTBs) persist despite low formal tariffs
- Ugandan firms export 230 products to 68 markets; larger firms (<10% of registered firms have 10+ employees) have ~25% chance of maintaining export relationship 5 years
- Border delays, documentation requirements, and weak quality infrastructure raise compliance costs
- Kenya market: critical but politically volatile (multiple import bans 2019–2021 on dairy, maize, fish, poultry, sugar)

### Skills Market
- Majority of frontline workforce: skills not a binding constraint (demand-driven market)
- Specialist/technical skills: meaningful gaps in construction, energy transmission, cold chain management
- Professional skills (engineers, architects, surveyors): mobile and prone to emigration
- Work permits for non-Ugandans: currently a constraint to filling critical gaps

## 6. Digital Economy Opportunity

- Digital solutions priority areas: utility/tax payments, e-commerce platforms, digital SME finance
- Mobile money: primary financial infrastructure; 56% adult penetration
- AgTech/FinTech for farmers: currently low penetration but expanding
- Internet: 14% penetration; costs high; limited outside urban centres
- NDP III target: middle-income status by 2040 via digital transformation

## 7. Implications for Business Plans

### Market-Sizing Notes
- Uganda's 44.7 million population and 3.8% growth rate (2018) creates large addressable markets
- But 76% of households earn <US$1,000/month — purchasing power is the binding constraint
- AfCFTA ratification opens larger East/Central African market; Uganda is gateway to South Sudan and eastern DRC

### Competitor Landscape
- 7 out of 10 jobs in microbusinesses (as of 2010 census) — highly fragmented markets
- Formal sector concentrated among a few large exporters; MSMEs dominate domestic markets
- Informal competition universal: only 4% of businesses formally registered (URSB data, NLFS 2021)

### Risk Flags for Business Plans
- Agricultural and agribusiness businesses: factoring in high perceived credit risk and 6% SME loan access rate
- Any business dependent on Kenya market: flag political trade risk
- Power-dependent businesses: budget for backup generation; 50% of manufacturers lack access
- Housing/real estate: strong demand signals but high regulatory, land, and finance complexity
