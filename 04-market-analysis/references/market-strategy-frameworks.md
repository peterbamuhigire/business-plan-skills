## 1. Market Sizing Methods

*Source: Evans, Ch 2–3*

There are **six main methods** for sizing a market. Use several in combination and triangulate.

### 1.1 The Six Methods

| # | Method | How it works |
|---|--------|-------------|
| 1 | **Top-down market research** | Start with a known, researched market size and remove inapplicable sections or apply relevant proportions to drill down to the target market. |
| 2 | **Bottom-up market research** | Take disaggregated data from a research report and assemble the relevant portions that constitute the target market. |
| 3 | **Bottom-up customer sizing** | Estimate how much each major customer spends in the target market; add an allowance for minor customers. |
| 4 | **Bottom-up competitor sizing (marketcrafting)** | Estimate the scale of each competitor in the target market relative to your own sales. See detailed method below. |
| 5 | **Related market triangulation** | Use two or three known sizes of related markets to gauge a rough estimate of the target market. |
| 6 | **Final triangulation** | Juggle estimates from the above sources, apply sanity checks, assign reliability ratings to each, compute a probability-weighted average. |

### 1.2 Top-Down vs Bottom-Up

| Approach | Direction | Strength | Weakness |
|----------|-----------|----------|----------|
| **Top-down** | Total market → segment → obtainable share | Quick; uses published data | Risks inflated figures; investors are sceptical |
| **Bottom-up** | Individual customers/competitors → aggregate | Grounded in observable reality | Time-consuming; requires market knowledge |

**Always use both and reconcile.** Investors flag plans that rely only on top-down sizing.

### 1.3 TAM / SAM / SOM

| Term | Definition | Typical filter |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| **TAM** (Total Addressable Market) | Total demand for the product/service globally or nationally | None — full universe |
| **SAM** (Serviceable Available Market) | Portion of TAM reachable given your geography, channel, and segment focus | Geography, customer type, product fit |
| **SOM** (Serviceable Obtainable Market) | Portion of SAM you can realistically capture | Competitive position, capacity, brand awareness |

**Reasonableness rules:**
- Startups: SOM typically 1–5% of SAM in years 1–3.
- Established businesses entering new markets: 5–15% of SAM.
- If SOM exceeds 20% of SAM, provide extraordinary justification.

### 1.4 Marketcrafting — Evans's Competitor-Based Sizing Method

Marketcrafting is particularly useful for niche markets where published data does not exist. It yields market size, market share, and share change in one exercise.

**Seven steps:**

1. **List main competitors** — those you pitch against regularly, exhibit alongside at trade shows, including foreign competitors.
2. **Index your own sales at 100.** This is your baseline.
3. **Estimate each competitor's sales relative to yours.** Does competitor A sell more or less than you? By roughly how much? Assign an index number (e.g., 90 if ~10% less than you, 120 if ~20% more).
4. **Repeat for every named competitor.**
5. **Add an "Others" category** — small or occasional competitors; assign an index (e.g., 50 if collectively about half your sales).
6. **Calculate:** Total all index numbers. Market size = (Total index / 100) x your sales.
7. **Cross-check with colleagues** — sales director, operations director, anyone with competitor intelligence. Discuss, refine, converge.

**Worked example (adapted from Evans):**

| Competitor | Index (latest year) | Implied market share |
|------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Your company | 100 | 17% |
| Competitor A | 120 | 21% |
| Competitor B | 85 | 15% |
| Competitor group C | 125 | 21% |
| Competitor group D | 65 | 11% |
| Competitor E | 30 | 5% |
| Competitor F | 20 | 3% |
| Others | 40 | 7% |
| **Total** | **585** | **100%** |

If your turnover in this segment is UGX 110 billion, the estimated market size is (585/100) x 110bn = **UGX 643.5 billion**. Your share is 100/585 = 17%.

**Bonus outputs from marketcrafting:**
- **Market growth** — repeat the exercise for 3 years ago to get two data points and compute a compound annual growth rate.
- **Market share** — your sales / estimated market size.
- **Share change** — compare today's share with 3 years ago for you and each competitor.

## 2. Market Demand Forecasting — The HOOF Approach

*Source: Evans, Ch 3*

HOOF is a four-step method for translating market demand trends and drivers into forecasts. Apply it **per business segment**.

### 2.1 The Four Steps

| Step | Letter | Action |
|------|--------|--------|
| 1 | **H** — Historic growth | Assess how market demand has grown over the past 3–4 years. Use compound annual growth rate (CAGR) in real terms. Avoid relying on a single recent year. |
| 2 | **O** — Drivers past | Identify what has been driving that growth: income growth, population growth, awareness, government policy, price changes, structural shifts, fashion, weather, demand in customer sectors. |
| 3 | **O** — Drivers future | Assess whether each driver will strengthen (+), weaken (−), or remain unchanged (0) over the next few years. Consider the economic cycle, elasticity, and complementary sectors. |
| 4 | **F** — Forecast growth | Weave together past trends and future driver impacts. More pluses than before = acceleration; more minuses = deceleration. |

### 2.2 Worked Example: New Service for the Elderly

| Demand driver | Recent past | Now | Next few years |
|--------------|-------------|-----|----------------|
| Growth in incomes | + | + | + |
| Growth in elderly population | + | + | ++ |
| Increased awareness of service | ++ | ++ | +++ |
| **Overall impact** | **+** | **++** | **+++** |
| **Market growth rate** | **5–10%/yr** | **5–10%/yr** | **>10%/yr** |

**Key:** + Positive, 0 None, − Negative

**Interpretation:** All three drivers are strengthening. The elderly population will grow faster, and awareness can only increase. Forecast: market growth accelerates from ~7% to >10% per year.

### 2.3 Practical Tips

- Measure growth in **real terms** (deflated by market-specific price indices), not nominal.
- Use 3–4 year averages; smooth out cyclical ups and downs with moving averages if necessary.
- For **new markets with no history**, use the HOOF approach on comparable or adjacent markets, supplemented by primary test-marketing research.
- Display HOOF results **diagrammatically** — one chart per segment, with +/−/0 symbols showing driver impact over time.

## 3. Porter's Five Forces Analysis

*Sources: Harris Ch 4; Portable MBA Ch 8 (Collis & Ghemawat)*

Five Forces analysis determines the **attractiveness of an industry** — its profit potential and the ease of mobility between strategic positions. Use it when assessing entry/exit decisions, identifying threats and opportunities, or understanding why an industry is (un)profitable.

### 3.1 The Five Forces — Assessment Criteria

#### Force 1: Rivalry Among Existing Competitors

| Factor | Increases rivalry | Decreases rivalry |
|--------|------------------|-------------------|
| Number and size of firms | Many equally sized firms | Few firms; one dominant player |
| Industry growth | Slow or declining growth | Rapid growth |
| Product differentiation | Commodity; minimal differentiation | Highly differentiated offerings |
| Fixed costs / capacity | High fixed costs; excess capacity | Low fixed costs; tight capacity |
| Exit barriers | High (specialised assets, labour obligations, cleanup costs) | Low (easy to redeploy assets) |
| Diversity of competitors | Diverse origins, strategies, stakes | Homogeneous competitors |
| Coordination potential | Low (many firms, no price leader) | High (few firms, tacit price leadership) |

#### Force 2: Threat of New Entrants

Entry is **less likely** when:

| Barrier type | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| **High sunk costs** | R&D expenditure, brand-building, irrecoverable investment |
| **Incumbent advantage** | Patents, licences, brand loyalty, pre-commitment contracts, economies of scale, learning curves |
| **Credible retaliation** | Incumbents with excess capacity, high exit costs, reputation for aggression |

#### Force 3: Threat of Substitutes (and Complements)

| Factor | Increases threat | Decreases threat |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Cross-price elasticity | High (easy to switch, e.g. butter/margarine) | Low (no functional equivalent) |
| Switching costs | Low or zero | High (retraining, retooling) |
| Price-performance ratio | Substitute offers better value | Current product superior on price-performance |
| Complements | Few complementary products | Strong complements increase lock-in |

*Note: Substitution often follows an S-curve — slow start, rapid acceleration, then levelling off.*

#### Force 4: Buyer Power

| Factor | Increases buyer power | Decreases buyer power |
|--------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Concentration | Few large buyers | Many fragmented buyers |
| Information | Buyers well-informed on costs/alternatives | Asymmetric information favouring sellers |
| Backward integration | Credible threat | Not credible |
| Switching costs | Low | High |
| Product share of buyer's costs | High (buyers scrutinise large cost items) | Low |
| Risk of product failure | Low (commodity, easy to evaluate) | High (buyers stick with proven suppliers) |

#### Force 5: Supplier Power

| Factor | Increases supplier power | Decreases supplier power |
|--------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Concentration | Few suppliers, many buyers | Many suppliers, easy to switch |
| Differentiation of inputs | Highly specialised inputs | Commodity inputs |
| Forward integration threat | Credible | Not credible |
| Switching costs | High | Low |
| Unionisation/collective action | Strong unions (e.g. steelworkers) | Fragmented labour supply |

### 3.2 Rating Methodology

**For each force, rate on a three- or five-point scale:**

| Rating | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| 1 — Very weak | Force exerts minimal pressure; favourable to industry profitability |
| 2 — Weak | Some pressure but manageable |
| 3 — Moderate | Meaningful impact; requires strategic attention |
| 4 — Strong | Significant pressure on margins |
| 5 — Very strong | Force severely limits profitability |

### 3.3 Integration and Interpretation

1. **Rate each force** individually with supporting evidence.
2. **Summarise in a single diagram** — the classic five-forces map with each force annotated as high/medium/low.
3. **Overall industry attractiveness:** No simple formula — the analyst must weigh the totality of forces. One overwhelmingly strong force (e.g. substitutes) can render an industry unattractive regardless of other forces.
4. **Identify actionable levers:** Which forces can the firm influence? (e.g. raising switching costs to reduce buyer power, investing in differentiation to reduce rivalry).

**Attractive industry profile (Harris):** Few firms, high growth, unequal size, differentiated offerings, many alternative buyers, scale economies, few substitutes, significant complements.

**Unattractive industry profile:** Many firms, low growth, equal size, commodity products, few alternative buyers, low barriers to entry, many substitutes.

## 4. The Value Net Framework

*Source: Portable MBA Ch 8 (Brandenburger & Nalebuff)*

The Value Net **extends** the Five Forces by adding **complementors** and highlighting **cooperative** as well as competitive dynamics.

### 4.1 Structure

```
                 Customers
                     |
    Competitors — YOUR FIRM — Complementors
                     |
                  Suppliers
```

- **Competitors:** Players from whom customers can buy substitute products/services.
- **Complementors:** Players from whom customers buy complementary products/services (or who supply complementary inputs to your suppliers).

**Key insight:** The same player can be both a competitor and a complementor simultaneously — the "Jekyll and Hyde" effect.

### 4.2 Cooperative vs Competitive Dynamics

| Dimension | Competitive lens | Cooperative lens |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Pie size** | Fight over fixed pie | Expand the pie together |
| **Buyer value** | Substitutes reduce willingness to pay | Complements increase willingness to pay |
| **Supplier value** | Rivals bid up input costs | Complements reduce input prices |
| **Strategic focus** | Capture value from others | Create new value jointly |

**"Thinking complements is about finding ways to make the pie bigger rather than fighting with competitors over a fixed pie."** — Brandenburger & Nalebuff

### 4.3 Determinants of Complementor Power

When assessing whether complementors will capture value at your expense, consider:

| Factor | Complementors gain power when... |
|--------|--------------------------------|
| **Relative concentration** | Complementors are concentrated relative to competitors |
| **Switching costs** | Buyer/supplier switching costs across complementors exceed those across rivals |
| **Bundling difficulty** | Products cannot be purchased or used independently of complements |
| **Pull-through effect** | Complementors play a greater role in driving demand or supply |
| **Integration threats** | Complementors can credibly invade competitors' territory |
| **Pie growth rate** | Slow growth intensifies competition between competitors and complementors |

### 4.4 Application Steps

1. **Map all players** — customers, suppliers, competitors, and complementors around your firm.
2. **Identify dual roles** — which players are simultaneously competitors and complementors?
3. **Assess cooperative opportunities** — where can you expand the pie? Joint ventures, standards, co-marketing?
4. **Assess competitive threats** — which complementors could capture disproportionate value?
5. **Design strategy** — balance cooperation (pie expansion) with competition (pie division).

## 5. Environmental / Macro Analysis — PESTEL Framework

*Source: Harris Ch 3*

Environmental analysis (or "environmental scanning") examines **broader societal forces** that currently affect, or may in future influence, the nature of competition within an industry. It complements firm-level and industry-level tools (competitor analysis, Five Forces).

### 5.1 The PESTEL Categories

| Category | Key considerations |
|----------|-------------------|
| **Political–Legal** | Current and pending legislation; regulatory bodies and processes; public funding/grants; lobbying/advocacy influence; government stability |
| **Economic** | Business cycle phase; home and overseas economic trends; taxation; seasonality of demand; market volatility from wars/conflicts; interest and exchange rates |
| **Sociocultural** | Lifestyle and fashion trends; social issues; media influence; ethnic/religious differences; values and attitudes |
| **Technological** | Maturity of current technology; emergent developments; R&D funding (public and private); patents and IP legislation |
| **Environmental** | Environmental/social pressures; sustainability regulation; climate-related demand shifts |
| **Legal / Global Trade** | International trade and monetary policies; international pressure groups; globalisation impacts; international legislation |

### 5.2 Scanning Methodology — Three Steps

**Step 1. List factors.**
Identify a comprehensive set of factors relevant to the competitive setting. Use the PESTEL categories as a checklist. Do not limit to obvious factors — technological disruption and sociocultural shifts often have the most dramatic impact (e.g. the personal computer eliminated typewriter demand; economic recession devastated travel and tourism).

**Step 2. Collect data.**
For each factor, gather quantitative and qualitative data. Many but not all factors are quantifiable. Use Table 5.1 considerations as a prompt:

| Demographics | Sociocultural | Macroeconomic |
|-------------|--------------|---------------|
| Population growth/death rates | Lifestyle/fashion trends | Business cycle influence |
| Age distribution trends | Social issues | Home/overseas economic trends |
| Migration/immigration | Media views and influence | Taxation issues |
| Population segmentation | Ethnic/religious differences | Seasonality of demand |

| Political–Legal | Technological | Global Trade |
|-----------------|--------------|-------------|
| Current/pending legislation | Technology maturity | Interest/exchange rates |
| Regulatory bodies/processes | Emergent technology | International trade policies |
| Environmental/social pressures | R&D funding | International pressure groups |
| Public funding/grants | Patents and IP | Globalisation impacts |

**Step 3. Analyse implications.**
For each significant factor: What is the current trend? What is the likely future direction? What is the impact on demand, growth, entry/exit, innovation, and regulation in your industry? Integrate findings into your Five Forces and demand forecasting (HOOF) analyses.

### 5.3 Why It Matters

Environmental factors can overwhelm competitive advantage. A firm with a superior competitive position can be destroyed by macro shifts (typewriters vs computers). Environmental analysis can also:
- **Predict competitor behaviour** — macro shifts may force competitors into specific strategic responses.
- **Reveal opportunities to influence** — direct engagement with regulators, standard-setters, or media.
- **Feed into scenario planning** — predetermined factors (e.g. demographic trends) vs critical uncertainties form the basis of alternative scenarios.

## 6. Industry Life Cycle

*Source: Harris Ch 5*

Technologies and product markets progress along well-defined **sigmoid (S) curves**. The Competitive Life Cycle (CLC) has three phases, separated by transitory inflection points.

### 6.1 The S-Curve and Three Phases

```
Cumulative
Revenue
    |                          ___________
    |                        /   MATURE
    |                      /    PHASE
    |                    /
    |                  / GROWTH
    |                /  PHASE
    |              /
    |           /
    |        _/  EMERGENT
    |      /     PHASE
    |____/
    |__________________________________ Time
```

### 6.2 Phases and Inflection Points

| Phase | Begins with | Characteristics | Strategic focus |
|-------|-------------|----------------|----------------|
| **Emergent** | **Disruption** — introduction of a new technology or product concept | Experimentation with designs; slow initial adoption; widespread entry by diversifying incumbents and new ventures; no dominant design yet | Innovation, R&D, product–market fit, securing early customers |
| **Growth** | **Annealing** — convergence around a dominant design; customer adoption accelerates | Significant demand growth; business focus shifts from development to scaling; increasing head-to-head competition; downward price pressure; complementary capabilities (manufacturing, sales, service) grow in importance | Scaling operations, market share capture, manufacturing efficiency, brand building |
| **Mature** | **Shakeout** — marginally competitive firms exit; handful of dominant players emerge | Growth slows; competition for market share intensifies; strong downward price pressure; focus on incremental innovation and cost savings; small niche entrants possible | Cost efficiency, incremental innovation, defending share, portfolio of next-generation initiatives |

### 6.3 Characterising Your Life Cycle

Use the CLC worksheet (Harris):

| Inflection point | Timing question | Severity question |
|-----------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Disruption** | How long is the mature phase before disruption? | Radical, incremental, or architectural? |
| **Annealing** | How long is the emergent phase? | Dominant design, or multiple viable designs? |
| **Shakeout** | How long is the growth phase? | Winner-take-all, duopoly, or contested? |
| **Overall speed** | How long is one full cycle? | Hyperdynamic or slow burn? |

### 6.4 Application Steps

1. **Chart existing products/services** on the CLC — identify which phase each is in.
2. **Estimate remaining life** — how much time before you must replace each revenue stream?
3. **Characterise timing and severity** of each inflection point for your industry.
4. **Chart competitors' positions** — who is where? Who is investing in next-generation S-curves?
5. **Plan your growth portfolio** — the amount of revenue you must replace (declining products) plus the amount you need to add. Create a portfolio of initiatives; most will not produce new S-curves, so hedge your bets.

### 6.5 Key Insight

The purpose of strategy is to **create new S-curves**. Not all initiatives succeed, which is why management must maintain a portfolio of growth initiatives in anticipation of the decline of current curves.

## 7. Customer Buying Criteria — Evans's E2-P2-R2 Framework

*Source: Evans, Ch 5*

Customer buying criteria are **what customers need from their suppliers**. They can be grouped into six categories, remembered as **E2-P2-R2**.

### 7.1 The Six Criteria

| Code | Criterion | Examples |
|------|----------|---------|
| **E** | **Effectiveness** | Quality, design, features, specifications, functionality, reliability of the product or service |
| **E** | **Efficiency** | Convenience, delivery speed, on-time delivery, ease of doing business |
| **P** | **Premises** | Quality, location, and ambience of supplier's premises (applicable only if the customer visits) |
| **P** | **Price** | Tends to be more important the less essential the product/service |
| **R** | **Relationship** | Trust, responsiveness, communication, personal rapport with the supplier |
| **R** | **Range** | Breadth and depth of products/services offered |

### 7.2 How to Identify Buying Criteria

1. **Ask customers directly.** After just a few discussions with customers from any one group, a predictable pattern emerges.
2. **Rate importance.** Ask customers to rate each criterion as "very important", "important", or "unimportant". Customers within a given segment tend to have similar needs.
3. **Segment differences.** Repeat for each major segment — importance weightings will differ.
4. **Watch for hidden agendas.** Some customers use surveys to push for lower prices or extra service at no cost. They may over-state the importance of price when their real concern is product quality. Apply judgement.

### 7.3 Practical Application (Ugandan Context)

For a food processing business selling to Kampala supermarkets:

| Criterion | Importance | Notes |
|----------|-----------|-------|
| Effectiveness (product quality, taste, shelf life) | Very high | Non-negotiable for premium retailers |
| Efficiency (reliable delivery to schedule) | High | Supermarkets penalise late or incomplete deliveries |
| Price | High | Price-sensitive market; margins squeezed by imports |
| Range | Medium | Retailers prefer suppliers offering a full product line |
| Relationship | Medium | Personal relationships matter in Ugandan business culture |
| Premises | Low | Buyers rarely visit the factory (but may for audits) |

## 8. Deriving Key Success Factors

*Source: Evans, Ch 5–6*

Key success factors (KSFs) are **what firms must do** to satisfy customer buying criteria and run a sound business. They are derived directly from customer buying criteria.

### 8.1 Four-Step Process

#### Step 1: Convert Buying Criteria into Success Factors

Customer buying criteria are the **demand side**; success factors are the **supply side**. They are often the flipside of each other:

| Buying criterion (customer wants...) | Success factor (firm must have...) | Type |
|--------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|------|
| Product functionality | R&D capability | Differentiation |
| Product reliability | Quality control systems | Differentiation |
| Delivery to schedule | Spare capacity / manufacturing efficiency | Differentiation |
| Breadth of range | Product development pipeline | Differentiation |
| Customer service quality | Trained service team, CRM systems | Differentiation |
| Keen price | Location of facilities, operational efficiency, scale economies, overhead control, outsourcing, IT systems | Cost |

**Two categories emerge:**
- **Differentiation-related factors** — factors where you can distinguish your product/service from competitors.
- **Cost-related factors** — determinants of cost competitiveness (location, materials cost, operational efficiency, scale).

#### Step 2: Add Management and Market Share

Two additional factors that are not directly derived from buying criteria but critically affect competitive position:

- **Management quality** — would a well-managed company with an average product outperform a poorly managed company with a superb product in your industry?
- **Market share** — the power of the incumbent. Higher share = broader/deeper customer relationships, repeat business advantage, lower unit costs through economies of scale.

#### Step 3: Rate Factors for Importance

Assign a **weight** (to the nearest 5 or 10%) to each factor, ensuring weights total 100%.

**Worked example — niche engineering market:**

| Factor | Importance | Weight |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Market share | Med/High | 15% |
| **Cost factors** | **Very high** | **35%** |
| **Differentiation factors:** | | |
| Product capability and range | Med/High | 15% |
| Product reliability | Med/High | 15% |
| Engineering service network | Medium | 10% |
| Customer service | Medium | 10% |
| **Total** | | **100%** |

**Guideline:** Aim for ~10 factors total — market share, management, 2–3 cost-related, and 5–6 differentiation-related. More than that and you lose the wood for the trees.

#### Step 4: Identify Must-Haves

This is the crucial final step. A **must-have** factor is one so critical that if you do not rank highly against it, you should not be in the business at all. You will not win any orders or cannot deliver on orders won.

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Must-have (screening factor)** | Binary pass/fail. Without it, you cannot compete. | ISO certification in a regulated market; specific professional qualification; IT security for a confidential service |
| **Should-have (ranking factor)** | Determines your relative competitive position. Better performance = stronger position. | Product quality, delivery speed, cost competitiveness |

**How to test for must-haves:**
- Is there a factor where a score of zero means you are out of the game entirely?
- Could you lose all your business if you fail on this single factor, regardless of strength on others?
- Is there a regulatory, legal, or certification requirement that is an absolute prerequisite?

**The Ashley Madison test (Evans):** A dating agency built rapid growth on controversy and low marketing costs, but failed on the one must-have — **confidentiality**. Its IT security was breached, the customer database was hacked, and the business was devastated. Confidentiality was not a ranking factor but a screening factor.

### 8.2 Summary — KSF Derivation Flow

```
Customer Buying Criteria (E2-P2-R2)
        |
        v
Convert to Success Factors
        |
        +--> Differentiation-related factors
        +--> Cost-related factors
        |
        v
Add: Management quality + Market share
        |
        v
Weight each factor (total = 100%)
        |
        v
Screen for must-haves (pass/fail)
        |
        v
Final list of ~10 Key Success Factors
```

Use this final list to **rate your firm and each competitor** against every factor. This produces a competitive position assessment that feeds directly into strategy formulation.
