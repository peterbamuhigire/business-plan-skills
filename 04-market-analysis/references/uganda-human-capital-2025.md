# Uganda Human Capital Development & Growth Review 2025
**Source:** World Bank / Government of Uganda — *Uganda Human Capital Development and Growth Review* (UHCDGR), 2025
**Relevance:** Market opportunity sizing, demographic dividend framing, education/health investment context, Vision 2040 targets

---

## 1. Overall Human Capital Index (HCI)

- **HCI score (2022):** 0.39 — meaning Uganda currently harnesses only **39% of its human capital potential**
- **HCI (2010):** 0.34 → **HCI (2025 estimate):** 0.40 — modest 10-year gain
- **Business-as-usual projection to 2040:** HCI ≈ 0.45
- **Vision 2040 target:** HCI of **0.56** by 2040 — requires deliberate, accelerated investment
- **Utilisation-adjusted HCI (UHCI):**
  - 0.34 if all workers counted
  - **0.19** when only paid/profit workers counted — reflecting severe underutilisation
- **Implication for investors:** Uganda's GDP per capita could be **3–5× higher** if every child received quality education and healthcare and used that capital productively

## 2. Economic Growth Targets & Vision 2040

| Indicator | Current (2024) | Vision 2040 Target |
|---|---|---|
| GDP | US$50 billion | US$500 billion (10-fold growth) |
| GDP per capita | US$1,046 | US$7,000–9,500 |
| HCI score | 0.39–0.40 | 0.56 |
| Adult literacy rate | 73% (unchanged since 2010) | 95% |
| Average years of schooling | 6.8 years | 11 years |
| Learning-adjusted years of schooling (LAYS) | 4.3 years | 7 years |

**Ten-Fold Growth Strategy (2024):** Requires doubling GDP roughly every five years — this is Uganda's overarching economic frame for the 2024–2040 period.

## 3. Demographic Dividend — The Core Opportunity

Uganda stands at a **one-time, historic window** to capture a demographic dividend.

- **Population (2024):** 46 million — one of the youngest in the world
- **Under-18 share:** More than 50% of the population
- **Under-17 share (UNPHC 2024):** 49.6% of total population
- **Projected population by 2060:** 104 million (more than double), of which **70% will be of working age**
- **Annual new labour market entrants:** 1.1–1.2 million new workers per year
- **Labour market pressure to 2040:** 14 million additional workers must be absorbed
- **Dependency ratio (2016):** 92 dependants per 100 economically active persons — **second highest in East Africa** (after Somalia)
- **Total fertility rate (2022):** 4.47 per woman (declining from 7.0 in 1990, but still high)

**For business plans:** The demographic dividend is the single most powerful market driver for any Uganda consumer-facing business. A growing working-age population creates demand for financial services, agribusiness, housing, education, and healthcare.

## 4. Education — Key Statistics

### Access and Enrolment
- Primary enrolment: grew from 2.6 million (1995) to 8.7 million (2017) after Universal Primary Education (1997)
- Gross attendance ratio exceeds 100% (over-age and under-age enrolment)
- Pre-primary attendance (poorest quintile): **17%** vs **57%** (wealthiest quintile)
- Tertiary enrolment: top income quintile accounts for **more than two-thirds** of total

### Quality and Learning Outcomes
- UNEB 2023 NAPE: **57% of P6 pupils** below minimum literacy; **42%** below minimum numeracy
- Uganda's LAYS (4.3) are **11% lower** than countries at similar GDP and expenditure levels
- Only **60% of children** who start P1 complete P7 — mostly due to cost
- COVID school closures (22 months — among longest in the world) **erased literacy gains** from early grade reading programmes

### Education Expenditure (Critical for Workforce Cost Assumptions)
| Indicator | Uganda | Benchmark |
|---|---|---|
| Public education spend (% of GDP) | 2.7% | 4.2% (East Africa average); 4% minimum (UNESCO) |
| Per-student spend (primary, vs regional peers) | 1/3 of regional average | — |
| % of government budget on education | 11.3% | 15–20% recommended |
| Annual cost of teacher absenteeism | UGX 450B–1.5 trillion | — |
| Annual cost of grade repetition (low pre-primary) | UGX 665 billion | — |
| Household spend per UGX 1,000 government invests | UGX 1,450 | — |
| Ghost teachers/schools annual waste | UGX 50 billion | — |
| Diverted funds/supplies | UGX 244.6 billion/year | — |

**For business plans:** Low education quality increases **training costs** for businesses hiring school leavers. Budget for induction training of 2–4 weeks beyond what peer markets require. A secondary-educated employee in Uganda often performs at P7 level in numeracy/literacy — calibrate job specifications accordingly.

### TVET (Technical & Vocational Education)
- 2025 TVET ACT enacted — TVET Council to be established
- Skills Development Facility (with Private Sector Foundation Uganda) launched
- Focus sectors: agriculture, construction, manufacturing
- TVET fees higher than comparator countries — a barrier to skilled labour supply

## 5. Health — Key Statistics

### Trends in Key Health Outcomes (1989/90–2023/24)

| Indicator | 1989/90 | 2022–24 | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Life expectancy (years) | 46.3 | **68.5** | +48% |
| HIV prevalence (adults, %) | 22% | 5.1% | −77% |
| Total fertility rate (per woman) | 7.0 | **4.5** | −36% |
| Maternal mortality (per 100,000 live births) | 550 | **189** | −66% |
| Under-5 mortality (per 1,000 live births) | 180 | **52** | −71% |
| Infant mortality (per 1,000 live births) | 100 | **36** | −64% |
| Under-5 stunting (%) | 45% | **24.4%** | −46% |
| Teenage pregnancy (% of women 15–19) | 37% | **24%** | −35% |

**Source:** UBOS (UDHS and Census), World Bank WDI.

### Health System Capacity
- Universal Health Coverage (UHC) index: improved from **22% (2000) to 49% (2021)**
- Population within 5km of a health facility: **91% (2019/20)**, up from 83% (2012/13)
- Public health sector vacancies filled: only **34%**
- Essential medicines budgeted: approximately **50%** of quantified requirements
- Health facilities assessed as ready to deliver services: **59%**
- Road traffic deaths: **29 per 100,000** — among highest in Africa (regional rate: 24.1)
- Health sector workforce: **158,932 employees** (public + private) — largest skilled employment group in rural areas

### Health Financing Gap
| Indicator | Uganda | Benchmark |
|---|---|---|
| Public health expenditure (% of GDP) | ~1% | 2%+ (Kenya, Rwanda, Burundi, Malawi) |
| Current health expenditure per capita | US$35.43 | US$86 (McIntyre benchmark) |
| Government health spend from domestic sources (% of govt budget) | 3.9% | 15% (Abuja target) |
| External + household health financing share | 83% | — |
| Households facing financial hardship accessing care | ~11% | — |

**For business plans:** Employee health benefits (even basic group health coverage) are a significant differentiator for talent retention. Medical leave impacts (malaria — 29.1% of outpatient visits) should be factored into HR planning as 3–5 sick days per employee per year.

### NCDs and Emerging Health Risks
- NCDs share of disease burden: rose from 18.3% (2001) to **37.3% (2019)** of total DALYs
- Malaria: 29.1% of outpatient visits, 39.5% of inpatient admissions, 10.9% of hospital deaths
- Uganda experienced approximately **14 disease outbreaks in 2020–2021**

## 6. Water and Sanitation

- **12 million people** have no access to basic water supply (2024 census)
- **38 million people** have no access to safely managed sanitation
- Schools with water access: **32.5%** only
- Public health facilities with water access: **56.8%** only
- Kampala: fewer than **17%** of health facilities connected to sewer
- Estimated annual sector financing need: **UGX 6.8 trillion** — more than 5× current budget
- Rainwater harvesting tanks: **97%** of samples contaminated with E. coli (recent survey)

**For business plans:** WASH gaps are a market opportunity for WASH enterprises. For other businesses, factor in the cost of providing water on-site (boreholes, tanks) in remote/peri-urban operations.

## 7. Social Protection — Coverage and Financing

| Indicator | Uganda | Comparators |
|---|---|---|
| Population covered by safety nets (2020/21) | **3%** | — |
| Public spending on social assistance (% of GDP) | **0.14%** (FY 2017/18) | Kenya 0.4%; Rwanda 0.3%; LMIC average 1.1% |
| Multidimensional poverty rate | **41.2%** | — |
| Likelihood of achieving SDG 1 (end poverty) | Off-track | — |

### Active Programmes
- **Senior Citizens Grant (SCG):** Cash transfers to individuals aged 80+; approximately 300,000 active beneficiaries
- **NUSAF IV:** Livelihoods, resilience, and social registry; Northern and Eastern Uganda focus; nearly 2.4 million beneficiaries with direct income support under previous phases
- **DRDIP:** Services for refugee-hosting communities; nearly 3 million beneficiaries across 15 districts

### NUSAF 3 Impact Evidence (2016–2021)
- Household savings rate: increased from 54.6% to **70%** during programme
- Livestock ownership: **+45.6%**
- Equipment ownership: **+16%**
- Government emergency fund saved: **51%** (UGX 19 billion) due to improved household adaptive capacity
- 3,460 community assets built (soil/water conservation, rural road rehabilitation)

**For grant proposals:** Reference NUSAF outcomes as evidence base for livelihoods interventions. The 51% emergency fund saving demonstrates concrete fiscal value of social protection.

## 8. Spending Requirements to Hit Vision 2040

### Education
- Education spending must rise from current **2.7% of GDP to ~5% of GDP** (or 20% of government budget) to achieve "improved equilibrium" by 2040
- Infrastructure need: 48,000 additional primary teachers, 35,000 classrooms, 6.2 million textbooks (business-as-usual scenario, 2020–2040)
- New classroom construction need: 6,070 per year throughout the 2020s

### Health
- Health spending must rise from ~2% to **3.4% of GDP** to achieve 90% UHC index score

### Social Protection
- Current spend: <2% of GDP — "significantly below" what is needed for effective coverage

## 9. Market Development Implications for Business Plans

### Consumer Market Growth
The combination of a rapidly growing young population + rising education attainment + urbanisation creates a structurally expanding consumer market in:
- **Food and agribusiness** (large volumes, low-income segments)
- **Housing and construction** (see IFC CPSD: 2 million unit shortage)
- **Financial services** (first-time savers and borrowers entering formal economy)
- **Healthcare and pharmaceuticals** (rising NCD burden, growing middle class)
- **Education and skills training** (TVET gap, private sector must fill public shortfall)
- **Digital services** (urbanising youth cohort, mobile money penetration)

### Public Investment Flow
Uganda's NDP IV and Vision 2040 commitments signal significant government and donor spending in education, health, and social protection infrastructure. Businesses in construction, logistics, pharmaceuticals, and agribusiness that can supply government and development partner procurement are positioned at the intersection of public investment and commercial opportunity.

### Risk Factor
Human capital underinvestment is also a **business risk** — a workforce with 4.3 LAYS requires higher training investment, is more prone to health-related absenteeism, and has lower baseline productivity than regional peers. Build this into HR cost assumptions and operational efficiency projections.

## 10. Using This Data in Business Plans

### Market Analysis Section
- Use HCI (0.39 → 0.56 trajectory) to frame Uganda's development arc and position the business within it
- Use demographic dividend data to establish market size growth drivers
- Cite Vision 2040 GDP targets (US$50B → US$500B) as the macro frame

### Grant Proposals (Problem Statement)
- "Uganda's HCI stands at 0.39 — the country is failing to harness 61% of its human capital potential (World Bank, 2025)"
- NEET youth (5.25 million aged 18–30) provide an evidence base for youth employment grant proposals
- Health outcomes table provides baseline data for health-sector grant proposals
- Social protection coverage (3% of population) substantiates market failure arguments

### HR Planning / Management Section
- Factor in low LAYS (4.3) when estimating training costs for new staff
- Reference teacher salary data (37–42% below national professional average) when benchmarking skill-specific pay
- Use health burden data (malaria, NCDs) for staff welfare and sick leave planning

### Funding Request / Risk Section
- Uganda's underinvestment in education (2.7% vs 4.2% EA average) is a systemic workforce risk — flag it, then show how the business mitigates it through training investment
