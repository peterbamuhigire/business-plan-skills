## 1. EAC Common Market — What It Is

The EAC Common Market was established by the Protocol signed on 20 November 2009 by the five Partner States: Burundi, Kenya, Rwanda, Tanzania, and Uganda. It came into force upon ratification by all Partner States. South Sudan and the Democratic Republic of Congo have since joined the EAC, expanding the market further.

The Common Market provides for seven freedoms and rights (EAC Common Market Protocol, Art. 2):

| Freedom / Right | Legal Basis |
|---|---|
| Free movement of goods | Art. 6 |
| Free movement of persons | Art. 7 |
| Free movement of workers (labour) | Art. 10 |
| Right of establishment | Art. 13 |
| Right of residence | Art. 14 |
| Free movement of services | Art. 16 |
| Free movement of capital | Art. 24 |

**Core principle:** Partner States must not discriminate against nationals of other Partner States on grounds of nationality (Art. 3). Each Partner State must accord to EAC nationals treatment no less favourable than it accords to its own nationals or to third parties.

---

## 2. Free Movement of Goods

Governed by the EAC Customs Union Protocol and the EAC Standardisation, Quality Assurance, Metrology and Testing Act, 2006 (Art. 6). The Common External Tariff means goods circulate within the EAC without internal import duties once customs cleared at the external border.

**Business implication for Uganda SMEs:**
- Goods manufactured in Uganda can be exported to Kenya, Rwanda, Tanzania, Burundi, South Sudan, and DRC without import duties at the EAC border.
- EAC standards (quality certifications, SPS measures) apply — compliance is a prerequisite for regional trade.
- Kenya enforces quality standards at milling level; lead firms in dairy and maize demand EAC-certified inputs from Ugandan suppliers (Daly et al., 2016).

---

## 3. Free Movement of Services

EAC nationals and their companies have the right to supply services across borders through four modes (Art. 16):

1. Cross-border supply (e.g., professional advice from Uganda to a Kenyan client)
2. Consumption abroad (Kenyan client visits Uganda to receive the service)
3. Commercial presence (Ugandan firm opens a branch in Rwanda)
4. Presence of natural persons (Ugandan professional works temporarily in Tanzania)

**National treatment** (Art. 17): a service supplier from another EAC Partner State must be treated no less favourably than a domestic supplier.

**Most Favoured Nation treatment** (Art. 18): EAC suppliers receive treatment at least as good as any third-party country's suppliers.

Partner States may not introduce new restrictions on cross-border service supply by EAC nationals (Art. 16(5)).

**Business implication:** A Uganda-registered professional services firm (consulting, ICT, logistics, engineering, tourism) can legally supply clients in Kenya and Rwanda and open a branch there on the same terms as local firms. Clients cannot be charged discriminatory rates or denied service solely on grounds of the supplier's Ugandan nationality.

## 4. Free Movement of Workers

### 4.1 Core Rights (Art. 10)

EAC citizens are guaranteed the right to work in any Partner State. A Kenyan, Rwandan, Tanzanian, or Burundian national working in Uganda (and vice versa) is entitled to:

- Apply for and accept employment offers
- Move freely within the EAC for employment purposes
- Conclude contracts on the same terms as Ugandan nationals
- Enjoy freedom of association and collective bargaining under Ugandan law
- Access social security benefits on the same basis as Ugandan workers

**Family rights:** A worker's spouse may also take up employment or self-employment in the host country. Children may work subject to host-country age limits (Art. 10(5)).

**Public service exclusion:** These rights do not apply to public service posts unless national law permits (Art. 10(10)).

**Limitations:** A host country may limit free movement on grounds of public policy, public security, or public health — but must notify other Partner States (Art. 10(11–12)).

### 4.2 Work Permit Liberalisation in Practice

All three IOM case-study countries (Kenya, Rwanda, Uganda) have removed work permit fees for EAC Partner State nationals as a concrete step towards implementing free movement of workers (IOM / BRMM, 2022). Uganda specifically waives work permit fees for EAC citizens, making EAC cross-border hiring administratively straightforward compared to hiring from outside the region.

### 4.3 Qualification Recognition

**Mutual Recognition of Qualifications (Art. 11):** Partner States have committed to:
- Mutually recognise academic and professional qualifications, experience, licences, and certifications obtained in other Partner States
- Harmonise curricula, examinations, standards, certification, and accreditation of educational and training institutions

**EAC Qualifications Framework for Higher Education (EAQFHE):** Developed in 2015. It builds on a long history of regional recognition dating to the 1960s East Africa University (campuses in Kampala, Nairobi, and Dar es Salaam) (IOM / BRMM, 2022).

**IGAD Regional Qualification Framework:** Still under development as of 2022 for the broader Horn of Africa.

**Mutual Recognition Agreements (MRAs):** Countries are negotiating MRAs with professional associations at national level. Harmonisation is progressing but remains incomplete — confirm specific profession recognition before hiring or deploying staff across borders.

**Social security portability:** Kenya and Uganda have similar laws that consider migrant workers, but social security systems are not fully integrated across the EAC. Portability of benefits remains an ongoing challenge (IOM / BRMM, 2022).

## 5. Right of Establishment

### 5.1 Core Rights (Art. 13)

EAC nationals may:
- Take up and pursue economic activities as self-employed persons in another Partner State
- Set up and manage economic undertakings (companies, firms) in another Partner State
- Join the host country's social security scheme

**Companies and firms** incorporated under the laws of a Partner State and with their registered office, central administration, or principal place of business there are entitled to non-discriminatory treatment when establishing in another Partner State (Art. 13(6)).

**Mutual recognition of licences:** Licences, permits, and relevant experience obtained in one Partner State must be mutually recognised for the purpose of establishment in another (Art. 13(7)).

**No new restrictions:** Partner States must not introduce new restrictions on establishment and must progressively remove existing ones, including restrictions on:
- Setting up agencies, branches, or subsidiaries of EAC companies (Art. 13(11)(b)(i))
- Entry of personnel of EAC companies into managerial or supervisory roles in branches (Art. 13(11)(b)(ii))

### 5.2 Right of Residence (Art. 14)

Workers and self-employed persons exercising their EAC rights are entitled to residence permits in the host country. Families accompanying them (spouse, children, dependants) also receive residence rights.

## 6. Free Movement of Capital

### 6.1 Core Commitments (Art. 24)

Partner States must:
- Remove restrictions on capital movement by persons resident in the EAC
- Remove discrimination based on nationality, place of residence, or place of investment
- Not introduce new restrictions on capital and related payments/transfers

### 6.2 What "Capital" Covers (Art. 28)

- Direct investment
- Equity and portfolio investments
- Bank and credit transactions
- Payment of interest on loans and amortisation
- Dividends and other income on investments
- Repatriation of proceeds from sale of assets
- Other transfers and payments relating to investment flows

### 6.3 Investment Protection (Art. 29)

EAC Partner States undertake to protect cross-border investments including:
- Full protection and security of investments
- Non-discriminatory treatment (at least as good as domestic investors or third parties)
- In case of expropriation: public purpose, non-discrimination, due process, and prompt reasonable compensation

"Investment" is defined broadly to include enterprises, shares, bonds, derivatives, contracts, intellectual property rights, licences, and tangible/intangible property (Art. 29(4)).

**Business implication:** A Ugandan company investing in Kenya (or vice versa) has treaty-level protection against discriminatory treatment and expropriation without compensation.

## 7. Other Relevant Co-operation Areas

### Tax Harmonisation (Art. 32)

Partner States are harmonising tax policies and laws to facilitate free movement of goods, services, and capital and promote investment. Double-taxation provisions may apply under bilateral agreements — relevant when structuring EAC-wide businesses or receiving income from multiple countries.

### Prohibited Business Practices (Art. 33) and Subsidies (Art. 34)

Anti-competitive practices and prohibited subsidies are banned within the Common Market. This protects EAC businesses from unfair competition by state-backed rivals.

### Intellectual Property Co-operation (Art. 43)

Partner States cooperate to protect IP rights, fight piracy and counterfeits, and promote cultural industries. Register trademarks and patents in Uganda first; consider registration in key EAC markets (Kenya, Tanzania) for brands with regional ambition.

### Industrial Development (Art. 44)

The EAC actively promotes linkages among industries, value addition, product diversification, micro/small/medium enterprises, and indigenous entrepreneurship across the Common Market.

### Agriculture and Food Security (Art. 45)

Partner States cooperate on crop, livestock, fish, and forestry value chains; agro-processing and value addition; market development; seed quality; SPS standards; and an Agricultural Development Fund for credit access by small-scale farmers and agricultural entrepreneurs.

## 8. Regional Value Chains — Sectors with EAC Integration

Research by Daly, Abdulsam & Gereffi (IGC, 2016) studied three value chains in Rwanda and Uganda: dairy processing, maize, and tourism.

### 8.1 Dairy Processing

| Dimension | Finding |
|---|---|
| Primary orientation | Domestic, with emerging regional trade |
| Regional trade | Less than 1% of regional milk production traded formally across borders |
| Lead firm | Brookside Dairy Ltd (Kenya), now present in Uganda |
| Uganda's position | Low-cost producer and exporter of raw milk to Kenyan processors |
| Recent FDI | Amos Dairies Ltd (India) investment in Uganda processing |
| Informal market | High — significant informal cross-border trade |

**Upgrading opportunity for Uganda:** Move from raw-milk exporter to processed-product exporter. Process and organisational upgrades at the farmer–processor level are the priority. EAC SPS standards compliance is the gateway to formal regional markets. A recent Brookside investment in Uganda integrates the company's processing and marketing capabilities with Uganda's lower-cost milk production — a model others can replicate.

### 8.2 Maize

| Dimension | Finding |
|---|---|
| Primary orientation | Regional (Kenya as major consumer) |
| Uganda's position | Large producer and exporter of raw maize |
| Lead firms | Kenyan processors (Unga Limited, Pembe Flour, Nairobi Flour Mills, TSS Grain Millers, others) |
| Aggregation | Ugandan traders (Aponye, Joseph Initiative) aggregate for Kenyan processors |
| Standards | EAC maize quality certification; Kenya enforces at milling level |
| Informal market | High |

**Upgrading opportunity for Uganda:** Increase certification capacity at production and aggregation stages. Warehouse Receipt Systems (both Uganda and Rwanda) improve storage, reduce post-harvest loss, and give farmers access to liquidity from financial institutions. TradeMark East Africa funded a Nakaseke District programme requiring adherence to EAC maize standards with capacity building support (2015).

**Regional integration model:** Uganda's extensive production + Kenya's processing capacity + FDI (Cargill) = potential "strategic slicing" of value chain by comparative advantage across the region.

### 8.3 Tourism

| Dimension | Finding |
|---|---|
| Primary orientation | Global (European and North American consumers) |
| Uganda's position | Gorilla tourism, ecotourism — between Rwanda's luxury segment and Kenya/Tanzania's high-volume safari |
| Lead firms | Global tour operators (Abercrombie & Kent, Cox & Kings, Thomas Cook, &Beyond, Wilderness Safaris) |
| Regional achievement | Common tourist visa covering Kenya, Rwanda, and Uganda |
| EAC platform | East Africa Tourism Platform (within EAC, works with national tourism boards) |

**Upgrading opportunity for Uganda:** Diversify beyond gorilla tourism, develop domestic and regional distribution intermediaries, link inbound tour operators to global lead firms, and participate in the common tourist visa to attract multi-country safari packages.

### 8.4 Key Barriers to EAC Value Chain Integration

Identified by Daly et al. (2016) across all three value chains:

1. **Scale** — Smallholder farmers and small aggregators lack the scale demanded by lead firms
2. **Access to finance** — Low farmer liquidity prevents investment in quality inputs and standards compliance
3. **Human capital** — Many producers are unaware of EAC standards or best practices; marketing skills to link with global buyers are weak
4. **Informal trade dominance** — EAC standards compliance is undermined by high informal market volumes
5. **Weak market signals** — Traders fail to communicate the value of quality compliance back to farmers

## 9. Labour Mobility — Practical Implications for Uganda Businesses

### 9.1 Hiring EAC Nationals

Under the EAC Common Market Protocol:
- **No work permit fees** for EAC nationals working in Uganda (already implemented)
- **No discriminatory terms** — must offer pay and conditions equivalent to Ugandan nationals in like roles
- **EAC qualifications recognised** — degrees, professional licences, and experience from Kenya, Rwanda, Tanzania, Burundi are mutually recognised under EAQFHE (2015) and developing MRAs
- **Spouses may also work** — reduces relocation friction for senior hires from the region

**Practical use cases:**
- Hire a Kenyan food technologist to manage EAC standards compliance for a dairy or maize-processing business
- Recruit a Rwandan hospitality manager with experience in luxury segment operations for a Ugandan lodge
- Bring in a Tanzanian ICT professional on a fixed-term contract for a technology project

**Caution on professional licensing:** MRAs with professional bodies (e.g., engineers, accountants, medical practitioners) are still being negotiated at national level. Verify specific profession recognition with the relevant Uganda professional body before advertising an EAC-wide role.

### 9.2 Deploying Ugandan Staff Regionally

A Ugandan business expanding into Kenya or Rwanda may post staff to manage branches or subsidiaries. Under Art. 13(11)(b)(ii), EAC companies have the right to enter personnel into managerial or supervisory roles in branches in other Partner States.

**Social security:** Uganda NSSF contributions apply to Uganda-based staff. Social security portability across borders is not yet fully resolved — clarify with the host country's relevant authority for long-term postings.

### 9.3 Overlapping Memberships: EAC and IGAD

Uganda and Kenya are members of both the EAC and IGAD (Intergovernmental Authority on Development). The IGAD Free Movement Protocol (FMP) extends free movement to a broader Horn of Africa region (Ethiopia, Somalia, Eritrea, Djibouti, Sudan, South Sudan). Countries with dual memberships can access a wider labour market pool (IOM / BRMM, 2022). However, coordination challenges and duplication of effort arise — Uganda businesses should use the EAC framework as the primary basis for EAC hiring, and monitor IGAD-FMP developments for potential expansion to Horn of Africa talent pools.

**Remittance indicator:** Kenya received USD 3.7 billion in remittances at end 2021, up from USD 2.5 billion in 2019 — demonstrating the scale and resilience of labour migration flows from the EHoA region, even through COVID-19 (IOM / BRMM, 2022).

## 10. Uganda SME Growth Pathways via the EAC Market

### 10.1 Export as Growth Strategy

The EAC Common Market of 300 million+ people (including recent DRC and South Sudan members) is the primary growth market for Uganda businesses ready to scale beyond the domestic market. Key steps:

1. **Achieve EAC standards compliance** — EAC SPS certification, quality marks, and SQMT Act compliance are prerequisites for regional trade
2. **Register IP regionally** — Trademark in Uganda (URSB), then Kenya (KIPI) and Tanzania (BRELA) for brands with regional potential
3. **Target Kenya as first export market** — Largest consumer market in the region; lead firms in dairy, maize, and retail are Kenya-based
4. **Use the common tourist visa** — Tourists with an East Africa Tourist Visa (Kenya, Rwanda, Uganda) are potential customers for Uganda tourism products

### 10.2 Structural Opportunities by Sector

| Sector | Uganda Advantage | EAC Opportunity |
|---|---|---|
| Dairy | Low-cost milk production | Supply Kenyan processors; attract processing FDI |
| Maize / Grain | Large production base | Aggregate, certify, and supply Kenyan millers |
| Tourism | Gorilla/ecotourism unique products | Join multi-country safari packages, MICE (conferences) |
| Agro-processing | Labour cost advantage | Process for EAC-wide distribution |
| ICT / Professional services | Growing talent pool | Cross-border service supply under Art. 16 |
| Light manufacturing | Competitive costs | Export within Common External Tariff area duty-free |

### 10.3 Negotiating as a Regional Block

The IGC research (Daly et al., 2016) specifically recommends that EAC countries negotiate jointly as a "regional block" to attract foreign investment — particularly in dairy, where global US and EU market dynamics create opportunities to bring in leading global firms. Uganda businesses can benefit from EAC-coordinated investment promotion and joint regional standards.

### 10.4 Pitfalls to Navigate

- **Informal market competition:** High volumes of informal cross-border trade in dairy and maize undercut formally-compliant exporters on price. Differentiation on quality and standards is the counter-strategy.
- **Weak institutional support in Uganda:** Compared to Rwanda's proactive PPPs and government-led value chain programmes, Uganda's institutions are less likely to facilitate private sector linkages — businesses must often source market signals, finance, and capacity building independently or through NGOs and TradeMark East Africa.
- **Elite capture risk:** In Uganda's maize seed market and tourism concession sector, powerful stakeholders have blocked reforms (Daly et al., 2016). Market entry strategies must navigate existing power structures.
- **Progressive implementation:** While the EAC Protocol is in force, implementation of specific annexes (qualifications recognition, capital liberalisation schedules) is progressive. Some provisions are still being implemented — verify current status with MTIC (Uganda) or the EAC Secretariat (Arusha) before making major cross-border investment decisions.

## 11. Key EAC Milestones Achieved (as of research date)

From Daly et al. (2016):

1. EAC Common Market operationalised — free movement provisions in force
2. Harmonisation of regional standards for agricultural and food products (dairy, maize)
3. EAC Customs Union and Common External Tariff established
4. Common tourist visa operational for Kenya, Rwanda, and Uganda
