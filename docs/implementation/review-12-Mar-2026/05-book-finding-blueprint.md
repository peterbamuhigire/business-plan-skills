# Book-Finding Blueprint
## Prioritised reading list to close identified gaps

Books are grouped by priority tier, then by topic. For each book: why it belongs in the suite,
which skills it enhances, and what to extract when you read it.

---

## TIER 1 — Critical: Read These First

These close the largest gaps and affect the most skills.

---

### T1.1 — Barbara Minto — *The Pyramid Principle: Logic in Writing and Thinking* (Pearson)

**Why:** Every section in the suite is written bottom-up (evidence → conclusion). McKinsey/Bain/Deloitte write top-down (conclusion → evidence). Minto invented the methodology that all three firms teach their consultants. This one book would upgrade every skill in the suite.

**Skills enhanced:** All skills, but especially 01-executive-summary, 04-market-analysis, 10-financial-projections, 06-competitive-analysis
**New reference file:** `01-executive-summary/references/pyramid-principle.md`
**What to extract:**
- The Pyramid structure (governing thought → key lines → supporting data)
- SCQA (Situation, Complication, Question, Answer) — the opening framework
- Deductive vs. inductive argumentation
- How to structure recommendations and analyses
- Common structural errors (Procrustean bed; false groupings)

---

### T1.2 — Ethan Rasiel — *The McKinsey Way* (McGraw-Hill)

**Why:** Covers McKinsey's problem-solving methodology end-to-end: MECE thinking, hypothesis-driven analysis, issue trees, fact-based analysis, structuring findings. The sister book (*The McKinsey Mind* with Paul Friga) covers the application and client management side.

**Skills enhanced:** 04-market-analysis, 06-competitive-analysis, 12-risk-analysis, meta-market-validation
**New reference file:** `meta-market-validation/references/mckinsey-problem-solving.md`
**What to extract:**
- MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) — definition, examples, how to check
- Issue tree construction — how to decompose a problem into a MECE tree
- Hypothesis-first approach — how to frame analysis as a testable claim
- The 80:20 rule (McKinsey application — find the 20% of facts that drive 80% of insights)
- Fact-based analysis vs. opinion-based analysis
- How McKinsey structures its final presentations

---

### T1.3 — Aswath Damodaran — *The Little Book of Valuation* (Wiley) OR *Investment Valuation* (Wiley)

**Why:** No valuation methodology exists in the suite. Equity investors, DFIs, and any business sale requires valuation. Damodaran is the global authority. *The Little Book* is accessible (200 pages); *Investment Valuation* is the graduate-level reference.

**Skills enhanced:** 11-funding-request, 10-financial-projections; NEW skill: `11c-valuation` or section within 11
**New reference file:** `11-funding-request/references/business-valuation-methods.md`
**What to extract:**
- DCF valuation — terminal value, discount rate (WACC), growth rate assumptions
- Revenue multiple valuation — how sector comparables work
- Pre-revenue startup valuation methods (Berkus, Risk Factor Summation, VC Method)
- Asset-based valuation for collateral-rich businesses
- The relationship between risk and value
- When each method is appropriate (stage of business, data availability)

---

### T1.4 — Brad Feld & Jason Mendelson — *Venture Deals: Be Smarter Than Your Lawyer and VC* (Wiley)

**Why:** The equity investor section of 11-funding-request covers what investors want to see but cannot explain the terms they offer. A founder who does not understand term sheets is at a negotiating disadvantage. This is the definitive plain-English guide.

**Skills enhanced:** 11-funding-request (equity section), 02-company-overview (cap table), 09-management-team (equity compensation)
**New reference file:** `11-funding-request/references/equity-term-sheets.md`
**What to extract:**
- Cap table construction and dilution modelling
- Liquidation preference (participating vs. non-participating)
- Anti-dilution provisions (broad-based weighted average vs. ratchet)
- Pro-rata rights, drag-along, tag-along
- Board composition and control provisions
- Founder vesting (standard 4-year/1-year cliff)
- Convertible notes and SAFEs (pre-seed financing alternatives)
- What terms to fight for and what to concede

---

### T1.5 — Agrawal, Gans & Goldfarb — *Prediction Machines: The Simple Economics of Artificial Intelligence* (HBR Press, 2018)

**Why:** `14-ai-integration` has zero AI-specific book grounding. It currently explains AI automation using BPM books (Dumas, Page) and IT systems books (Dennis et al.). This book provides the economic framework for AI decisions — it is used by Harvard, McKinsey, and Deloitte in their AI strategy work.

**Skills enhanced:** 14-ai-integration (dramatically)
**New reference file:** `14-ai-integration/references/ai-economics-framework.md`
**What to extract:**
- AI = prediction machine (the unifying concept)
- The five components of any AI-powered task (input data, prediction, judgment, action, outcome)
- How falling prediction costs change business strategy
- AI complementarity: what becomes more valuable when AI makes prediction cheap (judgment, data, action)
- When to build AI capability vs. buy AI tools vs. use AI APIs
- Risk of AI in decision-making (when errors are catastrophic vs. recoverable)
- Workflow redesign around AI (not automating existing workflows — redesigning them)

---

### T1.6 — Simon Sinek — *Start with Why* (Portfolio/Penguin)

**Why:** `02-company-overview` is 60 lines with no mission/vision methodology. Sinek's Golden Circle (Why → How → What) is the most widely used mission/vision framework in the world. Every consulting firm and MBA programme teaches it.

**Skills enhanced:** 02-company-overview (dramatically), 01-executive-summary, 09-management-team (founder purpose)
**New reference file:** `02-company-overview/references/mission-vision-why.md`
**What to extract:**
- The Golden Circle — Why (purpose/belief) → How (process/approach) → What (products/services)
- Why most organisations communicate from the outside in (What → How → Why) — and why this is weak
- How to write a genuine Why statement (not a platitude)
- How Why connects to customer loyalty and team motivation
- The Celery Test for strategic coherence
- Tipping point diffusion — how Why-clarity enables early adopter strategy
- Integration with founder purpose (Kaza's framework — cross-reference)

## TIER 2 — High Priority: Major Skill Enhancements

---

### T2.1 — Clayton Christensen — *The Innovator's Dilemma* (HBR Press)

**Why:** Jobs-to-be-Done theory (an offshoot of Christensen's disruption work) is now central to product strategy and competitive analysis in consulting. Understanding disruptive innovation patterns is essential for competitive positioning.

**Skills enhanced:** 03-products-services, 06-competitive-analysis, 04-market-analysis
**What to extract:** Disruptive vs. sustaining innovation distinction; Jobs-to-be-Done framework; low-end vs. new-market disruption; how incumbents fail; identifying disruptive threats in the competitive analysis

---

### T2.2 — Kim & Mauborgne — *Blue Ocean Strategy* (HBR Press)

**Why:** Referenced in `positioning-strategy.md` but not extracted. Blue Ocean is McKinsey-quality strategic positioning — it moves beyond "compete better" to "make competition irrelevant."

**Skills enhanced:** 06-competitive-analysis, 07-marketing-sales-strategy, 03-products-services
**What to extract:** Value innovation vs. competitive benchmarking; ERRC grid (Eliminate/Reduce/Raise/Create); Strategy Canvas; six paths framework; buyer utility map; reaching non-customers

---

### T2.3 — Kaplan & Norton — *The Balanced Scorecard* (HBR Press)

**Why:** The Balanced Scorecard is used by every major consulting firm and DFI for operational KPI design. It integrates financial and non-financial performance. Partially referenced in the suite but not extracted.

**Skills enhanced:** 08-operations-plan, meta-monitoring-evaluation, meta-quarterly-gameplan, 13-implementation-timeline
**What to extract:** Four perspective framework (financial/customer/internal processes/learning & growth); strategy maps; KPI selection methodology; BSC implementation process; linking BSC to compensation

---

### T2.4 — Gene Zelazny — *Say It with Charts: The Executive's Guide to Visual Communication* (McGraw-Hill)

**Why:** McKinsey's internal chart standard. Zelazny was McKinsey's director of visual communications. This book teaches when to use which chart type and how to construct "so what" headlines. Directly upgrades the visual quality of any plan produced from these skills.

**Skills enhanced:** 01-executive-summary, 04-market-analysis, 10-financial-projections
**What to extract:** Chart selection framework (comparison/composition/distribution/relationship/time-series); "so what" headline discipline; horizontal bar vs. column chart rules; waterfall chart construction; common charting errors; slide layout principles

---

### T2.5 — Chopra & Meindl — *Supply Chain Management: Strategy, Planning, and Operation* (Pearson)

**Why:** `08-operations-plan` mentions supply chain but has no reference material for it. Supply chain design (push/pull, EOQ, safety stock, supplier selection) is a core operational topic.

**Skills enhanced:** 08-operations-plan (new supply chain section)
**New reference file:** `08-operations-plan/references/supply-chain-management.md`
**What to extract:** Supply chain drivers framework (facilities/inventory/transportation/information/sourcing/pricing); EOQ and safety stock formulas; push vs. pull supply chain design; supplier evaluation criteria; supply chain risk management; Uganda-specific adaptations (Mombasa corridor, cold chain gaps)

---

### T2.6 — IFC Corporate Governance Toolkit for SMEs (IFC/World Bank publication)

**Why:** `02-company-overview` has no governance framework. The IFC toolkit is specifically designed for growing SMEs in developing markets — it covers board composition, family business governance, audit, disclosure, and shareholder rights.

**Skills enhanced:** 02-company-overview (dramatically), 09-management-team
**New reference file:** `02-company-overview/references/corporate-governance-sme.md`
**What to extract:** Governance evolution as a business grows (from sole trader to board-managed); shareholder agreements essentials; family council structure; audit committee formation; disclosure best practices; conflict of interest management; DFI governance requirements for loan eligibility

---

### T2.7 — John L. Ward — *Keeping the Family Business Healthy* (Jossey-Bass) or Gersick et al. — *Generation to Generation* (HBR Press)

**Why:** Most Uganda SMEs are family businesses. Family business dynamics (sibling rivalry, succession disputes, professionalisation resistance) are not addressed in the suite despite being the #1 governance failure mode in East African SMEs (corroborated by Sardanis's African business data already in the suite).

**Skills enhanced:** 02-company-overview, 09-management-team
**What to extract:** Three-circle model; family council vs. board of directors; succession planning timeline; professionalisation — when to hire outside management; sibling partnership agreement; buy-sell agreement basics; next-generation development; family business in Uganda context

---

### T2.8 — Fridson & Alvarez — *Financial Statement Analysis: A Practitioner's Guide* (Wiley)

**Why:** Bank credit analysts use ratio analysis to assess every loan application. The suite covers DSCR but not the full battery of ratios credit departments run. Understanding how a banker reads financial statements enables better plan construction.

**Skills enhanced:** 10-financial-projections, 11-funding-request, 15-appendices
**New reference file:** `10-financial-projections/references/financial-ratio-analysis.md`
**What to extract:** Liquidity ratios (current, quick, cash); efficiency ratios (receivables days, inventory days, payables days, asset turnover); profitability ratios (EBITDA margin, ROCE, ROE, ROA); leverage ratios (debt/equity, interest coverage); Altman Z-Score components; DuPont decomposition; how banks use ratio analysis in credit assessment; red flags that trigger loan rejection

## Multi-Country Localisation (Architecture, Not Books)

**The user's requirement:** Skills should work for Fiji, Algeria, Canada — any country where the user provides research.

**Action needed (no book required — this is an architecture change):**

1. Create `country-context/template.md` — a structured fill-in-the-blank file covering:
   - Country name, currency, exchange rate to USD
   - GDP, GDP per capita, growth rate, inflation rate
   - Primary regulatory bodies (company registration, tax authority, central bank, sector regulators)
   - Corporate tax rate, VAT/GST rate, payroll tax/social security rates
   - Major banks and lending rates
   - Key data sources (national statistics bureau, central bank, development bank)
   - Country-specific risk factors
   - Labour law basics (minimum wage, notice periods, leave entitlements)

2. Update CLAUDE.md with: "If a `country-context/{country-name}.md` file exists, use it as the regulatory and financial context for all projections. Fall back to Uganda defaults only when no country file is present."

3. Update each skill's generation process to check for country context before generating Uganda-specific content.

**Estimated effort:** 1 session to build the template and update CLAUDE.md. Country files are then built by the user as they research each new market.
