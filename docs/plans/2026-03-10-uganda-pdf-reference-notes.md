## SECTION A: Informal Business and Record-Keeping

### A1 — URA Business Records Requirements (FILE 1, Chapter 12.4)

URA requires **all businesses** to keep business records. A business record is a document that details transactions conducted by a business within a specified period.

**Required record types include:**
- Receipts and invoices
- Contracts
- Bank statements
- Appointment letters
- Utility bills
- Stock records
- Asset registers
- Import schedules
- Income statements
- Balance sheets
- Payroll statements

**Income records** must contain: date of transaction, items sold, quantity, unit price, total amount.

**Expense records** must contain: date of transaction, description of items purchased, quantity, unit price, seller information, buyer information, addresses of both parties.

**Retention period: five years** (or longer if URA proceedings are ongoing).

Records may be kept on physical paper, in electronic form, or both. Records not in English must be translated at the entity's expense (or with Commissioner's permission to use another language).

*Source: Section 12.4 of FILE 1; further detail at https://ura.go.ug/en/business-records/*

### A2 — Importance of Record Keeping (FILE 1, Chapter 12.4.1)

Effective record-keeping is crucial for:
- Determining whether the business is making profits, losses, or breaking even
- Tracking business expenses and ensuring fair tax payments
- Evaluating performance when income is earned from multiple sources
- Facilitating business budgeting and providing proof for financing (e.g., bank loans)
- Serving as basis for fair tax assessments, audits, and objections

### A3 — Informal Sector Scale (FILE 2, UNBS Interview)

**52% of Uganda's businesses** operate informally (UNBS Executive Director, FILE 2). UNBS's new five-year strategic plan targets tackling this high informality rate. The UNBS Digital Conformity Marking programme lowers certification costs **from $500 per batch to $0.27 per product**.

### A4 — SACCO and Microfinance Pathways (FILE 2, Salaam Bank / Finance sector)

Uganda has many SACCOs with greater reach than formal institutions. Salaam Bank (Islamic banking, launched March 2024) works with SACCO groups: "if they produce commodities with UGX 10M, we might help them scale up to UGX 50M."

**Financial inclusion reached 81% in 2023** (FinScope Uganda Survey, July 2024). National Financial Inclusion Strategy (NFIS) 2023-28 targets at least **85% financial inclusion by 2028**.

**dfcu Foundation credit linkages (2018-24):** Over $22.8M in credit linkages for 4,950 smallholder farmers and 104 enterprises.

### A5 — Taxpayer Register Expansion Programme (FILE 2, URA Interview)

URA's **Taxpayer Register Expansion Programme (TREP)** — in collaboration with URSB, KCCA, and local governments — established one-stop centres where all business registration can be handled. The tax register has grown from 1.3M taxpayers a decade ago to **over 4.5M taxpayers** (FILE 2).

Domestic taxes now contribute **about 60% of revenue**, up from 40% previously.

## SECTION B: Self-Employment and Sole Trader Guidance

### B1 — Definition of Self-Employment and Traders (FILE 1, Chapters 5.1, 5.4)

**Self-employment**: A situation where an individual works for themselves rather than being employed. Self-employed individuals manage their own businesses or work as freelancers, contractors, or consultants.

**Trader**: An individual or entity that buys and sells, financial instruments, commodities, goods or services with the intention of earning a profit. Traders are considered self-employed.

**Examples in Uganda**: rolex makers (street food vendors), women who sell vegetables, boda boda riders (motorcycle taxi operators), shop owners, clothes sellers.

**Categories of traders:**
- Professional services (lawyers, accountants, doctors, architects/engineers, IT consultants) — governed by specific regulatory bodies such as Law Council and ICPA
- Non-professional services (artisans, transporters, cleaning services, market vendors, graphic designers)
- Traders dealing in goods (wholesalers, retailers, commodity dealers)

### B2 — Sole Proprietorship Structure (FILE 1, Chapter 7.1 and Step 1)

A sole proprietor can operate under a business name with a trading licence issued at the district level. This requires less initial capital — registration and compliance costs are lower.

**Sole proprietor characteristics:**
- **Unlimited liability** — personal assets are at risk if business incurs debt
- Fewer reporting and compliance requirements (lighter administrative burden)
- Can operate without registering a company, using Business Names Registration Act Cap 105
- Simpler to start; expanding later to include investors may involve additional paperwork

### B3 — Foreign Nationals and Refugees as Sole Traders (FILE 1, Chapters 5.2, 5.3)

**Foreign nationals including refugees** can run small businesses in Uganda without registering as a company. Under the Business Names Registration Act Cap 105, they can register as sole proprietors.

Refugees have the right to engage in income-generating activities and operate small businesses under the Refugees Act Cap 312 (Section 29(e)), under the same conditions as other foreign nationals. Refugees may register companies and act as shareholders, provided they have a valid Refugee ID.

Certain sectors — commercial agriculture, petroleum, and mining — are restricted/reserved for Ugandan citizens or companies at least 51% Ugandan-owned.

### B4 — NSSF for Self-Employed Individuals (FILE 1, Chapter 11)

**NSSF contributions for employers:**
- Employer must deduct **5% from employee's gross monthly wage**
- Plus add **10% of gross monthly wage** (employer contribution)
- Total: **15% per employee**
- Payment due by **15th day of the following month**

**Self-employed individuals**: May **voluntarily register for NSSF** and make contributions (Section 7(a) of NSSF Amendment Act). This enables retirement savings and other benefits even though registration is mandatory only for employers.

**NSSF registration requirements for individuals:**
- Valid National ID or passport
- Email address
- Telephone number

**For companies**: Business Registration Number or TIN. NSSF clearance can be applied for online at https://eservices.nssfug.org/ecertificate/

**NSSF benefits available:**
- Age Benefit: paid at retirement age 55
- Midterm Benefit: at age 45+ after 10 years of contributions (up to 20% of accrued benefits)
- Invalidity Benefit: for loss of earning capacity
- Survivors Benefit: to relatives of deceased member
- Withdrawal Benefit: at age 50 after one year out of employment
- Emigration Benefit: for members leaving Uganda permanently

**NSSF penalties**: Non-compliance — fine not exceeding 500 currency points (1 currency point = UGX 20,000 as of December 2024, i.e., max fine = UGX 10M), plus up to six months imprisonment, or both.

### B5 — Sole Proprietor vs Company Comparison (FILE 1, Chapter 7)

| Feature | Sole Proprietor | Company (LLC) |
|---------|-----------------|---------------|
| Liability | Unlimited (personal assets at risk) | Limited (personal assets protected) |
| Registration | Business name + trading licence | Certificate of Incorporation via URSB/OBRS |
| Compliance | Light (fewer reporting obligations) | Stricter (annual returns, audits) |
| Setup cost | Lower | Higher (legal fees, share capital) |
| Investor-ready | Difficult (requires restructuring) | More suitable |
| Tax regime | Presumptive tax if turnover < UGX 150M | Corporate income tax 30% |
| Foreign ownership | Possible under BNA Cap 105 | 100% foreign ownership allowed |

### B6 — Business Registration Time (FILE 2, URSB Interview)

URSB Registrar General: In 2020, registering a business required around 14 working days. Through digitisation and automation, the process now takes **2 hours for legal document registrations** and **4–6 hours for new registrations** (FILE 2).

## SECTION C: Small Business Start-Up Practical Guidance

### C1 — The 7-Step Registration Process (FILE 1, Chapter 3)

**STEP 1: Choose business structure**
Decide whether to operate as a sole trader with a trading licence, or register a business/company/partnership. Factors: desired structure, scale, industry regulations.

**STEP 2: Complete registration**
- Platform: **Online Business Registration System (OBRS)** at https://obrs.ursb.go.ug/
- Name reservation, then Certificate of Registration (sole proprietor/partnership) or Certificate of Incorporation (company)
- Company types: Limited Liability Company (LLC), Single Member Company (SMC), Public Limited Company (PLC), Company Limited by Guarantee

**STEP 3: Register for TIN and VAT**
- Every person engaged in income-generating activities must register for a **TIN (Tax Identification Number)**
- TIN is a 10-digit unique number, issued **free of charge**
- **VAT registration mandatory** if annual taxable supplies exceed UGX 150,000,000 (VAT rate: 18%)
- Portal: https://ura.go.ug/en/domestic-taxes/tin-application/

**STEP 4: Register for NSSF (if employing staff)**
Every employer must register with NSSF. Self-employed may register voluntarily.

**STEP 5: Register for a Trading Licence**
Required for any person selling goods and services (exemptions under Section 8(2) Trade Licensing Act).
- In Kampala: KCCA (Kampala Capital City Authority)
- Outside Kampala: local municipal or town councils
- Processing time: **7 to 14 working days** after submission
- Requirements: business registration documents, TIN, proof of address, ID documents, proof of fee payment

**STEP 6: Open a bank account**
Banks may require refugee ID or National ID, plus certified copies of registration documents.

**STEP 7: Obtain business-specific approvals**
Depending on nature of business:
- **NEMA** (National Environment Management Authority): for manufacturing, mining, construction, petrol stations, wetland operations — Environmental Impact Assessment may be required
- **UNBS** (Uganda National Bureau of Standards): for manufacturers and importers of goods — quality/safety certification
- **Ministry of Health**: for food, pharmaceutical, health-related businesses
- **Ministry of Trade, Industry and Cooperatives**: see https://www.businesslicences.go.ug/

### C7 — Taxation Framework Summary (FILE 1, Chapter 12)

**VAT:**
- Standard rate: **18%** (applicable to most goods and services)
- Zero-rated: exports and specific goods/services
- Exempt: financial, educational, and medical services
- Registration threshold: annual taxable supplies ≥ **UGX 150 million**

**Corporate Income Tax:**
- Resident companies: **30%** of taxable income
- Non-resident companies: **30%** on Uganda-sourced income

**Presumptive Tax Regime (Small Business):**
- For businesses with turnover **< UGX 150 million**
- Simplified/flat tax — no deductions allowed
- Designed to ease compliance burden for small businesses

**Income Tax Return Threshold:**
- Individuals earning > **UGX 2,820,000 annually** (excluding employees with a single employer) must file a return

**Filing deadlines:**
- Annual returns (e.g., Income Tax): within **6 months** from end of financial period
- Monthly returns (VAT, WHT, PAYE, LED): due **15th of following month**

**Tax penalties:**
- Late filing: **UGX 200,000** or **2% of tax liability**, whichever is higher
- Late income tax payment: **2% interest** (capped at principal + penalties)
- Non-compliance with EFRIS (Electronic Fiscal Receipting and Invoicing): **UGX 8,000,000/month** for failure to use EFRIS; **UGX 6,000,000/month** for not issuing e-receipts/invoices
- Late VAT return: fine up to **UGX 300,000** or up to 6 months imprisonment, or both
- Late filing extension permitted: up to **90 days** (does not change payment due date)

**East African context (FILE 2, URA Interview):** Uganda's VAT rate of 18% aligns with Rwanda, Tanzania, and Burundi. Kenya's VAT is 16%. Corporate income tax is generally 30% throughout the region. EAC common external tariff means zero import duty for goods originating within the region.

## SECTION D: Grant Funding and Development Partner Support

### D1 — Parish Development Model (PDM) (FILE 2, Economy & Investment section)

The **Parish Development Model (PDM)** aims to transform subsistence households at the parish level into economic actors. Targets **3.5 million households** for integration into commercial activity.

**Five PDM pillars:**
1. Production
2. Infrastructure
3. Financial inclusion
4. Mindset change
5. Localised decision-making

**Parish SACCOs**: PDM uses parish-level SACCOs to boost credit access and enterprise development. Each parish can access government funding through its SACCO for enterprise development.

**Agriculture linkage (FILE 2, Industrialisation section):** PDM aims to increase agriculture's growth rate from 3.8% (2017/18) to 6% (2024/25). Parish-based pilot projects for dairy, animal feed, and mechanised agriculture.

**UNBS linkage (FILE 2):** UNBS will collaborate with cooperatives and PDM to align agriculture with global standards (e.g., GlobalG.A.P. certification).

**Sharia-compliant PDM**: Salaam Bank in discussion with government regarding sharia-compliant PDM approach.

**About 33% of households** remain outside the money economy (down from 39% in 2019-20) (FILE 2).

### D2 — Emyooga Initiative (FILE 2, Economy & Investment section)

The **Emyooga Initiative**, launched in 2019, targets **specialised enterprise groups** ranging from:
- Boda bodas
- Market vendors
- Tailors
- Mechanics

Provides **financial inclusion and vocational support** to previously excluded groups. Aims to shift over two-thirds of homesteads from subsistence to market-oriented production.

### D3 — Presidential Skilling Programs (FILE 2, Economy & Investment section)

**Presidential Skilling Programs** focus on practical trades:
- Carpentry
- Welding
- Tailoring
- Baking

Leverages regional industrial hubs to equip youth with job-ready skills. **Over 12,000 trainees benefit annually** from these initiatives.

### D4 — dfcu Foundation FEAT and BAP Programmes (FILE 2, dfcu Foundation interview)

**dfcu Foundation** (previously Agribusiness Development Centre, rebranded 2025). Active since 2017.

**Five-year strategy (2025–29):**
- Target: impact **100,000 beneficiaries** (60% women, 40% youth)
- Two pillars: enterprise development + expanding financial access

**FEAT — Financial Expansion for Agricultural Transformation:**
- Co-funded with Rabo Foundation
- Focuses on four agricultural value chains: dairy and livestock, coffee, cereals, and oilseeds
- Supports farmers improving key agricultural value chains

**BAP — Business Acceleration Programme:**
- Targets trade and business sectors
- Provides mentorship, training, networking, and funding access

**SOMA platform**: Free online business training platform for entrepreneurs.

**Impact to date (2018–24):**
- Supported 1,281 enterprises (52.6% women-owned)
- Trained 59,707 learners
- Facilitated over **$22.8M in credit linkages** for 4,950 smallholder farmers and 104 enterprises

**Catalytic Fund**: Introducing revolving micro-loans for women entrepreneurs.

## SECTION E: Plan Assembly and Submission

### E1 — What Banks Require for Business Loans

From FILE 1 (Tax / Record-keeping sections) and FILE 2 (Finance interviews):

**Records banks require:**
- Business registration documents (Certificate of Registration or Certificate of Incorporation)
- TIN (Tax Identification Number)
- Financial statements — income statements and balance sheets
- Bank statements (3–6 months, sometimes 90 days minimum)
- Tax compliance / Tax Clearance Certificate (URA)
- Proof of address
- National ID or Refugee ID

**Housing Finance Bank (FILE 2):** Only 20% of Uganda's landowners possess formal land titles. Community organisations and associations have played a pivotal role in securing financing without formal collateral (Incremental Housing Loan model — nearly 6,000 customers, UGX 40B disbursed since 2022).

**Bank of Uganda – UEDCL transition**: Moving from private concessionaire (20% ROI model) to state-controlled distribution, with government providing $50M in funding for the first year.

**Bank of Baroda (FILE 2):** Loan book grew from UGX 1,111.89B (Dec 2022) to UGX 1,370B (Sept 2024) to UGX 1,450B (current). Over 20% of loans allocated to agriculture and manufacturing sectors.

**Credit approval times**: Since Financial Institutions Act amendment (effective May 9, 2024), credit approvals extended from 15–20 days to approximately **six weeks** due to new full board approval requirements.

### E2 — Business Registration Documents for Plan Submission

For a business plan submission to a bank or investor, the following registration documents are typically required (drawn from FILE 1):

1. **Business Name Registration Certificate** (sole proprietors/partnerships) or **Certificate of Incorporation** (companies)
2. **TIN certificate** from URA
3. **Trading Licence** from KCCA (Kampala) or local council (upcountry)
4. **NSSF clearance certificate** (if employing staff)
5. **Tax Clearance Certificate** from URA (for government contracts; confirms no outstanding tax liabilities)
6. **NEMA permit** (if applicable — manufacturing, construction, wetland operations)
7. **UNBS certification** (if manufacturing or importing goods)
8. **Health permits** (food, pharmaceutical, health businesses)

*Note: Tax Clearance Certificate is valid for a specific period and must be renewed regularly.*

**Export targets**: Elevate share of exports in GDP from 15% (FY 2022/23) to 50% by 2040; increase manufactured goods share in exports from 13% to 50%.

### F4 — Coffee Sector (FILE 2)

- Uganda earns about **$1.2B from coffee** sold at ~$2.5/kg to international buyers; buyers in Italy, Germany, USA pay $40/kg (Inspire Africa Group)
- Coffee exports: **$900M–$990M annually** (cross-referenced: Minister of Trade cites $900M, OEC data cites $990M)
- Goal: triple coffee earnings to **$5B by 2030** (Inspire Africa Group)
- Target farm productivity: 3 kg of Fair Average Quality coffee per tree by 2028 (Rubanga Cooperative, currently 1.7 kg up from 0.6 kg)
- Funzo Coffee export volume: grew from 640 tonnes (2019) to over **5,000 tonnes (2025)**
- Rubanga Cooperative: 10,578 members, 23,000 farmers; exported **2,100 tonnes** to Europe, South Africa, South Korea in 2024
- **EU is a key destination for 67% of Uganda's coffee** (EUDR risk: effective December 31, 2025)
- Inspire Africa Coffee Park: 120 acres in Ntungamo; projected **15,000 tonnes/year**; total investment $150M (government holds 35% stake via $46M invested)
- **Vanilla**: Uganda contributes ~10% of global vanilla supply; two annual harvests; vanillin content 4.5% (global average 2.3%); 2024 production surged to 604 tonnes; potential net income ~$3,000 per acre intercropped

### F5 — Dairy Sector (FILE 2, President interview and Pearl Dairy)

- Uganda currently produces **5.4 billion litres of milk** but internal market absorbs only ~**800 million litres**
- WHO recommends 210 litres per person per year; Uganda's population requires >9B litres annually — indicating structural undercomsumption
- **Pearl Dairy Farms (Lato)**: 550,000 litres/day current production, expanding to 800,000 litres/day; operates in 12+ African countries; serves 300,000+ distribution points daily; works with 15,000 smallholder farmers in Uganda and Kenya; established 2009

### F6 — Steel Sector (FILE 2, Tembo Steels)

- Tembo Steels: Uganda's only primary steel producer; plants in Iganga and Lugazi; 100% made in Uganda using virgin iron ore
- DRI (Direct Reduced Iron) plant: fully automated, Level 2, PLC and SCADA systems
- Steel consumption projected to grow **5–6%** with 1–2% increase in demand per year
- **Raw materials and power tariffs = 90% of total manufacturing costs** (major competitiveness constraint)
- Every 2 jobs in primary steel = 13 more jobs throughout supply chain
- Steel products: TMT rebars (first in Uganda 2004), 5mm wire rod (first in continent 2014), lightest structural sections (first in world 2007), 0.7mm hot rolled sheet (first in world 2008)

### F7 — Sugar Sector (FILE 2, Modern Group and Mehta Group)

- Sugar production exceeding **600,000 tonnes annually**, including 90,000 tonnes industrial sugar
- Mehta Group (Kakira Sugar): 100 years of operation; 9,000 employees, 36,000 dependents; 30-MW power generation plant inaugurated 2024
- Kaliro (Modern Group): crushing 2,500 tonnes cane/day, producing ~250 tonnes sugar/day
- Modern Sugar (Kidera): opening 2025, planned crushing capacity **10,000 tonnes cane/day**, **1 million tonnes sugar/year** — set to be **largest sugar mill in East Africa**
- Sugar Amendment Bill 2023 (signed May 2025): new stakeholder council, millers to share proceeds with farmers, sugar development fund (70% millers / 30% outgrowers)

### F8 — Oil and Gas Sector (FILE 2, Energy section)

**Four flagship projects:**
1. **Kingfisher** (CNOOC Uganda): 4 oilfields, 30+ wells planned, total investment ~$2B; 60% project complete; peak production ~**40,000 bpd**; 13 of 31 wells drilled
2. **Tilenga** (TotalEnergies): 6 oilfields, 400+ wells planned, total investment ~$5B; 48% complete; peak production ~**190,000 bpd**; 107 of 180 wells for first oil drilled
3. **EACOP** (East African Crude Oil Pipeline): 1,443 km, 24-inch diameter, Hoima to Tanga; cost $5B; TotalEnergies 62%, CNOOC 8%, Tanzania PDC 15%, UNOC 15%; 62% complete; 70km+ connected on Uganda side, 200km+ Tanzania side; land acquisition 98%
4. **Refinery** (Kabalega Industrial Park, Hoima): initial 60,000 bpd, expandable to 120,000 bpd; estimated cost $3–4B; UNOC 40%, Alpha MBM Investments 60%; implementation agreement signed April 2025; 15–20% ROI projection

**First oil expected: 2026 or early 2027** (adjusted from earlier 2025 target due to financing delays).

**Local content:** Oil and gas directly employs ~17,000 people (90% Ugandans); 150,000 indirect/induced jobs; total ~200,000 jobs. $2.2B of total $5.4B in contracts awarded by end-2024 to local businesses.

**Electricity target**: President committed to reducing electricity cost to **$0.05/kWh**. Current generation: 2,051.6 MW (93% hydropower). Target: **52,000 MW by 2040** (28,000 MW solar/bioenergy/gas + 24,000 MW nuclear).

**Regional electricity revenues (UETCL):** ~$1M/month from Rwanda, $600K–$1.5M from Kenya, $300K–$600K from Tanzania = ~**$2M monthly revenue** from regional sales.

**LPG/clean cooking**: $638M World Bank loan over 4 years from 2025; clean cooking subsidies of 30–40%.

## SECTION G: Financial Stress Test Reference Data

### G1 — Exchange Rate Risk (CLAUDE.md + FILE 2 context)

- Official rate at time of compilation: approximately **UGX 3,550/$1** (Q1 2025/26, UBOS KEI)
- **Conservative planning rate: UGX 3,700/$1** (to account for exchange-rate depreciation risk)
- 50% of Ugandan business involves foreign currency (Yako Bank interview, FILE 2)
- Dollar reserves globally have fallen from 70% to under 40% in 20 years — risk of USD appreciation affecting import costs

### G2 — Production Cost Pressures (FILE 2, Manufacturing interviews)

- **Raw materials + power tariffs = 90% of total manufacturing costs** (Tembo Steels) — primary stress point for manufacturers
- Electricity cost target: $0.05/kWh (government commitment); current costs described as "considerably higher than international market"
- Umeme operated on 20% ROI model; transition to UEDCL aims to reduce cost and improve reliability
- Government provided $50M for first year of UEDCL operation

### G3 — Interest Rate Environment (FILE 2, Finance section)

- High interest rates primarily driven by government borrowing costs
- Uganda banking sector profits rose 16% in 2024 to **$420M** (5 major banks)
- Government turned down $414M loan at 8.4% interest rate in 2024, choosing instead to reduce expenditure
- Oil production (2026/27) could save **$300M–$400M** on petroleum expenses, potentially reducing borrowing needs and interest rates

### G4 — Inflation and Input Cost Risk

- Uganda blending cassava flour with wheat at 70/30 ratio could reduce $120M wheat import bill by 30% (TUA interview)
- 20–30% post-harvest loss on fruits and vegetables (Avotein/AgriMove initiative)
- Key commodity price context: coffee exported at $2.5/kg, global buyers pay $40/kg — value-addition opportunity and price volatility risk

### G5 — Fiscal Context (FILE 2, MoFPED Interview)

- Government implementing **fiscal consolidation strategy** from FY 2025/26
- Reducing reliance on external borrowing; 98% reduction target
- Saved over **UGX 1.7 trillion** by rationalising expenses on travel, workshops, frequent purchases
- RAPEX programme (Rationalisation of Government Agencies) expected to save at least **UGX 1 trillion annually**
- Domestic revenues: UGX 37.55 trillion (51.9% of budget), reducing dependence on external funding

### G6 — Sector-Specific Risks Noted in FILE 2

**Agriculture:**
- Post-harvest losses, quality control, inadequate rural infrastructure constrain growth
- Climate change: shifting rainfall patterns observed (Mehta Group sugarcane)
- EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) compliance risk for coffee sector (effective December 31, 2025); EU represents 67% of Uganda's coffee market

**Manufacturing:**
- High production costs due to unreliable electricity
- High price of imported inputs
- Logistical bottlenecks

**Oil and gas:**
- Infrastructure for mineral transportation underdeveloped
- Environmental/social concerns from NGOs
- Financing delays pushed first oil from 2025 to 2026/2027

**Uganda Railway Corporation (FILE 2):**
- Operating ratio: **$2.50 spent for every $1 earned** (unsustainable)
- Currently transports 250,000 tonnes annually; needs 550,000 tonnes to break even
- **$409M maintenance backlog**

### H4 — Plan Assembly Notes (Legal, Compliance, Filing)

**Consumer protection**: Consumer Protection Act 2022 and Competition Act 2024 — businesses must disclose all material facts, provide goods of reasonable quality, refrain from deceptive advertising.

**Dispute resolution:**
- Commercial Division of High Court: business/commercial disputes
- Arbitration and Conciliation Act Cap 5: ADR (arbitration, mediation) — faster and less formal
- Small Claims Procedure: for disputes involving amounts **< UGX 10 million**, under Judicature (Small Claims Procedure) Rules 2011, no legal representation required

**IP protection:**
- Trademark: 7-year initial registration, renewed every 10 years
- Copyright: immediate automatic protection; registration recommended for evidence of ownership; UGX 50,000 application fee
- Patent: novelty, inventive step, industrial application required; UGX 100,000 application + UGX 150,000 examination + UGX 90,000 grant

**NGO/CBO notes**: NGOs must demonstrate charitable purpose, file annual returns, provide detailed reports on funding and activities. CBO registration at Sub-County level; CBOs cannot sue/be sued unless also registered as company limited by guarantee.
