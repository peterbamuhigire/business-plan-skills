# Financial Stress Test Methodology

*A stress test that changes revenue by −20% without justifying why −20% is the correct shock is not credible. This reference file provides calibrated shock magnitudes from Uganda's actual economic history, sector-specific vulnerability data, and a formal scenario construction methodology.*

---

## 1. Scenario Construction Framework

Every stress test must produce four scenarios — not three. The four-scenario model gives lenders confidence that the business has been honestly tested.

| Scenario | Definition | Revenue Adjustment | Cost Adjustment | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Optimistic** | Favourable conditions: strong demand, stable costs, no disruptions | +20 to +30% vs. base | −5% (efficiency gains) | Shows upside for investor; must be realistic, not fantasy |
| **Base case** | Most likely outcome: moderate assumptions, normal operating conditions | 0% (the projection as written) | 0% | The primary business case |
| **Pessimistic** | Adverse but plausible: weaker demand, cost pressures, minor disruptions | −15 to −25% | +10 to +15% | Tests whether business survives a bad year |
| **Extreme / Tail risk** | Severe shock: major disruption, sector crisis, macro shock | −35 to −50% | +20 to +30% | Tests survival threshold; identifies minimum rescue point |

**Rule:** The pessimistic scenario must be grounded in a plausible real-world event, not an arbitrary percentage. Use the Uganda historical shock data in Section 2 to justify your shock magnitude.

## 2. Uganda Historical Shock Data

Use these actual shocks to calibrate scenario assumptions. Every shock listed below has happened to Ugandan businesses in the past 10 years.

### Shock 1: COVID-19 Lockdown (2020–2021)

**National impact:** Uganda GDP growth fell from +6.7% (2019) to −1.1% (2020) — a 7.8 percentage-point swing. First recession in 30 years.

**Sector-specific impacts:**

| Sector | Revenue Impact | Recovery Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| Tourism/hospitality | −80 to −95% (border closures, curfews) | 3+ years |
| Aviation/transport | −70 to −85% | 2 years |
| Retail (non-essential) | −40 to −60% during lockdowns | 6–12 months |
| Food and grocery retail | −10 to +20% (essential goods surge) | Immediate |
| Agribusiness (domestic food) | −5 to +10% | Immediate |
| Coffee/export agriculture | −15 to −25% (logistics disruptions) | 12–18 months |
| Construction | −30 to −50% (supply chains, permits frozen) | 12–24 months |
| Manufacturing (consumer goods) | −20 to −40% | 6–18 months |
| Financial services | −10 to −20% (NPLs rose; BoU allowed restructuring) | 18 months |
| Digital services / e-commerce | +30 to +100% (demand surge) | Sustained |

**Stress test use:** For any hospitality, tourism, events, or non-essential retail business: the tail-risk scenario should model a revenue decline of 80%+ for 3–6 months (full lockdown equivalent). The pessimistic scenario should model 30–40% decline (partial operating restrictions).

### Shock 2: Exchange Rate Depreciation (2021–2023)

**Magnitude:** UGX depreciated from approximately UGX 3,500/$ (early 2021) to UGX 3,800/$ (late 2022) — approximately 8–9% depreciation over 18 months. UGX further weakened to approximately UGX 3,700–3,800 range through 2024–2025.

**Business impact:**
- Import-dependent businesses: input costs rose 8–15% over 18 months
- Businesses with USD debt: repayment costs rose proportionally
- Coffee exporters: partially offset by higher USD revenue converted to more UGX
- Businesses pricing in UGX but sourcing in USD: margin compression of 8–15%

**Stress test calibration:**
- Pessimistic exchange rate scenario: UGX 4,200/$ (+14% depreciation from UGX 3,700 base)
- Extreme scenario: UGX 4,800/$ (+30% depreciation)
- Impact on import-dependent businesses: multiply imported input cost line by exchange rate change factor

---

### Shock 3: Global Fuel Price Shock (2022)

**Magnitude:** Global crude oil prices rose from ~$75/barrel (2021 average) to $120+ (June 2022 peak). In Uganda, pump prices for petrol rose approximately 40–50% between January and August 2022.

**Business impact in Uganda:**
- Transport and logistics costs: +30 to +45% for any business with delivery
- Generator fuel costs: +40 to +50% (critical for businesses off the UMEME grid)
- Agricultural inputs (fertiliser, chemicals): +20 to +35% (petrochemical-linked)
- Matatu/boda-boda freight costs: +25 to +40%

**Stress test calibration:**
- Any business with fuel/transport as >10% of operating cost: apply +40% fuel cost shock in pessimistic scenario
- Generator-dependent businesses: budget an additional UGX 800,000–1,500,000/month at peak fuel prices

---

### Shock 4: LGBTQ Law Economic Fallout (2023)

**Uganda Anti-Homosexuality Act signed May 2023.**

**Business impact:**
- USAID, World Bank suspended certain direct budget support
- Several international companies froze Uganda expansion plans (Barclays, others)
- Tourism: decline in international leisure tourists from Western markets (~15–20% for some segments)
- NGO/grant sector: funding from US government sources significantly reduced
- Foreign direct investment: some announced investments deferred

**Stress test use:** For businesses dependent on Western donor funding, international tourists, or multinational corporate clients: include geopolitical / regulatory risk as a scenario factor. Pessimistic scenario: −20% revenue from international clients.

---

### Shock 5: Coffee Price Cycle

Uganda is the second-largest coffee exporter in Africa. Coffee price volatility directly affects:
- Coffee farmers and cooperatives
- Coffee processors and exporters
- Banks with exposure to coffee sector
- Input suppliers to coffee farming

**Historical price range (ICO robusta benchmark):**
- Low (2018–2019): ~80–100 US cents/lb
- Recovery (2020–2021): ~90–120 US cents/lb
- Peak (2021–2022): ~200+ US cents/lb (supply disruptions)
- Normalisation (2023–2024): ~120–160 US cents/lb

**Stress test calibration for coffee businesses:**
- Pessimistic: commodity price −30% from base assumption
- Extreme: commodity price −50% from base assumption
- Note: price decline often coincides with exchange rate depreciation (offsetting effect for exporters)

---

### Shock 6: Regional Security Disruptions

East Africa faces periodic security disruptions affecting logistics and business confidence:
- DRC eastern conflict: disrupts trade via western Uganda corridor
- Somalia/Al-Shabaab: affects northern Kenya/Mombasa port, impacts Uganda import routes
- Sudan crisis: disrupts northeast trade routes

**Business impact:** For import-dependent businesses using Mombasa port:
- Shipping delays: +7–21 additional days in transit
- Freight cost increase: +15–25% during disruptions
- Insurance cost increase: +10–20% for high-risk-route cargo

**Stress test use:** Supply chain disruption scenario: working capital requirements increase by 20–30% to cover higher inventory (longer lead times); freight costs +20%.

## 3. Sector-Specific Shock Factors

Use these calibrated factors when building scenarios for specific industries:

### Agriculture / Agribusiness

| Risk Factor | Pessimistic Magnitude | Extreme Magnitude | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rainfall failure (drought) | −20 to −35% crop yield | −50 to −70% crop yield | El Niño years: every 4–7 years |
| Commodity price decline | −25 to −30% | −40 to −50% | 2–3 year cycles |
| Pest/disease outbreak (FAW, armyworm) | −15 to −25% | −40 to −60% | Every 2–3 years in some regions |
| Export ban (fish, maize, etc.) | −40 to −70% for affected product | −100% | Periodic; government policy-driven |
| Input cost inflation (fertiliser) | +25 to +40% | +60 to +80% | Follows fuel and global markets |

| Geopolitical/reputational event | −15 to −20% (international) | −30 to −40% |

## 4. Breakeven Sensitivity Table

Every stress test must include a breakeven sensitivity table showing at what revenue level the business goes cash-flow negative:

```
BREAKEVEN SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS

Fixed costs per month (from §10): UGX ___________
Variable cost ratio (COGS / Revenue): ___________%
Contribution margin per UGX of revenue: ___________ (1 − variable cost ratio)
Break-even revenue per month: Fixed costs ÷ Contribution margin = UGX ___________

REVENUE SENSITIVITY:

Revenue Level | Monthly Net Cash Flow | Months of Cash Runway | Status
100% of plan  |      UGX [X]          |   > 12 months         | Safe
90% of plan   |      UGX [X]          |   X months            |
80% of plan   |      UGX [X]          |   X months            |
70% of plan   |      UGX [X]          |   X months            |
60% of plan   |      UGX [X]          |   X months            |
50% of plan   |      UGX [X]          |   X months            | ← Likely cash-flow negative
Break-even %  |           0           |   N/A                 | Minimum viable

CONCLUSION: The business goes cash-flow negative if revenues fall below ___% of plan
for more than ___ consecutive months.
```

## 5. Formal Scenario Assumption Table

Every scenario must have explicit assumptions documented. Vague scenarios ("things go badly") are not credible.

```
SCENARIO ASSUMPTION TABLE

Assumption | Optimistic | Base Case | Pessimistic | Extreme
---|---|---|---|---
Revenue growth (Year 1) | +30% | [as projected] | −20% | −45%
Revenue growth (Year 2) | +35% | [as projected] | −10% | −30%
Gross margin | +5pp | [as projected] | −8pp | −15pp
Fixed costs | −5% | [as projected] | +10% | +20%
UGX/$ rate | 3,500 | 3,700 | 4,200 | 4,800
Fuel cost vs. plan | −10% | [as projected] | +40% | +70%
Interest rate | −2pp | [CBR + bank margin] | +3pp | +6pp
Loan repayment start | Month 3 | Month 3 | Month 3 | Month 6 (grace period needed)
Justification | [Market growth | [Normal ops] | [Exchange rate | [COVID-equivalent
               | +demand surge] |             | depreciation +  | sector disruption]
               |               |             | cost pressures] |
```

## 6. DSCR Stress Test

The bank's minimum DSCR is 1.25x. The stress test must show the DSCR under each scenario:

```
DSCR STRESS TEST

Scenario | EBITDA | Annual Debt Service | DSCR | Status
Optimistic  | UGX [X] | UGX [X] | [X.Xx] | ✓ Strong
Base case   | UGX [X] | UGX [X] | [X.Xx] | ✓ Above 1.25
Pessimistic | UGX [X] | UGX [X] | [X.Xx] | ✓ Above 1.00 / ⚠ Below 1.25
Extreme     | UGX [X] | UGX [X] | [X.Xx] | ⚠ Covenant breach

CONCLUSION: The business maintains DSCR above 1.25 in base and optimistic scenarios.
In the pessimistic scenario, DSCR of [X.Xx] is below the covenant minimum —
mitigated by [cash reserve / credit line / owner injection / harvest timing].
```

If the pessimistic DSCR falls below 1.0, the bank will likely require additional collateral or a shorter loan term. The plan must address this proactively.

## 7. Early Warning Dashboard

Define specific numerical triggers that activate management response:

```
EARLY WARNING INDICATORS

GREEN (on track):
  ✓ Monthly revenue ≥ 90% of plan
  ✓ DSCR calculated monthly ≥ 1.35
  ✓ Cash reserve ≥ 2 months operating costs
  ✓ Receivables collection within 30 days
  ✓ Stock turnover within [X] days of plan

AMBER (watch — review within 1 week):
  ⚠ Monthly revenue 75–89% of plan for 2 consecutive months
  ⚠ DSCR 1.10–1.24
  ⚠ Cash reserve 1.0–1.9 months operating costs
  ⚠ Key customer (>20% of revenue) signals reduced orders

RED (action required immediately):
  ✗ Monthly revenue < 75% of plan
  ✗ DSCR < 1.10
  ✗ Cash reserve < 1 month operating costs
  ✗ Loan repayment unable to be met from operations
  ✗ Major collateral asset damaged/destroyed

ACTIONS ON RED:
  1. Contact bank relationship manager within 48 hours (do not wait for them to call)
  2. Activate cost reduction protocol (freeze non-essential spending)
  3. Accelerate receivables collection
  4. Review financing options: overdraft, receivables factoring, owner injection
  5. Revise financial projections and present recovery plan to bank within 30 days
```
