# Cultivation and Marketing of Flowers

**Sources:** UNDP Uganda Compendium Vol. 2 (adjusted ×1.27 to March 2026 USD); Crack A Business Kenya (CAB) — Flowers & The City: Nairobi Flower Market Survey, 2014 | **Sub-sector:** Horticulture — Cut Flowers

> Covers flower production for local market (UNDP profile: 500 bundles/day capacity) and the domestic flower distribution and retail business in urban East Africa (Nairobi market survey). Kenya's flower industry is primarily export-oriented; the domestic market is served mainly by rejects and smallholder surplus, creating a distinct low-cost entry opportunity for urban distributors.

> Kenya's major flower market is Europe. Large commercial farms consider the domestic market "immature" and logistically unattractive. The domestic market is served primarily by export-quality rejects and smallholder surplus — making entry capital and quality standards more accessible.

### Nairobi Flower Supply Chain

**Market levels:**
```
Farmers → Distributors/Wholesalers → Retailers → Consumers
Farmers → Wholesalers → Direct consumers (bulk: weddings, corporates)
Farmers → Retailers (direct purchase, bypassing wholesaler)
```

- **City Market** (between Koinange St and Muindi Mbingu): epicentre of Nairobi's domestic flower trade; ~90% of domestically sold flowers pass through here
- Delivery hours: 3am–9am daily (tradition + perishability + customer habit)
- On average a wholesaler makes 4 deliveries/week to the City Market

### Distributor / Wholesaler Entry

| Item | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Minimum entry capital | KES 5,000 ($60) |
| Special registration required | None — walk-in, walk-out with farms |
| Markup on farm prices | 20–40% |
| Average sales per delivery (City Market) | KES 12,000 ($144) |
| Highest sales recorded | KES 168,000 ($2,013) |

**To become a flower distributor in Nairobi:**
1. Identify flower farms selling to local distributors (Naivasha, Narok, Mt. Kenya region)
2. Identify your market (City Market vendors vs. direct supply to churches, offices, estates)
3. Purchase flowers at farm gate and transport to market as quickly as possible
4. Build relationships with retailers — vendor loyalty is based on price consistency and credit

**Shelf life:** Roses: 3–4 days (weather and storage dependent). No cold storage = sell on arrival.

### Retail Flower Operations

- Retailers purchase from distributors at City Market then resell in their local area
- Premium service: curated bouquets for weddings, funerals, corporate events, hospitals
- Consumers are not highly demanding on export quality specifications (stem length, head size) — export rejects sell well locally
- Direct-to-consumer supply for bulk orders (weddings, events) is a growing model bypassing City Market

### Flower Varieties in Demand (Domestic)

- Roses (most popular), carnations, lilies, sunflowers
- Seasonal: demand spikes for Christmas, Valentine's Day, Mother's Day, Easter
- Funeral wreaths: steady demand; morning purchases by mourners is a reliable segment

### Revenue Dynamics

- **Location economics:** Vendors outside City Market (Westlands, Nairobi Hospital, Aga Khan Hospital vicinity) pay higher per-stem prices at City Market but save on travel — wholesalers can gain market share by delivering directly to these locations
- **Trust and credit:** Vendor loyalty shifts to distributors who extend credit; without credit, relationships are transactional
- **Seasonality:** February (Valentine's Day) is peak; December (Christmas) second peak; July–August are slow months

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Flower farming for local market is active in Wakiso, Mukono, and Mpigi districts. Local demand is served mainly by smallholder growers and horticultural cooperatives. Kampala's event industry (weddings, churches, corporate) is the primary demand driver. NAADS and NAFSIP have promoted floriculture under the commercial agriculture programmes.
- **Tanzania:** Arusha and Kilimanjaro regions have commercial flower farms; most production is export-oriented (European market). The Arusha domestic market has active flower stalls. Dar es Salaam retail flower market is smaller but growing with event culture.
- **Rwanda:** NAEB (National Agricultural Export Development Board) promotes floriculture as an export crop; domestic market is small but growing with Kigali's event and hospitality sector. Imported cut flowers from Kenya available at Kigali City Market; local production is limited.
- **Demand driver across EA:** Urbanisation → growth of event culture (weddings, funerals, office decoration) → expansion of local cut flower demand in all capital cities and secondary towns.

## Data Quality Notes

UNDP production profile ($4,800 capital, 500 bundles/day) is for a formal farming setup. Nairobi distributor data (2014) reflects entry-level distributors; prices will have moved but margins (20–40% markup) are structural and likely persist. Minimum entry of KES 5,000 ($60) is for informal distributors with no cold storage or transport — viable only with farm proximity and strong vendor relationships at City Market.
