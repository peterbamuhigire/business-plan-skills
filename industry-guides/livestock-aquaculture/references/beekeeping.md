## Uganda Context

Uganda is one of East Africa's significant honey producers. Key production regions include:

- **Lake Victoria basin** — year-round floral diversity, high humidity favourable to colony strength
- **Mt Elgon region** — high-altitude forests with dense floral resources; some of Uganda's highest-quality honey
- **Murchison Falls area and northern Uganda** — established production zones, strong artisanal tradition
- **Western highlands (Rwenzori foothills)** — multi-flora honey, proximity to DRC cross-border trade

Beekeeping requires minimal land — a quarter of a hectare can accommodate up to 200 hives — making it one of the few enterprises that can be layered onto existing farmland without displacing food crops. This is particularly relevant for smallholder farmers in Uganda's land-constrained districts.

Uganda exports honey to regional markets (Kenya, South Sudan, DRC) and, in smaller volumes, to European specialty markets. Quality certification (UNBS US 107: Honey — Specification) and residue-free production are prerequisites for export.

## Business Case for Beekeeping

### Why Beekeeping Works for Rural Uganda

- **Low land requirement:** hives can be placed on forest edges, in gardens, or alongside crops
- **Complementary to farming:** bees pollinate crops (beans, sunflowers, coffee, vegetables), increasing yields on the same land by an estimated 20–30%
- **Accessible across demographics:** women, youth, and persons with disabilities can participate; hive management is not physically demanding compared to most livestock enterprises
- **Multiple revenue streams:** honey (primary), beeswax (candles, cosmetics, pharmaceuticals), propolis (health products), and pollination services
- **Low daily labour:** once established, colonies require inspection every 10–14 days; harvesting is periodic (typically two seasons per year in Uganda)
- **Scalable:** a beekeeper can start with 5–10 hives and expand incrementally using colony divisions without purchasing new packages

### Market Demand

High and growing demand for honey in Uganda across multiple segments:

- **Home consumption** — traditional sweetener and health remedy; demand peaks during festive seasons
- **Pharmaceutical and health sector** — wound healing, cough remedies, infant nutrition (note: not for infants under 12 months)
- **Food processing** — baked goods, beverages, confectionery
- **Cosmetics and personal care** — beeswax for lip balms, creams, hair products
- **Export** — regional (Kenya, South Sudan, Rwanda) and long-haul (EU, Middle East) for certified organic/traceable honey

Beeswax fetches UGX 15,000–25,000/kg and is in consistent demand from candle manufacturers and cosmetics producers in Kampala.

## Hive Types: Traditional, Transitional, and Modern

Uganda beekeepers choose from three hive types, each with distinct cost, skill, and productivity profiles. The right choice depends on capital availability, skill level, market ambitions, and location.

### 1. Log Hive (Traditional Hive)

The oldest form of hive in Uganda and across sub-Saharan Africa. A hollowed log or bark cylinder, sealed at both ends with mud or wood, suspended in a tree or placed on a raised platform.

**Construction and materials:**
- Hollowed log (locally sourced hardwood or softwood), or bark cylinder
- Mud, clay, or dung for sealing
- Rope or wire for suspension
- **Estimated cost in Uganda:** UGX 5,000–20,000 per hive (materials only; labour often self-supplied)

**Honey yield:**
- Approximately 5–8 kg per harvest; two harvests per year in most Uganda regions
- Annual yield: ~10–16 kg per hive

**Pros:**
- Near-zero capital requirement; built from materials available in most rural areas
- No specialist skills or tools needed
- Familiar to most rural beekeepers; culturally embedded practice
- No transportation costs for materials

**Cons:**
- Very low honey yield compared to modern hives
- Harvesting is destructive — combs are crushed, killing brood and damaging the colony
- Hives placed in trees pose safety risks (falling, forest fires)
- Cannot be inspected or managed for disease and pests
- Honey quality is variable; smoke contamination common during harvest
- Not suitable for producing export-grade or certified honey

**Suitability:** Appropriate for subsistence production and as a first step to familiarise oneself with bee behaviour. Not recommended as a commercial enterprise hive type.

---

### 2. Kenya Top Bar Hive (KTBH) — Transitional

The KTBH is the recommended entry point for Uganda smallholders moving beyond subsistence. It is the most widely promoted hive type by Uganda's apiculture extension programmes and NGOs.

**Dimensions (standard KTBH):**
- Length: 35 inches (~89 cm)
- Width: 18⅛ inches (~46 cm)
- Depth: 9½ inches (~24 cm)
- Top bars: 28 per hive; each bar 19 inches long × 1¼ inches wide × 1 inch thick

**Construction and materials:**
- Timber planks (cypress or pine preferred for durability); local hardwoods acceptable
- Wire mesh for ventilation
- Metal sheet or treated wood for lid
- **Estimated cost in Uganda:**
  - Self-built from local timber: UGX 40,000–80,000 per hive
  - Purchased from an apiculture supplier (e.g., Bivatec Ltd): UGX 80,000–150,000 per hive

**Honey yield:**
- Approximately 8–15 kg per harvest; two harvests per year
- Annual yield: ~16–30 kg per hive

**Pros:**
- Significantly more durable than log hives; lifespan 5–10 years with maintenance
- Non-destructive harvesting — only capped honey combs are removed, brood is left intact
- Inspection possible without specialist tools
- Cost-effective relative to Langstroth
- Good transitional step; skills transfer to Langstroth management

**Cons:**
- Fixed body — cannot add supers to expand capacity
- Lower honey production ceiling than Langstroth
- Combs must be extracted by crushing and straining (lower quality than spun extraction)
- Bulky; difficult to transport if apiary is relocated
- Requires accurate measurements during construction to ensure correct bee space

**Suitability:** Strongly recommended as the entry-level commercial hive for Uganda smallholders. A starter apiary of 10–20 KTBHs is achievable on a modest budget and provides a viable income stream within the first production year.

---

### 3. Langstroth Hive (Modern Hive)

The international standard for commercial beekeeping. A rectangular box system with removable frames and the ability to stack additional honey super boxes on top of the brood box as the colony grows.

**Dimensions:**
- Standard (full-depth) Langstroth: 20 inches × 16¼ inches × 9⅝ inches body depth
- 10 frames per brood box (some configurations use 8 frames)
- Super boxes (shallower) stacked above the brood box; a productive colony may fill 2–3 supers per season

**Construction and materials:**
- Treated pine or hardwood; requires precision joinery for frame alignment and bee space (¼ inch / 6–8 mm)
- Foundation sheets (beeswax or plastic) for frames
- Metal excluder (queen excluder) between brood box and supers
- **Estimated cost in Uganda:**
  - Imported assembled Langstroth: UGX 150,000–300,000 per complete unit (brood box + one super + frames + lid + floor)
  - Locally fabricated: UGX 100,000–200,000 with skilled carpenter; quality control is critical
  - Foundation sheets: UGX 5,000–15,000 per sheet

**Honey yield:**
- 15–30 kg per harvest from a strong colony; two harvests per year
- Annual yield: ~30–60 kg per hive with good management
- Productive apiaries with 100+ Langstroth hives can produce 3,000–6,000 kg annually

**Pros:**
- Highest honey yields of any hive type
- Expandable — supers added as colony grows, no swarming pressure
- Clean, separated honey (brood and honey in distinct boxes)
- Enables centrifuge extraction — frames are returned intact, bees rebuild faster
- Supports controlled queen rearing and colony multiplication
- Easy transportation (standardised box dimensions fit on vehicle racks)
- Best suited for certified/export-grade honey production

**Cons:**
- Highest initial investment
- Requires regular inspections (every 10–14 days during active season) to prevent swarming
- Pest hiding in frame crevices (small hive beetle, wax moth) if colonies weaken
- Requires centrifuge extractor for full quality benefit (shared equipment or co-operative purchase)
- Frame alignment precision required during construction; poorly built frames reduce productivity

**Suitability:** Recommended for medium and large commercial operations. A motivated smallholder with access to a co-operative extractor can profitably operate 20–50 Langstroth hives.

## Bee Forage and Apiary Siting

### Forage Radius and Foraging Behaviour

Honeybees (*Apis mellifera* — the African honeybee subspecies *Apis mellifera scutellata* dominates in Uganda) forage within a radius of approximately **3 km** from the hive, though they prefer sources within 1.5 km. The foraging area of a single hive therefore covers roughly 28 km². Apiary siting must consider what is blooming within that radius across all seasons.

### Apiary Siting Criteria

A well-sited apiary significantly reduces management effort and increases colony productivity. Key criteria:

1. **Water source within 500 m** — bees require water daily for hive cooling and larval feeding. A pond, stream, or permanent shallow trough is essential. Stagnant water is acceptable to bees; a small tray with floating cork or straw prevents drowning.
2. **Dappled shade** — hives should not be in full sun (overheating stresses colonies and increases water demand) nor in deep shade (reduces colony activity). East-facing entrances allow morning sun to stimulate early foraging.
3. **Flight path clearance** — hives should face away from footpaths, homesteads, livestock kraals, and roads. A minimum of 50 m from human activity is recommended; 100 m from schools or markets. Plant a hedge or fence 2 m in front of the entrance to force bees to gain altitude quickly, reducing conflict.
4. **Wind shelter** — site hives on the lee side of a hedge, tree line, or building to reduce wind chill on the brood nest.
5. **Elevated stands** — hives on stands 40–60 cm above ground reduce small hive beetle and ant pressure; prevent grass from blocking the entrance.
6. **Away from agrochemicals** — avoid siting within 3 km of heavily sprayed fields (cotton, vegetables with systemic insecticides). Organophosphate and pyrethroid sprays are highly toxic to bees.
7. **Security** — apiaries in Uganda face risks from honey badgers (*Mellivora capensis*), monitor lizards, and theft. Barbed wire perimeter fencing or thorn-branch barriers are advisable.

### Forage Plant Categories

Bivatec classifies bee forage plants into functional groups relevant to apiary management:

- **Attractant/nectar-secreting plants (primary forage):** Calliandra, sunflower, mango flowers, bottlebrush (*Callistemon* spp.), sweet potatoes, pawpaw, coffee, and various eucalyptus species. These are the core honey-producing flora.
- **Climate-dependent bloomers** requiring supplementary forage planning: sugarcane, coffee, bananas — useful but unreliable across all seasons; beekeepers must plan alternative sources during off-bloom periods.
- **Avoid:** carnivorous plants (family Droseraceae) and some legumes with spring-loaded pollen mechanisms that injure bees.

### Recommended Bee Forage Plants for Uganda

Plant a mix of species with staggered blooming periods to eliminate or reduce seasonal dearth:

| Plant | Bloom Season (Uganda) | Nectar/Pollen Value | Notes |
|-------|----------------------|---------------------|-------|
| Calliandra (*Calliandra calothyrsus*) | Year-round (peaks dry season) | Very high nectar | Fast-growing; also fixes nitrogen; plant as hedgerow |
| Grevillea (*Grevillea robusta*) | Feb–Apr, Jul–Sep | High nectar | Shade tree; compatible with coffee agroforestry |
| Eucalyptus (*E. grandis*, *E. saligna*) | Variable; often dry season | High nectar | Large canopy; plant on boundaries; controversial on water tables |
| Sunflower (*Helianthus annuus*) | Jan–Mar, Jul–Sep (planted) | Very high pollen + nectar | Annual crop; co-plant with apiary or nearby farms |
| Tithonia (*Tithonia diversifolia*) | Jun–Oct | High nectar | Mexican sunflower; fast-growing hedge; cuts as mulch |
| Coffee (*Coffea arabica/robusta*) | Mar–May; Oct–Nov | Moderate nectar | Existing crop on many farms; bees improve coffee yields |
| Cassava (*Manihot esculenta*) | Variable | Moderate pollen | Staple crop; provides forage when few alternatives bloom |
| Mango (*Mangifera indica*) | Aug–Oct (flowering) | High nectar | Large tree; single mango tree covers significant area |
| Pawpaw (*Carica papaya*) | Near year-round | Moderate | Female flowers produce less; male plants critical |
| Bottlebrush (*Callistemon* spp.) | May–Aug | High nectar | Ornamental; suitable for homestead planting |

*Plant at least 4–6 species with overlapping bloom periods. Calliandra as a hedgerow combined with Grevillea shade trees and seasonal sunflower is a practical minimum forage system for a Uganda smallholder apiary.*

### Seasonal Forage Calendar (Uganda — approximate)

Uganda has two rainy seasons (Mar–May, Oct–Nov) and two dry seasons (Jun–Aug, Dec–Feb), creating a complex forage calendar:

| Month | Key Blooming Plants | Forage Outlook |
|-------|---------------------|----------------|
| Jan–Feb | Mango, Eucalyptus, Calliandra | Moderate–good (dry season bloom) |
| Mar–May | Coffee, Grevillea, Sunflower, many wildflowers | Excellent (long rains flush) |
| Jun–Aug | Tithonia, Eucalyptus, Acacia, Sunflower (second crop) | Good–very good |
| Sep–Oct | Mango (early), Calliandra, Cassava | Moderate |
| Oct–Nov | Coffee (second flush), wildflowers | Good (short rains) |
| Dec | Post-rain dearth begins | Variable — watch colonies |

*The Dec–Jan period is the most common dearth period in many Uganda regions. Monitor hive weight or food stores in November.*

### Seasonal Dearth Management

When natural forage is insufficient, colonies can be fed sugar syrup to prevent starvation and maintain brood-rearing:

- **Thin syrup (1:1 sugar:water by weight)** — stimulates brood rearing; use in spring build-up or after a dearth to encourage queen laying
- **Thick syrup (2:1 sugar:water)** — provides food stores; use in late dearth to supplement without over-stimulating
- **Candy (fondant)** — solid sugar paste placed directly on the top bars; useful in cool highland conditions where liquid syrup may ferment
- Feed at dusk to avoid robbing from neighbouring hives
- Never feed honey of unknown origin (disease transmission risk)
- Supplementary feeding should be a management bridge, not a substitute for good apiary siting with adequate forage

Nine situations that warrant supplementary feeding:
1. Breeding colonies requiring nutritional support
2. Newly established colonies before first forage flush
3. Colonies recently united (temporary disruption)
4. Weak colonies recovering from disease or pest damage
5. Adverse weather periods (extended cold or drought)
6. Colony strength boosting before a major nectar flow
7. After agrochemical contamination of local forage
8. Overwintered colonies in highland Uganda (rare but applicable at altitude)
9. Scarcity of blooming flowers during seasonal dearth

## Hive Colonisation and Queen Rearing

### Colonisation Methods

Getting bees into a new hive is the first practical challenge for a new beekeeper. There are four main methods used in Uganda:

#### 1. Bait Hive Method (Passive Colonisation)

The most common method for KTBHs and log hives in Uganda. The hive is prepared to attract a naturally swarming colony:

- Coat the interior walls and top bars with melted beeswax (old comb melted and painted on with a brush)
- Treat the entrance with a small smear of propolis or old comb to provide colony scent
- Position the hive in a location with good shade, near water, and at an appropriate height (1–2 m above ground for KTBH; suspended 3–6 m for log hives)
- Monitor every 2–3 days; do not disturb until the colony has been resident for at least 3–4 weeks and has established brood comb
- Success rates vary by season; the best colonisation period in Uganda is at the onset of the rainy season (Mar–Apr, Oct–Nov) when swarming is most active

#### 2. Swarm Capture

When a swarm clusters on a branch, fence post, or structure (common in Uganda's peri-urban areas during March–May):

- Work in late afternoon when the swarm is calm and clustered
- Wear full protective gear; use minimal smoke (swarms are generally docile as they carry no stores to defend)
- Hold a cardboard box or wooden catcher box below the cluster
- Sharply shake or cut the branch so the bees fall into the box in one motion
- Cover loosely and leave for 30 minutes to allow flying bees to re-join
- Transfer to the prepared hive at dusk; place the box entrance at the hive entrance and allow bees to walk in, or tip gently
- The queen must be included — if she is, the colony will stay; if she is not, the bees will abandon the hive within 24–48 hours

#### 3. Colony Capture from Natural Locations (Tree, Hollow, or Structure)

For colonies established in a hollow tree, earthen mound, or building:

- Apply smoke at the colony entrance to calm bees before opening
- Cut brood combs carefully and attach them to KTBH top bars using rubber bands or thin wire (brood side facing down, matching natural comb orientation)
- Transfer as much brood and the queen as possible; adult bees will follow if the queen is present
- Close the hive and leave undisturbed for at least 12 hours to allow field bees to return and orient to the new location
- Block the original cavity to prevent re-colonisation

#### 4. Colony Division (Splitting)

A productive method to expand an apiary without cost once an established colony is available:

- Select a strong colony with at least 8 full combs of brood, bees, and honey
- Divide workers, eggs, young brood, and honey stores between the original hive and a new empty hive
- One unit retains the original queen; the other (queenless unit) will raise a new queen from eggs if young enough brood (less than 3 days old) is present
- The queenless unit will produce emergency queen cells within 24–48 hours; a new queen will emerge in approximately 16 days and begin laying 5–7 days after mating
- Do not inspect the queenless unit for at least 3 weeks after splitting to avoid disturbing queen development

### Queen Rearing

Queen quality is the single most important factor in colony productivity. Queen rearing allows beekeepers to multiply selected genetics, replace failing queens, and produce queens for sale.

#### Understanding Queen Development

| Stage | Duration |
|-------|----------|
| Egg | 3 days |
| Larva (open cell) | 5 days |
| Cell sealed | Day 8 from egg |
| Queen emerges | Day 16 from egg |
| Mating flights | Days 16–20 after emergence |
| First eggs laid | ~Day 21–25 from emergence |

#### Natural Queen Cell Method (Recommended for Beginners)

The simplest approach — allow the bees to raise queens from naturally selected larvae:

**Swarm cells:** Built on the bottom edges of frames when a colony is preparing to swarm. The bees select and provision multiple cells (typically 5–20). Swarm cells produce high-quality queens. The original queen departs with the swarm before the first virgin queen emerges.

**Emergency queen cells:** Produced when a colony suddenly loses its queen (accidental crushing during inspection, disease, or death). Bees convert 1–3 day old worker larvae in existing cells into queen cells by enlarging the cell and changing the larval diet to royal jelly. Emergency cells appear throughout the comb (not just the bottom edge). Queens produced from older larvae (2–3 days) may be slightly inferior to those raised from younger larvae.

**Signs of queenlessness:**
- Restless, "roaring" hive sound when the lid is removed
- Bees clustering on the sides of the hive or beard excessively
- No eggs visible on inspection (note: a new beekeeper should look with a torch; eggs are tiny and best seen with the comb held toward the light)
- Emergency queen cells visible (irregular, peanut-shaped cells on face of comb)
- Worker-laid eggs (multiple eggs per cell, laid on cell walls rather than centre base) — indicates the colony has been queenless for more than 3 weeks and workers have begun laying infertile eggs
- Rapid decline in colony population without obvious cause

#### Artificial Queen Rearing (Cell Cup Method)

For beekeepers with 20+ Langstroth hives who wish to produce multiple queens simultaneously:

1. **Prepare wax cups:** Melt clean beeswax and dip a smooth wooden mould (6–8 mm diameter tip) repeatedly to build a cup ~1 inch deep. Allow to cool; twist gently to release.
2. **Attach cups to frame:** Fix the cups upside-down (open end facing down) in rows of 10–15 on a wooden bar using melted wax.
3. **Graft larvae (grafting method):** Using a fine grafting tool (or a sharpened toothpick), transfer a 12–18-hour-old larva from the selected mother colony into each cup, floating it on a small amount of royal jelly drawn from another cell.
4. **Introduce to a strong queenless "starter" colony:** The queenless bees will recognise the larvae and begin feeding and building queen cells over the cups.
5. **Transfer to "finisher" colony** after 24 hours if using a two-colony system. A very strong colony can raise more than five queens simultaneously.
6. **Protect cells with wire mesh** after Day 10 to prevent the first queen to emerge from destroying the others.
7. **Harvest mature queen cells** at approximately Day 14–15 (2 days before expected emergence) and introduce to queenless nucleus colonies or hives requiring requeening.

*Note: Grafting requires good eyesight and practice; many Uganda beekeepers find the natural cell method adequate for small-scale operations.*

#### Queen Introduction Protocol

Introducing a mated queen or mature queen cell to an established colony requires care — bees will kill a foreign queen unless introduced correctly:

**Queen cell introduction:**
- Remove all existing queen cells from the recipient colony
- Fix the mature cell gently to a central frame, brood side, without crushing
- Leave undisturbed for 10 days; the queen will emerge, mate, and begin laying

**Mated queen introduction (push-in cage or candy cage):**
- Cage the queen in a small wire or plastic cage with a candy plug sealing one end
- The bees slowly eat through the candy (3–5 days), during which they acclimatise to the queen's pheromone
- Check after 5 days; if bees are calm around the cage (not biting the wire), the introduction is likely successful
- Release the queen by removing the cage; do not force release early

**Re-queening timing:**
- Best done during a nectar flow when the colony is in a generous mood
- Avoid requeening immediately after a colony has been disturbed, robbed, or inspected aggressively

## Regulatory and Certification Requirements

- **UNBS US 107** — Uganda Standard for Honey: maximum moisture 20%, no added sugars, freedom from antibiotics and pesticide residues
- **UNBS US 367** — Beeswax standard
- **NDA (National Drug Authority)** — Registration required if honey is marketed as a medicinal product
- **NEMA** — Environmental Impact Assessment may be required for large commercial apiaries (100+ hives) near protected forest areas
- **District Local Government** — Business licence and, in some districts, an apiculture registration with the District Production Office
- **For export:** Uganda Export Promotions Board (UEPB) registration; phytosanitary certificate from Ministry of Agriculture; EU export requires residue-free certification and registered establishment number

## Key Risks

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| Colony absconding (bees leave hive) | High if siting is poor | Correct siting; beeswax lining; avoid disturbance |
| Small hive beetle (*Aethina tumida*) | High in Uganda | Strong colonies; reduce hive space to match colony size; beetle traps |
| Wax moth (*Galleria mellonella*) | High in weak colonies | Maintain strong colonies; freeze or sun-treat combs not in use |
| Seasonal honey dearth | Medium | Forage planting; supplementary feeding plan |
| Pesticide poisoning | Medium (near farming areas) | Apiary siting away from sprayed crops; notify neighbouring farmers |
| Honey adulteration (market fraud) | Medium | Direct-to-consumer channels; branding; refractometer moisture certification |
| Foulbrood disease | Low–medium | Regular inspection; destroy and burn infected comb; do not move equipment between apiaries without disinfection |
| Market price volatility | Medium | Diversify (wax, propolis); process into value-added products (creamed honey, beeswax candles) |
| African bee aggression (*A. m. scutellata*) | Present | Full protective equipment; work in morning or early afternoon; avoid inspecting during thunder or rain |
