# Tomato Greenhouse

**Sources:** UNDP Uganda Compendium Vol. 2 (adjusted ×1.27 to March 2026 USD); Crack A Business Kenya (CAB) — Quick Guide to Tomato Farming (Greenhouse): Market & Production, 2014 | **Sub-sector:** Horticulture — Controlled-Environment Crop Production

> Covers greenhouse tomato production for controlled-environment, year-round supply (UNDP: 110 kg/day). Also covers the Kenyan tomato market, value chain dynamics, price volatility, disease risks, and the realities of farming commercially — essential context before committing capital to greenhouse investment.

## Kenyan Tomato Market (CAB Survey, 2014)

### Industry Overview

| Fact | Figure |
|------|--------|
| Value of Kenya tomato industry | KES 14–20 billion/year ($168M–$240M) |
| Standard harvest unit | 64 kg wooden crate |
| Farm-gate price range (2012–2014) | KES 15–110/kg ($0.18–$1.32/kg) |
| Wholesale price range (Nairobi) | KES 1,000–1,300/crate (varies by season) |
| Retail price (per tomato) | KES 7–15/tomato ($0.08–$0.18) |
| % of farmers selling at farm gate (to brokers) | ~85% |
| Market position of tomatoes in Kenyan diet | Listed in KNBS inflation basket |

### Value Chain

```
Farmer → Collecting Broker → Market Broker → Wholesaler → Retailer → Consumer
Farmer → Collecting Broker → Supermarket/Hotel/Processor
Farmer → Hotel/Supermarket/Processor (direct)
```

**Margin allocation (example, per crate):**
- Farmer buys inputs, harvests, sells to collecting broker at KES 600
- Collecting broker transports to Nairobi market, sells via market broker at KES 1,000
- Market broker sets price at KES 1,300 (takes KES 300 spread)
- Wholesaler buys at KES 1,300, sells to retailer
- Retailer marks up for consumer

### Why Farmers Sell at Farm Gate (Not Market)

1. **No cold storage** — tomatoes are perishable; fear of waste = sell on harvest
2. **No transport** — 20 crates needs a pick-up; hiring one cuts margin
3. **Market ignorance** — unfamiliarity with Nairobi market logistics, gatekeepers, county fees
4. **Market broker coercion** — newcomers to Wakulima Market face organized brokers who "book" consignments; direct selling to wholesalers is systematically blocked

### Collecting Brokers

- Grade tomatoes 1–3 (or 1–5) by size, bruises, appearance, shelf life
- Purchase Grade 1 and 2; leave Grade 3 in glut situations
- Transport when load reaches break-even (full pick-up or canter)
- Communication with market brokers before departure guides purchase price decisions

### Consumer Preference (Retail)

- Large size preferred
- Semi-ripe or ripe (not green)
- Long shelf life
- Good appearance / healthy-looking
- Variety matters for some: certain varieties have longer shelf life

## Price Volatility — The Core Risk

Tomato prices are highly volatile and unpredictable. No reliable data source exists for accurate advance price forecasting.

| Price Scenario | Typical Driver |
|---------------|---------------|
| High prices (>KES 70/kg) | Pest destruction, adverse weather, supply shortage |
| Low prices (<KES 25/kg) | Oversupply when many farmers time the same season |
| Seasonal peak | Dry season (Jan/Feb) and cold season — fewer open-field farmers |
| Seasonal trough | Post-rains (heavy harvest, low prices) |

**Greenhouse advantage for price volatility:** A greenhouse delivers harvest over 5–8 months, allowing the farmer to sell across multiple price levels rather than facing a single harvest-moment price.

**Timing risk:** Many farmers try to time the dry season — but if many do so simultaneously, the market floods and prices collapse regardless.

## Disease & Pest Risk

| Pest/Disease | Impact |
|-------------|--------|
| Tuta Absoluta (leaf miner) | Destroys fruit and stalk as it ripens; spread from Ethiopia via Kenya (Isiolo, Meru, Kirinyaga, Nakuru); resistant to many pesticides; destroys even greenhouse crops |
| Late blight | Fungal; weather-triggered; destroys entire crop quickly |
| Bacterial wilt | Soil-borne; persists in soil across seasons |
| Spider mites | Greenhouse-specific problem in hot, dry conditions |

> **Key message:** Tomatoes are fragile commercial crops. Losses are common. Even experienced farmers with greenhouses face complete crop failure from pests and disease. Capital must include a loss buffer.

## Feasibility Assessment

**Yes, greenhouse tomato farming is viable — with realistic expectations:**

| Positive Factor | Risk Factor |
|----------------|-------------|
| Year-round supply = premium off-season prices | High pest and disease pressure |
| Growing urban demand; no substitutes | Price volatility — KES 15–110/kg range |
| Government and infrastructure support | Market broker system disadvantages small farmers |
| Greenhouse enables timing flexibility | Cold storage costs add to capital |
| Export quality possible from premium greenhouses | High input cost compresses margins |

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** NAADS and Operation Wealth Creation have promoted greenhouse tomato farming nationally. Uganda Seed Alliance provides improved tomato varieties. Kampala's fresh produce market at Nakawa, St. Balikuddembe, and Kalerwe are key off-take points. Price dynamics similar to Kenya — collecting brokers dominant, market broker system at Owino. Tuta Absoluta has spread into Uganda; MAAIF provides pesticide guidance.
- **Tanzania:** Tomato farming concentrated in Arusha, Kilimanjaro (Moshi), Singida, and Dodoma regions. Dar es Salaam's Kariakoo Market is the equivalent of Nairobi's Wakulima Market. Export-grade tomatoes to Zanzibar and regional markets command premium. Climate more favourable for open-field production in highland areas; greenhouses less common but growing.
- **Rwanda:** RAB (Rwanda Agriculture Board) promotes improved tomato varieties through seed multiplication. Musanze, Rwamagana, and Bugesera are key production districts. Kigali City Market and supermarket chains (Nakumatt, Simba) are end markets. Rwanda's compact geography and good roads reduce transport cost from farm to market compared to Kenya.
- **Regional note:** All EA markets have active broker systems at wholesale level. Farmers with direct access to hotel, school, and restaurant buyers avoid the broker margin — worth pursuing as a first step.

## Data Quality Notes

UNDP financial profile ($67,800 capital, 110 kg/day) reflects a substantial, formal greenhouse operation with cold room. Smaller single-greenhouse operations can start for $5,000–$15,000 (1–2 tunnels). Kenyan market data is from 2014 and prices have shifted, but structural dynamics (broker systems, price volatility, disease risk) remain relevant across East Africa.
