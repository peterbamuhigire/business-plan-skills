# Honey Processing and Bottling

**Sub-sector:** Natural Products / Food Processing (Light Manufacturing)
**Sources:** Thankaraju, P. (Startup Manufacturing Business Ideas 200, NestFame 2018) | UNDP Uganda Compendium context

> Uganda is among Africa's top honey producers. Raw honey from smallholder beekeepers is typically sold in bulk at low farm-gate prices. A processing and bottling enterprise adds value by filtering, grading, branding, and packaging honey for urban retail, institutional, and export markets — capturing a significantly higher share of the retail price.

**Cross-reference:** See `industry-guides/agriculture/references/` — beekeeping production context. This file covers the **manufacturing/processing side** (buying raw honey, processing, and selling branded product).

## Raw Material — Honey Sourcing

**Sourcing options:**

| Option | Pros | Cons |
|--------|------|------|
| Own apiary | Full quality control; lowest cost | Requires beekeeping investment and knowledge |
| Long-term contracts with beekeeping groups | Predictable supply; negotiate quality standards | Requires farmer development and monitoring |
| Spot purchase from aggregators | Flexible; no long-term commitment | Variable quality; inconsistent supply |
| Uganda Honey Council / cooperative | Quality-graded supply available | May require membership |

**Honey quality grades (Uganda):**
- **Grade A (premium):** Moisture content <20%, clean, light amber, strong aroma
- **Grade B (standard):** Moisture 20–23%, amber, some debris, acceptable aroma
- **Grade C (processing grade):** High moisture, fermented risk; requires processing/blending

Only purchase Grade A and B honey for consumer bottling. Reject fermented honey (identifiable by slight alcoholic smell and bubbling).

## Production Process

1. **Honey reception and testing** — check moisture content with refractometer (reject >23%); taste and smell test for fermentation; grade and log by supplier
2. **Warming** — place drums/containers in warming cabinet at 35–40°C to liquefy if crystallised
3. **Uncapping** — if working with comb frames, uncap frames with uncapping knife
4. **Extraction** — load frames into extractor; spin at correct speed; collect extracted honey
5. **Coarse straining** — pass through 1–2mm mesh to remove large wax pieces
6. **Settling** — hold in settling tank for 24–72 hours at room temperature; skim foam and wax from surface
7. **Fine filtration** — pass through 100–200 micron stainless screen
8. **Warming (if needed)** — warm gently to 40°C maximum to ease filling; never exceed 45°C (destroys enzymes and darkens colour)
9. **Filling** — fill jars to consistent weight using filling machine; adjust for air gap allowance
10. **Capping** — apply lids; check torque; tamper-evident seal if applicable
11. **Labelling** — apply front and back labels with required information
12. **Final QC** — check each jar for: correct weight, seal integrity, label accuracy, clarity (no visible debris)
13. **Batch recording** — record batch number, date, supplier, honey grade for traceability

## Uganda Regulatory Requirements

| Requirement | Authority | Notes |
|------------|-----------|-------|
| UNBS product certification | Uganda National Bureau of Standards | US 792: Uganda Standard for Honey |
| Food hygiene certificate | Ministry of Health / district health office | Annual inspection |
| Business registration | URA TIN + KCCA / district licence | Standard |
| Export permit (for export) | Uganda Export Promotions Board (UEPB) | Phytosanitary certificate for export shipments |
| Organic certification (for premium export) | UgoCert / international body (ECOCERT, IFOAM) | 3-year process; high premium justifies cost |
| NEMA | National Environment Management Authority | If using fumigants or chemical cleaning agents |

**UNBS US 792 key parameters:**
- Moisture content: ≤ 21% (strained honey); ≤ 23% (comb honey)
- HMF (Hydroxymethylfurfural): ≤ 40 mg/kg (freshness indicator)
- Water activity: Must not support fermentation
- Free acidity: ≤ 50 meq/kg

## Market and Distribution

**Key customer segments:**
- **Urban supermarkets and specialty stores:** Nakumatt-equivalent, Game, Carrefour, organic food shops — demand branded, quality-labelled product
- **Health food market:** Growing in Kampala; buyers of natural honey (manuka-type positioning possible)
- **Hotels and restaurants:** Breakfast buffet, cooking, tea service; buy in bulk 5kg containers
- **Bakeries and confectioneries:** Cooking and baking ingredient
- **Export (high value):** European and Middle Eastern markets; requires organic certification and EU/UK food safety compliance
- **Direct to consumer:** WhatsApp orders, farmers markets, social media sales

**Branding strategy:** Emphasise Uganda origin, pure/natural claim, specific floral variety if traceable (eucalyptus, acacia, forest honey). Photography of Ugandan landscape and beekeepers builds authenticity and premium positioning.

## Key Risks

| Risk | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|
| Raw honey quality inconsistency | Establish grading standards with suppliers; train beekeepers in best practices |
| Seasonal supply variation | Maintain 60–90 day processed stock; build diverse supplier base across regions |
| Crystallisation complaints | Educate consumers that crystallisation is natural; provide warming guidance on label |
| Moisture-driven fermentation | Strict incoming moisture testing; reject high-moisture batches |
| Adulteration (sugar syrup addition by suppliers) | Brix + refractometer tests + periodic lab analysis |
| UNBS certification cost and delays | Budget UGX 2,000,000–4,000,000 for initial certification; start process before launch |
