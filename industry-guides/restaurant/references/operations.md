# Restaurant Operations Reference

> Source: *The Upstart Guide to Owning and Managing a Restaurant*, 2nd Edition, by Roy S. Alonzo (Kaplan Publishing, 2007).

## Start-Up Expense Categories

1. **Initial planning:** accounting/legal resources, market research, G&A expenses (phone, copying, transport), consulting, design, cost estimating
2. **Construction and acquisition:** building/renovating, purchasing and installing equipment, initial inventories, operating supplies
3. **Preopening expenses and working capital:** advertising, hiring and training staff, post-construction cleanup, adequate funds to meet payroll and bills until cash flow is self-sustaining

**Critical warning:** The biggest mistake is wanting to start with the "ultimate dream" restaurant. Start with what you can afford, build reputation and customer base, then upgrade in stages.

## Front of House Operations

### Space Allocation and Layout

**Minimum aisle widths:**
| Aisle Type | Minimum Width |
|---|---|
| Main traffic aisle (host stand through dining room) | 54 inches |
| Access aisles (main aisle to wait stations) | 36 inches |
| Service aisles (back of one chair to another) | 18-24 inches |

**Typical wait station size:** ~18 seats per station

### Table Size Calculation Method
Analyse clientele mix, then allocate seating proportionally:
```
Example for 160-seat restaurant:
- Parties of 1-2: 30% x 160 = 48 seats / 2 = 24 tables for 2
- Parties of 3-4: 50% x 160 = 80 seats / 4 = 20 tables for 4
- Parties of 5-6: 20% x 160 = 32 seats / 6 = 6 tables for 6
```
Use expandable tables (fours with fold-out leaves) for flexibility.

### Service System Design
Walk through the entire customer experience:
- Arrival and greeting
- Seating and drink orders
- Food order taking (wireless POS, written slips, or verbal)
- Order delivery to kitchen
- Food pickup and service
- Check presentation and cashiering
- Departure

**Buddy system:** Waitstaff assist stations on either side of their own during overload periods.

### Common Customer Service Failures
- Opening late or closing early
- Impersonal greeting
- Dirty tables visible to waiting guests
- Tattered or smudged menus
- Chipped glassware or spotted flatware
- Long waits for orders
- Food sitting under heat lamps
- Flat beverages, stale baked goods, limp salads
- Running out of advertised items
- Water glasses not refilled
- Greasy fingerprints on guest checks
- No "thank you" at departure

### Take-Out as Profit Centre
- Adds volume without major investment
- Attracts new customers
- Offsets sluggish table turnover on busy nights
- Better utilisation of existing production capacity
- Works especially well for pizza, Asian food, and increasingly upscale cuisine

## Purchasing and Inventory Control

### Purchasing Principles
- Purchasing dollars are "high-powered" -- if netting 10% profit, it takes $10 in additional sales to compensate for every $1 lost through poor purchasing
- Buy the right quantities, grades, and ripeness stage
- Avoid slow-moving items (money on shelves gathers dust, not interest)
- Cross-check prices with multiple vendors

### Inventory Turnover
Four reasons for slow-moving/dead stock:
1. Over-purchased
2. Ingredients bought for items that didn't sell, never reused
3. Inventory not taken regularly; dead stock goes unnoticed
4. No "forced issues" system requiring chef to use up old items

### Receiving Best Practices
- Count and inspect ALL incoming shipments before signing invoices
- Check for proper quantity, quality, and pricing
- Verify against purchase specifications (purveyor, brand, size)
- Check temperatures of frozen/refrigerated products
- Inspect for broken packages, dented/swollen cans, signs of damage

### Storage Best Practices
- Put away immediately: frozen first, then refrigerated, then dry storage
- **Recommended storage temperatures:**
  | Product | Temperature | Humidity |
  |---|---|---|
  | Meat and poultry | 32-36 F | 75-85% |
  | Fish | 30-34 F | 75-85% |
  | Dairy products | 38-40 F | 75-85% |
  | Most fruits and vegetables | 40-45 F | 85-95% |
- Raw products stored on lower shelves, below cooked products
- Items that may drip: wrapped tightly, placed on lower shelves
- All foods raised off the floor
- Dry storage: dry, dark, preferably cool
- Storeroom kept locked; only authorised persons make withdrawals
- All withdrawals recorded

### Standard Recipes
- Ensure consistent taste and portion size
- Specify all ingredients and step-by-step method
- Example standard: "cut 8 slices or wedges out of every tomato"

### Portion Control
- Establish standard portion sizes for ALL menu items
- Use measuring tools: scales, numbered scoops, ladles
- Ensures consistency of production cost and customer satisfaction

### Inventory Control System (Simple)
Requires only three things:
1. Periodic inventory (beginning and end of period, not exceeding one month)
2. Record of purchases
3. Record of sales

```
Beginning inventory     $5,900
Plus: Purchases         $16,500
Total                   $22,400
Less: Ending inventory  $6,400
Cost of food sold       $16,000
```

### Control Loop Summary
1. **Purchasing** -- establish specifications (purveyor, brand, size, min/max stock levels)
2. **Receiving** -- count, inspect, verify pricing before signing invoices
3. **Storing** -- secured locations, limited keys, immediate put-away
4. **Issuing** -- record all receipts and withdrawals in inventory book/system
5. **Inventory** -- physical count at least monthly to verify book balances
6. **Standard recipes** -- consistent quality
7. **Portion control** -- consistent cost
8. **Cashiering** -- every product served must be accounted for

### Chef Job Description (Key Responsibilities)
1. Menu planning and development
2. Pricing all menu items
3. Establishing portion sizes
4. Purchasing at competitive prices
5. Cost control (payroll, food, beverages, supplies, utilities)
6. Coordinating function/banquet service
7. Supervising and motivating employees
8. Kitchen cleanliness and equipment inspections
9. Monitoring production during rush periods
10. Hiring, terminating, evaluating, scheduling kitchen staff
11. Safety and security in back of house
12. Sanitation code compliance
13. Taking inventories and calculating food cost percentages

### Employee Orientation Checklist
- History and ownership of establishment
- Reporting structure
- Hours, schedule posting location
- Vacation, overtime, holiday policies
- Smoking and break policies
- Compensation: hourly rate, deductions, payday, tip reporting
- Fringe benefits
- Meal policy
- Absenteeism/tardiness policy
- Emergency call-in procedures
- Probationary period and warning policies
- Training plan
- Evaluation and advancement policies
- Guest check, complaint, breakage, portion control policies
- Dress code and appearance standards
- Facility tour (parking, restrooms, lockers, entrances)
- Termination-worthy actions
- Safety and fire procedures

### Four-Step Training Method
1. **Show** the employee how to do the task (demonstrate)
2. **Tell** the employee what you are doing and why
3. **Let** the employee do it under supervision
4. **Check back** periodically to ensure continued proper performance

### Tip Reporting Requirements (IRS)
- Employees receiving $20+/month in tips must report monthly to employer
- Large establishments (10+ employees on typical day, food/bev consumed on premises): allocate tips if total reported < 8% of gross sales
- Report allocated amount on W-2
- File Form 8027 annually regardless

## Sanitation and Food Safety

### HACCP Principles
Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Point -- FDA preventive programme:
- Breaks down every food process from raw state to consumption
- Identifies critical points where hazards may occur
- Recommends measures for each critical point
- Specifies minimum cooking times and temperatures
- NRA's **ServSafe** programme implements HACCP for restaurants

### Key Temperature Standards
- Hot foods held above **140 F**
- Cold foods held at **40 F or lower**
- Dishwashing: wash at **140-160 F**, sanitise at **180 F**
- Draught beer storage: **38-42 F**, gauge pressure 12-14 psi

### Sanitation Programme Components
1. **Purchasing** -- buy from reliable, inspected vendors
2. **Receiving** -- check for damage, temperature, quality
3. **Storing** -- proper temperatures, raw below cooked, off the floor
4. **Food handling** -- employee hygiene training, glove protocols
5. **Serving** -- cook in small batches, minimise holding time, check temperatures every meal period
6. **Cleaning and sanitising** -- proper dishwashing procedures, ongoing training

### Common Fire Types and Prevention
1. **Grease fires in exhaust hoods** (most common) -- regular cleaning of equipment and ductwork
2. **Electrical fires** -- proper equipment handling, turn off when not in use, report defects

## Responsible Alcohol Service

### Four Stages of Intoxication
1. **Lessening of inhibitions** -- relaxation, behavioural change
2. **Poor judgment** -- loud outbursts, ordering doubles, argumentative
3. **Impaired reactions** -- slurred speech, fumbling, inability to concentrate
4. **Loss of coordination** -- stumbling, weaving, spilling, falling asleep

### Intervention Strategies
- Serve snacks that don't create thirst
- Delay asking for reorders
- Do not solicit refills
- Limit strong drinks or develop weaker versions
- Offer nonalcoholic alternatives
- Staff adequately during peak periods for proper ID checking and monitoring

### Responsible Business Practices
- Conduct periodic in-house training on alcohol responsibility
- Post policies on table tents, menus, wine lists
- Check ID of anyone who doesn't appear at least 30 years old
- Maintain incident log at bar
- Send servers to TIPS programme or equivalent
- No happy hours encouraging excessive consumption
- Offer free coffee to departing customers
- Arrange safe transportation when needed

## Marketing and Promotion

### Seven-Step Marketing Plan
1. **Establish overall objective** (e.g., "increase sales by 40% next year")
2. **Identify strengths and weaknesses**
3. **List alternative strategies** (e.g., expand building, convert function room, increase seat turnover)
4. **Select best strategy**
5. **Develop detailed action plan** with specifics
6. **Implement** with timetable
7. **Evaluate results** against objectives

### Three Types of Market Information
1. **Demographics** -- age, income, education, occupation, race, religion, nationality
2. **Geographic** -- where people live and work, dining/drinking patterns
3. **Psychographic** -- brand consciousness, peer influence, social orientation, lifestyle, spending motivation

### Advertising Budget
Recommended: **2% to 3% of estimated sales**

### Advertising Life Cycle
1. **Awareness** -- let public know you exist, what you offer, where you are
2. **Trial** -- promotions to bring in first-time patrons and increase frequency
3. **Maintenance** -- capitalise on built reputation, remind of quality
4. **Rejuvenation** -- emphasise what's new or improved, restart cycle

### Media Selection Guide
| Medium | Best For | Key Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| Radio | Drive-time captive audience, demographic targeting | Audio only, can't be saved |
| Newspapers | Timely tie-ins with events, lower cost | Short life, may get buried |
| Television | Audio + visual appeal, demographic targeting | Expensive, longer production time |
| Magazines | Highly targeted demographics, long life | Long lead times, expensive |
| Billboards | Directions and reminders | Short messages only, limited locations |
| Internet | Huge potential, regional targeting | Must be promoted aggressively |
| Direct mail | Personalised, selective, can include coupons | Low return (<5%), expensive, "junk mail" |

### Menu as Marketing Tool
- **Three purposes:** inform what you offer, show prices, promote high-profit items
- **Boxing items** on menus emphasises them
- **Menu reading pattern** (three-fold menu): first spot seen is top of right panel, then moves counterclockwise
- Place high-profit items in high-visibility spots
- Use suggestive selling: train waitstaff, use clear descriptions, large type sizes
- Place unopened wine bottles on tables to increase wine sales
- Display wine rack in lobby

### Grand Opening Strategy
- Conduct "dry runs" (practice events) before opening to public
- Invite media, community leaders, potential function planners to preopening events
- Generate free publicity through press releases
- Build word-of-mouth through quality and service from day one

### Customer Retention Strategies
- Establish personal rapport
- Get feedback (compliments and complaints)
- Never embarrass customers
- Give reasons to come back (loyalty programmes, specials, events)
- Theme nights for slow days
- Early-bird specials to fill pre-rush seats
- Late-evening lighter menu for theatre/sports crowds
