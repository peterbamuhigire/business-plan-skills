## 1. RESTAURANT TYPES AND FORMATS

### Three Primary Restaurant Categories
- **Quick-service (fast-food)**: Limited menus, prepared quickly, relatively low prices. Offer drive-thru windows and takeout. Serve hamburgers, chicken, hot dogs, sandwiches, pizza, seafood, ethnic foods.
- **Midscale**: Full meals at prices customers perceive as good value. Range of limited- and full-service options. Includes steakhouses, casual dining, family dining, ethnic restaurants (Italian, Mexican, Asian, Mediterranean). Ambience tends to be casual even in full-service operations.
- **Upscale/Fine dining**: Full table service, focus on cuisine quality and ambience rather than value. Highest prices, highest end of the restaurant category.

### Specific Restaurant Concepts

**Seafood Restaurants**
- Quick-service: limited range, often restricted to fried seafood
- Midscale/upscale: wider selection (baked, broiled, grilled), plus steak and chicken
- Risk: prices always changing, many kinds of seafood are seasonal, quality varies
- Often located on or near waterfront

**Steakhouses**
- Midscale: family-oriented, casual, Western themes, value perception
- Upscale: more formal, larger/better-quality cuts, higher prices, more privacy

**Family-Style Restaurants**
- Geared toward families, reasonable prices, also appeal to seniors
- Service speed between quick-service and full-service
- Variety of selections to appeal to broad range (children to seniors)
- Booster seats and highchairs readily available

**Casual-Dining Restaurants**
- Appeal to wide audience (Gen Y through seniors)
- Variety of food items, comfortable atmospheres, midrange prices
- Many centre on a theme incorporated into menus and decor

**Ethnic Restaurants**
- Significant share of the US restaurant market
- Range from quick-service to upscale
- Three most popular: Italian, Mexican, Chinese
- Also popular: Indian, Thai, Caribbean, English, French, German, Japanese, Korean, Mediterranean, Vietnamese

### Pizzeria
- **Industry size**: $36-38 billion annual sales; 65,000-75,000 pizza restaurants plus ~10,000 Italian restaurants in the US
- **Pizza consumption**: 3+ billion pizzas sold in the US annually, another 2 billion worldwide
- **Operator stats**: 74% have dining rooms, 94% serve appetizers, 56% serve pasta/salads, 48% serve beer, 38% serve wine
- Popular toppings: pepperoni is #1 (beyond cheese); two-thirds prefer meat toppings, one-third prefer vegetarian
- Kids aged 3-11 prefer pizza for lunch and dinner above all other foods
- Many to-go stores also deliver, finding revenue from delivery substantial

### Sandwich Shop/Delicatessen
- Can be differentiated by the foods they serve
- Turnover is key -- must capitalise on a few peak periods (especially lunch) per day
- Competition from other quick-service outlets, chains like Subway, supermarket delis
- Can quickly change menus to adapt to current tastes (health trends, low-carb)
- Many adding delivery and catering to sit-down and takeout operations

### Coffeehouse
- Coffee ranks second only to petroleum in global trade dollars
- 400+ billion cups consumed yearly worldwide; most popular beverage globally
- 50%+ of US adult population drinks coffee daily (150+ million daily drinkers)
- ~17% of US adult population consumes gourmet/specialty coffee beverages
- Average purchase: $3-$5 per customer
- Successful coffeehouses serve up to 500 customers per day with up to 5 customer turnovers during lunch hour
- Profit margins on coffee/espresso drinks are "extremely high" -- product is 95%+ water
- People will linger for an hour sipping a $2 coffee, but huge margins mean even low-volume operations can profit if run correctly
- Coffee replacing alcohol as social beverage of choice in many situations
- At least 30% of total sales should be in coffee (high-volume coffee sales offset food prep costs)

### Bakery
- "Bread only" retail bakeries have almost disappeared; today's bakeries offer cakes, scones, bagels, coffee drinks, sometimes full menus
- Market is extremely competitive; need differentiation
- By 1850 there were 2,000+ commercial bakeries in the US
- Can sell wholesale, retail, or both
- Easier for wholesale baker to transition to retail than the reverse
- Budget 35% of weekly gross income for inventory; more than half goes to flour
- Paper goods (wrappings, bags) represent ~3% of gross revenue

### Food and Party Catering
- Three major markets: corporate clients, social events (weddings, parties), cultural organisations
- Most flexible food-service business from a cost-of-entry perspective
- Can start small, build equipment inventory as needed
- Can rent existing commercial kitchens rather than owning
- Strong growth in social catering; trend expected to continue
- Three basic catered social affairs: formal sit-down dinner, informal buffet dinner, cocktail party
- Catering is a great way to expand an existing food-service operation without major capital

## 2. STARTUP COSTS AND CAPITAL REQUIREMENTS

### Restaurant (Full-Service)
- **Heavy production equipment**: $30,000-$45,000
- **Small production items** (ladles, tongs, pans, etc.): $1,200-$2,700
- **Service kitchen equipment** (prep/steam table, toaster, heat lamps, microwave, beverage centre): $11,000-$20,000
- **Dishwashing equipment**: machine $2,000-$25,000; full installation $5,000-$25,000
- **Walk-in refrigerator/freezer**: $3,000-$8,000
- **Receiving area equipment** (scale, breakdown table, shelving): $2,000-$4,000
- **Bar**: $5,000-$9,000 for the bar itself; $12,000-$22,000 for bar equipment
- **Dinnerware** (complete set): $2,000-$4,000
- **First year inventory**: food $12,000-$85,000; beverages $3,500-$25,000; paper products $700-$2,000
- **Ventilation**: $175-$450 per linear foot for hood/grease filter; $20-$50 per linear foot for ductwork

### Pizzeria
- **Total startup range**: $70,000-$1.5 million (depending on type and resources)
- **New equipment**: $60,000-$125,000
- **Dinnerware** (full-service): $2,500-$4,000

### Sandwich Shop/Delicatessen
- **Service kitchen equipment**: $12,000-$20,000
- **Preparation kitchen** (heavy production): $30,000-$45,000
- **Cookware and miscellaneous**: $1,200-$2,500
- **Dining area** (tables/chairs): $300-$6,000
- **Dinnerware**: up to $3,000
- **Dishwashing (sink only)**: $600-$2,000
- **Dishwashing (full machine setup)**: $6,500-$20,000
- **Walk-in refrigerator/freezer** (if needed): $7,000-$10,000
- **Receiving/storage equipment** (small operation): under $1,500
- **Refrigerated display cases**: $1,600 (used) to $9,000+ (new)
- **Opening inventory**: $9,000-$12,000; full food/beverage inventory should turn over every ~2 weeks
- **Bread-baking equipment** (if making own bread): $20,000-$150,000

### Coffeehouse
- **Total facility**: $20,000-$200,000+ (depending on conversion vs. new build)
- **Equipment costs**: $12,000-$70,000+
- **Espresso machines** (two-group): $3,000-$9,000
- **Commercial coffeemakers**: $300-$1,200 each
- **Coffee grinder**: $700-$1,300 (or lease for $20-$35/month)
- **Initial coffee inventory**: $4,000-$9,000
- **Initial accessories inventory**: $1,700-$5,500
- **Starting coffee bean inventory**: 400-700 pounds

### Bakery
- **Facility total**: $20,000-$200,000+
- **High-volume automated equipment** (minimum): $50,000+
- **Used equipment packages**: starting under $25,000
- **Rounders**: $4,000-$9,000
- **Dough dividers**: $2,600-$10,000
- **Proofers**: $3,000-$60,000
- **Ovens**: $15,000-$26,000+
- **Pans** (100-200 needed): $2,500-$5,000+

### Catering
- Most flexible entry cost; can start by renting existing commercial kitchen
- One operator (Jim Amaral, Borealis Breads) started with just $10,000 borrowed from family/friends
- Another (Scott Redler, Timberline Steakhouse) invested $24,000 of own money plus investors

### Food Trucks
- **Used truck**: from ~$30,000
- **New fully-equipped truck**: up to $100,000

### Carts and Kiosks
- **New cart**: $3,000-$5,000+
- **Cart lease**: 8-12% of new price per month
- **New kiosk**: $10,000+
- **Mall cart space**: $400/month (class C mall, off-peak) to $4,000-$5,000/month (class A mall, December)

## 3. LOCATION SELECTION CRITERIA

### Key Site Evaluation Factors
1. **Traffic density**: Distinguish between auto and pedestrian traffic. Count potential customers during busy half-hour periods; multiply out to project weekly/monthly/quarterly figures
2. **Customer parking**: One parking spot for every three restaurant seats. Storefront parking is better than rear lot. Well-lit and secure. Consider valet parking as an option
3. **Proximity to other businesses**: Certain businesses cluster naturally. Restaurants often found near theatres and entertainment venues
4. **History of the site**: Research previous tenants and why they left. Sites with repeated business failures may have inherent problems
5. **Terms of the lease**: Negotiate before signing. All terms are negotiable
6. **Rent-advertising relationship**: High-traffic mall = higher rent but less need for advertising spend. Independent location = lower rent but need up to 6 months of advertising/promotion in working capital
7. **Future development**: Check with local planning board for planned construction or changes
8. **Going-home side of street**: Generally preferable to going-to-work side (people have more time to stop). Exception: coffee and breakfast operations
9. **Shady side of street**: Generally more desirable than sunny side, especially in warm climates (higher rents on shady side in high-priced areas)

### Space Requirements by Restaurant Type

| Type | Total Space | Notes |
|------|------------|-------|
| **Restaurant** | Varies | 35% production area, 12% food prep/cooking, 8% receiving/storage, 5% employee facilities |

| **Pizzeria (full-service)** | 2,500-4,000 sq ft | 20% customer service, 45% dining, 35% production |
| **Sandwich shop (takeout)** | 500-750 sq ft | Including kitchen space |

| **Bakery (takeout)** | 1,000-1,500 sq ft | 40%+ kitchen, 35-40% front retail/display |
| **Bakery (with seating)** | Up to 3,000 sq ft | -- |

### Seating Space Per Customer
- Casual-dining restaurant: 15-18 sq ft per seat
- Pizzeria (to-go, self-service): 12-15 sq ft per seat
- Pizzeria (full-service): 15-18 sq ft per seat
- Bar stools: ~2 sq ft per stool
- Tables: 10-12 sq ft per seated customer

### Bar-to-Dining Ratio
- One bar seat for every three dining seats (e.g., 150 dining seats = 50 bar seats)

### Alternative Locations
- Food courts (6-20 restaurants in one location, typically quick-service)
- Carts and kiosks (lower entry cost, test concepts)
- Food trucks (lower overhead, can be moved if location underperforms)
- Pop-up restaurants (temporary, non-traditional venues, test concepts)
- Bump-outs (extension of convenience store or gas station space)
- Dual-branding/co-branding (two brands in one location)

## 4. MENU DEVELOPMENT AND PRICING STRATEGIES

### Menu Pricing Formula
- **Food items**: Net profit ranges from 8-20% depending on the item. General guideline: menu price is approximately 3x the food cost
- **Drinks**: Net profit before taxes can be as high as 60%+ of total selling price. Charge 1,500-4,000% of cost for drinks
- **Catering**: Multiply total food ingredient cost by 3 to get the client price. Divide by number of guests for price per head. This basic method should cover food costs, labour, overhead, and leave 10-20% net profit margin

### Menu Design Principles
- Keep number of items in check; keep descriptions simple and straightforward
- Mark items low in fat, sodium, cholesterol
- Indicate dishes that can be prepared to meet special dietary requirements
- Consider "storytelling" on menus -- history of restaurant, food preparation details, origin info
- Include children's menu (4-5 items in smaller portions) if families are target market
- Consider sharable items (appetiser platters, half portions of entrees)
- Offer seasonal menus and smaller portions ("mini food")
- Value meals: main item + side dish + beverage at bundled lower price

### Menu Trends
- Vegetarian items, tortillas, locally grown produce, organic items
- Fusion dishes (combining two or more ethnic cuisines)
- Microbrewed/local beers
- Pita dishes and wraps as sandwich alternatives
- Espresso and specialty coffees
- Comfort food (redefined and reinvigorated classics)
- Ethnic dishes and spice-infused offerings
- Sustainable, local sourcing; farm-branded ingredients
- Gluten-free and food allergy-conscious items
- Back-to-basics cuisine

### Price Testing
- Balance between charging what customers will pay and generating acceptable profit
- Don't raise prices until customers can't bear it (greedy operators usually fail first)
- Consider competitors' pricing, unique selling proposition, and experience delivered
- For catering labour-intensive menus: increase prices beyond 3x food cost to cover labour
- For expensive-but-simple items (e.g., filet mignon): multiply steak cost by 2, other ingredients by 3

## 5. STAFFING REQUIREMENTS AND LABOUR STRUCTURE

### Restaurant Staffing Categories
- Managers, cooks/chefs, servers, buspersons, dishwashers, hosts, bartenders

### Target Payroll Costs
- **Payroll (including owner salary and managers): 24-35% of total gross sales**
- If payroll exceeds 35% of gross sales, cut costs or increase sales

### Salary Benchmarks
- **Seasoned restaurant manager**: $35,000-$55,000/year plus percentage of sales
- **Entry-level manager**: $28,000-$32,000/year
- **Chefs**: $1,300-$1,800/week (sometimes more)
- **Cooks (experienced, complex menu)**: $575-$650/week
- **Dishwashers**: minimum wage to minimum wage + $1.50/hour
- **Servers**: minimum wage for tipped employees (lower than standard minimum wage) or slightly above; tips are primary income
- **Baristas** (coffeehouse): $7.50-$10.50/hour plus tips
- **Baker/manager** (experienced): $900-$1,300+/week
- **Sandwich shop/deli employees**: 50 cents to $2.00 above minimum wage

### Server Staffing Ratios
- With runners supporting servers: limit each server to 25 seats/covers
- Without runners: limit to no more than 15 seats per server
- Traditional team service (captain, front/back waiter, busboy): 25-30 seats per team

### Restaurant Manager Requirements
- At least 3 years non-management restaurant experience plus 2 years managerial experience
- Work 50-60 hours per week typical
- Prefer background in small/independent restaurants (chain managers may lack independent buying experience)

### Catering Event Staffing Guidelines (for ~100 guests)
- **Formal sit-down dinner**: 2 cooks (or 1 cook + 1-2 assistants), 1 server per table (8-10 per table), 2 bartenders for cocktail service
- **Informal buffet dinner**: 3-4 servers, 1 cook, plus manager; 2 bartenders if cocktail service
- **Cocktail party**: 1-2 bartenders, 2-4 servers (depending on hot/cold hors d'oeuvres)
- **General bartender ratio**: 1 bartender per 50 guests

### Bakery Staffing
- Baker/manager + assistant baker (minimum)
- Salespeople for counter/register
- Delivery people if selling wholesale

### Key HR Insights
- 80% of restaurant owners started in entry-level positions
- Nearly half of all adults have worked in the restaurant industry at some point
- Restaurant industry is the second-largest private employer
- Hiring friends and family requires clear boundaries between professional and personal relationships

## 6. EQUIPMENT AND KITCHEN LAYOUT ESSENTIALS

### Space Allocation (General Restaurant)
- Production area: ~35% of total space
- Food preparation and cooking: ~12% of total space
- Dishwashing and trash: ~4% of total space
- Receiving and inventory storage: up to ~8% of total space
- Employee facilities: no more than ~5% of total space
- Balance for dining room, waiting area, bar, customer service

### Kitchen Design Principles
- Keep production lines short; minimise number of trips to exit for ingredients
- Set up so multiple duties performed at each stop in production process
- Eliminate bottlenecks caused by delays at strategic locations
- Misuse of space is as damaging as misuse of machinery and labour
- Place dishwashing toward rear of kitchen, in a corner
- Locate receiving/storage adjacent to each other, accessible to delivery vehicles
- Use double doors at receiving port; keep dolly/hand truck available
- Prep tables close to cooking surfaces; refrigerator drawers under stovetops

### Essential Equipment by Type

**Full-Service Restaurant Preparation Kitchen**:
Mixer, slicer, preparation sinks, hand-washing sinks, portion scale, food cutter, baker's bins and tables, meat grinder, blender, griddle-top range with oven, convection oven, fryer, cheese melter, broiler, pressureless steamer, steam kettle, refrigerator, freezer

**Service Kitchen**:
Prep and steam table, toaster, heat lamps, microwave oven, utensil rack, roll warmer, sandwich table, coffeemaker, ice machine, beverage stand, soda system, ice cream cabinet, water station

**Anti-fatigue Mats**: $30-$65 for a good 3x5-foot mat; place in all areas where employees stand for long periods

### Buying Used Equipment
- Look for ads in newspapers, trade publications, online
- Watch for bankruptcy sales and special equipment auctions
- Substantial savings possible on good used equipment
- Always inspect condition carefully

## 7. LICENSING, PERMITS, AND REGULATORY REQUIREMENTS

### Required Licences and Permits
1. **Business licence**: Required by virtually all municipalities
2. **Health department permit**: Required before opening; covers food handling, preparation, and storage standards
3. **Liquor licence** (full) or **beer-and-wine licence**: Required if serving alcohol. Beer-and-wine licence is easier to obtain than full liquor licence
4. **Fire department permit**: Required for occupancy and fire safety compliance
5. **Sign permit**: Required by many municipalities for exterior signage
6. **County permit**: May be required depending on jurisdiction
7. **State licences**: Various state-level requirements
8. **Zoning compliance**: Check that your planned location is zoned for food-service use
9. **Music licences**: Required if playing copyrighted music (through ASCAP, BMI, or SESAC)
10. **Federal menu nutrition labelling**: Required for chains with 20+ locations; many states/local governments require it for all

### Alcohol/Liquor Liability
- "Dramshop laws" impose special liability on those selling/serving alcoholic beverages
- Steps to take: train staff on responsible service, check IDs, refuse service to intoxicated patrons
- Consider liquor liability insurance

### Sanitation Standards
- Must comply with local health department regulations
- Regular inspections by health officials
- Corrosion-resistant, food-safe materials required for all food-contact surfaces
- Proper food storage temperatures, handling procedures, cross-contamination prevention

## 8. MARKETING STRATEGIES SPECIFIC TO RESTAURANTS

### Grand Opening
- Make your grand opening truly grand -- first impressions matter

### Key Marketing Approaches
- **Word-of-mouth**: Most powerful marketing tool; great service creates positive word-of-mouth, poor service creates devastating negative word-of-mouth
- **Social media**: Maintain active presence; respond to online reviews
- **Community involvement**: Restaurant owners receive ~75 requests/year from community groups; donate to ~35 projects. Most important reason: giving back; half cite image boost; 40% cite employee recruitment benefit
- **Public relations**: Build strong relationships with local media
- **Coupons**: Among the most popular marketing tools for food-service businesses
- **Trade shows**: Effective for wholesale and catering operations
- **Sampling**: Provide samples of menu items (in bar areas, at tables, at wholesale accounts)
- **Merchandising**: Presentation of food is as important to sales as taste. Attractive plating, creative garnishes, right plate/glass choices
- **Value meals**: Main item + side + beverage bundled at lower price (especially effective for quick-service)

### Coffeehouse-Specific Marketing
- Spend no more than 5% of advertising budget on conventional advertising (location and foot traffic are key)
- "Grab customers by the nose" -- use fans and vents to push coffee aromas out the door
- Set aside room for coffee tasting

### Online and Digital
- Website is essential; renew domain name before it expires
- Online reputation management (ORM) -- monitor and respond to mentions
- Search engine optimisation (SEO)
- Email marketing to customer lists
- Customer databases to track preferences and special occasions

### Advertising Budget Guideline
- Amount spent on advertising is closely related to choice of site and rent
- Mall/shopping centre location = high rent but built-in traffic, minimal advertising needed
- Independent location = lower rent but need advertising budget (up to 6 months in working capital)

## 9. FINANCIAL BENCHMARKS

### Industry-Level Statistics
- Restaurant industry share of the food dollar: 49%
- Restaurant industry sales equal 4% of US GDP
- US food-service operations: grown from 155,000 (40 years ago) to nearly 960,000
- Restaurant industry is the nation's second-largest private employer

### Profit Margins
- **Food items**: Net profit 8-20% depending on the item
- **Drinks/beverages**: Net profit up to 60%+ of selling price
- **Catering**: Net profit margin 10-20% using the 3x food cost formula
- **Coffee/espresso**: "Extremely high" margins (product is 95%+ water)

### Cost Structure Targets
- **Payroll costs** (including owner/managers): 24-35% of gross sales
- **Bakery inventory**: 35% of weekly gross income; 50%+ goes to flour
- **Bakery paper goods**: ~3% of gross revenue
- **Menu price**: Approximately 3x food cost (general guideline)
- **Drink pricing**: 1,500-4,000% markup on cost

### Pricing Formula
- Menu price = ~3x food cost (covers food, labour, overhead, plus profit)
- Net profit on food: 8-20%
- Net profit on drinks: up to 60%+
- Reducing expenses by $5,000 = same bottom-line result as increasing sales by $50,000 (at 10% net profit margin)

### Cost Reduction Strategies
- Reduce portions (use smaller plates so customers don't notice)
- Cut back on pricey ingredients (chicken instead of beef, pasta instead of chicken)
- Use everything (leftover meat/vegetables for soup, stale bread for croutons)
- Buy less expensive paper goods
- Train servers to promote highest-margin items (not necessarily highest-priced)
- Consolidate purchases with single suppliers for quantity discounts
- Put major purchases out for bid annually
- Take advantage of manufacturer rebates and promotions
- Seek co-op promotional support from vendors

### Customer Value Benchmarks
- If average check is $25/person and customer visits 25 times/year = $625/year or $3,000+ over 5 years
- Each customer who brings a friend doubles their value
- Every 10 seconds saved serving drive-thru customers = $1,000 in incremental sales

### Key Financial Statements
1. Profit and Loss (P&L) statement -- monthly, quarterly, or annually
2. Balance sheet -- monthly, quarterly, or annually
3. Cash-flow statement -- monthly, quarterly, or annually
- Review all three at least monthly

### Sources of Startup Funds
1. Personal resources (savings, retirement accounts, real estate equity, personal credit)
2. Family and friends (put everything in writing)
3. Partners (written partnership agreement essential)
4. Government programmes (SBA and special programmes for women, minorities, veterans)
- Most investors want to see owner's own money at risk alongside theirs

## 10. COMMON MISTAKES AND FAILURE FACTORS

### The Three Primary Reasons Food-Service Businesses Fail
1. **Undercapitalisation**
2. **Poor inventory control**
3. **Poor payroll management**

### Additional Failure Factors and Warnings
- **Lack of planning**: Many restaurants fail in first year due to inadequate planning
- **Trying to please everyone**: "When you try to please everyone, you end up pleasing no one." Focus on 5-10% of the market you can capture
- **Theme restaurant trap**: Most people visit once, twice, or maybe three times but don't come back. "Been there, done that" attitude kills repeat business
- **Greedy pricing**: "The greedy people in this business are usually the ones that go out of business first"
- **Inconsistent food**: Once you develop recipes your customers enjoy, be consistent. Don't change things without good reason
- **Ignoring the details**: "This business has a thousand moving parts, and you need to be knowledgeable of all of them"
- **Lack of experience**: Best preparation is to work in someone else's restaurant first (at least a couple of years, doing as many jobs as possible)
- **Poor service**: Guests remember poor service long after they've forgotten how good the food was
- **Arguing with customers**: "When you do, even if you win, you lose" -- customer won't return and will create negative word-of-mouth
- **No exit plan**: Need plan for eventually leaving the business (closing, selling, retiring)
- **Not putting things in writing**: Never assume you have an order until you have a signed contract (especially catering)
- **Poor partner selection**: Going into business with friends/family can damage relationships; need detailed written partnership agreement
- **Failure to listen to customers**: Give them opportunities for feedback (comment cards, website, social media)
- **Over-accommodating individual requests**: Don't stretch resources to unmanageable point for a few customers' wishes

### Success Factors (from experienced operators)
- Start with a job in a similar restaurant first
- Do basic market research before opening
- Test your real market (not just friends giving polite compliments)
- Find a market niche and stay focused
- Never let customers leave hungry or unhappy
- Make food consistent
- Recognise the lifetime value of a customer
- Build relationships with suppliers (they are your "life blood")
- Give back to the community
- Provide employees with feedback and recognition
- Stay open to new ideas
- Never say "no" to customers -- tell them what you CAN do instead
- Have an exit plan from the beginning

- Lunch is the most popular meal to eat away from home

- 66% of adults visit a restaurant on their own or someone else's birthday (most popular occasion)
