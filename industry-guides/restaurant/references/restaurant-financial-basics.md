# Restaurant Financial Basics - Industry Guide Reference

**Source:** Schmidgall, R.S., Hayes, D.K., & Ninemeier, J.D. *Restaurant Financial Basics*
**Accounting Standard Referenced:** Uniform System of Accounts for Restaurants, 7th Edition (National Restaurant Association, 1996)

> Figures adapted to Ugandan context (UGX). Percentages and ratios from the source are universal.

---

## 1. Restaurant Financial Statements

### 1.1 Income Statement (Profit & Loss) Structure

The restaurant income statement follows the Uniform System of Accounts for Restaurants format:

```
SALES:
  Food                                          UGX       %
  Beverage                                      UGX       %
  Total Sales                                   UGX     100%

COST OF SALES:
  Food
  Beverage
  Total Cost of Sales
Gross Profit

OPERATING EXPENSES:
  Salaries and Wages
  Employee Benefits
  Direct Operating Expenses
  Music and Entertainment
  Marketing
  Utility Services
  General and Administrative Expenses
  Repairs and Maintenance
  Occupancy Costs
  Depreciation
  Other Income
  Total Operating Expenses

Operating Income

  Interest
Income Before Income Taxes
  Income Taxes
NET INCOME                                      UGX       %
```

**Key distinctions in the restaurant P&L:**
- Food and beverage sales are reported separately
- Cost of sales is broken out by food and beverage
- Gross profit is calculated for each department
- Operating expenses are categorised into controllable and non-controllable
- "Operating income" represents the point below which expenses are generally outside the manager's control (occupancy costs and depreciation)

### 1.2 Balance Sheet Structure

Restaurant balance sheets contain:

**Assets:**
- **Current Assets:** Cash, accounts receivable (net), inventory (food/beverage), marketable securities, prepaid expenses
- **Fixed Assets:** Land, buildings, furniture & fixtures, equipment (FF&E), leasehold improvements, linens/china/glassware/silver/utensils/uniforms
- **Investments:** Surplus funds not convertible within one year
- **Other Assets:** Goodwill, bar licence costs, rental deposits, organisation costs, preopening costs, franchise fees

**Liabilities:**
- **Current Liabilities:** Accounts payable (trade), accounts payable (other), notes payable, salaries/wages payable, payroll/withholding taxes, sales tax payable, deposits on banquets, income taxes payable, current portion of long-term debt
- **Long-Term Liabilities:** Mortgage payable, notes payable (long-term), bonds payable, obligations under capital leases

**Owners' Equity:** Capital/retained earnings (proprietorship), stockholders' equity (corporation)

**Key formula:**
```
Assets = Liabilities + Owners' Equity
```

### 1.3 Statement of Cash Flows (SCF)

Three sections:
1. **Operating Activities** - Net income adjusted for non-cash items (depreciation, gains/losses) and changes in working capital
2. **Investing Activities** - Purchase/sale of investments, equipment
3. **Financing Activities** - Debt payments, dividends, stock issuance

**Example format (Anytown Restaurant):**
```
Net Cash Flow From Operating Activities:
  Net income                                     UGX 30,500,000
  Adjustments:
    Depreciation                                  20,600,000
    Gain on sale of investments                   (4,120,000)
    Decrease in accounts receivable                1,650,000
    Increase in inventory                           (824,000)
    Increase in accounts payable                     206,000
    Increase in accrued payroll                       206,000
    Decrease in income taxes payable                 (412,000)
  Net cash flow from operating activities        UGX 47,806,000

Investing Activities:
  Sale of investments                             22,660,000
  Purchase of investments                         (6,180,000)
  Purchase of equipment                           (8,240,000)
  Net cash flow used in investing activities    (UGX 12,360,000)

Financing Activities:
  Payment of long-term debt                      (10,712,000)
  Dividends paid                                 (12,360,000)
  Net cash flow used in financing activities   (UGX 23,072,000)

Net increase in cash                            UGX 12,374,000
```

## 2. Revenue Forecasting for Restaurants

### Revenue Estimation Factors

Food, beverage, and other revenue forecasts should be prepared **separately** and on a **monthly** basis (or weekly), then combined for an annual forecast.

**Factors to consider:**
- **Sales histories** - Past sales analysed for trends; if food revenue increased by a consistent percentage over prior years, project similar growth
- **Economic conditions** - Local economy, employment levels, consumer confidence
- **Competition** - New entrants, closures, competitor pricing changes
- **Menu changes** - New items, price adjustments, concept changes
- **Marketing initiatives** - Planned promotions, advertising campaigns
- **Capacity changes** - Seating expansions, new meal periods, extended hours
- **Seasonal patterns** - Monthly variations (e.g. college-town restaurants may see lower holiday-period trade)

### Revenue Calculation Method

```
Revenue = Number of Seats x Seat Turnover x Average Check x Days Open
```

**Seat turnover** varies significantly by restaurant type:
- Fast food: highest seat turnover (multiple times per meal period)
- Full-service/fine dining: lower seat turnover
- Typical range: 1.5-3.0 per meal period depending on concept

**Revenue by meal period calculation:**
```
Revenue per meal period = Total food revenue x Estimated % earned in that meal period
Meals sold per meal period = Days open x Seat turnover x Number of seats
Average meal price per period = Revenue per meal period / Meals sold per meal period
```

**Example (Segee's Restaurant, 100 seats):**
- Lunch seat turnover: 1.25; Dinner seat turnover: 0.75
- Revenue split: 40% lunch, 60% dinner
- Total food revenue required: UGX 673M
- Lunch revenue: UGX 269M; Dinner revenue: UGX 404M
- Lunch meals: 365 x 100 x 1.25 = 45,625; Dinner meals: 365 x 100 x 0.75 = 27,375
- Average lunch price: UGX 5,900; Average dinner price: UGX 14,760

## 3. Food Cost Percentage Targets and Management

### Key Formulas

```
Food Cost % = Cost of Food Sold / Food Sales x 100

Cost of Food Sold = Beginning Inventory + Purchases - Ending Inventory
                    - Employee Meals - Food Used to Garnish Beverages

Gross Profit (Food) = Food Sales - Cost of Food Sold

Gross Profit % (Food) = Gross Profit / Food Sales x 100
```

### Benchmarks and Targets

- **Typical food cost percentage:** 37-40% of food sales (the book's example restaurant, Smithville, shows 37.5% in Year 1 and 39.7% in Year 2, with the increase flagged as a concern)
- **Common "rule of thumb":** Many managers use 40% food cost as a baseline target
- **Gross profit percentage (food):** 60-62.5% is typical

**Important distinctions:**
- "Cost of food used" differs from "cost of food sold" -- employee meals are actually a labour cost, food used for beverage garnishes is a beverage cost
- Food costs are **variable costs** that change proportionally with food sales volume
- A 10% increase in food sales should produce approximately a 10% increase in cost of food sold

### Causes of Food Cost Increases
- Food costs increased faster than menu prices
- Sales mix shifted toward higher-cost menu items
- Physical inventory at period-end is understated
- Improper recording of other expenditures as food purchases
- Portion size inconsistency, waste, spoilage, theft

### Food Inventory Turnover

```
Food Inventory Turnover = Cost of Food Used / Average Food Inventory
```

**Benchmark:** A food inventory turnover of 29.4 times per year = approximately every 12.4 days. Fast food restaurants turn food inventory several times per week. Higher turnover is generally better, provided there are adequate stocks and few stockouts.

## 4. Labour Cost Percentage Targets and Management

### Key Formulas

```
Labour Cost % = Total Labour Costs / Total Sales x 100

Labour Costs = Payroll Costs + Employee Benefits

Payroll Costs = Fixed Payroll (salaries) + Variable Payroll (hourly wages)
```

### Components of Labour Cost

**Fixed Payroll (salaries):**
- Management salaries
- Remains unchanged from one pay period to the next
- Manager may have little control over these

**Variable Payroll (hourly wages):**
- Compensation paid to hourly employees
- Varies with changes in business volume
- Manager has significant control over these above minimum-staff levels

**Labour Expense vs. Payroll Expense:**
- Labour expense is always larger than payroll expense
- Labour expense = payroll + employee benefits + employment taxes
- As benefits costs increase, labour expense rises even if payroll stays constant

**Controllable vs. Non-controllable:**
- Controllable: payroll expenses (manager has total control)
- Non-controllable: certain benefits mandated by law, union-negotiated costs

### Benchmarks

- **Typical labour cost percentage:** 31-33% of total sales (Smithville Restaurant example: 31.7% in Year 1, 32.4% in Year 2 -- the increase was noted as requiring close monitoring)
- Labour is the second largest expense after food/beverage cost for most restaurants

### Labour Analysis Ratios

```
Total sales per full-time equivalent employee (FTE)
Food sales per FTE
Beverage sales per FTE
Average hourly wage per employee
```

**Labour category breakdown format:**
```
Labour Category        Payroll Costs     % of Total Payroll
Food Service           UGX               %
Beverage               UGX               %
Preparation            UGX               %
Sanitation             UGX               %
Purchasing/Storing     UGX               %
Administrative         UGX               %
Other                  UGX               %
Total                  UGX               100%
```

## 5. Prime Cost (Food + Labour) Benchmarks

### Definition and Formula

```
Prime Cost = Cost of Food Sold + Cost of Beverages Sold + Total Labour Costs

Prime Cost % = Prime Cost / Total Sales x 100
```

### Application in Pricing (Simple Prime Cost Method)

**Steps:**
1. Determine labour cost per guest: Labour costs / Number of expected guests
2. Determine prime cost per guest: Labour cost per guest + Menu item ingredient cost
3. Determine base selling price: Prime cost per guest / Desired prime cost %

**Example:**
```
Labour costs:          UGX 86,520,000
Expected guests:        75,000
Desired prime cost %:   62%
Ingredient cost:        UGX 4,800

Labour cost per guest = UGX 86,520,000 / 75,000 = UGX 1,154
Prime cost per guest  = UGX 4,800 + UGX 1,154 = UGX 5,954
Base selling price    = UGX 5,954 / 0.62 = UGX 9,603 --> round to UGX 9,500
```

### Benchmark

- **Desired prime cost percentage:** 62% of total sales (as used in the book's pricing example)
- This means food cost % + labour cost % should total approximately 60-65%
- Using typical benchmarks: Food ~37-40% + Labour ~31-33% = Prime cost ~68-73%
- A well-managed restaurant should target the lower end of this range

## 6. Operating Expense Categories and Benchmarks

### Expense Categories (per Uniform System of Accounts)

**Controllable Expenses:**
1. **Salaries and Wages** - All employee compensation
2. **Employee Benefits** - Insurance, retirement, taxes, meals
3. **Direct Operating Expenses** - Uniforms, laundry, supplies, menus, kitchen fuel, items incidental to dining room service, kitchen and storage support
4. **Music and Entertainment** - If significant; otherwise record as miscellaneous under direct operating expenses
5. **Marketing** - Newspaper, magazine, radio/TV advertising, outdoor signs, direct mailings, donations, promotional entertainment
6. **Utility Services** - All utilities except kitchen fuel (which is a direct operating expense); if facilities are rented and restaurant pays utilities, record as utilities not rent
7. **General and Administrative** - Office supplies, professional fees, credit card fees, telephone, postage, insurance (not employee benefits)
8. **Repairs and Maintenance** - Building and equipment upkeep

**Non-Controllable Expenses (generally outside manager's control):**
9. **Occupancy Costs** - Rent, property taxes, property insurance
10. **Depreciation** - Systematic allocation of fixed asset costs

### Operating Expense Analysis

```
Operating Expense % = Specific Expense / Total Revenue x 100
```

**Example benchmarks (Smithville Restaurant):**
```
Expense Category                Year 1      Year 2
Utilities expense %             3.0%        3.1%
Depreciation %                  ~2.7%       ~2.7%
```

### Key Principle

- Certain expenses contain both fixed and variable elements
- As total sales increase, the expense percentage based on total revenue should decline (for expenses with fixed components)
- All controllable expense categories should be routinely compared to total revenue as operating ratios
- **Profit margin for most restaurants is less than 10%**, meaning more than 90% of revenue goes to costs
