**Source:** Schmidgall, R.S., Hayes, D.K., & Ninemeier, J.D. *Restaurant Financial Basics*
**Accounting Standard Referenced:** Uniform System of Accounts for Restaurants, 7th Edition (National Restaurant Association, 1996)

## 1. Restaurant Financial Statements

### 1.1 Income Statement (Profit & Loss) Structure

The restaurant income statement follows the Uniform System of Accounts for Restaurants format:

```
SALES:
  Food                                          $        %
  Beverage                                      $        %
  Total Sales                                   $      100%

COST OF SALES:
  Food
  Beverage
  Total Cost of Sales
Gross Profit

OPERATING EXPENSES:
  Salaries and Wages
  Employee Benefits
  Direct Operating Expenses
  Music and Entertainment
  Marketing
  Utility Services
  General and Administrative Expenses
  Repairs and Maintenance
  Occupancy Costs
  Depreciation
  Other Income
  Total Operating Expenses

Operating Income

  Interest
Income Before Income Taxes
  Income Taxes
NET INCOME                                      $        %
```

**Key distinctions in the restaurant P&L:**
- Food and beverage sales are reported separately
- Cost of sales is broken out by food and beverage
- Gross profit is calculated for each department
- Operating expenses are categorised into controllable and non-controllable
- "Operating income" represents the point below which expenses are generally outside the manager's control (occupancy costs and depreciation)

### 1.2 Balance Sheet Structure

Restaurant balance sheets contain:

**Assets:**
- **Current Assets:** Cash, accounts receivable (net), inventory (food/beverage), marketable securities, prepaid expenses
- **Fixed Assets:** Land, buildings, furniture & fixtures, equipment (FF&E), leasehold improvements, linens/china/glassware/silver/utensils/uniforms
- **Investments:** Surplus funds not convertible within one year
- **Other Assets:** Goodwill, bar licence costs, rental deposits, organisation costs, preopening costs, franchise fees

**Liabilities:**
- **Current Liabilities:** Accounts payable (trade), accounts payable (other), notes payable, salaries/wages payable, payroll/withholding taxes, sales tax payable, deposits on banquets, income taxes payable, current portion of long-term debt
- **Long-Term Liabilities:** Mortgage payable, notes payable (long-term), bonds payable, obligations under capital leases

**Owners' Equity:** Capital/retained earnings (proprietorship), stockholders' equity (corporation)

**Key formula:**
```
Assets = Liabilities + Owners' Equity
```

### 1.3 Statement of Cash Flows (SCF)

Three sections:
1. **Operating Activities** - Net income adjusted for non-cash items (depreciation, gains/losses) and changes in working capital
2. **Investing Activities** - Purchase/sale of investments, equipment
3. **Financing Activities** - Debt payments, dividends, stock issuance

**Example format (Anytown Restaurant):**
```
Net Cash Flow From Operating Activities:
  Net income                                     $74,000
  Adjustments:
    Depreciation                                  50,000
    Gain on sale of investments                  (10,000)
    Decrease in accounts receivable                4,000
    Increase in inventory                         (2,000)
    Increase in accounts payable                     500
    Increase in accrued payroll                       500
    Decrease in income taxes payable              (1,000)
  Net cash flow from operating activities       $116,000

Investing Activities:
  Sale of investments                             55,000
  Purchase of investments                        (15,000)
  Purchase of equipment                          (20,000)
  Net cash flow used in investing activities     ($30,000)

Financing Activities:
  Payment of long-term debt                      (26,000)
  Dividends paid                                 (30,000)
  Net cash flow used in financing activities    ($56,000)

Net increase in cash                             $30,000
```

## 2. Revenue Forecasting for Restaurants

### Revenue Estimation Factors

Food, beverage, and other revenue forecasts should be prepared **separately** and on a **monthly** basis (or weekly), then combined for an annual forecast.

**Factors to consider:**
- **Sales histories** - Past sales analysed for trends; if food revenue increased by a consistent percentage over prior years, project similar growth
- **Economic conditions** - Local economy, employment levels, consumer confidence
- **Competition** - New entrants, closures, competitor pricing changes
- **Menu changes** - New items, price adjustments, concept changes
- **Marketing initiatives** - Planned promotions, advertising campaigns
- **Capacity changes** - Seating expansions, new meal periods, extended hours
- **Seasonal patterns** - Monthly variations (e.g. college-town restaurants may see lower summer trade)

### Revenue Calculation Method

```
Revenue = Number of Seats x Seat Turnover x Average Check x Days Open
```

**Seat turnover** varies significantly by restaurant type:
- Fast food: highest seat turnover (multiple times per meal period)
- Full-service/fine dining: lower seat turnover
- Typical range: 1.5-3.0 per meal period depending on concept

**Revenue by meal period calculation:**
```
Revenue per meal period = Total food revenue x Estimated % earned in that meal period
Meals sold per meal period = Days open x Seat turnover x Number of seats
Average meal price per period = Revenue per meal period / Meals sold per meal period
```

**Example (Segee's Restaurant, 100 seats):**
- Lunch seat turnover: 1.25; Dinner seat turnover: 0.75
- Revenue split: 40% lunch, 60% dinner
- Total food revenue required: $1,633,333
- Lunch revenue: $653,333; Dinner revenue: $980,000
- Lunch meals: 365 x 100 x 1.25 = 45,625; Dinner meals: 365 x 100 x 0.75 = 27,375
- Average lunch price: $14.32; Average dinner price: $35.80

## 3. Food Cost Percentage Targets and Management

### Key Formulas

```
Food Cost % = Cost of Food Sold / Food Sales x 100

Cost of Food Sold = Beginning Inventory + Purchases - Ending Inventory
                    - Employee Meals - Food Used to Garnish Beverages

Gross Profit (Food) = Food Sales - Cost of Food Sold

Gross Profit % (Food) = Gross Profit / Food Sales x 100
```

### Benchmarks and Targets

- **Typical food cost percentage:** 37-40% of food sales (the book's example restaurant, Smithville, shows 37.5% in Year 1 and 39.7% in Year 2, with the increase flagged as a concern)
- **Common "rule of thumb":** Many managers use 40% food cost as a baseline target
- **Gross profit percentage (food):** 60-62.5% is typical

**Important distinctions:**
- "Cost of food used" differs from "cost of food sold" -- employee meals are actually a labour cost, food used for beverage garnishes is a beverage cost
- Food costs are **variable costs** that change proportionally with food sales volume
- A 10% increase in food sales should produce approximately a 10% increase in cost of food sold

### Causes of Food Cost Increases
- Food costs increased faster than menu prices
- Sales mix shifted toward higher-cost menu items
- Physical inventory at period-end is understated
- Improper recording of other expenditures as food purchases
- Portion size inconsistency, waste, spoilage, theft

### Food Inventory Turnover

```
Food Inventory Turnover = Cost of Food Used / Average Food Inventory
```

**Benchmark:** A food inventory turnover of 29.4 times per year = approximately every 12.4 days. Fast food restaurants turn food inventory several times per week. Higher turnover is generally better, provided there are adequate stocks and few stockouts.

## 4. Labour Cost Percentage Targets and Management

### Key Formulas

```
Labour Cost % = Total Labour Costs / Total Sales x 100

Labour Costs = Payroll Costs + Employee Benefits

Payroll Costs = Fixed Payroll (salaries) + Variable Payroll (hourly wages)
```

### Components of Labour Cost

**Fixed Payroll (salaries):**
- Management salaries
- Remains unchanged from one pay period to the next
- Manager may have little control over these

**Variable Payroll (hourly wages):**
- Compensation paid to hourly employees
- Varies with changes in business volume
- Manager has significant control over these above minimum-staff levels

**Labour Expense vs. Payroll Expense:**
- Labour expense is always larger than payroll expense
- Labour expense = payroll + employee benefits + employment taxes
- As benefits costs increase, labour expense rises even if payroll stays constant

**Controllable vs. Non-controllable:**
- Controllable: payroll expenses (manager has total control)
- Non-controllable: certain benefits mandated by law, union-negotiated costs

### Benchmarks

- **Typical labour cost percentage:** 31-33% of total sales (Smithville Restaurant example: 31.7% in Year 1, 32.4% in Year 2 -- the increase was noted as requiring close monitoring)
- Labour is the second largest expense after food/beverage cost for most restaurants

### Labour Analysis Ratios

```
Total sales per full-time equivalent employee (FTE)
Food sales per FTE
Beverage sales per FTE
Average hourly wage per employee
```

**Labour category breakdown format:**
```
Labour Category        Payroll Costs     % of Total Payroll
Food Service           $                 %
Beverage               $                 %
Preparation            $                 %
Sanitation             $                 %
Purchasing/Storing     $                 %
Administrative         $                 %
Other                  $                 %
Total                  $                 100%
```

## 5. Prime Cost (Food + Labour) Benchmarks

### Definition and Formula

```
Prime Cost = Cost of Food Sold + Cost of Beverages Sold + Total Labour Costs

Prime Cost % = Prime Cost / Total Sales x 100
```

### Application in Pricing (Simple Prime Cost Method)

**Steps:**
1. Determine labour cost per guest: Labour costs / Number of expected guests
2. Determine prime cost per guest: Labour cost per guest + Menu item ingredient cost
3. Determine base selling price: Prime cost per guest / Desired prime cost %

**Example:**
```
Labour costs:          $210,000
Expected guests:        75,000
Desired prime cost %:   62%
Ingredient cost:        $3.75

Labour cost per guest = $210,000 / 75,000 = $2.80
Prime cost per guest  = $3.75 + $2.80 = $6.55
Base selling price    = $6.55 / 0.62 = $10.56
```

### Benchmark

- **Desired prime cost percentage:** 62% of total sales (as used in the book's pricing example)
- This means food cost % + labour cost % should total approximately 60-65%
- Using typical benchmarks: Food ~37-40% + Labour ~31-33% = Prime cost ~68-73%
- A well-managed restaurant should target the lower end of this range

## 6. Operating Expense Categories and Benchmarks

### Expense Categories (per Uniform System of Accounts)

**Controllable Expenses:**
1. **Salaries and Wages** - All employee compensation
2. **Employee Benefits** - Insurance, retirement, taxes, meals
3. **Direct Operating Expenses** - Uniforms, laundry, supplies, menus, kitchen fuel, items incidental to dining room service, kitchen and storage support
4. **Music and Entertainment** - If significant; otherwise record as miscellaneous under direct operating expenses
5. **Marketing** - Newspaper, magazine, radio/TV advertising, outdoor signs, direct mailings, donations, promotional entertainment
6. **Utility Services** - All utilities except kitchen fuel (which is a direct operating expense); if facilities are rented and restaurant pays utilities, record as utilities not rent
7. **General and Administrative** - Office supplies, professional fees, credit card fees, telephone, postage, insurance (not employee benefits)
8. **Repairs and Maintenance** - Building and equipment upkeep

**Non-Controllable Expenses (generally outside manager's control):**
9. **Occupancy Costs** - Rent, property taxes, property insurance
10. **Depreciation** - Systematic allocation of fixed asset costs

### Operating Expense Analysis

```
Operating Expense % = Specific Expense / Total Revenue x 100
```

**Example benchmarks (Smithville Restaurant):**
```
Expense Category                Year 1      Year 2
Utilities expense %             3.0%        3.1%
Depreciation %                  ~2.7%       ~2.7%
```

### Key Principle

- Certain expenses contain both fixed and variable elements
- As total sales increase, the expense percentage based on total revenue should decline (for expenses with fixed components)
- All controllable expense categories should be routinely compared to total revenue as operating ratios
- **Profit margin for most restaurants is less than 10%**, meaning more than 90% of revenue goes to costs

## 7. Break-Even Analysis for Restaurants

### Core Formula

```
Break-Even Point: Net Income = 0

0 = (Selling Price x Units Sold) - (Variable Cost per Unit x Units Sold) - Total Fixed Costs

Rearranged:

Units at Break-Even = Total Fixed Costs / (Selling Price - Variable Cost per Unit)

Revenue at Break-Even = Total Fixed Costs / Contribution Margin Ratio
```

### The Four Break-Even Variables

```
1. Meals at break-even = Fixed Costs / (Selling Price - Variable Cost per Meal)

2. Fixed costs at break-even = (Selling Price x Meals Sold) - (Variable Cost x Meals Sold)

3. Selling price at break-even = (Fixed Costs / Meals Sold) + Variable Cost per Meal

4. Variable cost at break-even = Selling Price - (Fixed Costs / Meals Sold)
```

### Worked Example (Michael's Bistro)

```
Annual fixed costs:           $108,000
Average selling price/meal:   $15
Variable cost per meal:       $6
Contribution margin per meal: $9  ($15 - $6)

Break-even meals = $108,000 / ($15 - $6) = 12,000 meals
Break-even revenue = 12,000 x $15 = $180,000

To earn $18,000 pretax profit:
Meals required = ($108,000 + $18,000) / ($15 - $6) = 14,000 meals
Revenue required = 14,000 x $15 = $210,000
Additional meals beyond break-even: 2,000
Proof: 2,000 x $9 CM = $18,000 profit
```

### Contribution Margin

```
Contribution Margin (CM) = Selling Price - Variable Costs per Unit
```

- The CM represents what each meal contributes toward fixed costs and profit after variable costs are covered
- Beyond break-even, each additional meal sold generates profit equal to its CM
- Below break-even, each unsold meal represents a loss equal to its CM

### Margin of Safety

```
Margin of Safety = Budgeted (or Actual) Sales - Break-Even Sales
```

**Example:**
```
                    Break-Even      Target ($18K profit)    Margin of Safety
Sales ($)           $180,000        $210,000                $30,000
Sales (meals)       12,000          14,000                  2,000
```

### Break-Even Assumptions and Limitations
- Fixed costs remain fixed during the period
- Variable costs change linearly with revenues
- Revenues are directly proportional to volume
- Mixed costs can be separated into fixed and variable components
- Only quantitative factors are considered (not qualitative factors like employee morale, guest goodwill)

## 8. Cash Flow Management

### Cash Increases When:
- Accounts receivable decrease (monies owed are collected)
- Inventories decrease (products withdrawn from inventory rather than purchased)
- Borrowing increases (cash received from financial institutions)
- Accounts payable and accrued expenses increase (purchases made on credit rather than cash)

### Cash Decreases When:
- Accounts receivable increase (money owed is not being paid)
- Inventory increases (products purchased and stored)
- Borrowing decreases (cash used to pay interest and debt)
- Accounts payable and accrued expenses decrease (cash used to pay debts)

### Cash Budget Format

```
Cash Budget (Monthly, up to 6 months forward)

Cash - Beginning of Month                       $
Estimated Cash Receipts:
  Cash Sales
  Collection of Accounts Receivable
  Proceeds from Bank Loans
  Proceeds from Sale of Fixed Assets
  Other
  Total Estimated Cash Receipts                  $
Estimated Cash Available                         $

Estimated Cash Disbursements:
  Inventory (Food/Beverage Purchases)
  Payroll
  Operating Expenses
  Taxes
  Insurance
  Mortgage Payments
  Rent
  Other
  Total Estimated Cash Disbursements             $

Estimated Cash - End of Month                    $
Minimum Cash Required                           $
Cash Excess (Shortage)                           $
```

### Key Cash Budget Inputs
- **Cash vs. credit sales split** (example: 80% cash, 20% charge)
- **Collection patterns** for credit sales (example: 20% collected in month of sale, 80% in following month)
- **Food purchase payment patterns** (example: 30% paid in month prior, 70% in current month)
- **Salaries/wages/benefits** paid in month expensed
- **Other operating expenses** (example: 50% paid in month expensed, 50% in following month)
- **Marketing** may be prepaid (e.g. annual advertising contract paid in January)
- **Depreciation is a non-cash expense** -- requires no cash outflow
- **Debt payments** include both interest (on P&L) and principal reduction (not on P&L)

### Effective Interest Rate (Compensating Balances)

```
Effective Interest Rate = Annual Interest on Loan / (Loan - Compensating Balance)
```

**Example:** $100,000 loan at 10% with $10,000 compensating balance:
```
Interest = $100,000 x 10% x 1 year = $10,000
Effective rate = $10,000 / ($100,000 - $10,000) = 11.1%
```

### Minimum Cash Cushion
- Maintain a predetermined minimum cash balance (e.g. $5,000) at end of each month
- Excess above cushion should be invested temporarily
- If projected cash falls below minimum, liquidate temporary investments or arrange short-term financing

## 9. Menu Engineering and Contribution Margin Analysis

### Menu Engineering Framework

Menu engineering classifies each menu item based on two dimensions:
1. **Contribution Margin (CM):** Item's selling price minus food cost
2. **Popularity (Menu Mix %):** Percentage of total units sold

### The Four Menu Item Classifications

| Classification | CM Level | Popularity | Strategy |
|---------------|----------|------------|----------|
| **Star**      | High     | High       | Maintain quality, feature prominently |
| **Plowhorse** | Low      | High       | Increase price carefully, reduce cost, reposition |
| **Puzzle**    | High     | Low        | Promote more, reposition on menu, rename |
| **Dog**       | Low      | Low        | Remove from menu, replace, or raise price significantly |

### Popularity Threshold (70% Rule)

An item is considered "popular" if it sells at least **70% of expected sales**:
```
Expected sales per item = 100% / Number of menu items
Popularity threshold = Expected sales per item x 70%

Example: 6 menu items
Expected per item: 100% / 6 = 16.7%
Popularity threshold: 16.7% x 70% = 11.7%
Items with mix % >= 11.7% are "High" popularity
```

### CM Threshold

An item has "High" CM if its individual CM is equal to or greater than the **weighted average CM** for all items.

```
Weighted Average CM = Total Menu CM / Total Number of Items Sold
```

### Menu Engineering Worksheet

```
Columns:
A - Menu Item Name
B - Number Sold
C - Menu Mix % (B / Total B)
D - Item Food Cost
E - Item Selling Price
F - Item CM (E - D)
G - Menu Costs (D x B)
H - Menu Revenues (E x B)
I - Total Menu Costs (sum of G)
J - Total Menu Revenues (sum of H)
K - Average Food Cost (I / Total B)
L - Menu CM (H - G)
M - Total Menu CM (sum of L)
N - Average CM (M / Total B)
O - CM Category (High if F >= N, else Low)
P - MM% Category (High if C >= threshold, else Low)
Q - Popularity threshold = (100% / number of items) x 70%
R - Classification (Star/Plowhorse/Puzzle/Dog)
```

### Worked Example (Midtown Plaza, Dinner)

```
Item                 Sold    Mix%    Cost    Price   CM      Classification
Beef Stew            350     17.5%   $4.10   $10.00  $5.90   Plowhorse
Chow Mein            210     10.5%   $4.40   $11.25  $6.85   Puzzle
Ham Cheese Sandwich  100      5.0%   $4.80   $10.50  $5.70   Dog
BLT Sandwich         450     22.5%   $4.90   $12.50  $7.60   Star
Hamburger Platter    510     25.5%   $4.45   $11.45  $7.00   Star
Chicken Sandwich     380     19.0%   $2.75   $18.50  $5.75   Plowhorse
Total              2,000    100.0%

Average CM = $13,248.50 / 2,000 = $6.62
Popularity threshold = (100% / 6) x 70% = 11.7%
```

## 10. Financial Ratios Specific to Restaurant Industry

### 10.1 Liquidity Ratios

**Current Ratio:**
```
Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
```
Indicates ability to pay current obligations. Higher is better but context matters.

**Accounts Receivable to Charge Sales:**
```
AR Turnover = Charge Sales / Average Accounts Receivable
Average Collection Period = 365 / AR Turnover
```
Example: AR Turnover of 7.7 times = average collection period of 47.4 days. Compare to credit terms (e.g. 30-day terms would indicate a collection problem).

### 10.2 Solvency Ratios

**Solvency Ratio:**
```
Solvency Ratio = Total Assets / Total Liabilities
```
A restaurant is solvent when assets exceed liabilities (ratio > 1.0).

**Debt-Equity Ratio:**
```
Debt-Equity Ratio = Total Liabilities / Total Owners' Equity
```
Higher ratio = greater financial risk. The greater the debt financing relative to equity, the harder it is to secure additional financing.

**Times Interest Earned:**
```
Times Interest Earned = (Net Income + Interest Expense + Income Tax) / Interest Expense
```

### 10.3 Activity Ratios

**Food Inventory Turnover:**
```
Food Inventory Turnover = Cost of Food Used / Average Food Inventory
Days in Inventory = 365 / Food Inventory Turnover
```
Example: 29.4 times/year = every 12.4 days. Fast food may turn inventory several times weekly.

**Fixed Asset Turnover:**
```
Fixed Asset Turnover = Total Revenue / Average Fixed Assets
```
Example: 1.5-1.6 times (measures how well fixed assets generate revenue).

**Asset Turnover:**
```
Asset Turnover = Total Revenue / Average Total Assets
```
Example: 1.15-1.18 times.

**Seat Turnover (non-financial):**
```
Seat Turnover = Guests Served / (Days Open x Available Seats)
```
Example: 2.05-2.08 times average daily. Calculate separately by meal period, day of week, season, and profit centre (restaurant vs. bar/lounge).

### 10.4 Profitability Ratios

**Return on Owners' Equity:**
```
ROE = Net Income / Average Owners' Equity
```
Example: 15-16%.

**Return on Assets:**
```
ROA = Net Income / Average Total Assets
```
Example: 6.9-7.3%.

**Profit Margin:**
```
Profit Margin = Net Income / Total Revenue
```
Example: 5.8-6.3%. Most restaurants have a profit margin of **less than 10%**.

**Managerial Operating Efficiency:**
```
MOE = (Operating Income + Depreciation + Occupancy Expenses) / Total Revenue
```
Measures manager's performance on items within their control. Example: 18.8-19.7%.

**Earnings Per Share (corporations):**
```
EPS = Net Income / Average Number of Shares Outstanding
```

### 10.5 Operating Ratios

**Food/Beverage Sales Ratio:**
```
Food Sales % = Food Sales / Total Sales
Beverage Sales % = Beverage Sales / Total Sales
```
Example: Food 69-70%, Beverage 30-31%. Higher beverage percentage generally suggests greater profits due to higher beverage contribution margins.

**Sales Per Seat:**
```
Food Sales Per Seat = Food Sales / Restaurant Seats
Beverage Sales Per Seat = Beverage Sales / Lounge/Bar Seats
```
Example: Food $5,000-$5,250/seat; Beverage $6,450-$7,025/seat (annually).

**Cost of Sales Ratios:**
```
Food Cost % = Cost of Food Sold / Food Sales
Beverage Cost % = Cost of Beverages Sold / Beverage Sales
```
Example: Food cost 37.5-39.7%; Beverage cost 24.2-24.4%.

**Labour Cost Ratio:**
```
Labour Cost % = Total Labour Costs / Total Sales
```
Example: 31.7-32.4%.

### Bases for Ratio Comparison (in order of preference)
1. **Budget (plan)** - Preferred; considers past, competition, and future expectations
2. **Prior period** - Common but limited; dynamic environment makes direct comparisons imprecise
3. **Industry averages** - Useful for new operations but are broad averages across diverse geographies and concepts
4. **Specific competitors** - Often limited by unavailability of detailed competitor data

### Limitations of Ratio Analysis
- Based on historical costs, not current market value
- Different restaurants use different accounting procedures (depreciation methods, inventory costing)
- Ratios alert to potential problems but do not identify causes or solutions
- A higher food cost % may result from a more profitable sales mix despite the percentage increase

## 11. Capital Budgeting for Restaurant Startups

### Fixed Asset Capitalisation

**Capital expenditures** (recorded as assets) vs. **revenue expenditures** (expensed immediately):
- Capital expenditures: purchase of equipment, buildings, land, leasehold improvements
- Revenue expenditures: repairs, maintenance, supplies

**Fixed asset cost includes:**
- Purchase price
- Taxes and duties
- Freight/shipping charges
- Insurance during transit
- Installation charges
- Construction costs (materials, labour, overhead, architect fees, permits, construction financing)

### Startup Cost Categories (Other Assets)

**Organisation Costs:**
- Legal fees, promotional costs, stock certificate costs, accounting fees, state incorporation fees
- Amortised against revenue over **3-5 years**

**Preopening Costs:**
- Payroll prior to opening, training, promotional costs
- Amortised against revenue over **1-3 years**

**Franchise Fees:**
- Initial fee plus legal and other costs to obtain franchise
- Amortised over the **life of the franchise agreement**
- Continuing royalties based on revenue are expensed as incurred

**Bar/Liquor Licence:**
- Recorded as other asset
- Amortised over a reasonable number of years

**Leasehold Improvements:**
- Amortised over the shorter of: life of the lease OR life of the improvement

### Depreciation Methods

**Straight-Line (SL):**
```
Annual Depreciation = (Cost - Salvage Value) / Useful Life

Example: Furniture cost $34,000, salvage $4,000, life 5 years
Annual depreciation = ($34,000 - $4,000) / 5 = $6,000/year
```

**Double Declining Balance (DDB):**
```
SL Rate = 1 / Number of Years
DDB Rate = 2 x SL Rate
Annual Depreciation = DDB Rate x Undepreciated Balance (ignore salvage value)

Example: POS device cost $4,000, salvage $400, life 8 years
SL Rate = 1/8 = 12.5%; DDB Rate = 25%
Year 1: $4,000 x 0.25 = $1,000
Year 2: $3,000 x 0.25 = $750
Year 3: $2,250 x 0.25 = $562.50
(Total depreciation may not exceed Cost - Salvage Value)
```

DDB produces higher depreciation in early years, reducing taxable income. SL produces equal annual charges. Both are GAAP-compliant; consistency requires the same method throughout an asset's life (though different methods may be used for different assets).

### Uniforms, Linens, China, Glassware, Silver, Utensils

**Preferred method:**
- Capitalise cost of initial stock
- Depreciate capitalised cost over **maximum 36 months**
- Expense replacements when placed into service
- Reserve stocks treated as inventory until placed into service

## 12. Industry Benchmark Comparisons

### Summary of Key Restaurant Financial Benchmarks

| Metric | Typical Range | Notes |
|--------|--------------|-------|
| **Food cost %** | 37-40% of food sales | Cost of food sold / Food sales |
| **Beverage cost %** | 24-25% of beverage sales | Cost of beverages sold / Beverage sales |
| **Food gross profit %** | 60-63% | Food sales - Food cost / Food sales |
| **Beverage gross profit %** | 75-76% | Beverage sales - Beverage cost / Beverage sales |
| **Labour cost %** | 31-33% of total sales | Total labour (payroll + benefits) / Total sales |
| **Prime cost %** | 60-65% of total sales | (Food cost + Beverage cost + Labour) / Total sales |
| **Net profit margin** | < 10% of total revenue | Net income / Total revenue; typically 5-8% |
| **Food/beverage split** | 70/30 | Food sales typically ~70%, beverage ~30% of total |
| **Food inventory turnover** | 25-30+ times/year | Higher is better; fast food may be several times/week |
| **Seat turnover** | 1.5-3.0 per meal period | Varies by concept (fast food highest) |
| **Return on equity** | 12-16% | Net income / Average owners' equity |
| **Return on assets** | 6-8% | Net income / Average total assets |
| **Current ratio** | > 1.0 | Current assets / Current liabilities |

### Restaurant-Specific Cost Classifications

**Variable Costs (change with sales volume):**
- Cost of food sold
- Cost of beverages sold
- Hourly wages (variable payroll)
- Some supplies

**Fixed Costs (constant in short run):**
- Management salaries (fixed payroll)
- Rent/lease payments
- Insurance premiums
- Property taxes
- Depreciation
- Interest expense
- Some salaries

**Mixed Costs (partially fixed, partially variable):**
- Utilities (base charge + usage)
- Building lease with percentage-of-revenue component
- Telephone (base + usage)

**Discretionary Fixed Costs (can be cut short-term without immediate operational impact):**
- Educational seminars
- Charitable contributions
- Employee training programmes
- Advertising

**Capacity Fixed Costs (relate to ability to provide goods/services):**
- Depreciation
- Property taxes
- Interest
- Certain salaries

### Objective Pricing Methods Summary

| Method | Formula | When to Use |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| **Mark-Up (Ingredients)** | Ingredient Cost x Multiplier (1 / Food Cost %) | Simple, widely used |
| **Contribution Margin** | Ingredient Cost + (Nonfood Costs + Profit) / Expected Guests | When per-guest costs are similar except food |
| **Ratio Pricing** | Ingredient Cost + (Ingredient Cost x Ratio of Nonfood+Profit to Food Cost) | Considers relationship between food and non-food costs |
| **Simple Prime Cost** | (Ingredient Cost + Labour Cost per Guest) / Desired Prime Cost % | Incorporates the two largest cost categories |
| **Bottom-Up** | Starts with required ROI, works down to required revenue and average meal price | Comprehensive; ties pricing to investment return |

### Mark-Up Multiplier Calculation

```
Multiplier = 1 / Budgeted Food Cost %

Example: Budgeted food cost % = 37.1%
Multiplier = 1 / 0.371 = 2.7

Alternative: Multiplier = Budgeted Food Revenue / Allowable Food Cost
Example: $750,000 / $310,000 = 2.42
```

### Allowable Food Cost Calculation

```
Allowable Food Cost = Total Forecasted Revenue - (All Non-Food Costs + Required Profit)

Example:
Revenue: $750,000
Non-food costs: $375,000
Required profit: $65,000
Allowable food cost: $750,000 - ($375,000 + $65,000) = $310,000
Food cost %: $310,000 / $750,000 = 41.3%
```

### Profit Allocation Between Food and Beverage Departments

For restaurants with both food and beverage:
1. Food department covers its direct food costs plus its prorated share of non-product costs
2. Remaining required profit is generated by the beverage department
3. Beverage department adjusts its allowable cost to meet remaining profit targets

**Example allocation:**
```
Total revenue: $725,000 (Food $600,000 = 83%, Beverage $125,000 = 17%)
Required profit: $60,000

Food profit: $600,000 - ($216,000 food cost + 83% x $399,000 non-product) = $52,830
Beverage profit required: $60,000 - $52,830 = $7,170

Food mark-up: $600,000 / $216,000 = 2.78
Beverage mark-up: $125,000 / $50,000 allowable bev cost = 2.5
```

## Key Formulas Quick Reference

```
Food Cost %              = Cost of Food Sold / Food Sales
Beverage Cost %          = Cost of Beverages Sold / Beverage Sales
Labour Cost %            = Total Labour / Total Sales
Prime Cost %             = (Food Cost + Bev Cost + Labour) / Total Sales
Gross Profit             = Sales - Cost of Sales
Net Profit Margin        = Net Income / Total Revenue
Break-Even (meals)       = Fixed Costs / (Selling Price - Variable Cost per Meal)
Break-Even (revenue)     = Fixed Costs / CM Ratio
Contribution Margin      = Selling Price - Variable Cost per Unit
CM Ratio                 = Contribution Margin / Selling Price
Margin of Safety         = Actual Sales - Break-Even Sales
Current Ratio            = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
Debt-Equity Ratio        = Total Liabilities / Owners' Equity
ROE                      = Net Income / Average Owners' Equity
ROA                      = Net Income / Average Total Assets
Seat Turnover            = Guests Served / (Days Open x Seats)
Food Inventory Turnover  = Cost of Food Used / Average Food Inventory
Fixed Asset Turnover     = Total Revenue / Average Fixed Assets
AR Turnover              = Charge Sales / Average AR
Average Collection Period = 365 / AR Turnover
Mark-Up Multiplier       = 1 / Desired Food Cost %
SL Depreciation          = (Cost - Salvage) / Useful Life
Effective Interest Rate  = Annual Interest / (Loan - Compensating Balance)
```
