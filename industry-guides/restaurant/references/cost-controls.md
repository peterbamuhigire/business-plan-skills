1. FUNDAMENTAL COST & SALES FORMULAS
================================================================================

THE COSTING TRIANGLE (Three Core Formulas)
-------------------------------------------
These three interrelated formulas are the foundation of all restaurant
financial analysis:

  Cost / Sales x 100 = Cost %
  Cost / Cost % (as decimal) = Sales (or Sales Price)
  Sales x Cost % (as decimal) = Cost

Applied to each cost category:

  Food Cost / Food Sales x 100 = Food Cost %
  Beverage Cost / Beverage Sales x 100 = Beverage Cost %
  Labour Cost / Total Sales x 100 = Labour Cost %

PRIME COST
----------
  Prime Cost = Food Cost + Beverage Cost + Labour Cost
             (salaries, wages, and employee benefits)

This represents the largest portion of total costs for virtually all
foodservice operations. Typically 60-70% of total sales.

OVERHEAD COST
-------------
  Overhead = All costs other than prime cost
           (rent, utilities, insurance, depreciation, interest, etc.)

SALES FORMULAS
--------------
  Average Check = Total Dollar Sales / Total Number of Covers

  Average Sale per Server = Total Sales for Server / Number of Customers Served

  Total Sales per Seat = Total Dollar Sales (annual) / Number of Seats

  Seat Turnover = Number of Customers Served / Number of Seats
  (also called "turns")

  Sales Mix % = Number Sold of Item / Total Number Sold of All Items x 100

  Covers per Hour = Total Covers / Number of Hours of Operation
  Covers per Day = Total Covers / Number of Days of Operation
  Covers per Server = Total Covers / Number of Servers

4. FOOD COST CALCULATION & CONTROL
================================================================================

MONTHLY FOOD COST DETERMINATION
-------------------------------
Basic Formula:
  Opening Inventory
  + Purchases
  = Total Available
  - Closing Inventory
  = Cost of Food Issued

Full Formula with Adjustments:
  Opening Inventory
  + Purchases
  = Total Available for Sale
  - Closing Inventory
  = Cost of Food Issued
  + Cooking Liquor (alcohol transferred from bar to kitchen)
  + Transfers from Other Units
  - Food to Bar (directs transferred kitchen to bar)
  - Transfers to Other Units
  - Steward Sales (food sold to employees at cost)
  - Gratis to Bar (complimentary hors d'oeuvres for bar)
  - Promotion Expense (food for entertaining potential clients)
  = Cost of Food Consumed
  - Cost of Employee Meals
  = Cost of Food Sold

  Food Cost % = Cost of Food Sold / Food Sales x 100

WORKED EXAMPLE (Grandview Bistro, February):
  Opening inventory:              $  9,010
  + Food purchases:               $ 23,570
  = Total available:              $ 32,580
  - Closing inventory:            $  9,356
  = Cost of food issued:          $ 23,224
  + Cooking liquor:               $    200
  = Subtotal:                     $ 23,424
  - Food to bar (directs):        $    170
  - Steward sales:                $     80
  - Gratis to bar:                $    290
  - Promotion expense:            $    250
  = Cost of food consumed:        $ 22,634
  - Employee meals:               $  1,050
  = Cost of food sold:            $ 21,584

  Food Cost % = $21,584 / $65,420 = 32.99%

EMPLOYEE MEAL COST METHODS
--------------------------
1. Cost of separate issues (separate requisitions for employee food)
2. Prescribed amount per meal ($1.50 breakfast, $2.50 lunch, $3.50 dinner)
3. Prescribed amount per period (flat daily/weekly amount)
4. Sales value x food cost % (employees record selections, multiply
   total menu price by average food cost percent)

5. BEVERAGE COST CALCULATION & CONTROL
================================================================================

MONTHLY BEVERAGE COST DETERMINATION
------------------------------------
  Opening Beverage Inventory
  + Beverage Purchases
  = Total Available for Sale
  - Closing Inventory
  = Value of Beverages Issued to Bar
  +/- Bar Inventory Differential*
  = Cost of Beverages Consumed

  * Bar Inventory Differential:
    Bar inventory at beginning of month
    - Bar inventory at end of month
    = Differential (positive = add; negative = subtract)

  Beverage Cost % = Cost of Beverages Consumed / Beverage Sales x 100

FULL ADJUSTMENT FORMULA:
  Cost of Liquor Consumed
  + Food to bar (directs: oranges, lemons, cream)
  + Storeroom issues (olives, cherries, onions)
  + Mixers
  = Subtotal
  - Cooking liquor (transferred to kitchen)
  - Officers' drinks
  - Special promotions
  = Cost of Beverages Sold (adjusted)

  Adjusted Beverage Cost % = Adjusted Cost / Net Beverage Sales

BEVERAGE COST BY CATEGORY (Grandview Bistro, February):
  Spirits:    18.63%   ($1,262.10 / $6,772.90)
  Wine:       39.14%   ($1,506.80 / $3,849.35)
  Beer:       34.62%   ($290.65 / $839.65)
  Overall:    26.69%   ($3,059.55 / $11,461.90)

THREE APPROACHES TO MONITORING BEVERAGE OPERATIONS:
1. Cost Approach - comparing cost of beverages sold with actual/standard cost
2. Liquid Measure Approach - comparing ounces sold vs ounces consumed
3. Sales Value Approach - comparing potential sales value with actual revenue

STANDARD COST METHOD FOR BEVERAGES:
  For each drink type:
    Number Sold x Standard Cost per Drink = Total Standard Cost
  Sum all drink types = Total Standard Beverage Cost for Period
  Compare standard cost with actual cost to identify variances

DAILY BEVERAGE COST TRACKING:
  Same cumulative approach as food:
  Beverage Cost to Date / Beverage Sales to Date x 100 = Cost % to Date

6. LABOUR COST CALCULATION & CONTROL
================================================================================

LABOUR COST RANGES
------------------
  General range:           15% to 45% of sales
  Well-managed full-service: ~24.5% (including benefits)
  Not-for-profit:          up to 60%

LABOUR COST FORMULA
-------------------
  Labour Cost % = (Salaries + Wages + Employee Benefits) / Total Sales x 100

EMPLOYEE CATEGORIES FOR CONTROL
-------------------------------
Variable-Cost Personnel (linked to business volume):
  - Servers, bussers, dishwashers, prep cooks
  - Number varies with volume; hours staggered to match demand

Fixed-Cost Personnel (unrelated to business volume):
  - Managers, bookkeepers, chefs, stewards, cashiers
  - Number remains relatively constant regardless of volume

STANDARD WORK HOURS
-------------------
  Standard Work Hours = Number of Employees x Hours Worked

  Example: 8 servers x 3-hour lunch = 24 standard work hours for 500 covers

  Covers per Standard Work Hour = Covers Served / Standard Work Hours
  Example: 500 / 24 = 20.83 covers per work hour

STANDARD LABOUR COST
--------------------
  Standard Labour Cost = Standard Work Hours x Hourly Wage Rate

  Example: 24 hours x $4.00/hour = $96.00 standard cost for servers

STANDARD STAFFING TABLE EXAMPLE:
  Covers     Servers  Warewashers  Prep Personnel
  200-299       5         2             3
  300-399       6         2             3
  400-499       7         3             4
  500-599       8         4             4

STANDARD WORK-HOUR REQUIREMENTS TABLE:
  Covers     Service  Warewashing  Preparation
  200-299      15         6            9
  300-399      18         6            9
  400-499      21         9           12
  500-599      24        12           12

SCHEDULING METHODOLOGY
----------------------
1. Maintain daily and hourly records of covers served
2. Calculate median volume (not average - unaffected by extremes)
3. Develop staffing tables based on test period observations
4. Create hourly schedules matching staff to anticipated demand
5. Use split shifts and part-time staff to cover peak periods
6. Track maximum, minimum, and median hourly volumes

COVERS PER SERVER GUIDELINE:
  Approximately 20 covers per server per hour (varies by service type)

7. MENU PRICING FORMULAS & STRATEGIES
================================================================================

THREE APPROACHES TO MENU PRICING
---------------------------------

1. MATCHING COMPETITORS' PRICES
   - Common but risky; ignores own cost structure
   - Works for homogeneous products (pizza, burgers)
   - Dangerous if costs differ from competitors'

2. CALCULATING PRICES FROM COSTS AND COST PERCENTS
   Two variations:

   Variation A - Target Cost Percent Method:
     Sales Price = Portion Cost / Desired Cost % (as decimal)

     Example: $3.60 cost / 0.30 (30% target) = $12.00 selling price

   Variation B - Menu Pre-Cost Method:
     - Set tentative prices, forecast volume
     - Calculate projected food cost % using Menu Pre-Cost form
     - Adjust prices/portions until satisfactory cost % achieved

   Determining the Target Food Cost %:
     100% - Profit % - Fixed Cost % - Labour Cost % = Food Cost %

3. ADDING CONTRIBUTION MARGINS TO PORTION COSTS
   Steps:
   a. Determine total sales, cost of sales, and gross profit
   b. Divide gross profit by number of customers = Average Contribution Margin
   c. Add this CM to each item's portion cost = Selling Price

   Example (Julio's Restaurant):
     Food Sales:      $500,000
     Cost of Sales:   $200,000
     Gross Profit:    $300,000
     Customers:       30,000
     Average Sale:    $16.67 per customer
     Average CM:      $10.00 per customer

   Each menu item priced at portion cost + $10.00:
     Steak ($8.00 cost) = $18.00 selling price
     Pasta ($2.50 cost) = $12.50 selling price

ADJUSTING FOR INFLATION:
  If annual inflation = 5%, for a 6-month future booking:
  Apply half the annual rate (2.5%) to current food cost
  Current cost: $4.00 -> Future cost: $4.10
  At 30% food cost: $4.10 / 0.30 = $13.67

MENU PRIME SPACE (where customers look first):
  One-page menu:   top one-third
  Two-page menu:   top centre of right-hand page
  Three-page menu: centre of middle page

8. PORTION CONTROL & STANDARDISED RECIPES
================================================================================

STANDARD PORTION COST - BASIC FORMULA
--------------------------------------
  Standard Portion Cost = Purchase Price per Unit / Number of Portions per Unit

  Example: $41.40 (case of eggs) / 180 (two-egg portions) = $0.23 per portion

RECIPE DETAIL AND COST CARD
----------------------------
  For each ingredient in the recipe:
    Ingredient Quantity x Unit Cost = Ingredient Cost
  Sum all ingredient costs = Total Recipe Cost
  Total Recipe Cost / Number of Portions = Standard Portion Cost

  Selling Price = Standard Portion Cost / Desired Cost % (as decimal)

BUTCHER TEST (for items portioned before cooking)
-------------------------------------------------
  Purpose: Determine standard portion cost for meats cut in-house

  Key Calculations:
  1. Ratio to Total Weight = Weight of Part / Weight of Whole
     (Also called Yield Percentage or Yield Factor)

  2. Total Value of Usable Meat = Total Cost - Value of Secondary Parts

  3. Cost per Usable Pound = Total Value of Usable Meat / Weight of Usable Meat

  4. Cost per Usable Ounce = Cost per Usable Pound / 16

  5. Portion Cost = Portion Size (oz) x Cost per Usable Ounce

  BUTCHER TEST EXAMPLE (Beef Tenderloin):
    Purchase: 9 lbs @ $6.11/lb = $54.99
    Breakdown:
      Fat:           3 lb 12 oz  (41.7%)  Value: $0.00
      Loss cutting:  0 lb  4 oz  ( 2.8%)  Value: $0.00
      Tip meat:      0 lb 12 oz  ( 8.3%)  Value: $1.87
      Usable meat:   4 lb  4 oz  (47.2%)  Value: $53.12

    Cost per usable lb: $53.12 / 4.25 = $12.4988
    Cost per usable oz: $12.4988 / 16 = $0.7812
    Portion cost (6 oz): 6 x $0.7812 = $4.69

COST FACTORS (for quick recalculation when prices change)
---------------------------------------------------------
  Cost Factor per Pound = Cost per Usable Lb / Purchase Price per Lb
  Example: $12.4988 / $6.11 = 2.0456

  Cost Factor per Portion = Portion Cost / Purchase Price per Lb
  Example: $4.6871 / $6.11 = 0.7671

  USING COST FACTORS:
  New cost per usable lb = Cost Factor per Lb x New Dealer Price
  Example: 2.0456 x $6.37 = $13.03

  New portion cost = Cost Factor per Portion x New Dealer Price
  Example: 0.7671 x $6.37 = $4.89

  UNIVERSAL FORMULA:
  Cost Factor per Lb x Portion Size (decimal) x Dealer Price = Portion Cost
  Example: 2.0456 x 0.375 (6oz) x $6.37 = $4.89

COOKING LOSS TEST (for items portioned after cooking)
-----------------------------------------------------
  Purpose: Determine portion cost accounting for cooking shrinkage

  Key Entries:
    Original Weight (as purchased)
    Trimmed Weight (after butchering before cooking)
    Loss in Trimming = Original - Trimmed
    Cooked Weight (after cooking)
    Loss in Cooking = Trimmed - Cooked
    Bones and Trim (waste after cooking)
    Salable Weight = Cooked - Bones & Trim

  COOKING LOSS EXAMPLE (Pork Loin):
    Original: 7 lb 8 oz @ $6.35/lb = $47.63
    Trimmed:  6 lb 8 oz  (86.67%)
    Cooked:   6 lb 0 oz  (80.00%)
    Salable:  5 lb 0 oz  (66.67%)

    Cost per salable lb: $47.63 / 5.0 = $9.5260
    Portion cost (5 oz): $2.9769
    Cost factor per portion: 0.4688

YIELD PERCENTAGE FORMULAS
--------------------------
  Yield % = Portionable Weight / Original Weight x 100

  Quantity Needed = (Number of Portions x Portion Size as decimal) / Yield %

  Number of Portions = (Quantity x Yield %) / Portion Size

  Portion Size = (Quantity x Yield %) / Number of Portions

  Example: How much pork loin for 32 five-oz portions at 66.67% yield?
    = (32 x 0.3125) / 0.6667 = 15 lbs

9. PURCHASING & INVENTORY MANAGEMENT
================================================================================

FIVE INVENTORY VALUATION METHODS
---------------------------------
1. Actual Purchase Price Method
   - Uses marked prices on each unit
   - Most accurate but requires marking

2. First-In, First-Out (FIFO) Method
   - Assumes oldest stock used first
   - Closing inventory valued at most recent prices
   - Most common in computerised systems

3. Weighted-Average Purchase Price Method
   - Total value of all units / Total number of units
   - Requires detailed purchase records

4. Latest Purchase Price Method
   - All closing inventory valued at most recent price
   - Simple, fast, widely used in food/beverage
   - Represents replacement cost

5. Last-In, First-Out (LIFO) Method
   - Closing inventory valued at earliest prices
   - Minimises profit on statements (tax advantage)

NOTE: Difference between methods can be 4%+ on total inventory value.

INVENTORY TURNOVER
------------------
  Average Inventory = (Opening Inventory + Closing Inventory) / 2

  Inventory Turnover = Cost of Food Sold / Average Inventory

  EXAMPLE:
    Opening: $9,010 + Closing: $9,356 = $18,366 / 2 = $9,183 average
    Turnover = $21,584 / $9,183 = 2.35 times per month

  GUIDELINE: Food inventory sufficient for 1-2 weeks is normal
    Monthly turnover of 2-4 times = healthy
    Annual turnover of 24-48 times = healthy

  Excessive inventory leads to:
  - Spoilage from food stored too long
  - Excessive cash tied up
  - Excessive labour for receiving/storing
  - Excessive storage space needed
  - Increased theft opportunities

BOOK vs ACTUAL INVENTORY COMPARISON
------------------------------------
  Opening Inventory
  + Purchases (stores only)
  = Total Available
  - Issues (from requisitions)
  = Closing Book Value

  Dollar Difference = Book Value - Actual Value
  Variance % = Dollar Difference / Total Issues x 100

  Acceptable causes: rounding errors, pricing method differences
  Unacceptable causes: theft, spoilage, issues without requisitions

10. BREAK-EVEN ANALYSIS
================================================================================

THE COST/VOLUME/PROFIT EQUATION
--------------------------------
  Sales = Variable Cost + Fixed Cost + Profit
  S = VC + FC + P

VARIABLE RATE (VR)
------------------
  VR = Variable Cost / Sales
  (Percentage of each sales dollar needed for variable costs)

  Example: $454,235 / $1,049,043 = 0.433 (43.3%)

CONTRIBUTION RATE (CR)
----------------------
  CR = 1 - VR
  (Percentage of each sales dollar available for fixed costs and profit)

  Example: 1 - 0.433 = 0.567 (56.7%)

CONTRIBUTION MARGIN (CM)
------------------------
  CM = Selling Price - Variable Cost of Item

  Example: $12.00 selling price - $5.00 cost = $7.00 CM

BREAK-EVEN POINT IN DOLLARS
----------------------------
  Break-Even Sales = Fixed Costs / Contribution Rate
  BE = FC / CR

  Example: $466,106 / 0.567 = $822,056

SALES REQUIRED FOR A TARGET PROFIT
-----------------------------------
  Required Sales = (Fixed Costs + Desired Profit) / Contribution Rate
  S = (FC + P) / CR

  Example: ($466,106 + $128,702) / 0.567 = $1,049,044

BREAK-EVEN IN NUMBER OF CUSTOMERS
----------------------------------
  Average CM per Customer = Average Sale x Contribution Rate

  BEP in Customers = Fixed Costs / Average CM per Customer

  Example:
    Average sale: $32.79
    Average CM: $32.79 x 0.567 = $18.59
    BEP: $466,106 / $18.59 = 25,073 customers

CUSTOMERS FOR TARGET PROFIT
----------------------------
  Customers = (FC + Profit) / Average CM
  Example: ($466,106 + $128,702) / $18.59 = 31,996 customers

VARIABLE COST CALCULATION
-------------------------
  VC = Sales x Variable Rate
  Example: $226,987 x 0.433 = $98,285

PROFIT BEYOND BREAK-EVEN
-------------------------
  Profit = Sales Beyond BE x Contribution Rate
  Example: $226,987 x 0.567 = $128,702

FORMULA FLOWCHART (decision tree for which formula to use):
  Is this a break-even problem? -> Yes -> FC / CR
  Is this a contribution rate problem? -> Yes -> 1 - VR  or  (FC + P) / S
  Is this a contribution margin problem? -> Yes -> S - VC  or  S x CR
  Is this a variable rate problem? -> Yes -> VC / S  or  1 - CR
  Is this a variable cost problem? -> Yes -> S - CM  or  S x VR
  Is this a profit problem? -> Yes -> S - VC - FC  or  CM - FC

11. MENU ENGINEERING & ANALYSIS
================================================================================

MENU ENGINEERING WORKSHEET (Kasavana & Smith Method)
----------------------------------------------------
For each menu item, calculate:
  A. Menu Item Name
  B. Number Sold
  C. Menu Mix % = Number Sold / Total Number Sold x 100
  D. Food Cost (per portion)
  E. Sales Price
  F. Item CM = Sales Price - Food Cost (E - D)
  G. Menu Cost = Food Cost x Number Sold (D x B)
  H. Menu Revenues = Sales Price x Number Sold (E x B)
  L. Menu CM = Item CM x Number Sold (F x B)

Totals:
  N. Total Covers (sum of column B)
  I. Total Menu Cost (sum of column G)
  J. Total Menu Revenues (sum of column H)
  M. Total Menu CM (sum of column L)

  K. Overall Food Cost % = Total Menu Cost / Total Menu Revenues (I / J)
  O. Average CM = Total Menu CM / Total Covers (M / N)
  Q. Item % Threshold = (1 / Number of Items) x 0.70 (70% of average)

CLASSIFICATION CRITERIA:
  P. CM Category: H (high) if Item CM > Average CM; L (low) if below
  R. MM Category: H (high) if Menu Mix % > Q threshold; L (low) if below

FOUR MENU ITEM CLASSIFICATIONS:
  STAR       (H/H) = High CM + High Volume   -> Leave alone; possibly raise price
  PLOWHORSE  (L/H) = Low CM + High Volume    -> Keep; try to increase CM
  PUZZLE     (H/L) = High CM + Low Volume     -> Keep; try to increase popularity
  DOG        (L/L) = Low CM + Low Volume      -> Remove or redesign

EXAMPLE (Grandview Bistro, February):
  Total Covers: 2,635
  Total Menu CM: $36,056.75
  Average CM: $13.68
  Q Threshold: 1/16 x 0.70 = 4.375%
  Overall Food Cost %: 32.11%

  Stars:      Strip Steak ($16.15 CM, 9.56% mix)
              Tournedos Rossini ($16.05 CM, 8.73% mix)
              Steak Diane ($17.00 CM, 6.22% mix)

  Plowhorses: Ginger Shrimp ($12.80 CM, 7.74% mix)
              Tea-Smoked Salmon ($13.45 CM, 9.03% mix)
              Pan-Seared Chicken ($11.85 CM, 7.40% mix)
              Chicken Albufera ($11.25 CM, 7.86% mix)
              Trout Grenobloise ($13.10 CM, 6.11% mix)
              Shrimp a la Marseille ($12.75 CM, 5.43% mix)
              Fruits de Mer ($13.50 CM, 5.88% mix)
              Catch of the Day ($11.20 CM, 7.89% mix)

  Puzzles:    Duck Breast ($14.10 CM, 3.07% mix)
              Lamb Chops ($14.60 CM, 3.87% mix)
              Loin of Pork ($14.20 CM, 3.72% mix)
              Parmesan Veal Steak ($14.50 CM, 3.72% mix)

  Dogs:       Vegetarian Burrito ($12.70 CM, 3.76% mix)

STRATEGIES AFTER ANALYSIS:
  After implementing changes (March):
  - Dogs eliminated (Burrito replaced with Tostadas -> became Plowhorse)
  - Plowhorse prices raised $0.50 (slight volume decrease)
  - Puzzles repositioned/renamed (Duck became Star, Veal Picata became Star)
  - Stars left unchanged

  Results:
  - Average CM increased from $13.68 to $13.92
  - Food cost % decreased from 32.11% to 31.46%
  - Total covers increased from 2,635 to 2,675
  - Total CM increased from $36,056.75 to $37,226.90

NOTE ON 70% vs 100%:
  Using 70% of average for volume threshold is the original method.
  Using 100% of average is also acceptable - matter of preference.
  Neither changes the financial picture; both are analytical tools.

12. DAILY/WEEKLY/MONTHLY COST TRACKING SYSTEMS
================================================================================

DAILY FOOD COST TRACKING
--------------------------
  Daily Cost of Food Sold =
    Cost of Directs (from Receiving Clerk's Daily Report)
    + Cost of Stores (from daily requisitions)
    + Adjustments increasing cost (bar-to-kitchen transfers)
    - Adjustments decreasing cost (kitchen-to-bar, steward sales, promos)
    = Cost of Food Consumed
    - Cost of Employee Meals
    = Daily Cost of Food Sold

  Daily Food Cost % = Daily Food Cost / Daily Food Sales x 100

CUMULATIVE (TO-DATE) TRACKING
-------------------------------
  Food Cost % to Date = Total Food Cost to Date / Total Food Sales to Date x 100

  This smooths out daily fluctuations caused by:
  - Directs purchased every other day
  - Stores issued in advance of use
  - Bulk issuing of staples (salt, flour, oil)

DAILY CUMULATIVE COST REPORT FORMAT:
  Date | Directs | Stores | Additions | Subtractions | Cost Today | Cost to Date |
       | Sales Today | Sales to Date | Cost % Today | Cost % to Date

EXAMPLE (Grandview Bistro, Feb 1-5):
  Feb 1: Cost $1,033 / Sales $2,778 = 37.2% today, 37.2% to date
  Feb 2: Cost $1,104 / Sales $2,919 = 37.8% today, 37.5% to date
  Feb 3: Cost $1,214 / Sales $3,057 = 39.7% today, 38.3% to date
  Feb 4: Cost $1,122 / Sales $3,094 = 36.3% today, 37.7% to date
  Feb 5: Cost $1,083 / Sales $3,427 = 31.6% today, 36.4% to date

DETAILED DAILY REPORT (cost breakdown as % of food cost):
  Directs as % of Food Cost
  Stores as % of Food Cost
  Additions as % of Food Cost
  Subtractions as % of Food Cost

  Stores Breakdown as % of Stores:
  - Beef and Veal
  - Poultry
  - Seafood
  - Pork
  - Duck/Game
  - Provisions

DAILY REPORT TO MANAGEMENT FORMAT:
  Shows: Today | Same Day Last Week | To Date This Week | To Date Last Week
  For: Food Sales, Food Cost, Cost %

BOOK INVENTORY TRACKING (daily):
  Opening Balance
  + Stores Purchases (from Receiving Report)
  - Stores Issues (from Requisitions)
  = Closing Book Balance

  Compare monthly: Book Value vs Physical Count
  Variance % = (Book - Actual) / Total Issues x 100

  Example: $500 variance / $7,630 issues = 6.55% (significant - investigate)

WEEKLY COMPARISON APPROACH:
  Compare same day this week vs last week
  Compare week-to-date this week vs last week
  Identify trends in cost categories

MONTHLY MONITORING:
  Monthly food cost and food cost %
  Monthly beverage cost and cost %
  Monthly inventory turnover
  Compare current month to:
    - Same month last year
    - Previous month
    - Budget/plan

KEY PRINCIPLE: "The more frequent the reports and the more detailed the
information they include, the better will be the opportunities they offer
management to maintain control over operations and to ensure that the
results conform to plans."

DAILY BEVERAGE COST TRACKING:
  Same cumulative approach as food cost
  With bar inventory differential for greater accuracy:
    Bar Inventory Differential = Opening Bar Inventory - Closing Bar Inventory
    Daily Cost = Issues +/- Bar Differential

  Record bottles in tenths (e.g., half-full = 0.5)
