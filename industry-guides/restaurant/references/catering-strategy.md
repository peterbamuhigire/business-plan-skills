1. CATERING AS A REVENUE STREAM FOR RESTAURANTS
================================================================================

CORE THESIS:
Catering is the next natural top-line growth channel for the multi-unit
restaurant industry, comparable in potential impact to what drive-thru and
breakfast represented in previous decades.

KEY PRINCIPLES:
- Catering is a "business within a business" -- a completely different business
  from retail restaurant operations
- It leverages existing assets (real estate, equipment, labor, brand equity,
  supply chain) with very few incremental costs
- The same customer who dines in-store is often the catering buyer, but the
  demand driver is entirely different
- 85% of catering business is corporate meetings; 15% personal gatherings
- Catering is a horizontal addition to existing business structures

WHY IT WORKS:
- Universal business logic: layering incremental revenue on top of already
  existing assets is a "home run"
- Uses idle capacity in existing production facilities (stores)
- Low barriers to entry (no new real estate or major capital expenditure)
- The multi-unit restaurant community is UNDER-SERVING the corporate catering
  market -- dollars are going to independent shops, Chinese food, local
  take-out, grocery, pizza delivery

THE OPPORTUNITY:
- National brands doing catering well generate $30 million to $150 million in
  incremental catering revenue per year
- Tony's Deli case study: $2 million annual sales from 1,700 sq ft store,
  with HALF ($1 million) from catering, generating over $1 million in free
  cash flow annually

CROSS-POLLINATION EFFECT:
- Catering builds brand awareness in corporate environments
- People who consume catered food may become retail customers
- Retail customers who learn about catering services become catering buyers
- The brand experience reinforces itself across both channels

2. CATERING BUSINESS MODEL AND PRICING
================================================================================

BUSINESS MODEL FRAMEWORK:
- Catering is B2B (business-to-business), not B2C
- The transaction is with a corporation, but the customer is the individual
  order-placer within that corporation
- Requires house accounts and credit facilities (paradigm shift from
  cash-based retail operations)
- Relationship-based selling is the core strategy

PRICING STRATEGY:
- Catering menu items CAN and SHOULD be priced HIGHER than retail menu items
- The demand driver is different (stress-driven, corporate-funded, premium
  service expectation)
- Operators can charge a "premium" for catering services due to:
  * Value-added service (delivery, setup, convenience)
  * Different consumption experience (branded packaging, presentation)
  * Corporate budgets (price sensitivity is lower than individual consumers)
  * Reliability and trust factor (order-placer's reputation is at stake)
- Create "upscale, healthier" versions of existing menu at premium prices
- DO NOT discount or use coupons to incentivize catering purchases

PLATTER PRICING EXAMPLE (Tony's Deli):
- Medium platter feeds 10-15 people: $69.95
- Small platter feeds 6-10 people: $39.95
- For 18 people: medium + small = $109.90
- Include leftover packaging as part of scripted service experience
- Always recommend the right amount of food (never undersell)

KEY PRICING INSIGHT:
"Although catering may only represent 20-30% of total sales mix, it can
contribute up to 40% of overall gross margin dollar contribution to bottom
lines."

3. CATERING OPERATIONS AND LOGISTICS
================================================================================

THREE-STEP PROCESS FOR SUCCESS:
1. Strategy (executive alignment, business plan)
2. Education (training, policy & procedure manuals)
3. Technology (catering-specific software, NOT POS)

ORDER EXECUTION WORKFLOW:
1. PRE-PRODUCTION (day before):
   - Before kitchen closes, portion next day's catering raw materials
   - Slice, dice, chop; measure portions for next day's assembly
   - Prepare shelf-stable items (paper service, beverages, chips)
   - Mark and date-rotate assembly items
   - Front-counter staff prepare beverages, fold boxes, organize supplies

2. MORNING PRODUCTION (day of):
   - Early morning crew begins catering production before store opens
   - Fresh items (baked goods, vendor deliveries) arrive earlier than retail
   - Focus on ASSEMBLY and PACKAGING, not cooking from scratch
   - "Production time is really assembly time"
   - Produce by menu item in bulk (NOT per-order)
   - Package orders according to packing slips on per-order basis

3. DISTRIBUTION TIMING:
   - Breakfast orders: on trucks/ready for pickup by 6:30-7:00 AM
   - Lunch orders: on trucks by 11:15 AM for on-time delivery

4. DISTRIBUTION MANAGEMENT:
   - Delivery coordinator maps out deliveries the day before
   - Truck safety check at end of each shift
   - Assign orders to drivers, generate labels
   - Pack slips checked off; drivers have instructions and invoices
   - Options: internal drivers, third-party drivers, or both
   - Urban markets may use "order walkers" with branded carts

KEY OPERATIONS PRINCIPLE:
"Think of catering as a high-volume production line, NOT made-to-order."

CATERING PICK-UP:
- Dedicate a separate, clearly marked "catering pick-up" space in stores
- Keep separate from "take-out pick-up" counters

FOOD SAFETY:
- Temperature controls from production through delivery
- Refrigeration planning is critical
- Holding times must be managed and can be a selling feature
- Cold programs are easier to execute than hot food programs
- Cold catering recommendation for beginners: platters, salads, sandwiches

4. MARKETING AND SALES FOR CATERING SERVICES
================================================================================

SALES METHODOLOGY (Simple, Repeatable):
1. Qualify leads -- identify likely B2B purchasers of catering services
2. Call by telephone to introduce brand's catering services
3. Arrange free promotional sampling delivery (e.g., "Gourmet Food Platter
   for Five")
4. Deliver sampling at OFF-PEAK hours (don't compete with real paying orders)
5. Include all branded packaging and marketing materials with sampling
6. Follow up... Follow up... Follow up
7. Keep prospects on marketing campaign with task-based follow-ups
8. "Sampling sells in our business"

IN-STORE MARKETING (First Priority):
- Make catering services VERY visible to in-store guests
- Use wall space, tabletops, menu boards
- Post "Today's Deliveries" board listing all organization names being
  catered that day (creates buzz and awareness)
- Tony's Deli had a branded chalkboard listing 40+ daily deliveries
- Train all frontline people to speak "the language of catering"
- Every staff member should be able to direct catering inquiries properly

EXTERNAL MARKETING:
- Use all preferred advertising methods
- DO NOT use discounting or couponing for catering
- Invest in outbound B2B selling (door-to-door, telemarketing, site visits)

CRITICAL MARKETING ASSETS:
- Business cards -- "get lots of them"
- Promotional sampling packages (consistent every time)
- Branded packaging that serves as ongoing brand ambassador
- Catering-specific marketing materials (separate from retail)

SALES CHANNELS:
- Cold calling / prospecting corporate accounts
- Pharmaceutical reps (special relationship opportunity)
- Referrals -- "the most powerful tool you have"
- Walk-in catering inquiries
- Telephone work -- "be relentless and watch catering sales soar"
- Mobilizing frontline people as brand ambassadors

CUSTOMER TYPES:
- Corporate meetings (85% of business)
- Training seminars and courses
- Personal gatherings (15% of business)
- Group orders -- lunchroom table (informal)
- Group orders -- boardroom table (formal, higher-value)
- Cruise ships, bus tours
- Graduations, private parties (secondary)

5. FINANCIAL METRICS SPECIFIC TO CATERING
================================================================================

REVENUE TARGETS PER UNIT:
- Target: $1,000/day per store, Monday-Friday
- Conservative target: $500/day per store
- Requires 5-8 catering orders per day (or 2-3 larger orders)
- Weekly potential: $3,125 to $5,000 per store
- Annual potential per store: ~$240,000 (based on 48 productive weeks)
- Incremental sales growth of 20-30% is achievable

SYSTEM-WIDE PROJECTIONS (100 stores):
- Daily: $100,000 in incremental catering sales system-wide
- Weekly: $312,500 to $500,000 in incremental sales
- Annual: $14.9 million to $24.0 million for the brand
- Adding one more order/day significantly increases these numbers

AVERAGE ORDER METRICS:
- Average catering check: $125 to $200 per order
- Average lunch price: ~$12.50 per person
- Average order size: ~16 people
- 5 orders/day x $125-$200 = $625-$1,000 daily revenue

MARGIN FRAMEWORK:
- Catering margins should be HIGHER than retail margins
- Catering at 20-30% of total sales can contribute up to 40% of gross margin
  dollar contribution
- Higher margins driven by: premium pricing, bulk production efficiency,
  leveraged existing assets, lower per-unit labor cost
- Tony's Deli: $2M annual revenue, $1M catering, >$1M free cash flow
  (50%+ margins on blended operations)

SCALING EXAMPLE (1,200 stores):
- Conservative at $500/day/store = $150 million revenue channel
- At $1,000/day/store = $300 million+ revenue channel

KEY METRICS TO TRACK:
1. Sales per store (daily, weekly, monthly, annual)
2. Average order size (dollar value and guest count)
3. Number of catering orders per day per store
4. Menu mix (80/20 rule: 80% revenue from 20% of menu items)
5. Sleepy clients (customers who haven't ordered in 60-90 days)
6. Lead conversion reports (time from prospect to first order)
7. Service performance reports (follow-up calls, complaint tracking)
8. Order frequency per client
9. Brand rotation position (where you sit on clients' catering lists)

6. STAFFING FOR CATERING OPERATIONS
================================================================================

KEY POSITIONS:

DIRECTOR OF CATERING:
- Every successful operator has committed to this leadership position
- Responsible for documenting, training, mobilizing workforce
- Owns catering strategy, P&L, and growth targets

CATERING SALES MANAGERS:
- Report to Director of Catering
- Work under Director depending on organization size
- Focus on B2B relationship development
- Door-to-door and telephone-based selling

CATERING CUSTOMER SERVICE REPRESENTATIVES:
- Handle order entry (conversational ordering process)
- Script-based interaction for consistent experience
- Follow-up calls after every order
- Dedicated to catering (not shared with retail counter)

DELIVERY COORDINATOR:
- Maps out deliveries the day before
- Assigns orders to drivers
- Generates labels and organizes packing slips
- Manages both internal and third-party drivers
- Coordinates truck maintenance/safety checks

PRIMARY DRIVERS:
- Keep trucks clean, maintained, in "ready" position
- Check driver log today for next day
- Sort assembled/packaged orders for routes
- Report all delivery problems back
- Say "please" and "thank you" at delivery point
- Ask for referrals and more business
- Core functions: ~4 hours/day; remaining time: help with production

CATERING LEAD (Kitchen):
- Late shift: ensures pre-production is complete for next day
- Early morning: leads assembly and packaging operations
- Quality control on order accuracy

LABOR MODEL:
- Leverage EXISTING labor pool first (add "catering-related tasks")
- Front-counter staff can prepare beverages, fold boxes in off-peak times
- Only add dedicated catering positions as volume grows
- Goal: drive down per-unit labor costs by spreading labor across
  both retail and catering revenue
- "Using your existing labor more effectively will increase your overall
  unit level margins"

FRANCHISE MODEL STAFFING:
- Centralize where possible: sales, marketing, call center, order management,
  payment processing, accounts receivable
- Franchisees focus ONLY on order execution (manufacturing + distribution)
- Centralized customer service provides consistent brand experience
- Takes pressure off unit-level operations

7. MENU DESIGN FOR CATERING VS. DINE-IN
================================================================================

FUNDAMENTAL PRINCIPLE:
"It is wholly possible that your current retail products should NOT be the same
as your catering products."

MENU DEVELOPMENT APPROACH:
- Start with existing ingredient/flavor profile (use what's already in
  coolers, freezers, dry goods)
- Create SIMPLER menu than retail (easier to scale across locations)
- Different packaging, pricing, presentation, and consumption experience
- Catering menu is a SUBSET of capabilities, not an extension of retail menu
- Products must TRAVEL WELL outside four walls
- Remove items that don't travel well

RECOMMENDED FORMATS (Priority Order):
1. TRAYS AND PLATTERS -- "cornerstone of a solid catering business"
   - Easiest format to execute at scale
   - Three sizes: small (6-10 people), medium (10-15), large (15+)
   - Price by platter, not per person (simplifies operations)
   - Include large salad bowls as accompaniments
   - Use for desserts and fruit presentations too

2. BOX LUNCHES -- focus on high volume
   - Minimum order: 100 box lunches recommended
   - Time-intensive assembly; only worthwhile at scale
   - Best for: tours, schools, large seminars

3. BEVERAGES
   - Create proprietary/signature beverages (iced tea, lemonade) sold by gallon
   - Higher margins than commodity beverages (Coke, water)
   - Limit number of beverage selections
   - Chill beverages the night before
   - Charge for extras (bags of ice, etc.)

4. HOT FOOD (Advanced)
   - More difficult to execute for delivery
   - Limited menu selection recommended
   - Pasta programs and fajita/taco bars work well
   - Requires specialized packaging for temperature control
   - NOT recommended for beginners

COLD VS. HOT:
- Cold programs are strongly recommended, especially for beginners
- Continental breakfast, lox & cream cheese platters, fresh fruit for AM
- Sandwich and salad programs for lunch
- Even "hamburger brands" should develop cold sandwich/salad catering lines
- Strongest corporate dayparts: breakfast and lunch

MENU MANAGEMENT:
- Run parallel LTO (Limited Time Offer) programs for catering and retail
- Monitor menu mix data: 80% of revenue from 20% of items
- Eliminate underperforming items regularly
- Engage menu development experts for brands with 100+ stores
- Keep it simple first, iterate based on market feedback

PACKAGING PRINCIPLES:
- Branded, high-quality custom packaging (NOT standard black plastic trays)
- Tony's Deli used custom corrugated boxes with wax lining (wet/dry items)
- Custom boxes were 50%+ cheaper than standard black trays
- Branded boxes serve as long-term brand ambassadors in offices
- Include leftover packaging as part of service
- Display stacked catering boxes prominently in-store

8. GROWTH STRATEGIES AND SCALING CATERING
================================================================================

SCALING FRAMEWORK:

PHASE 1: STRATEGY AND ALIGNMENT
- Achieve executive alignment first (most critical step)
- If ANY doubt from executive team, do not move forward
- Develop formal business plan for catering revenue channel
- Document strategy before doing anything else

PHASE 2: PROVE THE MODEL
- Start with corporate-owned stores before extending to franchisees
- Test and refine operations, menu, and systems
- Build policy-and-procedure manual from real experience

PHASE 3: SCALE ACROSS SYSTEM
- Roll out to franchise/licensee communities
- Centralize services where possible (sales, marketing, call center, order
  management, payment processing, AR management, IT)
- Franchisees focus solely on order execution

GROWTH DRIVERS:
1. Relationship selling (B2B, door-to-door, telemarketing)
2. Promotional sampling (free "Gourmet Food Platter for Five")
3. In-store cross-pollination (retail customers become catering buyers)
4. National accounts (negotiate contracts in advance)
5. Referrals from satisfied clients
6. Consistent follow-up on every order

SCALING CHALLENGES:
- Retail and catering channels will COMPETE for resources at unit level
- Must re-engineer roles/responsibilities to make room for catering
- Vendor relationships need adjustment (earlier/flexible deliveries)
- Keep catering and retail transactions SEPARATE (different checks)
- Never let catering phone calls disrupt retail customer experience

FRANCHISE SCALING MODEL:
- Franchisor provides: strategy, marketing, centralized call center, customer
  service, billing/AR, IT systems
- Franchisee provides: manufacturing (food production) and distribution
  (delivery)
- Single point of contact for brand's catering customers
- Franchisees don't get bogged down in administrative complexity

TECHNOLOGY FOR SCALE:
- Dedicated catering software (NOT POS systems)
- Web-based, enterprise-level, standardized database
- Catering system feeds data to: food cost systems, accounting systems,
  business intelligence, POS (for day-end data only)
- POS integration is NOT required for catering order execution
- Online ordering for catering is different from online ordering for take-out

FRAMEWORK 3: THE PSYCHOLOGY OF THE CATERING SALE
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Understanding: The order-placer is STRESSED. They:
- Are accountable internally if the order is wrong
- Face time pressure (meetings organized last minute)
- Risk their professional reputation on your execution
- Need reliability above all else

Your job: REDUCE THEIR STRESS by providing:
1. Predictable, consistent service every time
2. Accurate order execution (dietary requirements, timing)
3. Professional, scripted ordering process
4. Proactive communication and follow-up
5. Trust-building through flawless execution
6. Making it easy for them (house accounts, simple menus)

Result: They put you on their "catering rotation list" and keep ordering.

FRAMEWORK 4: CATERING VS. RETAIL -- KEY DIFFERENCES
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Dimension         | Retail              | Catering
------------------|---------------------|---------------------------
Customer          | Individual consumer | Corporation (via order-placer)
Transaction type  | Cash/card at POS    | Invoice, house accounts, AR
Order timing      | Immediate           | Advance orders (next day+)
Order size        | 1 person            | 10-50+ people
Production        | Made-to-order       | Bulk assembly line
Demand driver     | Personal hunger     | Corporate meetings/events
Price sensitivity | High (individual)   | Lower (corporate budget)
Delivery          | Dine-in/takeout     | Delivered to office/venue
Risk              | Within 4 walls      | Outside 4 walls (high risk)
Margin potential  | Standard            | Premium (higher margins)
Selling method    | Passive (walk-in)   | Active (B2B, outbound sales)
Payment terms     | Immediate           | Net 7-30 days (credit)
Follow-up         | Optional            | Required (every order)
Menu              | Full retail menu    | Simplified subset/different
Packaging         | Standard takeout    | Branded platters/boxes
Relationship      | Transactional       | Ongoing B2B relationship

FRAMEWORK 5: CENTRALIZATION VS. DECENTRALIZATION (Franchise Model)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
CENTRALIZE (Brand/Franchisor Level):
- Sales and marketing strategy
- Call center / order management
- Customer service and follow-up
- Billing, invoicing, AR management
- IT and catering software systems
- Menu development and pricing
- Training and education materials
- Quality standards and brand control

DECENTRALIZE (Franchisee/Unit Level):
- Food production / manufacturing
- Order assembly and packaging
- Distribution / delivery
- Local vendor management
- Day-of execution and quality control
- Local relationship maintenance (driver-level)
