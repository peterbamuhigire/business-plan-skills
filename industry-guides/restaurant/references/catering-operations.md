# Catering Operations -- Staffing, Menu Design, Scaling, and KPIs

**Source:** Dardick, E. *Get Catering and Grow Sales!* (2011)

> Figures adapted to Ugandan context (UGX). Percentages and ratios from the source are universal.

Covers staffing, menu design, growth strategies, KPIs, and actionable frameworks for catering operations.

---

## 1. Staffing for Catering Operations

### Key Positions

| Role | Responsibilities |
|------|-----------------|
| **Director of Catering** | Owns catering strategy, P&L, and growth targets; documents and trains workforce |
| **Catering Sales Managers** | B2B relationship development; door-to-door and telephone-based selling |
| **Customer Service Reps** | Script-based order entry; follow-up calls after every order; dedicated to catering |
| **Delivery Coordinator** | Maps deliveries day-before; assigns orders to drivers; manages labels and packing slips |
| **Primary Drivers** | Truck maintenance; sort and deliver orders; ask for referrals; help production in off-time (~4 hrs/day driving) |
| **Catering Lead (Kitchen)** | Ensures pre-production complete; leads assembly and packaging; quality control |

### Labour Model
- Leverage **existing** labour pool first (add catering tasks to current roles)
- Front-counter staff can prepare beverages, fold boxes in off-peak times
- Only add dedicated catering positions as volume grows
- Goal: drive down per-unit labour costs by spreading labour across both retail and catering revenue

### Franchise Staffing
- **Centralise:** Sales, marketing, call centre, order management, payment processing, AR
- **Franchisees focus ONLY on:** Order execution (manufacturing + distribution)
- Centralised customer service provides consistent brand experience

## 2. Menu Design for Catering vs Dine-In

### Fundamental Principle
Catering menu is a **subset** of capabilities, not an extension of the retail menu. Products must travel well outside four walls.

### Development Approach
- Start with existing ingredient/flavour profile
- Create **simpler** menu than retail (easier to scale across locations)
- Different packaging, pricing, presentation, and consumption experience
- Remove items that do not travel well

### Recommended Formats (Priority Order)

| Format | Details | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| **Trays & Platters** | Three sizes: small (6-10), medium (10-15), large (15+); price by platter not per person | Cornerstone of catering; easiest to scale |
| **Box Lunches** | Minimum order ~100 recommended | Only worthwhile at scale; best for tours, schools, large seminars |
| **Beverages** | Proprietary/signature drinks sold by gallon | Higher margins than commodity beverages; limit selections |
| **Hot Food** | Limited menu; pasta/fajita/taco bars work well | NOT recommended for beginners; requires specialised packaging |

### Cold vs Hot
- Cold programmes strongly recommended for beginners
- Strongest corporate dayparts: breakfast and lunch
- Continental breakfast, lox & cream cheese, fresh fruit for AM
- Sandwich and salad programmes for lunch

### Packaging Principles
- Branded, high-quality custom packaging (NOT standard black plastic trays)
- Custom corrugated boxes can be 50%+ cheaper than standard black trays
- Branded boxes serve as long-term brand ambassadors in offices

## 3. Growth Strategies and Scaling

### Three-Phase Scaling Framework

**Phase 1 -- Strategy and Alignment**
- Achieve executive alignment first (most critical step)
- Develop formal business plan for catering revenue channel
- Document strategy before doing anything else

**Phase 2 -- Prove the Model**
- Start with corporate-owned stores before extending to franchisees
- Test and refine operations, menu, and systems
- Build policy-and-procedure manual from real experience

**Phase 3 -- Scale Across System**
- Roll out to franchise/licensee communities
- Centralise services where possible
- Franchisees focus solely on order execution

### Growth Drivers
1. Relationship selling (B2B, door-to-door, telemarketing)
2. Promotional sampling (free "Gourmet Food Platter for Five")
3. In-store cross-pollination (retail customers become catering buyers)
4. National accounts (negotiate contracts in advance)
5. Referrals from satisfied clients
6. Consistent follow-up on every order

### Scaling Challenges
- Retail and catering will compete for resources at unit level
- Must re-engineer roles/responsibilities to accommodate catering
- Keep catering and retail transactions separate
- Never let catering calls disrupt retail customer experience

## 4. Key KPIs and Benchmarks

### Revenue Benchmarks

| Metric | Target | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Daily sales per store | UGX 500,000 | Mon-Fri; conservative: UGX 250,000/day |
| Annual per store | ~UGX 100M | 48 productive weeks |
| Catering as % of total sales | 20-30% | |
| Catering contribution to gross margin | Up to 40% | Premium margins vs retail |
| National brand total | UGX 12B-UGX 62B/year | System-wide |

### Order Benchmarks

| Metric | Benchmark |
|--------|-----------|
| Average order value | UGX 65,000-UGX 100,000 |
| Average per-person price | ~UGX 6,500 (lunch) |
| Orders per day per store | 5-8 (target) |
| Average order size | ~16 people |

### Operational KPIs
1. Daily catering sales per store
2. Number of orders per day per store
3. Average order value and guest count
4. On-time delivery rate
5. Order accuracy rate
6. Menu mix analysis (80/20 distribution)
7. Production efficiency (pre-production vs day-of assembly)

### Sales and Customer KPIs
1. Sleepy client rate (no orders in 60-90 days)
2. Lead-to-customer conversion time
3. Customer order frequency
4. Client retention rate
5. Referral generation rate
6. Brand rotation position
7. Sampling-to-customer conversion rate
8. Follow-up completion rate

### Financial KPIs
1. Catering gross margin % (should exceed retail)
2. Catering contribution to total gross margin shillings
3. Per-unit labour cost impact (should decrease with volume)
4. AR aging (house accounts, corporate credit)
5. Incremental cost per catering shilling
6. Delivery and packaging cost per order

## 5. Actionable Frameworks

### Revenue Math

```
Conservative (per store):
  5 orders/day x UGX 75,000 avg = UGX 375,000/day x 5 days = UGX 1,875,000/week x 48 weeks = UGX 90M/year

Target (per store):
  6-8 orders/day x UGX 75,000 avg = UGX 500,000/day x 5 days = UGX 2,500,000/week x 48 weeks = UGX 120M/year

100-Store System:
  Conservative: UGX 9B/year | Target: UGX 12B/year
  With margin premium: 20-30% of sales = 40% of gross margin shillings
```

### Catering Launch Checklist
- [ ] Executive alignment achieved (unanimous commitment)
- [ ] Catering business plan documented
- [ ] Director of Catering hired/appointed
- [ ] Catering menu developed (simple, leveraging existing ingredients)
- [ ] Branded packaging sourced (custom, cost-effective)
- [ ] Policy-and-procedure manual created
- [ ] Pricing strategy set (premium to retail)
- [ ] Billing/invoicing system in place (separate from POS)
- [ ] House account/credit facility policies established
- [ ] Staff trained in "language of catering"
- [ ] In-store marketing materials deployed
- [ ] Promotional sampling package created
- [ ] Sales team prospecting (B2B)
- [ ] Delivery logistics planned
- [ ] Follow-up systems in place
- [ ] Metrics tracking established

### Psychology of the Catering Sale
The order-placer is **stressed** -- accountable internally if wrong, facing time pressure, risking professional reputation. Reduce their stress by providing:
1. Predictable, consistent service every time
2. Accurate order execution (dietary requirements, timing)
3. Professional, scripted ordering process
4. Proactive communication and follow-up
5. Trust-building through flawless execution
6. Ease (house accounts, simple menus)

### Catering vs Retail -- Key Differences

| Dimension | Retail | Catering |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| Customer | Individual consumer | Corporation (via order-placer) |
| Transaction | Cash/card at POS | Invoice, house accounts, AR |
| Order timing | Immediate | Advance (next day+) |
| Order size | 1 person | 10-50+ people |
| Production | Made-to-order | Bulk assembly line |
| Price sensitivity | High (individual) | Lower (corporate budget) |
| Margin potential | Standard | Premium (higher margins) |
| Selling method | Passive (walk-in) | Active (B2B, outbound) |
| Follow-up | Optional | Required (every order) |
