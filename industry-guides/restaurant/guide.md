# Restaurant & Food Service Industry Guide

> Figures adapted to Ugandan context (UGX). Percentages and ratios from the source are universal.

Industry-specific context for generating bankable restaurant business plans. Covers all food-service formats from full-service restaurants to coffeehouses, bakeries, and catering operations.

## Industry Overview

The restaurant industry represents approximately 4% of GDP in developed economies. Over 50% of consumer food spending occurs at restaurants, driven by dual-income households, convenience-seeking lifestyles, and the social aspects of dining out.

**Failure rate:** 80% of new restaurants fail within the first five years. The three primary causes are:
1. **Undercapitalisation** -- spending too much on build-out, lacking working capital for the start-up period
2. **Poor inventory control** -- food waste, theft, inconsistent portioning
3. **Poor payroll management** -- overstaffing, inadequate scheduling, high turnover

## Restaurant Types and Formats

| Type | Startup Cost Range | Key Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **Full-service restaurant** | UGX 100M-UGX 500M+ | Complete menu, wait staff, highest labour costs, broadest menu |
| **Fast-casual** | UGX 30M-UGX 150M | Focused menu, counter/table service, faster turnover |
| **Pizzeria** | UGX 100M-UGX 500M+ | Growing urban segment; 74% have dining rooms; delivery significant |
| **Sandwich shop/Deli** | UGX 20M-UGX 100M | Peak-period dependent (lunch); fast menu adaptation |
| **Coffeehouse** | UGX 20M-UGX 80M+ | Extremely high margins on drinks (95%+ water); up to 500 customers/day |
| **Bakery** | UGX 20M-UGX 100M+ | Wholesale, retail, or both; 35% of gross to inventory |
| **Catering** | UGX 15M-UGX 100M+ | Most flexible entry; can rent commercial kitchen; corporate + social markets |
| **Food truck** | UGX 15M-UGX 50M | Lower overhead, mobile, concept-testing vehicle |
| **Cart/Kiosk** | UGX 3M-UGX 10M+ | Lowest entry cost; mall or event-based |

## Key Financial Benchmarks

| Metric | Industry Range | Target |
|---|---|---|
| **Food cost %** | 28-40% of food sales | 30-35% |
| **Beverage cost %** | 18-30% of beverage sales | 20-25% |
| **Labour cost %** | 24-35% of total sales | 28-33% |
| **Prime cost** (food + labour) | 55-65% of total sales | < 65% |
| **Occupancy cost** | 3.5-9.5% of total sales | 5-8% |
| **Net profit margin** | 3-15% of total sales | 8-12% |
| **Food inventory turnover** | 2-4x per month | ~29x per year |
| **Marketing spend** | 2-5% of gross sales | 3% |

## Key Success Factors

1. **Adequate capitalisation** -- budget for build-out, equipment, initial inventory, preopening expenses, AND 3-6 months working capital
2. **Sound business plan** -- feasibility study followed by detailed plan with realistic projections
3. **Superior location** -- accessibility, parking (1 spot per 3 seats), visibility, compatible demographics
4. **Effective cost controls** -- standardised recipes, portion control, purchasing procedures, daily cost tracking
5. **Menu engineering** -- Star/Plowhorse/Puzzle/Dog classification; weighted contribution margin analysis
6. **Customer knowledge** -- demographics, psychographics, dining occasion patterns
7. **Quality and consistency** -- standard recipes, trained staff, food safety (HACCP/ServSafe)
8. **Strong management** -- close supervision, clear policies, employee retention

## Business Plan Structure for Restaurants

A complete restaurant business plan contains three parts:

**Part One -- The Business:**
- Concept description, mission, unique positioning
- Industry trends and market opportunity
- Management team with relevant F&B experience
- Market analysis (demographics, competition, trade area)
- Marketing strategy (pre-opening, ongoing, loyalty)
- Personnel plan with organisational chart
- Risk assessment

**Part Two -- Financial Projections:**
- Start-up budget (equipment, leasehold, working capital, licences, inventory)
- Sales estimation (seats x turnover x average check x days)
- Projected income statement (annual and month-by-month)
- Cash-flow statement (month-by-month)
- Daily break-even analysis
- Sources and uses of funds

**Part Three -- Supporting Documents:**
- Floor plan and kitchen layout
- Sample menu with pricing
- Resumes of principals
- Lease terms or letter of intent
- Legal documents, licences, permits

## Sales Estimation Formula

```
Daily sales per meal period = Seats x Seat turnover rate x Average guest check

Weekly sales = Sum of daily sales across all meal periods and days

Annual sales = Weekly sales x 52
```

## Location Selection Criteria

Key site evaluation factors:
- **Traffic density** -- auto and pedestrian counts during peak periods
- **Customer parking** -- 1 parking spot per 3 restaurant seats
- **History of the site** -- research previous tenants and why they left
- **Going-home side of street** -- generally preferable (exception: breakfast/coffee)
- **Future development** -- check local planning board for planned changes
- **Lease terms** -- all terms are negotiable; budget for build-out tenant improvements

## Common Mistakes and Failure Factors

1. Opening undercapitalised -- no cash reserve for slow start-up months
2. Choosing a location based on low rent rather than customer access
3. Overbuilding -- spending too much on decor and equipment before proving the concept
4. No standardised recipes or portion control -- food costs spiral
5. Ignoring labour scheduling -- overstaffing during slow periods
6. Menu too large -- increases waste, prep time, and inventory costs
7. No daily cost tracking -- discovering problems at month-end instead of daily
8. Neglecting marketing -- assuming "build it and they will come"
9. Owner not present -- absentee ownership is a major failure predictor
10. No feasibility study -- skipping market research before committing capital

## Catering as a Growth Channel

For existing restaurants, catering represents a high-margin revenue expansion:
- Target UGX 500,000/day per store; average order UGX 65,000-UGX 100,000
- Catering at 20-30% of sales can deliver 40% of gross margin shillings
- B2B model with house accounts; items priced higher than retail menu
- Can start with cold food programmes; add hot food as volume grows

## Customer Demographics and Dining Behaviour

| Segment | Key Characteristics | Preferences |
|---------|-------------------|-------------|
| Gen Y (born 1980-2000) | 75M+ strong, most ethnically diverse | Low prices, fast-food, tech-friendly |
| Gen X (born 1965-1979) | Strong family values, value-focused | Quick-service, midscale, buffets |
| Baby Boomers (born 1946-1964) | Largest population segment, affluent | Upscale, family-friendly, formal dining |
| Empty Nesters (50s-64) | Highest per-capita/discretionary income | Upscale, excellent service focus |
| Seniors (65+) | Generally fixed incomes | Family-style, early-bird specials |

- Lunch is the most popular meal to eat away from home
- 40-50% of sit-down customers arrive in pairs; 30% alone or in threes
- 66% visit a restaurant on a birthday (most popular dining-out occasion)
- 75% ask for alternative preparation methods or substitutions
