## 1. Retail Strategy Framework

### 1.1 Organisational Mission

Every retail business begins with a clear **mission statement** that defines its purpose, target market, and competitive positioning. The mission guides all subsequent strategic decisions — from location choice to merchandise mix.

A Kampala-based electronics retailer might state: *"To provide affordable, quality electronics with expert after-sales support to middle-income households in the Greater Kampala Metropolitan Area."*

### 1.2 Situation Analysis

Situation analysis examines the retailer's current position across several dimensions:

| Dimension | Key Questions |
|-----------|--------------|
| Ownership and management | Sole proprietor, partnership, or limited company? Owner-managed or professional management? |
| Goods/service category | What line of business? General merchandise or specialty? |
| Personal abilities | Aptitude, education, and practical experience of the owner(s) |
| Financial resources | Start-up capital, working capital, access to credit facilities |
| Time demands | Hours of operation, seasonal patterns, owner involvement required |

### 1.3 Objectives

Retailers typically pursue objectives across four areas:

- **Sales** — growth targets, stability, and market share (e.g., achieving UGX 150,000,000 monthly turnover within 18 months)
- **Profit** — net profit margin, return on investment (e.g., 15% ROI target)
- **Satisfaction of publics** — customers, employees, suppliers, and regulatory bodies
- **Image and positioning** — how the retailer is perceived relative to competitors

### 1.4 SWOT Analysis

| | Helpful | Harmful |
|---|---------|---------|
| **Internal** | **Strengths** — e.g., prime Kampala Road location, strong supplier relationships, experienced staff | **Weaknesses** — e.g., limited capital, narrow product range, no online presence |
| **External** | **Opportunities** — e.g., growing urban middle class, new housing estates in Wakiso, rising smartphone penetration | **Threats** — e.g., new shopping mall competition, import duty increases, exchange rate volatility |

### 1.5 Controllable vs Uncontrollable Variables

**Controllable variables** are the elements a retailer can directly influence:

1. **Store location** — site selection, terms of tenancy, format
2. **Managing the business** — organisation structure, human resources, operations
3. **Merchandise management and pricing** — product assortment, buying, pricing strategy
4. **Communicating with the customer** — store image, advertising, promotions

**Uncontrollable variables** are external factors to which a retailer must adapt:

- Consumer demographics and lifestyles
- Economic conditions (inflation, interest rates, UGX exchange rate)
- Competition (number, size, and strategies of rival firms)
- Technology (e-commerce, mobile money adoption)
- Legal and regulatory environment (KCCA trading licences, tax policy, import regulations)
- Seasonality and cultural factors (festive seasons, school terms)

A sound retail strategy integrates both sets of variables, adapting to external realities whilst optimising internal decisions.

## 2. Retail Formats and Models

### 2.1 Comparison of Retail Formats

| Format | Ownership | Size | Product Range | Pricing | Ugandan Examples |
|--------|-----------|------|---------------|---------|-----------------|
| **Independent store** | Single owner/family | Small (20–100 m²) | Narrow, tailored to local demand | Flexible, often negotiable | Dukas in Owino, neighbourhood shops in Ntinda |
| **Chain** | Corporate, multiple units | Medium–large | Standardised across outlets | Uniform pricing | Hotpoint, Uchumi (historic) |
| **Franchise** | Franchisee with franchisor support | Varies | Prescribed by franchisor | Set by franchisor guidelines | Cafe Javas, KFC Uganda, Shell convenience stores |
| **Department store** | Corporate or family | Large (1,000+ m²) | Wide variety, multiple departments | Mid to upper range | Game Stores (Lugogo), Shoprite homeware sections |
| **Supermarket** | Corporate chain or independent | Medium–large (500–3,000 m²) | Groceries plus general merchandise | Competitive, promotional | Shoprite, Capital Shoppers, Carrefour (Kampala) |
| **Convenience store** | Independent or chain | Small (30–100 m²) | Limited, fast-moving essentials | Premium (convenience mark-up) | Petrol station shops, neighbourhood mini-marts |
| **Specialty store** | Independent or chain | Small–medium | Deep assortment in one category | Varies by positioning | Aristoc Booklex (books), Mr Price (fashion) |
| **Discount store** | Chain | Medium–large | Broad but shallow, value lines | Everyday low prices | Quality Supermarket (selected lines) |
| **Online retailer** | Any | No physical selling floor | Can be very broad | Competitive, transparent | Jumia Uganda, Kikuubo Online, SafeBoda Shop |

### 2.2 Forms of Ownership

**Independent retailers** enjoy full operational flexibility, personal customer relationships, and low overhead. However, they face limited capital, dependence on the owner's skills, and difficulty achieving economies of scale. In Uganda, independent retailers dominate the retail landscape, from Kikuubo wholesale-retail traders to suburban dukas.

**Chain retailers** operate multiple outlets under common ownership, benefiting from centralised buying (bulk discounts from suppliers), standardised operations, and stronger brand recognition. Chains can negotiate better terms with landlords and suppliers.

**Franchise operations** combine independent ownership with franchisor support: strategic planning assistance, a known brand, cooperative advertising, and training. The franchisee pays initial fees (often UGX 50,000,000–500,000,000 depending on the brand) plus ongoing royalties (typically 4–8% of sales). Restrictions apply to product lines, suppliers, and operating standards.

### 2.3 Store-Based vs Non-Store Retailing

Non-store formats increasingly complement physical shops:

- **Direct selling** — door-to-door and agent-based (common for cosmetics, household goods)
- **Mobile commerce** — ordering via apps and social media (WhatsApp catalogues, Instagram shops)
- **Automated vending** — limited but growing in Kampala office buildings and fuel stations
- **Market/periodic trading** — weekly and bi-weekly markets in district trading centres

## 3. Trading Area Analysis

### 3.1 Defining the Trading Area

A **trading area** is the geographical zone from which a retail outlet draws its customers. Every trading area has three segments:

| Segment | Share of Customers | Description |
|---------|--------------------|-------------|
| **Primary** | 50–80% | Closest to the store; highest customer density; little overlap with competitors |
| **Secondary** | 15–25% | Wider zone; customers are more dispersed |
| **Fringe** | 5–10% | Most distant customers; includes outshoppers willing to travel for specific goods |

**Example:** A supermarket in Ntinda might have a primary trading area of 2 km radius (Ntinda, Kisaasi, parts of Bukoto), a secondary area of 4 km (Naguru, Kyanja, Kulambiro), and a fringe area extending 8 km (Naalya, Kiwatule, Muyenga — customers who pass through on commute routes).

### 3.2 Factors Affecting Trading Area Size

- **Store type and size** — larger stores with deeper assortment draw from wider areas
- **Competitor locations** — nearby competitors reduce each store's trading area
- **Transport access** — main roads (e.g., Jinja Road, Northern Bypass) expand reach; poor roads or boda-boda-only access contracts it
- **Traffic barriers** — railway crossings, swamps, steep hills, and toll points
- **Population density** — densely settled areas (Kabalagala, Wandegeya) support smaller trading areas with more stores

### 3.3 Reilly's Law of Retail Gravitation

**Reilly's law** determines the boundary (point of indifference) between two competing trading centres based on their relative populations:

```
         d
Dab = ─────────────
       1 + √(Pb/Pa)
```

Where:
- **Dab** = distance from Town A to the point of indifference (in km)
- **d** = distance between Town A and Town B (in km)
- **Pa** = population of Town A
- **Pb** = population of Town B

**Ugandan example:** Mukono town (population ~160,000) and Jinja city (population ~80,000) are approximately 60 km apart along the Jinja Highway.

```
Dab = 60 / (1 + √(80,000/160,000))
    = 60 / (1 + √0.5)
    = 60 / (1 + 0.707)
    = 60 / 1.707
    ≈ 35 km
```

The point of indifference is roughly **35 km from Mukono** (and 25 km from Jinja). Customers living within 35 km of Mukono are more likely to shop there; those closer to Jinja gravitate toward Jinja. A retailer planning a new outlet between the two towns would use this to decide which centre offers a larger untapped market.

**Limitations of Reilly's law:**
1. Only measures distance along major roads; cross-streets and shortcuts are ignored
2. Travel time may matter more than distance (a 10 km journey through Kampala congestion may feel longer than 30 km on an open highway)
3. Perceived distance differs from actual distance — a pleasant, well-lit shopping environment feels "closer" than a congested, poorly maintained one

### 3.4 Huff's Law of Shopper Attraction

**Huff's law** refines trading area analysis by incorporating store size (selling space) and travel time sensitivity:

```
              Sj / (Tij)^λ
Pij = ─────────────────────────
       Σ [ Sj / (Tij)^λ ] for all j
```

Where:
- **Pij** = probability of a consumer at location *i* shopping at location *j*
- **Sj** = square metres of selling space at location *j*
- **Tij** = travel time from consumer's home *i* to location *j*
- **λ** = travel time sensitivity parameter (higher for convenience goods, lower for specialty goods)

This model helps retailers forecast market share when competing against multiple shopping locations of different sizes and distances.

### 3.5 Population Analysis

Key population characteristics to evaluate in any Ugandan trading area:

| Factor | Data Source |
|--------|------------|
| Total population and density | UBOS Census data, parish/sub-county records |
| Age distribution | UBOS demographic reports |
| Household income levels | UBOS household survey, local economic data |
| Employment patterns | District commercial officer reports |
| Education levels | District education statistics |
| Housing growth (new estates) | KCCA/municipal planning records, developer announcements |

### 3.6 Economic Base Analysis

The **economic base** is the commercial and industrial structure that provides residents' income. A trading area dependent on a single employer or industry (e.g., a sugar estate town) is riskier than a diversified one.

**Assessment checklist:**
- Dominant industries and employers in the area
- Percentage of labour force in each sector (agriculture, services, manufacturing, government)
- Availability of banking and financial services (mobile money agent density)
- Transport infrastructure (tarmac roads, public transport routes)
- Seasonal economic fluctuations (harvest periods, school terms)
- Future development plans (industrial parks, new roads, government investments)

### 3.7 Competition Mapping and Saturation Index

**Key saturation ratios:**

| Ratio | Formula | Interpretation |
|-------|---------|----------------|
| Persons per retail establishment | Population ÷ Number of stores | Higher = less saturated (opportunity) |
| Average sales per store | Total area retail sales ÷ Number of stores | Benchmark against your projected sales |
| Sales per capita | Total area retail sales ÷ Population | Spending power indicator |
| Sales per square metre | Total area retail sales ÷ Total selling space | Efficiency measure |

**Trading area saturation levels:**
- **Understored** — too few stores to serve the population; opportunity exists for new entrants
- **Saturated** — the right number of stores; stable but competitive
- **Overstored** — too many stores; some retailers cannot earn adequate profit; avoid or differentiate sharply

**Example:** If a sub-county of 50,000 people has 3 pharmacies generating combined annual sales of UGX 900,000,000, the sales per capita for pharmaceuticals is UGX 18,000. If the national urban average is UGX 25,000, the area may be **understored** — signalling an opportunity for a new pharmacy.

## 4. Site Selection

### 4.1 Location Types

#### 4.1.1 Isolated (Freestanding) Store

A standalone retail outlet, not adjacent to other stores.

| Advantages | Disadvantages |
|------------|---------------|
| No direct nearby competition | Difficult to attract initial customers |
| Lower rent | Consumers prefer variety and one-stop shopping |
| Operational flexibility (hours, signage, layout) | All costs borne alone (security, lighting, parking) |
| Good road visibility | Must generate own traffic through advertising |
| Ample parking | Zoning restrictions may apply |

**Best suited to:** Large-format stores (supermarkets, hardware), convenience stores on busy highways, fuel station forecourt shops. In Uganda, roadside hardware stores along Northern Bypass or Entebbe Road follow this model.

#### 4.1.2 Unplanned Business District

Stores cluster organically without coordinated planning. Four sub-types:

- **Central Business District (CBD)** — the commercial heart of a city (e.g., Kampala Road, William Street, Luwum Street). High pedestrian and vehicular traffic, wide variety of stores, access to public transport. Weaknesses: congestion, limited parking, high rents, aging infrastructure.
- **Secondary Business District (SBD)** — important intersections away from the CBD (e.g., Kabalagala, Wandegeya, Ntinda trading centre). Smaller scale than CBD but strong local draw.
- **Neighbourhood Business District (NBD)** — serves a single residential area with convenience goods (e.g., a cluster of shops at Bugolobi, Kyanja, or Munyonyo junction). Led by a supermarket or pharmacy.
- **String** — stores along a road with similar product lines (e.g., furniture shops along Nasser Road, auto parts dealers on Ben Kiwanuka Street).

#### 4.1.3 Planned Shopping Centre

Centrally owned or managed, designed as a unit with balanced tenancy. Characteristics in the Ugandan context:

| Feature | Regional Centre | Community Centre | Neighbourhood Centre |
|---------|----------------|------------------|---------------------|
| Typical examples | Acacia Mall, Garden City, Arena Mall | Ntinda Shopping Centre, Kisementi | Local strip malls in Naalya, Kira |
| Anchor tenant(s) | Supermarket + department store or cinema | Supermarket or large specialty store | Supermarket or pharmacy |
| Number of stores | 30–100+ | 10–25 | 3–15 |
| Trading area | Up to 30 min drive | Up to 20 min drive | Under 15 min drive |
| Typical rent (UGX/m²/month) | 50,000–120,000 | 25,000–60,000 | 15,000–35,000 |

### 4.2 Site Evaluation Criteria

When assessing a specific site, evaluate:

1. **Pedestrian traffic** — volume, type (workers, shoppers, students), and timing (peak hours, weekend patterns)
2. **Vehicular traffic** — daily counts on adjacent roads; high speed reduces accessibility
3. **Parking** — number of spaces, ease of entry/exit, cost to shoppers
4. **Transport access** — proximity to taxi/matatu stages, boda-boda access points, main bus routes
5. **Store composition** — compatibility with neighbouring retailers; benefit from affinity (complementary businesses draw shared traffic)
6. **Site characteristics** — visibility from main road, size and shape of premises, condition of building, signage opportunities, utility supply (UMEME reliability, water)
7. **Terms of occupancy** — rent structure, lease length, renewal options, restrictions

### 4.3 Traffic Analysis

Practical methods for assessing site traffic in the Ugandan context:

- **Manual pedestrian counts** — station observers at proposed locations during different times (morning, lunch, evening, weekend)
- **Vehicle counts** — UNRA traffic data for major roads; manual counts for local roads
- **Customer origin surveys** — ask existing nearby shop customers where they live and how they arrived
- **Mobile money transaction data** — agent transaction volumes as a proxy for commercial activity
- **Boda-boda stage activity** — number of riders and passenger volumes as an indicator of foot traffic

## 5. Location Cost Analysis

### 5.1 Lease Types

| Lease Type | Structure | Best For |
|------------|-----------|----------|
| **Straight (fixed) lease** | Constant monthly payment (e.g., UGX 3,000,000/month) | Established retailers with predictable sales; provides cost certainty |
| **Percentage lease** | Rent = base minimum + percentage of gross sales (e.g., 5–8% of monthly sales above UGX 10,000,000) | Shopping centres; aligns landlord and tenant interests |
| **Graduated lease** | Predetermined rent increases at set intervals (e.g., UGX 2,500,000/month for years 1–3, then UGX 3,200,000 for years 4–5) | New businesses expecting sales growth; avoids sudden rent shocks |
| **Net lease** | Tenant pays all maintenance costs (electricity, water, interior repairs, security) in addition to base rent | Standalone properties; gives tenant full control of premises |
| **Maintenance-increase-recoupment lease** | Base rent plus provisions for increases if landlord's costs (property rates, insurance) rise beyond a threshold | Long-term leases in areas with rising municipal charges |

### 5.2 Occupancy Costs as a Percentage of Sales

As a general benchmark, total occupancy costs (rent, rates, utilities, insurance, maintenance) should not exceed **8–15% of gross sales** for most retail formats. Higher percentages erode profitability severely.

| Format | Target Occupancy Cost (% of Sales) |
|--------|-----------------------------------|
| Supermarket | 3–6% |
| Specialty clothing | 8–12% |
| Restaurant/cafe | 8–15% |
| Convenience store | 5–8% |
| Pharmacy | 4–8% |

**Break-even check:** If monthly rent is UGX 5,000,000 and total occupancy costs are UGX 8,000,000, the shop must generate at least UGX 80,000,000–100,000,000 in monthly sales (at 8–10% occupancy ratio) to remain viable.

### 5.3 Ugandan Commercial Property Context

Key considerations for retail premises in Uganda:

- **Kampala CBD** — rents range from UGX 25,000–80,000 per square metre per month for ground-floor retail; premium locations (Kampala Road, Jinja Road frontage) command higher rates
- **Suburban shopping centres** — UGX 30,000–120,000/m²/month depending on the mall's profile and anchor tenants
- **District trading centres** — significantly lower rents (UGX 10,000–25,000/m²/month in upcountry towns like Gulu, Mbarara, Fort Portal)
- **Lease terms** — typically 2–5 years for small retail; 5–10 years for anchor tenants in malls; rent reviews commonly every 2–3 years
- **Currency clauses** — some landlords (particularly in newer malls) quote rents in USD, exposing tenants to exchange rate risk
- **Advance payments** — landlords commonly require 6–12 months' rent in advance, a significant capital outlay for start-ups
- **Rates and levies** — KCCA property rates, trading licences, and signage fees apply in Kampala; equivalent municipal charges in other urban areas
- **Utility costs** — UMEME electricity (often supplemented by generator costs for power reliability), NWSC water charges

### 5.4 Ownership vs Leasing Decision

| Factor | Own | Lease |
|--------|-----|-------|
| Initial capital required | Very high | Moderate (advance rent + deposits) |
| Monthly cost predictability | Stable (mortgage) | Subject to rent reviews |
| Operational flexibility | Full control | Landlord restrictions may apply |
| Location flexibility | Difficult to relocate | Can move at lease end |
| Long-term financial benefit | Property appreciation | No equity built |
| Risk | Property market risk | Lease non-renewal risk |

For most start-up retailers in Uganda, **leasing is strongly preferred** due to the high cost of commercial property ownership and the need to preserve capital for stock and operations.

## 6. Retail Life Cycle

### 6.1 The Four Stages

Retail formats and individual retail businesses pass through a life cycle analogous to the product life cycle:

| Stage | Duration | Characteristics | Profitability |
|-------|----------|-----------------|---------------|
| **1. Innovation (Introduction)** | 6 months – 5 years | New concept enters the market; few competitors; rapid initial growth; heavy investment in setup and marketing | Low to moderate |
| **2. Accelerated Growth** | 3 – 8 years | Sales rise rapidly; the format gains acceptance; competitors enter; investment remains high; the innovator holds a leadership position | High |
| **3. Maturity** | 5 – 15+ years | Growth slows; market becomes saturated; intense competition; pressure on margins; need for reinvention or diversification | Moderate, declining |
| **4. Decline** | Variable | Sales and profits fall; overheads become burdensome; new formats or competitors have captured the market; strategic repositioning or exit required | Low to negative |

### 6.2 Strategic Responses by Stage

| Stage | Recommended Actions |
|-------|-------------------|
| Innovation | Secure prime location early; build brand awareness; establish supplier relationships; invest in customer experience |
| Growth | Expand to additional locations; refine operations for efficiency; build customer loyalty programmes; negotiate better supplier terms |
| Maturity | Renovate or reformat stores; introduce new product lines or services; strengthen online/omnichannel presence; focus on cost control |
| Decline | Evaluate viability honestly; consider repositioning, downsizing, or converting to a new format; exit unprofitable locations; liquidate excess stock |

### 6.3 The Wheel of Retailing

The **wheel of retailing** theory (McNair, 1958) describes a cyclical pattern:

1. **Entry phase** — a new retailer enters the market with low costs, low margins, and low prices, attracting customers from established competitors
2. **Trading-up phase** — success leads to better facilities, wider ranges, and higher costs; the retailer becomes established but loses its low-cost advantage
3. **Vulnerability phase** — the now-established retailer is challenged by new low-cost entrants, and the cycle repeats

This pattern has been observed in Uganda as traditional market traders face competition from supermarkets, which in turn face pressure from discount formats and online sellers.

## 7. Competitive Strategy

### 7.1 Positioning

Retailers position themselves along key dimensions:

- **Price vs service** — from discount self-service (e.g., Shoprite) to full-service premium (e.g., boutique stores in Kololo)
- **Product breadth vs depth** — wide range with shallow stock (general merchandise) vs narrow range with deep expertise (specialty)
- **Mass merchandising vs niche retailing** — broad market appeal vs targeting a specific customer segment

**Positioning map example (Ugandan fashion retail):**

| | Budget | Mid-range | Premium |
|---|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Broad range** | Second-hand (mitumba) markets | Mr Price, Woolworths | — |
| **Focused range** | — | Local designer boutiques (Ntinda, Bugolobi) | Exclusive international brands (Acacia Mall) |

### 7.2 Competitive Advantages

A retailer's **competitive advantage** — its distinct competency relative to rivals — may derive from:

- **Location superiority** — the most accessible, visible, or convenient site
- **Price leadership** — lowest cost structure enabling lowest prices
- **Assortment uniqueness** — exclusive brands, curated selection, or hard-to-find products
- **Service excellence** — knowledgeable staff, after-sales support, delivery services
- **Customer relationships** — personal attention, loyalty programmes, community engagement
- **Technology adoption** — mobile money integration, online ordering, inventory systems

### 7.3 Target Market Approaches

| Approach | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Mass marketing** | Broad appeal, wide assortment, popular prices | Capital Shoppers Supermarket |
| **Concentrated marketing** | Single segment, tailored offering | A baby products specialty shop in Ntinda |
| **Differentiated marketing** | Multiple segments, distinct approach for each | A retailer with a flagship store in Acacia Mall and a value outlet in Nansana |

### 7.4 Responding to Competition

Practical competitive responses for Ugandan retailers:

1. **Monitor competitors regularly** — visit their stores, track their pricing, observe their customer traffic
2. **Differentiate on service** — in markets where products are similar, customer experience becomes the deciding factor
3. **Leverage local knowledge** — independent retailers can respond faster to local demand shifts than national chains
4. **Build supplier relationships** — exclusive or preferential supply agreements create barriers for competitors
5. **Invest in loyalty** — repeat customers are less expensive to serve than new ones; loyalty cards, WhatsApp groups, and personalised service build retention

## 8. Growth Strategies

### 8.1 Expansion Planning

Before expanding, a retailer should confirm:

- The existing store(s) are consistently profitable
- Systems (inventory, staffing, financial controls) can scale
- The target trading area for the new outlet has been analysed thoroughly
- Sufficient capital is available without over-leveraging the business
- Management capacity exists to oversee multiple locations

### 8.2 Multi-Store Strategy

Opening additional outlets requires careful attention to:

- **Cannibalisation** — will the new store take sales from existing locations rather than capturing new customers? Trading area overlap analysis is essential.
- **Standardisation vs localisation** — chain benefits come from consistent systems, but each store must adapt to its local trading area characteristics.
- **Central vs local management** — centralised buying and financial control with local operational management is a common effective model.

### 8.3 Franchising as a Growth Model

For a Ugandan retailer considering franchising to scale:

| Consideration | Detail |
|---------------|--------|
| **Franchisor benefits** | Rapid expansion with lower capital; franchisee motivation (owner-operators); local market knowledge |
| **Franchisor obligations** | Training programmes; operational manuals; marketing support; quality control; supply chain management |
| **Typical franchise fees** | Initial fee: UGX 20,000,000–200,000,000+; ongoing royalties: 4–8% of gross sales; marketing fund: 1–3% of sales |
| **Legal framework** | Uganda does not yet have dedicated franchise legislation; contracts governed by general contract law; seek specialist legal advice |
| **Key risk** | Franchisee non-compliance damaging the brand; mitigate through clear agreements, regular audits, and training |

### 8.4 Online Extension

Extending a physical retail business online offers:

- **Expanded trading area** — serve customers beyond the physical store's reach, including upcountry towns
- **Lower marginal costs** — no additional rent for each new customer served
- **Data collection** — digital transactions provide customer insights for better stock and marketing decisions
- **24/7 availability** — customers can browse and order outside shop hours

**Practical steps for a Ugandan retailer going online:**

1. Start with a curated product catalogue on social media (WhatsApp Business, Instagram, Facebook Shop)
2. Integrate mobile money payment (MTN MoMo, Airtel Money) for seamless checkout
3. Partner with delivery services (SafeBoda, Sendy, Bolt) for last-mile fulfilment
4. Consider a simple e-commerce website as the business scales
5. Track online vs in-store sales to understand channel contribution

### 8.5 Other Growth Options

- **Product range extension** — adding complementary categories (a clothing shop adding accessories and shoes)
- **Service addition** — offering tailoring, repairs, gift wrapping, or consultations alongside products
- **Market development** — entering new geographic markets (e.g., a Kampala retailer opening in Mbarara or Gulu)
- **Vertical integration** — manufacturing own-brand products or controlling supply to reduce costs and improve margins

## Summary Checklist: Retail Strategy and Location Decision

1. Define organisational mission and objectives
2. Conduct SWOT analysis
3. Identify target market and competitive advantages
4. Select retail format appropriate to resources and market
5. Analyse candidate trading areas (population, economic base, competition, saturation)
6. Apply Reilly's law and/or Huff's model to delineate trading area boundaries
7. Choose location type (isolated, business district, or shopping centre)
8. Evaluate specific sites (traffic, accessibility, parking, visibility, co-tenants)
9. Negotiate terms of occupancy (lease type, duration, cost as % of projected sales)
10. Plan for the retail life cycle — build flexibility for adaptation and growth
11. Develop competitive positioning and differentiation strategy
12. Plan growth pathway (multi-store, franchise, online, product extension)
