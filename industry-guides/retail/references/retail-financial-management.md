> Figures adapted to Ugandan context (UGX). Percentages and ratios from the sources are universal.

## 1. Strategic Profit Model (DuPont Model for Retail)

The Strategic Profit Model combines three performance levers that together determine a retailer's return on net worth (Berman & Evans 2013; Tiwari 2008).

### The Three Paths

| Component | Formula | What It Measures |
|---|---|---|
| **Net Profit Margin** | Net Profit After Tax / Net Sales | Profit per shilling of sales |
| **Asset Turnover** | Net Sales / Total Assets | Sales generated per shilling of assets |
| **Financial Leverage** | Total Assets / Net Worth | Degree of debt financing |

### Core Equations

```
Return on Assets (ROA) = Net Profit Margin x Asset Turnover

ROA = (Net Profit After Tax / Net Sales) x (Net Sales / Total Assets)
    = Net Profit After Tax / Total Assets

Return on Net Worth (RONW) = ROA x Financial Leverage

RONW = Net Profit Margin x Asset Turnover x Financial Leverage
      = (Net Profit / Net Sales) x (Net Sales / Total Assets) x (Total Assets / Net Worth)
      = Net Profit / Net Worth
```

### Worked Example — Kampala Gift Shop

| Item | Value |
|---|---|
| Net Sales | UGX 1,200,000,000 |
| Net Profit After Tax | UGX 72,000,000 |
| Total Assets | UGX 1,180,000,000 |
| Net Worth (Owner's Equity) | UGX 620,000,000 |

```
Net Profit Margin   = 72,000,000 / 1,200,000,000 = 6.0%
Asset Turnover      = 1,200,000,000 / 1,180,000,000 = 1.017
Financial Leverage  = 1,180,000,000 / 620,000,000   = 1.903

ROA  = 6.0% x 1.017 = 6.1%
RONW = 6.0% x 1.017 x 1.903 = 11.6%
```

**Interpretation:** The shop earns 6 shillings of profit on every 100 shillings of sales. Each shilling of assets generates about UGX 1.02 in sales. Because the business is moderately leveraged (assets are 1.9 times net worth), RONW (11.6%) is nearly double the ROA (6.1%).

### Improvement Levers

A retailer can raise RONW by (Berman & Evans 2013):
- **Raising net profit margin** — better buying, exclusive products, private-label lines, cost control.
- **Raising asset turnover** — longer hours, lower inventory, leasing rather than owning fixtures.
- **Increasing financial leverage** — using trade credit, short-term loans (with caution).

## 3. Key Financial Ratios

Financial ratios fall into four families (Okunev 2022; Berman & Evans 2013; Tiwari 2008).

### 3.1 Liquidity Ratios

| Ratio | Formula | Healthy Range |
|---|---|---|
| **Current Ratio** | Current Assets / Current Liabilities | 1.5 – 2.5 |
| **Quick Ratio** (Acid Test) | (Cash + Receivables) / Current Liabilities | > 1.0 |
| **Cash Ratio** | Cash & Equivalents / Current Liabilities | 0.5 – 1.0 |

- A current ratio below 1.0 signals inability to meet short-term obligations (Okunev 2022).
- The quick ratio strips out inventory, which may not be quickly convertible to cash.

### 3.2 Profitability Ratios

| Ratio | Formula | Benchmarks |
|---|---|---|
| **Gross Margin %** | (Net Sales – COGS) / Net Sales x 100 | 25–50% varies by format |
| **Net Profit Margin %** | Net Profit After Tax / Net Sales x 100 | 2–10% typical retail |
| **Return on Assets (ROA)** | Net Profit / Total Assets x 100 | > 5% considered good |
| **Return on Equity (ROE/RONW)** | Net Profit / Net Worth x 100 | 10–20% desirable |
| **Profit Margin %** | (Revenue – Total Expenses incl. COGS) / Revenue x 100 | > 20% is good; < 5% is poor |

A net profit margin of around 20% is considered good; 10% is acceptable; 5% is low (Okunev 2022).

### 3.3 Efficiency Ratios

| Ratio | Formula | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Inventory Turnover** | COGS / Average Inventory (at cost) | Or: Units Sold / Average Units on Hand |
| **Asset Turnover** | Net Sales / Total Assets | Higher = more productive assets |
| **GMROI** | Gross Margin (UGX) / Average Inventory at Cost | Combines margin and turnover |
| **Stock-to-Sales** | Inventory at Retail / Net Sales (monthly) | Good range: 0.16 – 0.25 |
| **Sell-Through %** | Units Sold / (Units Sold + Beginning Units) x 100 | 40–80% is healthy |

**GMROI explained** (Berman & Evans 2013):
```
GMROI = (Gross Margin % / 100) x (Net Sales / Average Inventory at Cost)

Example:
  Supermarket:  20% margin x 15 sales-to-stock ratio = 300% GMROI
  Clothing shop: 50% margin x 6 sales-to-stock ratio  = 300% GMROI
```
Both achieve the same GMROI through different strategies — high volume vs. high margin.

### 3.4 Leverage Ratios

| Ratio | Formula | Guidance |
|---|---|---|
| **Debt Ratio** | Total Liabilities / Total Assets | < 0.4 preferred; > 0.6 risky |
| **Debt-to-Equity** | Total Liabilities / Net Worth | < 1.0 conservative; > 2.0 highly leveraged |
| **Interest Coverage** | EBITDA / Interest Expense | > 2.0 comfortable |
| **Debt Service Coverage** | Net Operating Income / Total Debt Service | > 1.0 required; > 2.0 strong |

A debt ratio below 0.4 is preferred; above 0.6 the firm may have difficulty borrowing (Okunev 2022).

## 4. Retail Maths Essentials

### 4.1 Markup

Markup is the amount added to cost to arrive at a selling price (Okunev 2022; Penrith 2009).

**Markup on cost:**
```
Markup Amount = Selling Price – Cost
Markup % (on cost) = (Markup Amount / Cost) x 100
Selling Price = Cost x (1 + Markup % / 100)
```

**Markup on retail (initial markup percent):**
```
Initial Markup % = [(Ticket Price – COGS) / Ticket Price] x 100
```

**Worked example:**
```
A Kampala shop buys a handbag for UGX 60,000 and sells it for UGX 100,000.

Markup Amount      = 100,000 – 60,000 = UGX 40,000
Markup % on cost   = (40,000 / 60,000) x 100 = 66.7%
Markup % on retail = (40,000 / 100,000) x 100 = 40.0%
```

**Markup-to-margin conversion table** (adapted from Penrith 2009):

| Markup % on Cost | Selling Price (item costing UGX 50,000) | Gross Margin | Gross Margin % |
|---|---|---|---|
| 100% | UGX 100,000 | UGX 50,000 | 50.0% |
| 50% | UGX 75,000 | UGX 25,000 | 33.3% |
| 30% | UGX 65,000 | UGX 15,000 | 23.1% |
| 25% | UGX 62,500 | UGX 12,500 | 20.0% |

> Retail markup typically runs between 30% and 40% on cost for general merchandise (Penrith 2009). Luxury and hand-crafted items allow higher markups; essential goods carry smaller margins.

### 4.2 Required Initial Markup (Merchandise Budget)

The initial markup must cover all expenses, reductions, and profit target (Berman & Evans 2013):

```
Required Initial Markup % = (Planned Expenses + Planned Profit + Planned Reductions)
                            / (Planned Net Sales + Planned Reductions)
```

**Worked example:**
```
Planned Net Sales:   UGX 880,000,000
Planned Expenses:    UGX 290,000,000  (33.0% of sales)
Planned Profit:      UGX  60,000,000  ( 6.8% of sales)
Planned Reductions:  UGX  44,000,000  ( 5.0% of sales)
  (markdowns, employee discounts, shrinkage)

Required Initial Markup % = (290M + 60M + 44M) / (880M + 44M)
                          = 394,000,000 / 924,000,000
                          = 42.6%
```

### 4.3 Markdown

Markdowns reduce the original selling price (Okunev 2022).

```
Markdown Amount = Original Price – Sale Price
Markdown %      = (Markdown Amount / Original Price) x 100
```

**Worked example:**
```
A dress originally priced at UGX 180,000 is reduced to UGX 120,000.
Markdown Amount = 180,000 – 120,000 = UGX 60,000
Markdown %      = (60,000 / 180,000) x 100 = 33.3%
```

**Finding original price from sale price and markdown %:**
```
If sale price = UGX 120,000 and markdown = 33.3%:
Let X = original price
X – 0.333X = 120,000
0.667X = 120,000
X = UGX 179,910 (≈ UGX 180,000)
```

### 4.4 Maintained Markup and Gross Margin

```
Maintained Markup = Initial Markup – Markdowns – Shortages – Employee Discounts
Gross Margin      = Net Sales – Cost of Goods Sold
Gross Margin %    = (Gross Margin / Net Sales) x 100
```

### 4.5 Contribution Margin

```
Contribution Margin = Selling Price – Variable Cost per Unit
Contribution Margin % = (Contribution Margin / Selling Price) x 100
```

The contribution margin shows how much each sale contributes towards covering fixed costs and generating profit.

**Effect of discounting on required sales volume** (Penrith 2009):

| Trade Margin | Discount Given | Sales Increase Needed for Same Profit |
|---|---|---|
| 50% | 10% | 25% |
| 40% | 20% | 100% (sales must double) |
| 25% | 10% | 66% |

## 5. Inventory Valuation Methods

Retailers use two broad accounting systems (Berman & Evans 2013).

### 5.1 Cost Method

Every item's cost is recorded (on an accounting sheet or coded on a price tag). Inventory value is calculated by summing the cost of all items in stock.

**Physical inventory (cost method):**
- Count all merchandise at the end of a period
- Look up or decode the cost of each item
- Sum to get ending inventory at cost

**Book (perpetual) inventory (cost method):**
```
Ending Inventory = Beginning Inventory + Purchases – Cost of Goods Sold
```
This provides a running total without requiring a physical count, though periodic counts are still advisable.

### 5.2 FIFO (First-In, First-Out)

Assumes the oldest stock is sold first; ending inventory reflects the most recent purchase costs.

**Worked example:**
```
A shop sells 220 units at UGX 480,000 each (total sales = UGX 105,600,000).
Beginning inventory:   30 units @ UGX 225,000
January purchases:    100 units @ UGX 262,500
October purchases:    150 units @ UGX 337,500
                      280 units total; 220 sold, 60 remaining.

FIFO: The 60 remaining are valued at the latest cost (UGX 337,500 each).
  Ending Inventory = 60 x 337,500 = UGX 20,250,000
  COGS = Total available at cost – Ending Inventory
       = UGX 83,625,000 – UGX 20,250,000 = UGX 63,375,000
  Gross Profit = 105,600,000 – 63,375,000 = UGX 42,225,000
```

FIFO gives a more accurate picture of ending inventory value at current prices.

### 5.3 Weighted Average Cost

```
Weighted Average Cost per Unit = Total Cost of Goods Available / Total Units Available
```

Using the same data:
```
Weighted Avg = 83,625,000 / 280 = UGX 298,661 per unit
Ending Inventory = 60 x 298,661 = UGX 17,919,643
COGS = 220 x 298,661 = UGX 65,705,357
```

### 5.4 Retail Method

The retail method calculates a cost complement (the average cost-to-retail ratio) and applies it to ending inventory at retail to derive ending inventory at cost (Berman & Evans 2013).

**Steps:**
1. **Calculate the cost complement:**
   ```
   Cost Complement = Total Merchandise Available at Cost / Total Merchandise Available at Retail
   ```
2. **Calculate ending inventory at retail:**
   ```
   Ending Inventory (retail) = Merchandise Available at Retail
                              – Sales – Markdowns – Employee Discounts – Shortages
   ```
3. **Convert to cost:**
   ```
   Ending Inventory (at cost) = Ending Inventory (at retail) x Cost Complement
   ```

**Advantages:** Enables monthly P&L without physical counts, helps determine insurance values, and reveals stock shortages.

**Limitations:** Requires detailed bookkeeping of all markups, markdowns, and employee discounts.

## 6. Cash Flow Management

Cash flow is critical to business survival and must be monitored daily (Penrith 2009).

### 6.1 Five Components of Cash Flow

1. **Sales inflow** — the main source; delayed if selling on credit. A retailer that buys on credit and sells for cash has a natural advantage.
2. **New finance** — one-off boosts (loans, investor capital).
3. **Operating expenditure** — salaries (often the largest cost), stock purchases, rent, utilities.
4. **Tax payments** — VAT and income tax; tend to come in large lumps.
5. **Returns to owners/lenders** — interest payments, loan repayments, dividends.

### 6.2 Cash Conversion Cycle

```
Cash Conversion Cycle (days) = Days Inventory Outstanding
                              + Days Sales Outstanding
                              – Days Payable Outstanding

Where:
  Days Inventory Outstanding = (Average Inventory / COGS) x 365
  Days Sales Outstanding     = (Accounts Receivable / Net Sales) x 365
  Days Payable Outstanding   = (Accounts Payable / COGS) x 365
```

**Worked example:**
```
Average Inventory:    UGX 50,000,000
COGS:                 UGX 320,000,000
Accounts Receivable:  UGX 5,000,000
Net Sales:            UGX 530,000,000
Accounts Payable:     UGX 40,000,000

Days Inventory Outstanding = (50M / 320M) x 365 =  57 days
Days Sales Outstanding     = (5M / 530M) x 365   =   3 days
Days Payable Outstanding   = (40M / 320M) x 365  =  46 days

Cash Conversion Cycle = 57 + 3 – 46 = 14 days
```

A shorter cycle is better. This shop converts its inventory investment back to cash in just 14 days.

### 6.3 Seasonal Patterns and Working Capital

Ugandan retail shops typically experience peaks during:
- December / Christmas season
- School-term openings (February, May, September)
- Festival periods (Eid, Easter)

**Practical tips** (Penrith 2009):
- Prepare monthly (or weekly) cash flow forecasts for the next 12 months and update monthly.
- Set a target stock-turn for each product category and monitor it.
- Identify seasonal peaks and troughs; build stock ahead of peaks, reduce before troughs.
- Good stock control releases substantial sums of working capital.
- Today's sales are tomorrow's cash flow — if cash is tight, bring forward sales with promotions.
- Finance dead stock and buying errors are the main cause of cash flow difficulties.

## 7. Budgeting

### 7.1 Sales Forecasting

Use a **monthly sales index** to distribute annual forecasts (Berman & Evans 2013):

```
Monthly Sales Index = (Actual Monthly Sales / Average Monthly Sales) x 100

Monthly Sales Forecast = Forecasted Average Monthly Sales x (Monthly Index / 100)
```

**Worked example:**
```
Last year's total sales: UGX 840,000,000
Average monthly sales:   UGX 70,000,000
Next year's forecast:    UGX 900,000,000
Average monthly forecast: UGX 75,000,000

Month        Last Year Sales    Index   Forecast
January       46,800,000         67     75M x 0.67 = 50,250,000
June         103,800,000        148     75M x 1.48 = 111,000,000
December      94,792,000        135     75M x 1.35 = 101,250,000
```

### 7.2 Expense Budgeting

Include (Penrith 2009):
- **Fixed costs:** rent, rates, insurance, base wages, interest charges — these do not vary with sales volume.
- **Variable costs:** packaging, distribution, raw materials — these fluctuate with sales.
- **Semi-variable costs:** utilities, telephone — contain both fixed and variable elements.

On average, wages represent nearly 29% of outgoings, stock and materials 17.5%, tax 16%, and fuel/transport 10% (Penrith 2009).

### 7.3 Open-to-Buy (OTB) Planning

Open-to-buy is the amount a buyer has left to spend in a given period after accounting for commitments already made (Berman & Evans 2013).

```
Planned Purchases (retail) = Planned Sales + Planned Reductions
                            + Planned End-of-Month Inventory
                            – Beginning-of-Month Inventory

Open-to-Buy (retail) = Planned Purchases – Purchase Commitments Already Made

Open-to-Buy (cost) = OTB at Retail x (Merchandise Cost as % of Selling Price)
```

**Worked example:**
```
June planned sales:             UGX 108,000,000
Planned reductions (5%):        UGX   5,400,000
Planned end-of-month inventory: UGX  72,000,000
Beginning-of-month inventory:   UGX  80,000,000
Purchase commitments made:      UGX  55,000,000
Cost complement:                60%

Planned Purchases = 108M + 5.4M + 72M – 80M = UGX 105,400,000
OTB (retail) = 105,400,000 – 55,000,000 = UGX 50,400,000
OTB (cost)   = 50,400,000 x 0.60 = UGX 30,240,000
```

**Best practice:** Always keep a small OTB balance to take advantage of special deals, fill in items that sell out, or purchase new models when introduced.
