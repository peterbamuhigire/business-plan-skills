# Retail Operations and Merchandising Reference Guide

> Figures adapted to Ugandan context (UGX).

## 1. Store Operations

### Daily Operations Blueprint

Every retail business requires a structured operations blueprint that covers routine tasks performed each day. Operations management is the efficient and effective implementation of policies and tasks that satisfy a retailer's customers, employees, and management. The key operational decisions include staffing levels, store format, personnel utilisation, facility maintenance, inventory management, and security.

**Opening procedures:**
- Arrive 30--60 minutes before opening to prepare the store.
- Count and verify the cash float in each till (typical float: UGX 200,000--500,000 depending on store size).
- Check that the shop floor is clean, displays are tidy, and shelving is fully stocked.
- Switch on lighting, air conditioning, music, and security systems.
- Brief staff on the day's priorities: promotions, expected deliveries, and targets.

**During-trading procedures:**
- Monitor customer flow and adjust staffing to match peak periods.
- Replenish shelves continuously from back-of-store stock.
- Process deliveries: receive goods, check against purchase orders, mark prices, and move to shelves or storage.
- Maintain cleanliness of all areas, including fitting rooms and washrooms.
- Monitor security cameras and watch for suspicious behaviour.

**Closing procedures:**
- Stop admitting customers at the published closing time.
- Reconcile each till: count cash, compare with POS totals, and record variances.
- Secure cash in the safe or prepare a bank deposit.
- Conduct a building check to ensure no one remains inside.
- Lock all exits, activate the alarm system, and ensure exterior lighting is adequate.

### Staff Scheduling

Staff scheduling must balance customer service quality with labour cost control. Key principles:

- **Match staffing to traffic patterns.** Use historical sales data to identify peak hours (e.g., Saturday mornings, lunch breaks, pay-day weekends). Schedule more staff during these windows.
- **Full-time vs. part-time mix.** Full-time employees provide continuity; part-time staff offer flexibility and lower costs. In a small Ugandan retail shop, a typical roster might be one full-time manager and two to three part-time assistants.
- **Span of control.** Limit the number of employees reporting to a single supervisor. A flat organisation (few management levels) speeds communication but requires competent self-managing staff.
- **Shift premiums.** If the store operates extended hours (e.g., 7 AM--9 PM), night or Sunday shifts may warrant a premium of 25--50% above the base hourly rate.

**Benchmark:** Aim for labour costs of 8--15% of net sales for a small to medium retail shop.

### Customer Service Standards

Customer service is the number one goal in any long-term business relationship. The advantage of a small business is the ability to respond quickly and personally. Key standards:

1. **Greet every customer** within 30 seconds of entry. Staff should smile, make eye contact, and offer assistance.
2. **Product knowledge.** Staff must know the merchandise range, prices, features, and alternatives.
3. **Complaint handling.** Treat every complaint seriously. Resolve it on the spot where possible. It costs up to seven times as much to acquire a new customer as to retain an existing one.
4. **Return policy.** Post it clearly near the till and on receipts. State the number of days allowed, conditions (original receipt, unused packaging), and whether refunds are in cash or store credit.
5. **Follow-up.** For high-value items, a phone call or message after purchase builds loyalty. Word-of-mouth recommendations from satisfied customers are a powerful form of promotion.

## 2. Inventory Management

### Stock Control Systems

Inventory management involves planning, monitoring, and controlling the quantities and financial value of merchandise. Two primary systems exist:

- **Physical inventory (stock count).** Merchandise is physically counted at set intervals (monthly, quarterly, or annually). This establishes actual stock on hand but is labour-intensive and provides data only at the point of counting.
- **Perpetual (book) inventory.** A running total is maintained through record-keeping entries that adjust for sales, returns, transfers, receipts, and other transactions. POS systems automate this process, providing real-time stock visibility.

**Best practice:** Combine both systems. Use a perpetual system for day-to-day control and conduct physical counts periodically to detect discrepancies (shrinkage).

For a small shop, a weekly stock check and ordering routine should be sufficient. Keep accurate records of all purchases and devise a consistent system.

### Reorder Points and Safety Stock

A **reorder point** is the stock level at which a new order must be placed. It is based on three factors:

1. **Usage rate** -- average units sold per day.
2. **Order lead time** -- days from placing an order to goods being shelf-ready.
3. **Safety stock** -- extra inventory to protect against unexpected demand or delivery delays.

**Formula (without safety stock):**

    Reorder point = Usage rate x Lead time

**Formula (with safety stock):**

    Reorder point = (Usage rate x Lead time) + Safety stock

**Example (Ugandan context):**
A shop in Kampala sells 10 bars of soap per day. The supplier in the industrial area requires 5 days from order to delivery. The owner keeps 30% safety stock.

    Reorder point = (10 x 5) + (0.30 x 50) = 50 + 15 = 65 bars

When stock falls to 65 bars, a new order is placed.

### Economic Order Quantity (EOQ)

EOQ determines the optimal number of units per order to minimise total costs (ordering costs plus holding costs). Placing large orders reduces ordering frequency but ties up capital and increases storage, insurance, and deterioration costs. Placing small orders does the reverse.

**Practical rule for small retailers:** Order frequently in moderate quantities, especially for fast-moving and perishable goods. Negotiate with suppliers for free or subsidised delivery to reduce ordering costs.

### Stock-Taking

Conduct a full physical inventory at least once per year, and ideally twice. For high-value or theft-prone items, count quarterly. Compare physical counts with book inventory to compute shortages.

    Stock shortages = Book inventory value - Physical inventory value

If book inventory exceeds physical inventory, the difference represents shrinkage from theft, damage, or administrative errors.

### Shrinkage Prevention

Globally, inventory shrinkage averages about 1.45% of sales. The causes break down approximately as follows:

| Source | Percentage |
|---|---|
| Customer shoplifting | 43% |
| Employee theft | 35% |
| Administrative errors | 16% |
| Vendor fraud | 6% |

A small shop with annual sales of UGX 200 million could lose UGX 2.9 million or more to shrinkage. Prevention measures are discussed in Section 7 (Security).

## 3. Merchandising

### Assortment Planning

An **assortment** is the selection of merchandise a retailer carries, encompassing both the breadth of product categories and the depth (variety) within each category. Decisions include:

- **Quality positioning.** Will the shop carry top-line, mid-range, or budget merchandise? This must align with the target market and store image.
- **Width vs. depth.** A wide assortment (many categories) suits a general store. A deep assortment (many variants within fewer categories) suits a specialty shop.
- **Staple, convenience, and impulse goods.** Allocate prime space accordingly:
  - *Staple goods* (core sellers, ~50% of offerings) -- place at the centre or back of the store to draw customers through.
  - *Convenience goods* (~33%) -- position for easy, immediate access.
  - *Impulse goods* (~15% of sales) -- display near the till and entrance for maximum exposure.

### Category Management

Category management treats each product group as a strategic business unit. For each category, track:

- Sales per square foot (or square metre).
- Gross margin return on investment (GMROI).
- Stock turnover rate.

**GMROI formula:**

    GMROI = Gross margin (UGX) / Average inventory at cost (UGX)

A supermarket might target a GMROI of 300%. A clothing boutique might achieve the same GMROI through a higher margin (50%) but lower turnover, compared with a grocery's lower margin (20%) but much higher turnover.

**Typical stock turnover benchmarks:**

| Retail Type | Annual Median Turnover |
|---|---|
| Grocery / food store | 14--15 times |
| Hardware store | 2.5--3 times |
| Clothing store (women's) | 4--5 times |
| Jewellery store | 1--2 times |
| Convenience store | 20+ times |

### The Buying Process

Implementation of merchandise plans follows a sequential process:

1. **Gather information** about target market needs and prospective suppliers. Sources include customer feedback, trade shows, merchandise marts, supplier catalogues, and competitor analysis.
2. **Select suppliers.** Evaluate reliability, product quality, delivery terms, credit terms, and price competitiveness. Build good relationships -- agree payment terms, negotiate volume discounts, and ask for early-settlement discounts.
3. **Evaluate merchandise** by inspection, sampling, or description.
4. **Negotiate purchase terms.** Clarify delivery date, quantity, price, payment arrangements, discounts, and point of transfer of title.
5. **Place the order** and confirm in writing.
6. **Receive and check goods.** Verify quantities against the purchase order and inspect for damage before signing the delivery note.
7. **Price-mark and display** merchandise.
8. **Monitor and reorder** based on sales velocity and reorder points.

### Supplier Negotiations

- Always obtain quotes from at least two or three suppliers.
- Ask how often prices will rise, what influences price increases, and how you will be notified.
- Negotiate volume discounts, seasonal allowances, and promotional support.
- Agree delivery frequency and minimum order values. Aim to make delivery the supplier's responsibility.
- Do not squeeze a supplier excessively if you plan to buy regularly -- sustainable partnerships yield better long-term value.

## 4. Pricing Strategies

### Markup Pricing

Markup is the most widely used pricing technique. The **markup** is the difference between merchandise cost and selling price.

**Markup at retail (%):**

    Markup % = (Selling price - Cost) / Selling price x 100

**Example:**
A Ugandan retailer buys a handbag for UGX 40,000 and sells it for UGX 65,000.

    Markup = (65,000 - 40,000) / 65,000 x 100 = 38.5%

**Common retail markup equivalents:**

| Markup at Retail | Markup at Cost |
|---|---|
| 20% | 25% |
| 30% | 42.9% |
| 40% | 66.7% |
| 50% | 100% |

Retail markup typically runs between 30% and 50% for general merchandise, though it varies by category: consumer electronics may operate on under 20%, while jewellery may exceed 60%.

**Planned markup formula:**

    Required markup % = (Planned expenses + Planned profit + Planned reductions) / (Planned net sales + Planned reductions) x 100

### Competitive Pricing

A retailer may price **below**, **at**, or **above** the market, depending on its positioning:

| Strategy | Suited To |
|---|---|
| Below market | High-volume, low-service, self-service formats; discount stores |
| At market | Moderate service, branded merchandise, mainstream locations |
| Above market | Superior customer service, exclusive brands, prestige locations |

**Everyday Low Pricing (EDLP):** Set consistently low prices with few or no advertised specials, reducing advertising and re-pricing costs. This builds price credibility.

### Psychological Pricing

- **Odd pricing.** Set prices just below round numbers (e.g., UGX 9,900 instead of UGX 10,000; UGX 49,500 instead of UGX 50,000). The assumption is that consumers perceive these as discounts or that amounts are beneath their price ceilings.
- **Prestige pricing.** Some consumers associate higher prices with higher quality. A boutique in Kampala selling at UGX 150,000 may attract customers who would avoid the same item priced at UGX 50,000 elsewhere.
- **Price-quality association.** This is strongest when consumers cannot easily judge quality by other means, brand names are unfamiliar, or the product is a first-time purchase.
- **Price lining.** Offer merchandise at a limited number of price points (e.g., UGX 25,000, UGX 50,000, UGX 100,000) to simplify customer choice and streamline buying.

### Markdown Management

A **markdown** reduces an item's original price to stimulate sales, clear seasonal or slow-moving stock, match competitors, or increase traffic.

**Off-retail markdown percentage:**

    Markdown % = (Original price - New price) / Original price x 100

**Example:**
A dress originally priced at UGX 120,000 is reduced to UGX 84,000 at end of season.

    Markdown % = (120,000 - 84,000) / 120,000 x 100 = 30%

**Markdown planning principles:**
- Plan markdowns into the initial markup calculation. If you expect 20% of stock to be marked down, your initial markup must be higher to protect overall margins.
- Time markdowns strategically: early markdowns on fashion goods prevent deeper cuts later.
- Track markdowns by category to identify buying mistakes and adjust future purchasing.
- Total stock shortages (shrinkage + markdowns + employee discounts) below 2% of annual sales is generally considered good performance.

### Price Adjustment Policies

- **Leader pricing.** Advertise selected items at lower-than-usual margins to drive traffic, then sell regularly priced items alongside them.
- **Multiple-unit pricing.** Offer discounts for buying in quantity (e.g., three bars of soap for UGX 7,500 instead of UGX 3,000 each).
- **Bundled pricing.** Combine related items at a single price (e.g., a phone case, screen protector, and charger for UGX 35,000).

## 5. Supply Chain

### Sourcing and Supplier Selection

Retail logistics includes planning, implementing, and coordinating the movement of merchandise from supplier to retailer to customer. Goals include placing and receiving orders accurately, minimising lead times, coordinating shipments, and maintaining proper merchandise levels.

**Supplier selection criteria:**
- Product quality and consistency.
- Pricing and available trade discounts.
- Reliability of delivery schedules.
- Minimum order quantities and flexibility.
- Credit terms offered (e.g., 30-day, 60-day).
- Willingness to accept returns of defective goods.
- Geographic proximity (local sourcing can reduce transport costs and spoilage).

In Uganda, many retailers source from Kampala's wholesale markets (Kikuubo, Owino), direct importers, or regional manufacturers. For perishable goods, local sourcing within the same district reduces spoilage and transport costs significantly.

### Logistics and Receiving

**Receiving procedures:**
1. Check delivered quantities against the purchase order and delivery note.
2. Inspect goods for damage, defects, or discrepancies.
3. Record any shortages or damaged items; file chargebacks with the supplier promptly.
4. Price-mark goods immediately upon receipt.
5. Move stock to the selling floor or designated storage area.

**Direct store delivery (DSD):** Some suppliers (e.g., beverage companies, bread manufacturers) deliver directly to the store, bypassing central warehouses. This is common for perishable and high-turnover items in Uganda.

**Vendor-managed inventory (VMI):** The supplier monitors stock levels and replenishes automatically. This is growing in popularity among larger retailers, as it reduces the retailer's inventory management burden.

## 6. Visual Merchandising

### Store Layout Types

The store layout determines customer traffic flow, merchandise exposure, and the overall shopping experience.

**Grid layout:**
Aisles and fixtures are arranged at right angles in a rectangular pattern. Used primarily in supermarkets, pharmacies, and hardware stores. It is the most space-efficient design, allows easy product location, and facilitates restocking. However, it is not the most visually exciting arrangement.

**Free-form layout:**
Fixtures and merchandise are grouped in informal clusters, creating a relaxed, home-like atmosphere. Used in small specialty shops and boutiques. It encourages browsing and impulse purchases but uses floor space less efficiently and makes security monitoring more difficult.

**Racetrack (loop) layout:**
A major aisle loops around the store, guiding the customer past all departments and back to the entrance. Used in larger department stores and shopping outlets. It maximises product exposure and encourages impulse buying as customers' viewing angles change continuously.

**Other layouts** include straight, diagonal, angular, geometric, and mixed plans, each suited to different merchandise types and store sizes.

### Display Principles

- **Decompression zone.** Leave an open area (3--4 metres) just inside the entrance where no merchandise is displayed. This allows customers to adjust to the store environment.
- **Invariant right.** Most customers naturally turn right upon entering. Place high-margin or promotional merchandise on the right side of the entrance.
- **Eye-level placement.** Products at eye level sell best. Place premium or high-margin items at this height; lower-margin or bulk items on bottom shelves.
- **End caps.** Displays at the end of aisles catch consumer attention and are ideal for promotional items.
- **Cross-merchandising.** Place complementary items together (e.g., phone cases next to phone chargers).
- **Refresh displays regularly.** Change window and in-store displays at least monthly to keep the store compelling. When the shop is closed, the window display continues to work -- make it powerful.

### Signage

Signs are one of the lowest-cost and most effective methods of communicating with customers.

- **Exterior signs** should be large enough for passers-by to read. Use contrasting colours and simple typefaces. Coordinate sign design with the store's overall image.
- **Interior signs** guide customers, highlight promotions, and provide product information. They should look fresh and be relevant to the items displayed. Remove signs immediately when displays change -- irrelevant, faded signs do more harm than good.
- **Price tickets and point-of-sale materials** should be clearly legible with consistent formatting throughout the store.

## 7. Security and Loss Prevention

### Shoplifting Deterrents

Shoplifters may be professional or amateur and employ various methods: concealing merchandise, switching price labels, working in groups to distract staff, and targeting opening/closing times or peak-hour congestion.

**Prevention measures:**
- Design the shop so customers must pass the payment area and staff to exit.
- Never leave the cash register unlocked or unattended.
- Do not display merchandise near store exits.
- Use mirrors to eliminate blind spots in corners.
- Ensure adequate lighting in all areas.
- Display security-awareness signage ("CCTV in operation", "Shoplifters will be prosecuted").
- Train staff to greet every customer (this deters theft while improving service).
- Use electronic article surveillance (EAS) tags on high-value items.
- Limit the number of garments permitted in fitting rooms.

### Internal Theft Prevention

Employee theft accounts for approximately 35% of global shrinkage. Measures to combat it:

- Conduct background checks before hiring.
- Divide responsibilities -- have one employee record sales and another handle deposits.
- Lock up rubbish to prevent merchandise being discarded and retrieved later.
- Use CCTV to verify that all sales are rung up.
- Implement written ethical conduct policies signed by all staff, including management.
- Investigate all known losses promptly and act decisively on confirmed theft.
- Offer employee bonuses tied to overall reduction in stock shortages.

### Cash Handling

- Begin each day with a fixed, counted cash float.
- Reconcile tills at every shift change, not just at closing.
- Deposit takings daily (or use a secure safe with dual-key access).
- Two people should be present during cash counting.
- Use counterfeit note detectors (available from UGX 50,000).
- Monitor for till-skimming: compare POS transaction logs with actual cash totals.

### After-Hours Security

- Lock all exits, including fire exits.
- Conduct a thorough building check before locking up.
- Activate burglar alarms and test them periodically.
- Ensure exterior lighting is adequate.
- Install shatterproof glass or iron gates on windows and doors.
- Place valuables in a safe.

## 8. HR and Staffing

### Recruitment

When recruiting, look for candidates who are enthusiastic about the merchandise, caring, helpful, resourceful, and knowledgeable. Other desirable characteristics: a positive attitude, good communication skills, honesty, and reliability.

**Recruitment channels in Uganda:**
- Word of mouth and personal networks.
- Job boards and social media.
- Local colleges and vocational training institutes.
- Notices displayed in the shop window.

Always request references and verify them. Give candidates clear, written job descriptions outlining duties, hours, pay, and conditions.

### Training

Investing in staff training pays direct dividends in customer satisfaction and sales performance. Training should cover:

- Product knowledge for the entire merchandise range.
- POS system operation and cash handling procedures.
- Customer service skills: greeting, active listening, complaint resolution.
- Security awareness: recognising shoplifting behaviour, emergency procedures.
- Health and safety basics.

New employees should have a supervised probation period (typically 1--3 months) with a structured induction programme.

### Compensation Models

- **Basic salary plus commission.** Motivates selling effort while providing income stability.
- **Flat salary.** Simpler to administer; suitable where team selling rather than individual sales is the norm.
- **Performance bonuses.** Tied to store-level targets (e.g., monthly sales, stock-shortage reduction).
- **Employee discounts.** Typically 10--20% off retail price. This improves morale and product knowledge but must be tracked as a planned reduction in financial planning.

**Minimum wage benchmark (Uganda, 2025):** While Uganda does not have a legally enforced private-sector minimum wage, prevailing market rates for retail shop assistants in Kampala range from UGX 250,000 to UGX 500,000 per month.

### Productivity Metrics

- **Sales per employee.** Divide total net sales by the number of full-time equivalent (FTE) staff. Track monthly. A rising ratio indicates improving productivity.
- **Sales per labour hour.** For shops with variable staffing, divide daily or weekly sales by total labour hours worked. Use this to optimise scheduling.
- **Conversion rate.** Number of transactions divided by foot traffic (if counted). Indicates how effectively staff convert browsers into buyers.
- **Average transaction value.** Net sales divided by number of transactions. Train staff in up-selling and cross-selling to increase this metric.

## 9. Technology

### POS Systems

Point-of-sale systems are the operational backbone of a modern retail shop. Even small retailers benefit significantly from a basic POS system, which provides:

- Accurate transaction recording and receipt generation.
- Real-time sales tracking by product, category, time period, and staff member.
- Automated inventory updates with every sale.
- Cash and card payment processing (including mobile money in Uganda -- MTN MoMo, Airtel Money).
- End-of-day reconciliation reports.

**Entry-level POS options for Ugandan retailers:** Tablet-based systems (Android or iPad) with barcode scanners and receipt printers. Costs start from approximately UGX 1.5 million for a basic setup.

Modern POS systems have evolved beyond traditional cash registers. They can manage inventory across multiple channels, evaluate sales associate performance, track loss prevention, and forecast sales at the store level.

### Inventory Software

Dedicated inventory management software extends POS capabilities:

- **Automatic reorder alerts** when stock reaches preset minimum levels.
- **Barcode / UPC scanning** for fast, accurate stock counts and receiving.
- **Batch tracking** for perishable goods (expiry date management).
- **Supplier management** with purchase order generation and tracking.
- **Reporting:** stock turnover, slow movers, GMROI by category, shrinkage analysis.

For small shops, spreadsheet-based inventory systems are a cost-effective starting point before investing in dedicated software.

### E-Commerce Integration

Increasingly, retailers must consider multi-channel operations:

- **Online storefront.** A simple website or social media shop (Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp Business) extends reach beyond foot traffic.
- **Unified inventory.** Ensure that online and in-store stock levels are synchronised to prevent overselling.
- **Click-and-collect.** Allow customers to order online and pick up in store, combining digital convenience with physical experience.
- **Delivery logistics.** For e-commerce orders within Kampala, motorcycle (boda-boda) couriers or dedicated delivery services can provide same-day fulfilment.

When selling across multiple channels, inventory should not be attributed to a specific channel until it is sold. This allows each channel an equal opportunity to sell all goods, reducing the need for markdowns.

## Quick-Reference Formulas

| Metric | Formula |
|---|---|
| Markup % (at retail) | (Selling price - Cost) / Selling price x 100 |
| Markup % (at cost) | (Selling price - Cost) / Cost x 100 |
| Gross margin | Net sales - Cost of goods sold |
| Stock turnover (units) | Units sold / Average inventory on hand (units) |
| Stock turnover (retail UGX) | Net sales / Average inventory at retail |
| GMROI | Gross margin (UGX) / Average inventory at cost (UGX) |
| Reorder point | (Usage rate x Lead time) + Safety stock |
| EOQ (simplified) | sqrt(2 x Annual demand x Order cost / Holding cost per unit) |
| Markdown % (off-retail) | (Original price - New price) / Original price x 100 |
| Break-even sales | Fixed costs / Gross margin % |
| Sales per employee | Net sales / Number of FTE staff |
| Shrinkage % | Inventory shortage (UGX) / Net sales (UGX) x 100 |
