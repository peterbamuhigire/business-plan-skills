# Mitumba (Second-Hand Clothes) Retail

**Source:** Crack A Business Kenya (CAB) — The Mitumba Business Guide (Retail) (2015) | **Sub-sector:** Retail — Second-Hand Clothing
**Conversion:** KES ÷ 106 × 1.27 → March 2026 USD

> Retail of second-hand (mitumba) clothing — garments donated in Europe/America, collected, graded, baled, and exported to East Africa. Minimum entry: KES 2,000 (single items); KES 25,000 (one bale). Average profit per 45kg bale: KES 3,000–8,000. Ideal minimum capital: KES 50,000–60,000 (bale + backup). Market not saturated at retail level; profitability depends on selecting the right bale type for the location and understanding the grading/camera system.

## Supply Chain

```
First Consumer (Europe/Americas)
→ Collectors (charities, NGOs)
→ Textile Reclamation Companies (grade, sort, bale, export)
→ Large Importers (~150 importers; 20 control Mombasa Docks)
→ Wholesalers (Gikomba/Kariokor, Nairobi; Kongowea, Mombasa)
→ Retailers
→ Sub-Retailers / Individual Consumers
```

- **Container:** ~500 bales; KES 2.5M–4M plus freight, duty, and clearing
- **Bale:** Standard 45kg; average KES 25,000 from wholesaler (range from KES 12,000 for slow-moving categories)
- **Minimum wholesale lot:** ~50 bales for large importers; smaller wholesalers sell single bales
- A container holds mixed bale types — importers cannot select only fast-moving categories; they must take mixed lots

## The Camera System (Grading Within a Bale)

When a retailer opens a bale, items are sorted into three groups:

| Camera | Quality | Approx Items per Bale | Selling Strategy |
|--------|---------|----------------------|-----------------|
| 1st Camera | Best — almost new, fashionable, high-value pieces | ~30 per bale | Sell at premium; often display prominently |
| 2nd Camera | Average quality — the bulk of the bale | ~150 per bale | Standard mitumba retail price |
| Clearance | Below-average; older styles; some wear | Remainder | Sell cheaply to clear; sometimes in bulk lots |

> The 1st camera items often cover the cost of the bale; 2nd camera is where most of the profit sits; clearance items recover residual value.

## Stock Options

| Option | Minimum Capital | Risk | Profit Potential |
|--------|----------------|------|-----------------|
| Single items | KES 2,000–5,000 ($24–$60) | Low — buy tested items | Lower; limited selection |
| One bale | KES 25,000 ($299) | Medium — bale quality is variable | Higher per unit |
| Multiple bales | KES 50,000+ ($599+) | Higher but diversified | Proportionally higher |

**Recommended backup capital:** Have one additional bale's worth of capital (KES 25,000+) in reserve beyond the purchase price — covers restocking without waiting for current stock to fully sell.

## Selecting the Right Bale

Key decisions before purchasing:

1. **Category/type:** Dresses, trousers, coats, shirts, children's, shoes — each has different turnover rate and margin profile
2. **Season alignment:** Warm-weather items (dresses, shorts) sell slowly in cold seasons; coats sell slowly in hot months — buy ahead of season
3. **Location demand:** Rural areas have different preferences than Nairobi CBD; a children's bale sells differently in a school-area market vs. a CBD stall
4. **Demographics:** Working-class estate markets prefer functional workwear; youth markets prefer branded-look fashion; upmarket areas: 1st camera premium items

## Competition

- Competition is location-based; the mitumba with the best variety and prices in a given market wins
- Product differentiation is low — Rush Hour jeans are the same wherever sold
- **Customer loyalty drivers:** Variety (having what the customer wants), price, quality selection
- Repeated business from satisfied customers is essential; first purchase experience determines return visits
- Online selling (Facebook groups, classified sites) is a growing distribution channel for retailers unable to secure prime stall locations

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Owino Market (St. Balikuddembe) in Kampala is Uganda's equivalent of Gikomba — the largest second-hand clothing market in East Africa by some estimates. Mitumba trade is also active in Gulu, Mbarara, Mbale, and Fort Portal markets. Uganda imports mitumba through Mombasa (via Kenya) and increasingly through Dar es Salaam (via Tanzania). KCCA trade licence and URA TIN required for formal stall operation. EAC tariff discussions on banning second-hand clothes have affected Ugandan importers — stay current with policy developments. Owino wholesale bale prices differ slightly from Kenyan figures — verify locally.
- **Tanzania:** Kariakoo Market in Dar es Salaam is Tanzania's primary mitumba hub; Mwanza and Arusha have substantial regional markets. Tanzania also imports directly through Dar port, giving Tanzanian retailers some price advantage over Ugandan/Kenyan traders who source from Mombasa. BRELA registration and TRA TIN required. TBS quality standards technically apply but retail enforcement is inconsistent. Zanzibar has a smaller but distinct mitumba market given its different import regulations.
- **Rwanda:** Rwanda banned the import of second-hand clothing in 2016 as part of an East Africa Community initiative to protect domestic textile industries. The ban was implemented (with a phased enforcement timeline) and has significantly reduced the formal mitumba market in Rwanda. A domestic used-clothing market still exists but is substantially smaller and more regulated than in Kenya, Uganda, or Tanzania. Verify current Rwanda import regulations before planning any Rwanda-based mitumba business.
- **EA general:** The EAC has periodically discussed banning or heavily tariffing second-hand clothes to protect domestic textile industries. Kenya, Uganda, and Tanzania have resisted full implementation due to the economic importance of the trade, but the policy environment is volatile. Monitor EAC trade policy updates annually when planning capital investment in this sector.

## Data Quality Notes

Revenue figures and bale prices are from 2015 Kenya data. Bale prices at Gikomba and other wholesale markets change with import costs, exchange rates, and supply volumes — verify current wholesale prices before purchasing. The per-bale profit average (KES 3,000) is a useful planning benchmark but high variability (KES 1,000–8,000) means results depend heavily on bale selection skill, which develops with experience. The trench coat scenario (KES 15,500 net) represents an optimistic case; plan conservatively.
