# Innerwear Retail

**Source:** Crack A Business Kenya (CAB) — UnderCash: A Quick Guide to the Innerwear Retail Business (2017) | **Sub-sector:** Retail — Clothing / Innerwear
**Conversion:** KES ÷ 106 × 1.27 → March 2026 USD

> Retail of innerwear (panties, bras, boxers, vests, socks, lingerie, swimwear). Capital KES 235,000 ($2,817); ideal minimum KES 300,000. Average gross margins 20–50%. Women's and children's segments are the most active; male innerwear is less competitive and offers a niche opportunity. Consumers are largely impulse-driven (56% buy randomly); brand loyalty to vendors is low (83% not loyal). Entry is low-capital at street-vendor level (from KES 2,000).

### Margin Context

- Price variation between suppliers: KES 10–200 per item on the same SKU
- Import sources: China (dominant), Turkey (mid-range quality)
- Margins: 20–50% typical; lace and seamless panties have higher margins; basics (cotton panties, plain socks) lower
- Negotiate volume discounts; prices vary between Kamukunji, Eastleigh, and Lingerie Hub

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Innerwear retail is well-established in Kampala's Owino Market (St. Balikuddembe), Kikuubo, and Nakasero Market. Chinese imports (via Mombasa or directly through Dubai/Guangzhou channels) dominate the mass market. Kampala's young, growing urban population drives demand. KCCA trade licence and URA TIN required. Owino Market has the highest concentration of innerwear wholesale stalls; retail prices in Kampala estates are slightly lower than Nairobi due to lower rents. Men's innerwear (boxers, vests) is somewhat less openly displayed than women's — a cultural factor to consider in-shop layout.
- **Tanzania:** Dar es Salaam's Kariakoo Market is the main wholesale hub for innerwear. Zanzibar has an additional market given the tourist trade (swimwear, beach shorts). BRELA registration and TRA TIN required. TBS certification technically applies to textile imports but enforcement at retail level is inconsistent. Islamic modest-wear considerations apply in coastal areas — full-coverage styles and body-shaping underwear as alternatives to lingerie.
- **Rwanda:** Kigali's Nyabugogo and Kimironko markets stock innerwear for the mass market. Rwanda's import tariffs on clothing are part of the EAC common external tariff structure. RDB registration required. Rwanda's relatively stable middle class and urbanising population provide steady demand. Modesty norms apply — the lingerie niche is smaller relative to cotton basics.
- **EA general:** The niche opportunity across all EA markets is in **men's innerwear** — female vendors dominate most markets, creating less competition in the male segment (as demonstrated by the Tom Mboya case study). Second opportunity: **children's** innerwear, where parents make quality-driven rather than price-driven decisions, supporting better margins.

## Data Quality Notes

Capital breakdown and wholesale prices are from 2017 Kenya data. Actual prices will have changed; verify with current wholesaler price lists from Kamukunji or Lingerie Hub before purchasing stock. The consumer survey (10–15 panties owned, 83% not loyal to vendors) is structurally durable as a behavioural pattern — the impulse-buy nature of innerwear purchase is consistent across markets. Margins (20–50%) are structurally valid; the specific KES figures need verification.
