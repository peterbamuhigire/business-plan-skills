# Building Materials Yard

**Source:** Crack A Business Kenya (CAB), 2018 | **Sub-sector:** Construction Materials Retail & Distribution

> A materials yard buys sand, ballast, stones and chips from transporters in bulk, then breaks bulk for small to mid-scale construction buyers who need less than a full lorry load.

## Operations

- **Minimum yard size:** 10 m × 10 m; aim for 0.1–0.25 acre in active urban zones
- **Key equipment:** Wheelbarrows (3 minimum), spades/shovels (4–5)
- **Fast-moving stock (Tier 1):** Ballast, river sand, quarry chips, Mai Mahiu/Machakos sand
- **Secondary stock (Tier 2):** Stones (all sizes), hardcore, red soil, murram
- **Supply chain:** Source (quarry) → Transporter/lorry → Yard → Contractor/fundi/small builder
- **Minimum lorry purchase:** 7–22 tonnes depending on lorry type; yard breaks this into 1–5 tonne retail sales
- **Manpower:** 1 supervisor/owner + 2 casual labourers for loading; watchman optional but recommended

> Revenue velocity is the critical variable: selling 60 tonnes in 3 days is profitable; in 3 months it is a loss after rent and labour. Turnover speed drives all.

## Market

Primary customers are small to mid-scale builders, contractors, fundis, and repair site managers who need 1–10 tonnes — too small for direct lorry delivery but too large for a hardware shop. Secondary customers are lower-capacity brokers and transport operators who buy from yards when direct quarry sourcing is uneconomical. Demand tracks residential construction activity directly. Yards near active housing developments, plot subdivisions, or infill building zones outperform those in settled, low-activity areas.

## Competitive Dynamics

- Unstructured, informal market with no regulated standards for weights or grades
- Measurement units (wheelbarrows, "tonnes") are loosely defined and vary by location
- Competition is primarily on price, location convenience, and trust (honest weights, material quality)
- Revenue can be boosted by stocking complementary items: cement, nails, timber, metal bars

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Active quarry sources near Kampala include Muyenga, Mukono, and Lugazi. Business licence from KCCA/district. Monthly rent at Kampala city outskirts: UGX 150,000–500,000 (~$40–$135). Lorry types and weight ranges comparable to Kenya.
- **Tanzania / Rwanda:** Kigali and Dar es Salaam have comparable construction boom dynamics. Stone and aggregate demand in Kigali is driven by high-density residential development.
- **Licensing (Uganda):** District trading licence + environmental compliance if within 200 m of a wetland. Some districts require a mineral dealer licence for sand/stone resale.

## Data Quality Notes

Material prices are highly location-specific and volatile (fuel costs, quarry distance, season). The Kiambu/Nairobi case study is indicative only. Uganda and Tanzania prices differ materially — verify current buy prices with local transporters before incorporating into any business plan.
