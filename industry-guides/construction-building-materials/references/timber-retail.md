# Timber Yard Retail Business

**Source:** Crack A Business Kenya (CAB) — Sawing The Cash: A Timber Yard Retail Business Guide (updated 2016, original 2013) | **Sub-sector:** Construction & Building Materials — Timber Retail
**Conversion:** KES ÷ 106 × 1.27 → March 2026 USD

> A retail timber yard selling sawn timber to the construction and furniture industries. Capital KES 1,583,000 ($18,969). Gross margins 20–40% depending on supply source. The key business variable is where in the supply chain you access timber — the closer to the forest or sawmill, the higher the margin. Customer base: building contractors (high volume) and jua kali carpenters (steady volume).

## Timber Types and Uses

| Timber | Primary Use | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| Mukima (Grevillea robusta) | Shutters, temporary construction | Cheap; fast-moving; bulk sales (1,000+ ft at a go); not best quality |
| Cypress | Roofing, frames, fisherboards | Premium timber; rain-resistant; doesn't crack; loved by builders |
| Blue Gum (Eucalyptus) | Furniture, roofing | Used by carpenters; sensitive to humidity; two types (white = bends/cracks; red = better) |
| Poles | Shutter support (with Mukima) | KES 15 wholesale / KES 20 retail |
| Pine | Limited use | Bends under weight; not recommended for initial stock |

**Recommended starter stock:** Cypress + Mukima + Blue Gum (7 tonnes each average); Mukima and Blue Gum first if capital is limited.

## Timber Sizes and Measurement

Timber is measured and sold by the linear foot in standard cross-sections (width × height in inches):
- Common retail sizes: 2×2, 3×2, 4×2, 6×2
- Wholesale purchases typically in 6×2 or larger (10×2, 12×2)
- Retailer splits wholesale size into retail pieces: 1× (6×2) → 2× (3×2) or 3× (2×2)
- A circular/band saw is required to split wholesale timber

## Supply Chain

```
Forests / Private Farms / Imports
    ↓
Sawmillers / Timber Traders
    ↓
Brokers / Wholesalers (aggregators)
    ↓
Retail Timber Yards
    ↓
Consumers (builders, carpenters)
```

**Import sources:** Tanzania (softwood, mostly Iringa; enters via Mombasa) and DRC (hardwood). Kenya imported 38,507 m³ in 2013 (KFS data) — approximately 90% of hardwood destined for Nairobi.

**Local sourcing:** Elburgon, Njoro, Nyandarua, Aberadarey, Nakuru, Kinangop, Eldoret (rift valley and upper central province).

### Getting a Supplier (Practical Steps)

1. Research retail prices in the area where you plan to open — establishes your price ceiling
2. Visit bigger established yards in Nairobi (Kariobangi, Kawangware) — some double as wholesalers
3. Contact freelance brokers who aggregate and deliver from forests/sawmills
4. Sample contacts: John Gitau 0706 256815 (Kahawa West; sources from Kinangop/Eldoret); Joseph Ndungu 0723 523430 (Northern Bypass area)
5. Never pay for timber you cannot see — cash on delivery especially for new relationships
6. Build relationships with multiple suppliers; once running, suppliers will come to you

## Revenue Model

- **Primary revenue lever:** price per linear foot × footage sold per day
- **High-volume products:** Mukima (used in bulk for shutters — 1,000+ ft per contractor order)
- **Higher margin products:** Cypress (construction grade, premium, preferred by professional builders)
- **Target margin per foot:** Mukima KES 5–7/ft minimum; Cypress KES 17–33/ft depending on split

**Gross margin factors:**
1. Supply source — direct from sawmill/forest = higher margin than buying from another yard
2. Pricing strategy — must match local competition while preserving margin
3. Volume — lorry-load purchasing gives cost advantage vs. small broker deliveries
4. Product mix — cypress carries higher margins than mukima

## Competition and Survival

- Competition intensifies in established areas (Nairobi Kariobangi, Ngong Road, Eastlands)
- Newer towns and growing counties offer lower competition but smaller total market
- Differentiation is limited — price and reliability are primary competitive factors
- Fraud in the supply chain is common: under-measurement when purchasing wholesale is typical
- Physical inspection of every delivery is essential — never accept quantity estimates without verification
- Relationships with carpenters and contractors are long-term assets — serve them well and they return

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Uganda has significant timber resources (Buganda region forests, Elgon area, Mabira) but strict enforcement of National Forestry Authority (NFA) regulations limits logging. Most retail timber in Kampala comes from cross-border supply (Tanzania, DRC) and the Kabale/Masaka timber belt. Kampala's timber yards are concentrated in Nakawa, Mukono Road, and Kireka. Construction booming in Greater Kampala (Kira, Wakiso, Entebbe road corridor) drives strong timber demand. KCCA trade licence and URA TIN required. NFA transport permit required for locally sourced timber. Key equipment suppliers at Katwe jua kali cluster. Hardwood (Mvule, Mahogany) is protected — deal only with certified sources.
- **Tanzania:** Tanzania has large timber reserves (Kilosa, Sao Hill, Iringa, Mufindi plantations), making it an exporter to Kenya. Dar es Salaam's timber retail is concentrated in Kariakoo and Chang'ombe. Softwood (Pine, Cypress from Sao Hill Forest, Iringa) is available relatively cheaply. BRELA registration and TRA TIN required. Tanzania Forest Services (TFS) regulates timber harvesting — legal timber must have TFS permits. The construction boom in Dar, Arusha, and Dodoma creates strong demand. TBS standards apply to processed timber products. Tanzania's own timber industry gives local retailers a supply advantage over Kenyan importers.
- **Rwanda:** Rwanda has very limited domestic timber (small forest reserves). Almost all commercial timber is imported from Uganda, Tanzania, or DRC. This makes retail timber significantly more expensive than in Kenya or Tanzania (add 30–50% for transport from Kampala or Dar es Salaam). Kigali's timber yards are in Nyabugogo and Gikondo. Rwanda's rapid construction sector (Kigali masterplan, Vision 2050 housing) creates demand. RDB registration and RRA TIN required. RBS has limited timber standards. The higher landed cost of timber in Rwanda creates opportunity for any supplier who can establish reliable import logistics.
- **EA general:** Timber retail is a high-demand, relationship-driven business across all EA markets. The supply chain politics (logging licences, broker networks, informal levies) vary by country but are present everywhere. Success at scale requires either: (1) getting close to the source (sawmill relationships or import capability), or (2) dominant local relationships with building contractors who provide consistent volume. Across EA, the shift toward concrete-framed construction is slowly reducing timber use in structural applications but increasing demand for formwork/shuttering and interior joinery.
