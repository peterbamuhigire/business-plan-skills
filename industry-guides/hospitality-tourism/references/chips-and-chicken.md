## Revenue Model (Quick View)

Breakeven revenue calculation on chips alone: sell at KES 50/plate; potato sack at KES 3,500 → need to sell 70 plates to recover potato cost. Need ~30 more plates to cover oil, electricity, and overheads. A basic outlet requires 100+ plates/day to break even on chips alone before chicken revenue is counted.

## Operations

- **Potato supply:** Source from wholesale markets (Wakulima/Marikiti in Nairobi); prices are seasonal — budget for off-season spikes (up to KES 6,000/$72 per sack)
- **Frozen chips:** An alternative to raw potatoes — more consistent quality, higher input cost, lower labour
- **Cooking sequence:** Peeling → slicing → frying. Two fryers allow simultaneous batches and cut customer wait time
- **Chicken source:** City Market, Kiserian slaughterhouse, or Ngong Road wholesale for Nairobi outlets
- **Operating hours:** Extended hours (near 24h) significantly increase revenue from the nightlife and overnight shift crowd

## Market

Chips and chicken cafés are ubiquitous across East Africa's urban landscape. The format is mature in Nairobi — many outlets have opened and closed. Success depends on a location with sufficient foot traffic and a clear price-point that matches the local market's purchasing power.

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Chips/rolex (chips in chapati) is uniquely popular in Uganda. Standalone chips and chicken cafés common in Kampala estates (Kabalagala, Ntinda, Kireka, Nansana). Health inspection by KCCA required. City Market and Nakasero wholesale chicken supply for Kampala operators.
- **Tanzania:** Chips mayai (chips omelette) is Tanzania's dominant chips street food format. Chips and chicken cafés serve a similar market, particularly along main roads and in commercial districts of Dar es Salaam, Arusha, and Mwanza.
- **Rwanda:** Kigali has a growing fast-food café sector. Competition from formal fast-food chains (KFC, Chicken Inn) in city centre; informal chips/chicken cafés dominate estates. Strict hygiene enforcement — maintain clean premises.
- **Potato sourcing:** In Uganda, potato sourcing is from Kabale, Kisoro, and Mt Elgon regions; prices similarly seasonal. Ugandan sweet potato (empogola) is sometimes substituted in low-income market variants.

## Data Quality Notes

Capital figures are for a standard, fully-equipped Nairobi outlet. A basic jua kali setup can start at KES 300,000 ($3,594) with second-hand equipment and lower-spec furniture. Equipment prices vary significantly between jua kali, local branded, and imported options.
