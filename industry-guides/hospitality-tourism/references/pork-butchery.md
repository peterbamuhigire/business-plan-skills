### The Eatery Premium

The purpose of cooking is to capture customers who would not buy raw pork — lunch workers, passing trade. A quarter of raw pork retails at KES 100; a cooked quarter with ugali at KES 140 adds KES 40 — but labour, fuel, and cooking inputs consume roughly half of that premium. Net margin from cooked operations averages **12%**. The key contribution of cooking is **volume**: it converts foot traffic that would otherwise go to a beef or chicken eatery.

Some eateries maximise cooking margins by serving slightly underweight "quarters" (200g instead of 250g) using wide shallow plates to give the impression of a full serving.

## Pork Sourcing

All pork sold to the public must be veterinary-inspected and stamped at a licensed slaughterhouse. Unstamped pork is illegal and risks food poisoning liability.

**Sources:**
1. Licensed slaughterhouses — buy directly from farmers and brokers at the open market
2. Brokers — agree a price and they deliver to your premises
3. Own pigs taken to slaughterhouse for slaughter + stamp (most profitable if you can manage it)

**Key slaughterhouses (Kenya, 2016):**
- Datfarm — Ridgeways, Kiambu Road
- Kitengela Slaughterhouse
- Ndumboini Slaughterhouse — near Uthiru, Waiyaki Way
- Utugi (Nakuru); Kiserian

> Buying live pigs directly from farmers earns the highest margins. Experienced buyers estimate live weight visually — farmers often cannot, so there is a pricing advantage to the buyer.

## Operations

### Layout Principle
- Butchery counter near the entrance: customers buying raw pork do not need to pass through the eatery
- Kitchen at the far end with a separating wall — kitchen chaos stays out of sight
- Tap/sink near entrance for customer handwashing

### Revenue Controls
- Daily reconciliation: kilograms sold vs. cash at hand vs. unga used
- Single cashier receiving all payments
- Waitress issues receipts; customer pays cashier on exit
- Fixed daily cooking quantities (owner-absent operations) to prevent staff over-cooking and under-reporting

### Case Study — Kiambu County Eatery

Located behind a market in a busy centre with six pork eateries in the same area. Centre hosts 10+ financial institutions, several schools, government offices, and a Level 2 hospital. Two dedicated market days per week drive peak revenue.

## Key Success Factors

1. **Location** — near foot traffic that matches the target customer (workers, market-goers, students)
2. **Pork quality** — lean/fat balance, freshness, stamp compliance
3. **Cooking consistency** — quality variance destroys loyalty fast in a tight competitive market
4. **Operations control** — revenue leakages through kitchen theft are significant
5. **Speed of service** — eatery customers are usually on lunch breaks

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Pork is widely consumed across Uganda and is culturally accepted across most regions. Rolex (egg+chapati) and pork eateries are both common street food formats. Kampala's pork eatery clusters include Ggaba Road, Katwe, and Ndeeba. Pig slaughterhouses include Wambizi in Kampala. KCCA trade licence, public health permit, and UNBS food handling standards apply. Veterinary inspection at licensed slaughterhouses is legally required and more actively enforced in Kampala than rural areas. Wholesale pork prices in Uganda differ from Kenya — verify locally. Pork is not consumed in Muslim-majority areas (eastern and northern Uganda); site selection must account for this.
- **Tanzania:** Pork is consumed in Dar es Salaam (especially in areas with Christian-majority populations — Mikocheni, Kinondoni), Moshi, Arusha, and highland towns. Tanzania Food and Drug Authority (TFDA) and BRELA registration apply. Slaughterhouse veterinary stamps are required. Competition from mishkaki (grilled meat) and nyama choma businesses is significant.
- **Rwanda:** Pork is the most widely consumed red meat in Rwanda. Kigali has many pork eateries particularly in Nyabugogo, Kimironko, and Remera. Rwanda Biomedical Centre and district environmental health officers regulate food service premises. Pork must pass RBC veterinary inspection. Rwanda's relatively high average incomes in Kigali support slightly higher retail prices than Kenya.
- **EA general:** The key difference across markets is whether butchery and eatery operate from the same premises (Kenya model) vs. separate specialist venues. Both formats exist across EA. The veterinary inspection requirement is universal — source only from licensed slaughterhouses to avoid regulatory risk.

## Data Quality Notes

Capital figures are from October 2016 Kenya data. Pork wholesale prices (KES 200/kg average) and retail prices (KES 400/kg average) are from September 2016 Kenya surveys.
