# Smokies, Sausages and Boiled Eggs Street Vending

**Source:** Crack A Business Kenya (CAB) — Smoking The Money: A Survey of the Smokies, Sausages and Eggs Street Vending Business (2012–2013 field survey) | **Sub-sector:** Hospitality & Tourism — Street Food Vending
**Conversion:** KES ÷ 106 × 1.27 → March 2026 USD

> Small-scale street food vending of smokies (smoked sausages), regular sausages, and boiled eggs. Entry capital: KES 5,000–30,000 ($60–$360). Three models: cart vendor (fixed position), hot pot vendor (mobile), and specialised egg vendor (mobile). Revenue is driven almost entirely by location — impulse purchase by passing foot traffic. Average daily revenue: smokies KES 520, eggs KES 620, sausages KES 320.

## Business Models

| Model | Setup | Best For |
|-------|-------|----------|
| Cart Vendor | Metallic cart, wheeled; fixed position at busy corner | Smokies and eggs; highest volume; most stable |
| Hot Pot Vendor | Insulated pot; moves from corner to corner | Smokies and sausages; lower capital entry; bar-to-bar night circuit |
| Specialised Egg Vendor | Plastic basin or crate; mobile | Eggs only; lowest capital; widest mobility |

## Licences

| Licence | Cost (KES/year) | 2026 USD |
|---------|----------------|----------|
| County Government Trading Licence (hawker/vendor) | ~3,000 | $36 |
| Medical Certificate (cholera, typhoid test) | 1,000 | $12 |
| **Total formal** | **~4,000** | **~$48** |

> Many vendors — especially hot pot and egg hawkers — operate without formal licences. The practical cost of operations includes periodic informal payments (bribes) to county askari and police: KES 100–500 per incident. In Nairobi CBD, smokies carts are officially restricted to outer areas (bus stations, Nyamakima, Muthurwa) during the day; night operations (from ~8pm) move into CBD with higher risk/reward.

## Stock and Supply Chain

**Wholesale prices (2012–2013):**

| Product | Wholesale (per packet/tray) | Retail Price | 2026 USD (retail) | Items per pack |
|---------|-----------------------------|-------------|-------------------|---------------|
| Smokies (Farmer's Choice) | KES 300 | KES 20/piece | $0.24/piece | 22 per packet |
| Sausages (Farmer's Choice) | KES 390 | KES 20–30/piece | $0.24–$0.36/piece | 22 per packet |
| Eggs | KES 270–330/tray | KES 15–20/egg | $0.18–$0.24/egg | 30 per tray |

**Fuel/oil per packet:** ~KES 0.50 per item for deep-frying. Charcoal: KES 30–150/day depending on jiko quality and hours.

**Kachumbari (tomato/onion/pepper salad) accompaniment:** ~KES 15/packet of smokies, KES 40/crate of eggs in costs; significant competitive differentiator.

**Wholesale hotels:** Most towns have a central hotel that deep-fries smokies and sausages on behalf of vendors (fee ~KES 20/packet) or sells ready product in small quantities. Useful for new vendors to buffer stock risk and test daily volumes before committing to buying full packets.

### Other Sausage Brands

Alpha Fine Foods, Oscar Food Industries, Chef's Choice, Olive Enterprises — generally cheaper than Farmer's Choice but quality perception is lower; some reported to oil up and harden when deep-fried.

> Farmer's Choice dominates both smokies and sausages in EA markets with the best quality perception and distribution network. Farmer's Choice smokies are stamped with logo, identifiable by customers.

## Operating Economics

**Example daily P&L (cart vendor, smokies, average day):**
- Revenue: KES 520 (26 smokies × KES 20)
- Product cost: 1.5 packets × KES 300 = KES 450 (approximately)
- Charcoal/oil: ~KES 50
- Kachumbari: ~KES 15
- Licence/bribes (amortised): ~KES 20
- **Net daily profit: ~KES 0–50** on average day; higher on good days

> The economics are thin but fixed costs are essentially zero (no rent, no employees in most cases). Daily flexibility is the key advantage.

**Gross margin per packet (smokies, Farmer's Choice):**
- Buy 1 packet of 22 at KES 300; sell at KES 20 each = KES 440 gross revenue
- Gross margin = KES 140/packet (47%) before cooking costs

**Employee model:** Workers paid KES 150–200/day wage OR 20–40% commission on sales. Commission preferred — motivates productivity. Average staff turnover: 2 months (staff leave to start own carts).

## Location Principles

- **Best locations:** High foot-traffic areas — bus stages, markets, schools, industrial areas, hospital gates
- Impulse purchase basis means proximity to consumers at time of hunger = everything
- Nairobi CBD: officially restricted zones during day; higher revenue but police risk
- Kiambu town market days: average 70 smokies/day; spikes to 291 on market day
- Bar-to-bar egg vending at night: viable supplementary income

## Competitive Landscape

- Products are largely homogeneous (all Farmer's Choice) — limited differentiation
- Kachumbari and accompaniments are the primary differentiator (increasingly universal)
- Vendor presentation (cleanliness, grooming) signals quality — women more sensitive to this
- Price-based competition is rare — small price differences don't motivate location changes
- Sausages being gradually edged out in favour of smokies — quality perception issues

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Smokies (smoked sausages) are available but the dominant Ugandan street snack is rolex (egg and vegetables in a chapati/flatbread). Farmer's Choice operates in Uganda — smokies and sausages are available but street vending is less structured than in Kenya. Kampala's street food culture centres around rolex vendors, samosa stalls, and mandazi. A Kenyan-style smokies vending model can work in high-traffic Kampala areas (Old Taxi Park, Ben Kiwanuka Street, Garden City). KCCA hawker licence required (KES equivalent ~KES 2,000–5,000/year). MTN Mobile Money and Airtel Money used for resupply payments. Boiled egg vendors are common in most Ugandan towns.
- **Tanzania:** Street food vending is deeply embedded in Tanzanian urban culture (Dar es Salaam, Mwanza, Arusha). Smokies are less established as a product — sausages from local brands (Serengeti Sausage Company) or imported Farmer's Choice available in Dar. Boiled eggs are universal — sold at bus stands, markets, and evening food markets (vibanda). TFDA (Tanzania Medicines and Medical Devices Authority, formerly TFDA) has food safety oversight over street vendors; enforcement is inconsistent. Local authority (ward/street) trading permit required. Airtel Money and Vodacom M-Pesa are primary payment channels. Dar es Salaam's enormous street food culture and evening market scene offers significant opportunity.
- **Rwanda:** Street food vending is less developed in Rwanda compared to Kenya and Tanzania — Kigali has more formalized food stalls and less informal hawking due to strict urban planning enforcement by Kigali City. However, boiled egg vendors near taxi stages (moto parks), markets, and bus stations are present. Rwanda Bureau of Standards (RBS) has food safety standards; in practice, small vendors operate with minimal enforcement. Any vendor near Kigali must be aware of Kigali City cleanliness regulations — formal cart registration may be required. MTN Mobile Money dominant. The growing Kigali working population creates demand for quick affordable street food.
- **EA general:** Across EA, boiled eggs are the most universally viable street food item — low capital, no cooking equipment needed beyond a pot, and widely consumed. Smokies are Kenya-specific (Farmer's Choice dominance) but sausage-equivalent street food exists across EA. The mobile hot pot model has the lowest barriers to entry in any EA market. The key universal constraint is regulatory/municipal — understanding what your local authority permits before investing in cart equipment is essential in all markets.

## Data Quality Notes

Revenue and price data are from field surveys conducted August 2012 – October 2013 in Kenya. Farmer's Choice wholesale prices and retail prices will have changed significantly. The structural dynamics (impulse purchase, location dominance, low margins, police friction) remain valid across all EA urban markets. Verify current Farmer's Choice wholesale prices and local authority licence fees before starting. Note: sausage market share was already declining in 2013 — the data almost certainly understates the degree to which smokies now dominate over sausages.
