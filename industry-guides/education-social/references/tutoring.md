# Tutoring and Supplementary Education

**Sub-sector:** Private Education Services / Educational Support
**Sources:** Guillebeau (2017), Amitabh (2022), Gary Raj (2022), Beames/Higgins/Nicol/Smith (2024)
**Currency:** UGX (1 USD ≈ UGX 3,700 as of 2026)

> Supplementary tutoring serves students already enrolled in conventional schools, colleges, and universities. Demand in Uganda is structurally driven by large class sizes in government schools (often 60–100 pupils per teacher), the high-stakes nature of UNEB exams (UCE and UACE), and fierce competition for university places. The tutoring business rewards genuine expertise, strong results records, and word-of-mouth reputation — and can be started with minimal capital.

## Business Models

### 1. Home and Private 1-on-1 Tutoring

The most common entry point. The tutor visits the student at their home, or the student comes to the tutor's home.

**Advantages:**
- Zero fixed costs; no rent
- Premium pricing justified by personalised attention
- Strong parent relationship builds referrals
- Flexible scheduling around the tutor's other commitments

**Disadvantages:**
- Time inefficiency — one student per hour
- Transport costs if the tutor travels
- Income ceiling unless group sessions are added
- Dependent on individual student relationships (cancellations affect cash flow)

**Operations:**
- A tutor can realistically schedule 6–10 sessions per day on weekends and 3–4 on school evenings
- Session duration: 1–2 hours is standard
- Pre-term or monthly retainer fees (collected in advance) improve cash flow and reduce cancellations
- A simple WhatsApp group per student (tutor + parent) builds trust and enables homework follow-up

**Revenue example — full-time 1-on-1:**
- 20 active students, average 8 sessions/month each
- Rate: UGX 50,000/session (O-Level sciences/mathematics)
- Gross: 20 × 8 × UGX 50,000 = UGX 8,000,000/month
- Transport and materials: UGX 400,000
- Net: UGX 7,600,000/month (~$2,054)

### 2. Group Tutoring Sessions

Tutoring 4–15 students in a session — either at the tutor's home, a rented space, or a school that permits use of its premises after hours.

**Advantages:**
- Higher effective hourly rate (e.g., 10 students × UGX 20,000 = UGX 200,000/hour vs. UGX 50,000/hour for 1-on-1)
- Students benefit from peer discussion and collective problem-solving
- More sustainable if one student drops out
- Enables exam-focused group drilling (especially for UCE/UACE past papers)

**Optimal group size by level:**
- Primary (P5–P7): 8–15 students — high energy, needs structured activities
- O-Level (S1–S4): 6–12 students — past paper drilling works well in groups
- A-Level (S5–S6): 4–8 students — smaller, discussion-heavy sessions more effective
- University/professional: 6–15 students — lecture-style with problem sets

**Pricing structure for groups:**
- Per session fee (student pays per session attended): Lower commitment, higher dropout
- Monthly subscription (fixed fee regardless of sessions): Better cash flow, higher retention
- Term package (pay per academic term): Best for structured programmes aligned to school calendar

**Space options (Kampala):**
- Own home room: Free but may limit group size and professionalisation
- Church hall rental: UGX 50,000–150,000/day; UGX 200,000–500,000/month
- Hotel conference room (small): UGX 100,000–300,000/half-day
- Co-working space (e.g., Hive, Design Hub): UGX 80,000–200,000/day
- Commercial lease (small room, city outskirts): UGX 300,000–800,000/month

### 3. Online Tutoring

Delivery via Zoom, Google Meet, or Microsoft Teams. Serves students in Uganda, the East African diaspora, and internationally.

**Why online tutoring is growing in Uganda:**
- MTN and Airtel data bundles have improved and data costs have declined
- Smartphones are widespread among secondary and university students
- Post-COVID familiarity with video calls
- Diaspora families (UK, USA, Canada, Australia) want their children tutored in the Ugandan/East African curriculum

**Platform options:**
| Platform | Cost | Best For |
|----------|------|---------|
| Zoom (free) | Free up to 40 minutes | Short 1-on-1 sessions |
| Zoom Pro | ~UGX 150,000/month | Unlimited group sessions |
| Google Meet | Free | Students with Gmail accounts |
| WhatsApp Video | Free | Informal check-ins, homework review |
| Microsoft Teams | Free for education | University-level cohorts |

**Tools for online tutoring:**
- Digital whiteboard: Jamboard (free, Google), Miro (free tier), or tablet + stylus
- Screen sharing for solving problems live
- Google Classroom (free): Set assignments, share resources, collect work
- Google Forms: Quick comprehension quizzes after each session
- WhatsApp group: Between-session support, homework questions, resource sharing

**Diaspora market — pricing in USD:**
- Parents based in UK/USA/Canada prefer to pay in USD
- Rates: $20–$50/hour for 1-on-1; $10–$20/student/session for group
- Payment: Pesapal, Flutterwave, PayPal, Wise (to a Ugandan bank account)
- Key selling point: tutor familiar with NCDC or Cambridge International syllabus

**Revenue example — online group (diaspora):**
- 8 diaspora students, UCE Mathematics revision
- Rate: $15/student/session × 4 sessions/month = $60/student/month
- Total: 8 × $60 = $480/month = ~UGX 1,776,000/month at near-zero cost

### 4. Corporate and Professional Certification Tutoring

Teaching working adults preparing for professional qualifications: ACCA, CPA (ICPAU), CMA, CIMA, ICSA, PMI, NEBOSH, and postgraduate research support (statistics, research methodology, thesis supervision).

**Why this is the highest-value segment:**
- Students are employed adults investing in career advancement — they pay reliably
- Premium rates justified: UGX 80,000–250,000/hour
- Sessions can be delivered in the evening or on weekends, fitting around employment
- Employers sometimes sponsor training (invoice the company, not the individual)
- Small cohorts (6–15 students) are standard for professional programmes

**Key qualification markets in Uganda:**

| CPA (Certified Public Accountant) | ICPAU | Financial Reporting, Audit, Tax, Management Accounting | Accounting staff, finance graduates |

| CMA | IMA | Financial Planning, Performance, Analytics | Management accountants |

| NEBOSH | NEBOSH | Occupational Health and Safety | Health and safety officers |

**Delivery model for professional tutoring:**
- Evening cohorts (6:00–9:00 pm, weekday evenings): 3 sessions/week
- Weekend intensive (Saturday and Sunday, 9:00 am–4:00 pm): common for ACCA
- Online delivery increasingly preferred by working adults (saves commute time)
- Printed notes and practice past papers are core materials

### 5. Holiday and Exam-Prep Intensive Programmes

Structured 2–6 week intensive programmes during school holidays (April holidays, August holidays) and immediately before UNEB examinations. These are the highest-revenue-per-month events in the tutoring calendar.

**Uganda school calendar — key tutoring windows:**

| Window | Timing | Target Students | Focus |
|--------|--------|----------------|-------|
| April holidays | April (3–4 weeks) | S4, S6, P7, S2 | Mid-year revision, mock preparation |
| August holidays | August (4–6 weeks) | S4, S6, P7 | UNEB exam preparation (UCE October, UACE November) |
| December/January | December–January | P7, S6 bridging | Bridging for next level; results waiting period |
| Weekend boot camps | Saturdays during term | S4, S6 | Subject-specific drilling alongside school |

**Programme structure (example — 4-week UCE Mathematics intensive):**
- 20 sessions × 2.5 hours each
- Past paper analysis: UNEB past 10 years, question frequency mapping
- Topic-by-topic revision: algebra, geometry, trigonometry, statistics
- Daily timed past paper attempts with tutor feedback
- Final mock examination with UNEB-style marking scheme

**Pricing (holiday intensive):**
- Per-student block fee (entire programme): UGX 150,000–400,000
- Group size: 10–25 students per subject per session
- Revenue: 20 students × UGX 250,000 = UGX 5,000,000 per subject per holiday
- Running cost (venue, materials, transport): UGX 800,000–1,200,000
- Net: UGX 3,800,000–4,200,000 per subject

**Scaling:** A centre running 5 subjects simultaneously during August holiday can generate UGX 15,000,000–20,000,000 in a single 4-week window.

## Market Context — Uganda

### Education System Overview

Uganda's education ladder relevant to tutoring:
- **Primary:** P1–P7 (ages 6–13); culminates in Primary Leaving Examinations (PLE, administered by UNEB)
- **O-Level:** S1–S4 (ages 14–17); culminates in Uganda Certificate of Education (UCE, administered by UNEB)
- **A-Level:** S5–S6 (ages 18–19); culminates in Uganda Advanced Certificate of Education (UACE, administered by UNEB)
- **University:** 3–5 year degree programmes; Makerere, Kyambogo, Mbarara, Gulu, Islamic, private universities
- **Professional:** Post-graduate qualifications (ACCA, CPA, CMA, Masters, MBA)

### Structural Drivers of Tutoring Demand

**1. Class size crisis in government schools:**
Universal Primary Education (UPE) and Universal Secondary Education (USE) have dramatically expanded access to schooling. However, pupil-to-teacher ratios in government schools often exceed 60:1 in primary and 40:1 in secondary (Ministry of Education data). Individual attention is structurally impossible in this environment, making private tutoring a necessity, not a luxury, for parents who can afford it.

**2. High-stakes UNEB examinations:**
UCE and UACE results are the gatekeepers to university admission. Division 1 (UCE) or 3 principal passes (UACE) are required for competitive university programmes. A single grade difference can determine whether a student gains government sponsorship (free tuition at Makerere) or pays private fees (UGX 700,000–2,000,000/semester). This high-stakes environment creates intense demand for exam-focused tutoring, particularly in August (the months before October/November UNEB sittings).

**3. Curriculum-teaching gap:**
Many school teachers cover the NCDC syllabus incompletely due to absenteeism, school management weaknesses, or workload. Private tutors fill this gap by covering missed topics, particularly in sciences and mathematics.

**4. Professional examination market:**
ICPAU (the Institute of Certified Public Accountants of Uganda) administers the CPA qualification. ACCA is pursued by thousands of Ugandan finance professionals annually. Both require multiple sitting attempts (pass rates are low: typically 30–50%). Working professionals who have failed a paper once or twice are willing to invest significantly in tutoring to pass on their next attempt.

**5. University supplementary teaching:**
Makerere University and other institutions have large class cohorts (200–500 students per lecture), and tutorial time is limited. Students in quantitative courses (Statistics, Engineering Mathematics, Accounting) frequently seek private tutors for individualised problem-solving help.

**6. International and diaspora market:**
Uganda's East African Community obligations, the use of Cambridge International in elite private schools (Kampala International School, Aga Khan, etc.), and the large Ugandan diaspora in the UK create a market for online tutoring in the Ugandan and East African curriculum. Parents in the diaspora want their children to maintain connection with the Ugandan/Kenyan curriculum, especially those planning to return.

### Market Size Estimates (Kampala, 2026)

- Approximately 1.2 million students are enrolled in secondary school in Uganda (Ministry of Education)
- An estimated 15–25% of urban secondary students use private tutoring — approximately 180,000–300,000 students in urban areas
- Average monthly tutoring spend per student in Kampala: UGX 100,000–500,000
- Informal market size estimate: UGX 18,000,000,000–150,000,000,000/year (across urban Uganda) — a very large, fragmented, largely unregulated market

## Setting Up

### Qualifications and Registration

There is no specific government licence required to operate as a private tutor in Uganda for academic subjects below university level. However:

**For credibility and client confidence:**
- A relevant university degree is the baseline expectation (e.g., BSc for science tutoring, BA for arts)
- A Postgraduate Diploma in Education (PGDE) or teaching certificate strengthens credibility
- Professional qualifications (CPA, ACCA) are essential for professional exam tutoring
- UNEB past-paper results should be tracked and shared as evidence of student outcomes

**For a tutoring centre (registered business):**
- Register as a business with Uganda Registration Services Bureau (URSB): approximately UGX 100,000–200,000 for a sole proprietorship
- Apply for a trading licence from the relevant local government (KCCA in Kampala): UGX 50,000–150,000/year depending on grade
- Schools operating as formal institutions require a licence from the Ministry of Education and Sports, but small tutoring centres fall below this threshold
- If employing tutors, register with NSSF and URA as an employer

**Tax obligations:**
- Individual tutors earning below UGX 2,820,000/month (threshold, URA) are below the income tax bracket for PAYE
- Sole proprietors should file annual income tax returns with URA
- VAT registration is not required below UGX 150,000,000 annual turnover
- Once the business grows, engage a chartered accountant (a CPA tutor does this themselves)

### Pricing Strategy

**Factors that command higher rates:**
- Subject difficulty (Mathematics, Sciences, ACCA command more than History or General Paper)
- Level (A-Level commands more than O-Level, which commands more than Primary)
- Track record (documented student results and testimonials)
- Qualification of tutor (a Makerere lecturer charges more than a recent graduate)
- Location (Kampala commands more than upcountry)
- Urgency (last 4 weeks before UNEB — premium pricing is normal)

### Finding Students

**Initial pipeline (first 3 months):**
1. **Alumni networks:** Post in your university WhatsApp alumni group — "I am offering O-Level Chemistry tutoring. DM me."
2. **Church/mosque community boards:** Announce availability to the parent community
3. **School gates:** Distribute flyers at secondary school gates (obtain permission from the school administration first)
4. **Facebook groups:** "Kampala Parents Forum", "Makerere University Students", "Uganda Jobs and Opportunities" — post availability with subject, level, and rates
5. **Free first session:** Offer one free 30-minute diagnostic session. Students who experience your teaching quality convert to paying students.
6. **Referral incentive:** Tell existing students: "Refer a friend and get one free session."

**Sustained pipeline (months 4+):**
- WhatsApp broadcast list of past and current students (announce holiday programmes, new availability)
- Google My Business profile: Create a free listing so local parents can find you by search
- School notice boards: Build a relationship with school librarians or counsellors who can refer struggling students
- Corporate HR departments: For professional certification tutoring, approach HR managers of banks, audit firms, and government agencies — they often sponsor or recommend professional tutors

## Teaching Methodology: Active Learning Principles

Conventional school teaching in Uganda is predominantly transmission-based: the teacher lectures, students copy notes. Effective tutoring can be structurally different and should be. The following principles are drawn from the research literature on learning effectiveness (Beames, Higgins, Nicol and Smith, *Outdoor Learning Across the Curriculum*, Routledge 2024), adapted from their core pedagogical arguments about active, experiential, and learner-centred approaches.

### Principle 1: Active Learning Over Passive Copying

Beames et al. argue that "the opportunities for academic development . . . are extensive" when students are placed in active learning situations — doing, not just watching. The direct corollary for tutoring: students should be working problems, not watching the tutor work them.

**In practice:**
- The tutor works one example on the whiteboard (fully narrated, showing reasoning aloud)
- The student then attempts the next problem independently — the tutor watches silently
- The tutor corrects process errors before the student reaches a wrong answer (formative intervention)
- Aim for a 30% tutor talking / 70% student working ratio in each session

**Why this matters:** Students who can only reproduce an answer they have just watched being derived have not learned anything. They have only observed. The ability to independently reproduce the process — days later, under exam conditions — is the only metric that counts.

### Principle 2: Inquiry-Based and Socratic Questioning

Rather than giving students answers, effective tutors guide students to discover answers through structured questioning. This approach is rooted in Dewey's concept of placing students at "forks in the road" where they must reason and decide (Beames et al., 2024, p. 40, citing Dewey 1973).

**Socratic technique in tutoring:**
- "What do you know about this topic already?"
- "What does the question ask you to find?"
- "What formula or method might apply here — and why?"
- "Is your answer reasonable? Check it."
- "If you used a different method, would you get the same answer?"

**Why this matters:** Students who understand the reasoning behind a method can adapt when the exam question is slightly differently worded. Students who have only memorised a procedure cannot.

### Principle 3: Curiosity and Wonder as Learning Drivers

Beames et al. (2024, Chapter 6) distinguish between *curiosity* (wanting to find a specific answer) and *wonder* (the deeper affective engagement with the limits of what one knows). In tutoring terms, this translates to the difference between a student who wants to know "what is the answer to question 5" and a student who genuinely wonders "why does compound interest grow exponentially?" The tutor's role is to move students from the former to the latter.

**In practice:**
- Connect exam content to real-world examples the student recognises (e.g., mobile money compound interest for Mathematics; food spoilage for Biology microbiology)
- Ask "have you ever wondered why . . . ?" before introducing a new concept
- Allow students to ask their own questions — do not rush past a genuine "I don't understand why this works" moment. These are the highest-value learning moments.
- Use Heron and Reason's four-point epistemology (Beames et al., 2024, p. 78):
  1. **Experiential knowing** — connect concept to something the student has directly experienced
  2. **Presentational knowing** — student explains or draws the concept in their own words
  3. **Propositional knowing** — formal theory, formula, or rule is introduced and applied
  4. **Practical knowing** — student applies it independently to a new problem

### Principle 4: Mastery-Based Progression

Do not move on until the student can independently and correctly reproduce the current concept. This is the single most important difference between good and mediocre tutoring.

**The mastery check protocol:**
1. Teach the concept
2. Solve one example together (tutor leads)
3. Student solves a similar example independently (tutor observes without helping)
4. Student solves a third example without reference to previous examples
5. If all three pass: topic is mastered, proceed
6. If any fail: identify the specific point of breakdown, re-teach that point only, repeat

**Why students struggle in conventional school:** The school bell ends the lesson whether the student has understood or not. The next lesson assumes the previous one was understood. Private tutoring removes this structural problem — the session ends when the concept is mastered, not when the clock says so.

### Principle 5: Spaced Repetition Scheduling

Human memory decays predictably (Ebbinghaus forgetting curve). Material reviewed once is mostly forgotten within a week. Effective tutors build spaced review into their session planning.

**Simple spaced repetition schedule for exam preparation:**
- Session 1: Teach Topic A
- Session 2: Quick review Topic A (5–10 minutes), then teach Topic B
- Session 3: Review Topics A and B briefly, teach Topic C
- Session 4: Full re-test Topic A (student has not seen it for 2+ sessions), review B and C
- Weekly: Timed past-paper question on every topic covered so far

**Tool:** A simple table with topics as rows and sessions as columns, with tick marks showing when each topic was last reviewed. Share this with the student so they understand the system.

### Principle 6: Past-Paper Drilling with Feedback Loops

For UCE/UACE and professional examinations, past-paper practice under timed, exam-like conditions is the highest-impact activity in the last 4–6 weeks before an exam.

**Protocol:**
1. Obtain UNEB past papers for the last 10 years (for the relevant subject)
2. Map question frequency: which topics appear every year? These are high-priority
3. Student attempts past paper questions under timed conditions (no help from tutor during attempt)
4. Tutor marks with the official UNEB marking scheme
5. Tutor and student review errors together, identifying whether the error was: (a) a knowledge gap, (b) a process error, (c) a time management issue, or (d) a reading comprehension failure
6. Each category has a different remedy — do not treat all errors the same way

**Key insight:** UNEB examiners reuse question formats, phrasings, and contexts in predictable patterns. A student who has worked through 10 years of past papers in a subject has already "seen" most of what the exam will ask.

### Principle 7: Reflective Learning and Self-Monitoring

Beames et al. (2024, Chapter 12) describe reflective journalling as a tool for evidencing and deepening learning. Adapted for tutoring:

**Student session log (simple, 5 minutes at end of each session):**
Students answer four questions in a small notebook:
1. What did we cover today?
2. What did I understand well?
3. What am I still not confident about?
4. What will I review before the next session?

**Why this works:** Students who can articulate their own confusion points have already begun resolving them. The act of writing "I still don't understand how to factorise when the coefficient of x² is not 1" forces the student to locate their specific gap — which guides the next session's opening.

### Principle 8: Zone of Proximal Development — Pitch Correctly

Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) (referenced in Beames et al., 2024, p. 77) describes the space between what a student can do alone and what they can do with guidance. Effective tutoring operates in this zone — not too easy (boring, no growth), not too hard (frustrating, demoralising).

**In practice:**
- Start each topic with a brief diagnostic: ask the student a question from the topic without prior instruction. Observe the response carefully.
- If the student can already answer independently: move to a harder variant, or move on to the next topic
- If the student cannot begin at all: provide more foundational scaffolding before the target topic
- The tutor's job is constant calibration — every session reveals where the ZPD currently sits

## Scaling the Business

### Stage 1: Solo Tutor (Months 1–12)

Establish a reputation and track record. Focus on:
- A maximum of 2–3 subjects where the tutor has genuine expertise and exam result evidence
- Building a WhatsApp contact list of parents — this is the marketing asset
- Documenting student results (with permission): "My student Naluyima Aisha scored Division 1 in Mathematics UCE 2025 after 6 months of tutoring"
- Running one holiday intensive to test the group model

**Target:** 15–25 active students, UGX 4,000,000–8,000,000 net/month

### Stage 2: Associate Tutors (Months 12–24)

Bring in 1–2 additional tutors to cover subjects outside the founder's expertise, or to handle overflow demand.

**Model options:**
- **Revenue share:** Associate tutor receives 60–70% of fees collected for their sessions; founder retains 30–40% for the referral and admin
- **Employed tutor:** Fixed monthly salary (UGX 600,000–1,500,000/month depending on experience and hours), founder retains all revenue from those sessions
- **Anchor subjects:** Founder keeps the highest-margin subjects (ACCA, A-Level); associates teach O-Level and primary

**Risk:** Associate tutor relationships — the tutor may take students with them if they leave. Mitigate by maintaining the parent relationship (WhatsApp, results reporting, invoicing) at the centre/brand level, not the individual tutor level.

### Stage 3: Registered Tutoring Centre (Year 2–3)

Lease a dedicated space. Create a recognisable brand. Enable scheduling of multiple simultaneous sessions.

**Centre setup requirements:**
- 2–4 rooms (can be subdivided with partitions or room dividers)
- Reception/waiting area for parents
- Branded signage (improves perceived quality significantly)
- Simple student management system (even a Google Sheet: student name, subjects, attendance, payments)
- Fee payment via mobile money (create a dedicated business line)

**Centre capacity example:**
- 4 rooms × 2 sessions/day × 5 days/week = 40 session-slots/week
- At average UGX 25,000/session/student × 10 students/session = UGX 250,000/session
- 40 sessions × UGX 250,000 = UGX 10,000,000/week = UGX 40,000,000/month gross

### Stage 4: Curriculum Products and Online Courses

**Convert tutoring content to scalable products:**
- Recorded video lessons for each major O-Level and A-Level topic (record once, sell indefinitely)
- Sell on Gumroad, Teachable, or via WhatsApp broadcast at UGX 20,000–80,000/topic set
- UNEB past-paper workbooks with model answers and marking guides (print and sell)
- WhatsApp-based subscription group: UGX 15,000–30,000/month for access to daily practice questions, model answers, and weekly live Q&A

**Revenue model (online course, 200 students, UGX 40,000/course):** UGX 8,000,000 from one course sale cycle, created in 20–30 hours of work.

### Stage 5: Franchising or Licensing the Curriculum

A tutoring centre that has developed a branded curriculum, structured student materials, and a documented teaching system can license the model to tutors in other towns (Mbarara, Gulu, Jinja, Mbale) on a franchise or licensing basis.

**Licensing model:**
- Licence fee: UGX 1,000,000–3,000,000 upfront per licensee town
- Ongoing royalty: 10–15% of monthly revenue
- Licensor provides: branded materials, curriculum guides, marketing templates, quality checks
- Licensee provides: premises, tutors, local marketing

|-----------|--------|

### Professional Indemnity

There is no legal requirement for tutors to carry professional indemnity insurance in Uganda, but for professional certification tutoring (ACCA, CPA) — where a failed exam can have significant career consequences — the following risk management steps are advisable:
- Clear written agreement (even a WhatsApp message) confirming what the tutoring programme covers and what it does not guarantee (pass rates are never guaranteed)
- Collect fees in advance to avoid disputes over non-payment after a student fails an exam and blames the tutor
- Do not claim specific pass guarantees in marketing materials

## Key Risks and Mitigations

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Seasonal income volatility — school-term vs. holiday gaps | High | Medium | Build holiday programmes as a major revenue stream; collect term fees in advance; save 20% of peak-month income |
| Student dropout mid-term (illness, school change, fee default) | High | Medium | Collect fees in advance (at least half-term); maintain a waiting list of interested students to fill vacancies |
| Key tutor leaves and takes students (centre model) | Medium | High | Maintain parent relationships at the brand/centre level; document all student contacts; use a non-compete agreement with employed tutors |
| Reputational damage if students fail exams | Medium | High | Set realistic expectations upfront; document student attendance and homework completion; do not accept students who attend fewer than 70% of sessions and still expect exam results |
| Competition from other tutors offering lower prices | High | Medium | Compete on results, not price; publish documented student grades (with student permission); build a premium brand around track record |
| Power outages disrupting online sessions | High (Uganda) | Medium | Keep mobile data hotspot as backup; schedule sessions for morning (fewer outages); communicate outage policy upfront |
| Student safety concerns (home visits, especially for female tutors) | Medium | High | Meet new students initially in a neutral location (library, church, coffee shop); avoid isolated home environments for initial sessions; prefer studio to home visits for 1-on-1 |
| Over-reliance on one or two large clients (corporate cohort) | Medium | High | Ensure no single client represents more than 30% of revenue; diversify across individual students and corporate cohorts |
| Platform/payment dependency (e.g., Pesapal changing terms) | Low | Medium | Maintain MTN/Airtel mobile money as primary payment method; collect cash for in-person students |
| Online privacy and data protection | Low | Medium | Do not share student names or results publicly without written parent/student consent |
