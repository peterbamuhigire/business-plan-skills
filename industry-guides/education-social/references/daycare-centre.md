# Daycare Centre

**Source:** Crack A Business Kenya (CAB) | **Sub-sector:** Child Care Services — Infant & Toddler Day Care (6 months–3 years)
**Conversion:** Original KES figures at KES 106/USD (2020 base), adjusted ×1.27 for USD inflation → March 2026 USD

> A market survey of the daycare business in Kenyan urban and peri-urban estates — covering charges, capital requirements, competition dynamics, staffing, and viability across income segments. Minimum viable capital as low as $108 (home-based, basic); formal premises-based operations require $240–$12,000+.

> Regulatory clarity is inconsistent — some counties require an education licence for any daycare; others do not. Verify with local county education and public health offices before opening. Operating without a county permit carries real enforcement risk; the Public Health Licence is a strong trust signal to parents.

## Revenue Dynamics

- **Charge calibration:** Parents benchmark daycare fees against what they pay house helpers. If a househelp costs KES 3,000/month ($36), parents resist paying more than KES 3,600/month ($43) for daycare — even if the daycare is objectively superior. Know your local househelp rate before setting price.
- **Monthly equivalent:** KES 100/day × 26 working days = KES 2,600/month ($31). This is well within the house help benchmark in most peri-urban estates.
- **Breakeven children:** At KES 100/day, a daycare with KES 21,000/month in costs needs at least 9 children daily (9 × 100 × 26 days = KES 23,400) to cover costs. Below 7–8 children/day the operation is loss-making.
- **Seasonal variation:** January (school term start, parents returning from holiday with new work routines) and June (mid-year job changes) show highest new enrolments.

> The most common failure is running out of capital before reaching breakeven — not bad demand. A new daycare takes 6–16 weeks to build trust with local parents and reach minimum viable enrolment. Capital must cover this gap.

## Staffing

- **Optimal ratio:** 1 caregiver per 6–8 children; above 15 children per caregiver leads to burnout and declining child care quality
- **Preferred profile:** Women in late 20s to 30s; passion for children is the primary hiring criterion — more observable than formal qualifications
- **Salary:** KES 6,000–12,000/month ($72–$144); below KES 6,000 leads to high turnover and caregiver burnout
- **Reward models:** Fixed salary (best for retention); daily wage (flexible but reduces commitment); commission per child (lowest cost but highest turnover risk)
- **Labour turnover:** Average 2 months — one of the highest of any business type surveyed. Disruption in caregivers destabilises children and alarms parents. Reducing turnover is the single most important operational priority.

## Trust as the Core Product

Parents choose a daycare primarily on trust, not price or facilities. Trust is built through:
1. Consistent, named caregivers who know each child's needs
2. Responsive communication when children fall ill or have incidents
3. Clean environment (visible, not just claimed)
4. Positive child mood at pickup — the child's demeanour is the most powerful marketing signal
5. Established reputation via word of mouth among mothers in the estate

## Market Demand Drivers

- Growth of women in formal employment and entrepreneurship
- Frustration with unreliable or unavailable domestic house helpers
- Government minimum wage regulations for house helpers increasing formal cost of home-based care
- Urban nuclear families without extended family support networks

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Daycare demand is growing rapidly in Kampala, Wakiso, and Mukono districts, driven by the same urbanisation and working-mother dynamics. Government pre-primary registration required through district education offices. Baby Simon, Little Angels, and many informal home-based daycares operate in residential estates. Charge range: UGX 40,000–300,000/month ($10–$81/month) depending on estate and quality tier.
- **Tanzania:** Daycare sector formalising gradually in Dar es Salaam. MoEST registration recommended; NHIF registration for caregivers increasingly required. Charge range widely variable by neighbourhood; Masaki/Oyster Bay premium estates command TZS 200,000–500,000/month ($77–$192/month).
- **Rwanda:** Most structured EA daycare regulatory environment. MIGEPROF (Ministry of Gender) and district offices regulate child care centres. Quality standards are enforced more consistently than in Kenya or Uganda. Kigali's Kimihurura, Remera, and Kicukiro have active markets. Monthly fees: RWF 60,000–200,000 ($47–$155/month). Expat and NGO community creates strong demand for premium English-medium daycares.
- **ECDE integration:** Across all EA markets, daycares that add a basic Early Childhood Development Education (ECDE) component — songs, colours, alphabet — command higher fees and reduce perceived competition from pure child-minding competitors.

## Data Quality Notes

Revenue and cost figures are from a single case study in Kiambu peri-urban (2014). Urban Nairobi, Kampala, and Kigali formal daycares will have substantially higher costs (rent, salaries) and higher achievable fees. The minimum capital of KES 9,000 ($108) reflects an extremely informal home-based setup — viable only where the operator already has space and operates without formal licences.
