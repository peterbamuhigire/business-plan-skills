# Mining & Extractives Industry Guide

Industry-specific context for generating bankable business plans in mining and extractive industries. Covers stone quarrying, sand mining, mineral extraction, lime and chalk production, salt mining, and oil and gas support services.

## Industry Overview

Uganda possesses significant mineral resources including limestone, gold, tin, wolfram (tungsten), cobalt, iron ore, salt, sand, stone, marble, vermiculite, and beryllium. The country's nascent oil and gas sector in the Albertine Graben adds a further dimension. Despite this endowment, mining contributes only 3-4% of GDP, indicating substantial untapped potential. Artisanal and small-scale mining (ASM) dominates, employing an estimated 200,000-400,000 people, while industrial mining remains limited. The sector is governed by the Mining Act 2003 (amended 2022), which requires exploration and mining licences from the Directorate of Geological Survey and Mines (DGSM).

**Key insight:** The gap between Uganda's mineral wealth and its mining sector's GDP contribution represents both an opportunity and a caution. Success requires proper licensing, geological assessment, environmental compliance, and community engagement — shortcuts in any of these areas create existential risks for the business.

## Business Model Types

| Type | Startup Investment | Key Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **Artisanal/small-scale mining** | UGX 10M-100M | Manual or semi-mechanised, local permits, high labour intensity |
| **Medium quarrying operation** | UGX 100M-1B | Mechanised crushing/processing, district-level market, environmental permit |
| **Industrial mining** | UGX 1B-50B+ | Exploration licence, EIA, heavy equipment, export potential |
| **Mineral processing/value addition** | UGX 50M-500M | Beneficiation, smelting, or refining of raw minerals |
| **Sand and aggregate supply** | UGX 30M-300M | Extraction, washing, grading, transport to construction sites |
| **Oil and gas support services** | UGX 50M-500M | Catering, logistics, camp management, equipment hire |

## Sub-Sectors

### Stone Quarrying & Aggregate
The most accessible entry point, supplying crushed stone, gravel, and aggregate to the construction sector. Requires proximity to both the rock source and construction markets. Mechanised crushers significantly improve throughput but require UGX 200M+ investment. Key regions include Mukono, Wakiso, Tororo, and Karamoja (marble and limestone).

### Sand Mining
River sand and lake sand extraction for construction. Increasingly regulated due to environmental concerns (riverbank erosion, wetland degradation). Requires NEMA environmental permit and often community agreements. Manufactured sand (crushed rock) is emerging as an alternative where natural sand is scarce or restricted.

### Mineral Extraction & Processing
Covers precious metals (gold, silver), industrial minerals (limestone, marble, vermiculite), and base metals (tin, wolfram, cobalt, iron ore). Ranges from artisanal panning to mechanised extraction. Value addition through processing (e.g., silver extraction from waste, brass and bronze casting) can significantly improve margins.

The value chain typically follows: exploration, extraction, beneficiation (washing, sorting, crushing), processing (smelting, refining), and marketing. Most Ugandan miners operate only in extraction; moving up the value chain captures more margin but requires capital and technical expertise.

### Lime & Chalk Production
Limestone processing for lime (used in construction, agriculture, water treatment) and chalk production (educational supplies). Requires kiln infrastructure and access to limestone deposits. Major limestone deposits exist in Tororo, Karamoja, and parts of western Uganda.

### Salt Mining
Uganda has salt deposits, notably at Lake Katwe (crater salt) and Kibiro (rock salt). Traditional salt extraction competes with imported refined salt. Opportunities exist in improved processing, packaging, and branding for both food-grade and industrial salt markets.

### Oil & Gas Support Services
The Albertine Graben oil development (Tilenga and Kingfisher projects, East African Crude Oil Pipeline) creates demand for ancillary services: catering for remote oil field crews, logistics, camp management, equipment maintenance, and environmental services. These are service businesses that ride the oil sector's growth without the capital intensity of exploration. National content requirements under the Petroleum (Exploration, Development and Production) Act mandate local participation, creating opportunities for Ugandan entrepreneurs.

## Typical Cost Structures

Mining and extractive businesses are capital-intensive upfront but can generate strong returns once operational:

| Metric | Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Equipment/depreciation** | 30-40% of costs | Heavy equipment, crushers, vehicles |
| **Labour** | 20-30% of costs | Higher for artisanal; lower for mechanised |
| **Transport** | 15-25% of costs | Bulk materials are expensive to move |
| **Licensing and compliance** | 5-10% of costs | Exploration/mining licences, EIA, annual fees |
| **Environmental rehabilitation** | 2-5% of revenue | Set aside for mine closure and land restoration |
| **Net profit margin** | 10-35% | Quarrying at lower end; processed minerals higher |
| **Break-even timeline** | 1-5 years | Depends on scale and commodity prices |

**Royalty structure:** Uganda charges mining royalties at 5% of gross value for most minerals, with precious minerals (gold, gemstones) at higher rates. These are payable to URA and represent a fixed cost that cannot be avoided regardless of profitability.

**Scale economics:** Artisanal operations have low capital requirements but high per-unit costs due to manual labour. Mechanisation reduces unit costs dramatically but requires minimum production volumes to justify equipment investment. The transition from artisanal to semi-mechanised is often the most difficult phase financially.

## Key Success Factors

1. **Geological survey** — professional assessment of mineral deposits before investment; do not rely on surface indicators alone
2. **Licensing compliance** — valid exploration/mining licences from DGSM; location licence for artisanal mining
3. **Environmental compliance** — NEMA environmental impact assessment (EIA) or project brief as required
4. **Equipment selection** — match equipment capacity to deposit size and market demand; over-investment is a common mistake
5. **Market access** — secure off-take agreements or reliable buyer relationships before scaling production
6. **Community relations** — formal agreements with local communities; employment of local labour; transparent benefit-sharing
7. **Safety culture** — mining is inherently hazardous; invest in training, PPE, and safe working procedures from day one

## Regulatory Environment

- **Mining Act 2003 (amended 2022):** Governs all mining activities; requires exploration licence (3 years, renewable), mining lease (21 years), or location licence (artisanal, 2 years)
- **NEMA:** Environmental impact assessment mandatory for mining operations; environmental audit requirements
- **DGSM (Directorate of Geological Survey and Mines):** Issues licences, monitors compliance, maintains mineral rights registry
- **URA:** Mining royalties (5% of gross value for most minerals), corporate tax, VAT on processed products
- **Community consent:** Free, prior and informed consent (FPIC) increasingly expected; community development agreements
- **Occupational safety:** Mining Safety Regulations under the Mining Act; personal protective equipment requirements
- **Local government:** District-level approvals, land use agreements, environmental monitoring

## Key Risks

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| **Environmental liability** | Comprehensive EIA; rehabilitation plan and bond; ongoing monitoring |
| **Licence revocation** | Strict compliance with licence conditions; timely renewals; transparent reporting |
| **Commodity price volatility** | Diversify mineral portfolio; secure forward contracts; maintain cost discipline |
| **Community opposition** | Early engagement; benefit-sharing agreements; local employment; transparent operations |
| **Geological uncertainty** | Professional geological assessment; phased investment tied to confirmed reserves |
| **Equipment breakdown** | Preventive maintenance programme; spare parts inventory; service contracts |
| **Regulatory changes** | Monitor policy developments; maintain government relationships; industry association membership |
