# Chicken Butchery (Retail)

**Source:** Crack A Business Kenya (CAB) | **Sub-sector:** Poultry Retail
**Conversion:** Original KES figures at KES 106/USD (2020 base), adjusted ×1.27 for USD inflation → March 2026 USD

> A fixed-shop retail butchery selling raw chicken (whole, quarters, backs, necks, liver, gizzard, feet) sourced from wholesale markets, with an optional add-on of roasted/fried chicken sold directly to customers. Distinct from beef butchery in its cold-chain requirements and perishability profile.

## Revenue Observations

- **Value-adding:** Roasted/grilled chicken adds KES 20–30/kg premium over raw; deep-fried offal increases gross revenue by ~20%. These require additional equipment and a Public Health clearance.
- **Offal pricing:** Necks, backs, and liver sold in small ready-to-eat pieces (KES 10–20 each) convert lower-margin cuts to impulse-buy items, increasing volume.
- **Location sensitivity:** The same quarter-kilogram of chicken sells at KES 80 (Kangemi, low-income), KES 90 (Kiambu town), and KES 100 (Kariobangi, mid-income). Retail price calibration must match the local purchasing power.
- **Competition:** Where a competing chicken butchery is within sight, price-cutting of KES 5–10/kg is common. Avoid entering a location that already has a well-established competitor without a clear differentiation plan.

## Operations

- **Cold chain:** Chicken is highly perishable; the deep freezer is non-negotiable for any shop that does not sell out daily. Starting with a cooler box is only viable for extremely low-volume operations.
- **Supplier options:** City Market and Kiserian slaughterhouses (Nairobi); wholesale prices at KES 280–320/kg. Some butcheries source directly from poultry farms in peri-urban areas for slightly lower prices.
- **Weighing scale certification:** The Department of Weights and Measures (Ministry of Trade) certifies scales — certification costs KES 2,000 ($24)/year. Officials patrol shopping centres; an uncertified scale creates legal risk.
- **Medical certificates:** All staff handling food must hold a valid health certificate (from local government clinic).
- **Capital sufficiency:** Working capital should cover at least 5 months of rent, salaries, and utilities before breakeven — undercapitalised launches are the primary failure cause.

## Market

Chicken has become a mainstream protein across all urban income levels in East Africa, driven by price competitiveness relative to beef, health consciousness (white meat preference), and rising urban middle class. It is also the dominant protein in institutional feeding (schools, hospitals, canteen catering).

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Chicken wholesale from Kampala's Owino Market, Nakasero, or directly from hatcheries in Gayaza and Mukono. Retail price: UGX 8,000–12,000/kg ($2.16–$3.24) depending on cut and area. Village chicken (local breed) commands a 30–50% premium over broilers — viable niche for butcheries near wealthy residential areas. KCCA health licence required.
- **Tanzania:** Poultry wholesale concentrated in Kariakoo and Ilala markets in Dar es Salaam. TBS food hygiene standards apply. Broiler supply from peri-urban farms (Kibaha, Morogoro corridor).
- **Rwanda:** Rwanda Bureau of Standards (RBS) food safety requirements. Kigali's formal butcheries are required to have cold storage; informal street chicken selling is less tolerated than in Kenya or Uganda. Premium segment: whole fresh chicken sold to hotels and restaurants commands higher margins.
- **Halal:** In Muslim-majority areas (Mombasa, Zanzibar, Eastleigh/Nairobi, Kampala Kisenyi), halal slaughter certification is commercially essential and a significant customer attraction for both retail and institutional buyers.

## Data Quality Notes

Wholesale prices are from Nairobi (Kariobangi case study). Prices vary by source, distance from wholesale markets, and season. Deep freezer prices vary widely by brand and whether new or second-hand.
