# Ice Lollies (Lamba Lamba) Production

**Source:** Crack A Business Kenya (CAB) — Icing The Cash: An Ice Lollies Business Quick Guide | **Sub-sector:** Food Manufacturing — Frozen Confectionery
**Conversion:** KES ÷ 106 × 1.27 → March 2026 USD

> A small-scale production business making and distributing ice lollies (locally known as "Lamba Lamba") — flavoured, coloured frozen water on a stick. Capital ranges from KES 110,000–305,400 depending on machine capacity. Margins are thin (KES 2–4/lollie) so volume and distribution network are critical. Best located in high-density, warm areas with a large school-age population.

## Machine Notes

- **Capacity:** A 240-capacity maker produces 240 lollies per cycle; cycle time 30–60 minutes. Practical hourly output is less due to preparation and extraction time — budget 1.5 hours per 120 lollies in realistic scheduling.
- **Components:** Machine sold in two parts — freezing component + moulds; priced separately. Moulds can be replaced/added independently.
- **Supplier:** Benmatt Refrigeration Services, Eastleigh, Nairobi (0722 669 300) — recommended; jua kali alternatives available near Shauri Moyo but less reliable.
- **Second-hand:** Available on OLX and classifieds; test thoroughly before purchase. New machine with warranty recommended for start-up.
- **Electricity requirement:** Must operate from a room with stable power connection.

## Distribution Models

### Model 1 — Salesperson/Commission (Recommended for start-up)

- Recruit salespeople; supply each with a cooler box stocked with lollies
- Salespeople sell at consumer price (KES 10); return proceeds + unsold stock at end of day
- Producer pays commission (KES 3/lollie sold)
- **Advantage:** Wide geographic coverage; you control production and pricing
- **Disadvantage:** Lower salesperson commitment (no own capital at risk); higher supervision required

### Model 2 — Wholesale to Retailers

- Retailers purchase at wholesale (KES 6–7) and sell at KES 10 — their own risk
- Prepayment required — no credit
- **Advantage:** Cash upfront; fewer management responsibilities
- **Disadvantage:** Retailers may not push sales actively

> Map routes geographically; assign one salesperson per area to avoid cannibalisation and maintain motivation.

## Location and Market

| Factor | Guidance |
|--------|----------|
| Primary consumers | School-age children (largest segment) + young women under 26 |
| Ideal location | High-density estate near primary schools; warm climate |
| Production premises | Mid-size room with electricity; proximity to sales area |
| Rural opportunity | Many peri-urban and smaller towns have no ice lollie vendors — first-mover advantage |
| Seasonal factor | Sales peak in hot months; plan cash reserves for cold season |

> Urban areas: larger and more diverse market but higher competition. Rural/peri-urban: lower competition but lower purchasing power and seasonal variation.

## Flavours and Inputs

- Flavours and food colours available at specialist shops (e.g., Pradrip, Keekrok Road, Nairobi)
- Standard flavours: orange, pineapple, strawberry, lemon
- Innovation opportunity: mixed colours (two-tone lollies), unique flavours, novelty mould shapes — provide temporary differentiation though competitors can copy easily

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Lamba Lamba-equivalent (known locally as "freezies" or "ice ice") are popular in Kampala schools and markets. Lira, Gulu, Mbarara, and Jinja have small-scale producers. The concept follows identical production economics. Machine suppliers available in Kampala's Industrial Area and Kikuubo. Hawker licences required from KCCA; Public Health (MFPED) food handler certificate required for production facility.
- **Tanzania:** Frozen lollies sold as "barafu" in school neighbourhoods and bus stands across Dar es Salaam, Mwanza, and Arusha. TFDA food production certificate required for commercial output. Electricity reliability is a constraint in some areas — invest in a backup cooling option.
- **Rwanda:** Ice lollie production is relatively less common in Rwanda than Kenya/Uganda — genuine first-mover opportunity, particularly in secondary towns (Huye, Musanze, Rubavu) with large school populations. RFDA food safety registration required. Kigali's more formal business environment means proper licensing is enforced.
- **EA pricing note:** Convert KES retail price (KES 10) to local currency at prevailing rate: approximately UGX 37, TZS 125, RWF 14 per lollie. All are well within the impulse-purchase threshold for school-age consumers.
