# Fish Supply to Hotels (B2B)

**Source:** Crack A Business Kenya (CAB) — Fish Supply and Consumption in Nairobi Hotels: A Quick Overview, 2017 | **Sub-sector:** Food Wholesale & Distribution — Fresh Fish
**Conversion:** KES ÷ 106 × 1.27 → March 2026 USD

> A survey-based guide to supplying fresh tilapia and other fish to mid-level urban hotels (large dining-hall-type restaurants). Based on research across Nairobi CBD and environs. Individual suppliers dominate — formal companies are not required to win hotel accounts. Success depends on reliable, fresh supply at competitive prices delivered on agreed days.

## Hotel Market Survey Results (Nairobi, 2017)

### Fish Type

| Type | % of Hotels |
|------|-------------|
| Tilapia only | 60% |
| Tilapia + fillet | 17% |
| Tilapia + omena | Small % |
| Other combinations | Remainder |

> **Start with tilapia** — if you can supply tilapia reliably, other types follow naturally.

### Volume per Hotel (Daily)

| Volume | % of Hotels |
|--------|-------------|
| 10–50 fish/day | 67% |
| 51–100 fish/day | 17% |
| Over 100 fish/day | 16% |

> **Plan for 10–15 fish/day per hotel in capital budgeting** — optimistic projections of 50+/day are risky for a new supplier.

### Supplier Profile

| Supplier Type | % of Hotels |
|--------------|-------------|
| Individual suppliers | 62% |
| Own sourcing (hotel goes to market) | 25% |
| Companies (formal) | 13% |

> Registration as a limited company is not required to supply most hotels. Individual suppliers dominate.

### Supply Frequency

| Frequency | % of Hotels |
|-----------|-------------|
| Twice weekly | 50% |
| Once weekly | 25% |
| Thrice weekly | 13% |
| Daily | 12% |

> **Plan for twice-weekly delivery minimum.** Hotels keep fish in deep freezers between deliveries.

### Payment Terms

| Terms | % of Hotels |
|-------|-------------|
| Cash on delivery | 38% |
| Cash and credit mixed | 37% |
| Credit (post-delivery) | 25% |

> Credit can be 2 days to 1 month. **Working capital to bridge this gap is essential.**

### Willingness to Change Suppliers

| Response | % |
|----------|---|
| Yes — willing to change | 70% |
| No | 20% |
| Maybe | 10% |

> High willingness means market is accessible — but it also means **your customers can easily leave you** if a better supplier emerges.

### Primary Reasons Hotels Would Switch Supplier

- Price (38%) — cheapest single factor
- Price and quality combined (50%) — most influential combination
- Price and time/reliability (12%)

## Fish Sourcing in Nairobi

### Preferred Source

| Source | Notes |
|--------|-------|
| Lake Victoria (Kisumu tilapia) | Preferred for taste ("darker, heavier, more flavourful") |
| Lake Naivasha tilapia | "Whitish" appearance; less preferred by quality-conscious hotels |
| Fish farms (pond fish) | Less preferred — "tastes less than lake fish and weighs less" |

### Supply Channels

1. **Gikomba Fish Market (Nairobi)** — largest wholesale fish market; earliest morning purchases; entry point for most new suppliers
2. **Fish Processing Companies** — supply deep-frozen product; lower price but some hotels resist frozen fish
3. **City Market** — smaller volume, more expensive but convenient for small top-ups
4. **Direct from Kisumu** — highest quality, higher logistics cost; viable for serious suppliers with refrigerated transport

> **Preference note:** Hotels selling large quantities of fish specifically request Kisumu tilapia and pay a small premium.

## Key Operational Rules

1. **Freshness is the product** — hotels buy from individual suppliers specifically because suppliers deliver fresh, not deep-frozen fish
2. **Reliability beats price** — hotels keep multiple suppliers specifically because they fear a single supplier going absent; turn up consistently and you become the preferred primary supplier
3. **Delivery timing** — late delivery = hotel runs out of fish = lost revenue = angry management = lost account
4. **Credit management** — establish clear credit terms in writing; 2-day and 7-day terms are common; never let outstanding credit accumulate beyond one cycle before addressing it
5. **Hotel loyalty is multi-supplier** — most hotels deliberately maintain 2 suppliers; winning "preferred" status means consistent delivery and fair price, not exclusivity

## Entry Strategy

**Minimum viable start:**
- Identify 3 hotels within a tight geographic area (CBD cluster or estate)
- Source 30–50 fish from Gikomba at KES 70–130/fish (market level)
- Supply at KES 200–250/fish → gross margin KES 70–130/fish
- Deliver twice weekly; build trust over 4–6 weeks
- Expand to additional hotels as working capital grows

**Logistics option:** Public transport for small volumes; own motorcycle or hired vehicle for 5+ hotels on same route.

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Nile Perch and tilapia from Lake Victoria (Jinja, Entebbe landings) are the primary species. Kampala's hotels and rooftop restaurants are active tilapia buyers. St. Balikuddembe (Owino) market and Nakawa Market are wholesale sources. MAAIF certification required for commercial fish trading.
- **Tanzania:** Lake Victoria tilapia from Mwanza and Bukoba; Nile Perch (sangara) from same source. Dar es Salaam's hotels also consume marine fish (kingfish, prawns). Kariakoo Fish Section is the key wholesale market. TFDA food safety standards apply.
- **Rwanda:** Kigali's growing hospitality sector (hotels, guesthouses, restaurants) creates steady fish demand. Lake Kivu tilapia and Nile Perch are the main commercial species. Kigali City Market and Kimironko Market are supply points. NAEB and RDB regulate commercial fish trade.
- **EA market trend:** As urbanisation grows and disposable incomes rise, fish consumption in formal dining establishments increases — particularly tilapia, which is culturally embedded across all EA cuisines.

## Data Quality Notes

Survey is from 2017 Nairobi CBD and environs; prices (KES 200–250 hotel buying price) are illustrative and will have changed. The market structure (individual suppliers dominant, twice-weekly delivery, credit terms) is structurally stable. Capital to start is very low — the main barrier is working capital to absorb credit terms, not equipment or licences.
