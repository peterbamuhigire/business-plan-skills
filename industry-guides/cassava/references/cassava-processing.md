# Cassava Processing Technologies

## Processing Pathways Overview

```
FRESH CASSAVA ROOTS
        │
        ├─→ [Peel → Wash → Grate → Dewater → Dry → Mill]  ──→  HQCF (unfermented flour)
        │
        ├─→ [Peel → Wash → Chip → Sun/Air Dry]  ──────────────→  DRIED CHIPS (industrial)
        │
        ├─→ [Peel → Wash → Grate → Ferment → Dewater → Roast]  →  GARI (fermented granules)
        │
        ├─→ [HQCF → Bake/Fry]  ────────────────────────────────→  BAKERY / PASTRIES / PASTA
        │
        ├─→ [Wet mill → Separate fibre → Dry starch]  ──────────→  INDUSTRIAL STARCH
        │
        └─→ [Peels → Grate → Press → Dry]  ─────────────────────→  PEEL ANIMAL FEED
```

**Critical distinction:** Unfermented HQCF (white, odourless, used in baking) is **completely different** from traditional fermented flour (gari, fufu). Confusing the two has caused consumer distrust in several markets. HQCF must be produced from fresh roots within 24 hours of harvest.

## Technology 1: High Quality Cassava Flour (HQCF)

### Process Flow (seven steps)

1. **Raw material selection** — high starch/dry matter varieties; harvest mature roots (9–18 months); avoid diseased, fibrous, or woody roots; process within 24 hours
2. **Peeling** — manual (knife) or motorised peeler; remove outer brown skin AND inner cream layer; peel immediately before washing; do not leave peeled roots >1 hour in water (fermentation risk)
3. **Washing** — clean water; wash 2–3 times until all soil removed; transfer to clean surface
4. **Grating/chipping** — grate into mash (grating method) or chip diagonally (chipping method; use only low-cyanide varieties)
5. **Dewatering/pressing** — hydraulic or screw press to remove water from mash or chips; reduces drying time; wet cake = stiff, not sloppy
6. **Drying** — sun drying on raised platform with black tarpaulin; or solar dryer; or flash dryer (industrial). Target **10–12% moisture content** (dry to hand). For HQCF: maximum **5 kg per square metre** on drying surface. Turn product periodically; protect from rain and contamination
7. **Milling** — mill dried chips/cake through screen of desired aperture; allow product to cool before packaging

### HQCF Quality Specifications

| Parameter | Standard |
|-----------|---------|
| Moisture content | ≤ 12% |
| Starch content | ≥ 60% |
| Crude fibre | ≤ 2% |
| Crude ash | ≤ 3% |
| Fineness | ≥ 90% pass through 0.6 mm sieve |
| Particle size | 250–500 µm |
| Total cyanogens (HCN equivalent) | ≤ 10 mg/kg |
| Total aflatoxins | < 10 µg/kg |
| Yeast and mould | < 100 CFU/g |
| Harmful organisms (Salmonella etc.) | Nil |
| Acidity (pH) | > 5.8 |
| Protein content | 1.3–2.0% |
| Crude fat | 0.3–0.5% |

*Source: TAAT Clearinghouse (2022); NRI/CAVA Training Manual*

### Production Economics

- **Conversion rate:** 3.5–4.0 tonnes of fresh cassava roots → 1 tonne HQCF
- **Production cost (Madagascar pilot):** US$60/tonne fresh root equivalent; US$17,238 total cost for 288 MT roots processed/year
- **Market price:** US$550–650/tonne HQCF
- **Net margin:** US$79/tonne starting from dried chips (Uganda TAAT study)
- **At UGX 3,700/$1:** Selling price ≈ UGX 2.0–2.4M/tonne HQCF

## Technology 2: Dried Cassava Chips

### Purpose and Uses

- Dried chips: dried interiors of starchy roots cut diagonally; major international trade commodity
- Global trade value: US$2.7 billion/year (2020); top exporters are Thailand, Cambodia, Vietnam
- Uganda price differential: traditional chips **US$113/tonne**; high-quality mechanical chips = **3× price premium** (~US$339/tonne)
- Uses: starch production (tapioca), animal feed (energy source; partial maize substitute), bioethanol substrate, HQCF milling base

### Production Process

1. Wash fresh roots to remove soil
2. Peel (remove brown skin)
3. Chip diagonally (oval/semi-rectangular shape preferred; shorter drying time than cylindrical)
4. Dry in hot-air convection oven 45–165°C or sun-dry; target **14–17% moisture content**
5. Store in HDPE bags (50 kg or bulk 500 kg sacks); shelf life up to **6 months**; may require insecticide

### Chips Quality Specifications

| Parameter | Marketable standard |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Starch content | ≥ 68–70% |
| Moisture content (final) | 14–17% |
| Fibre content | ≤ 5% |
| Sand/foreign matter | ≤ 3% |
| Colour | White; oval shape preferred |

### Equipment Costs

| Unit | Capacity | Cost |
|------|----------|------|
| Combined peeler + chipper (community scale) | 2–4 tonnes/hr fresh roots | US$750 |
| Combined peeler + chipper (industrial) | 5–10 tonnes/hr fresh roots | US$8,000 |

## Technology 3: Gari and Fermented Products

### What is Gari?

Gari is a granular fermented product from shredded, pressed, fermented, and roasted cassava — the dominant processed cassava product in West Africa, gaining traction in East Africa:

- **Composition:** 86% carbohydrates, 7% moisture, <2% protein/fat; low cholesterol; high fibre
- **Shelf life:** Up to one year in airtight bags
- **Grades:** Extra-fine (< 0.5 mm), fine (0.5–1 mm), coarse (1–1.25 mm), extra-coarse (1.25–2.0 mm)
- **Cyanide standard:** < 10 ppm (critical — fermented flours are NOT interchangeable with HQCF)

### Gari Production Process

1. Wash and peel fresh roots
2. Grate by hand or machine into mash/pulp
3. Place mash in porous bags; stack in rack for drainage and fermentation **1–2 days** (fermentation time determines sourness)
4. Dewater using hydraulic or screw press until firm wet cake results
5. Sieve into "grits"
6. Roast on hot tray until dry, crispy product
7. Dry-sieve; grind larger granules if needed
8. Pack into airtight labelled bags; store under cool, dry conditions

### Commercial Equipment

- Screw press (small-scale): US$600
- Hydraulic press (commercial): US$2,000–5,000
- Grater + roaster combination: US$1,500–4,000

## Technology 4: HQCF as Bakery Ingredient

### Wheat Substitution Rates

| Product | Maximum HQCF substitution | Notes |
|---------|--------------------------|-------|
| Bread | 10–20% (up to 30% with xanthan gum) | Higher rates reduce leavening and volume |
| Sponge cake | Up to 75% | Most compatible product |
| Butter cookies | Up to 50% | |
| Doughnuts | Up to 25% | |
| Pasta/noodles | 25–50% (replaces rice starch in oriental noodles) | Softer, more elastic noodles |
| Biscuits | 20–35% | |

**HQCF vs wheat flour properties:**
- Gluten-free: does not rise well with yeast; limits bread substitution
- Absorbs more water (higher bulk density)
- Lower in calories; higher in fibre; contains vitamin C
- Slightly nutty/earthy taste
- Anti-inflammatory properties; nourishes beneficial gut bacteria
- Adding **xanthan gum at 1%** greatly improves bread structure at higher substitution rates

**Price parity:** Imported wheat flour: US$450/tonne; HQCF produced in Africa: ~US$450/tonne — cost parity achieved; bakeries benefit from foreign exchange savings and local supply security. **25% substitution = 25% raw material cost savings** for that portion; a bakery using 200 MT flour/month saves ~US$130,560/year.

### Bakery Equipment Costs

| Equipment | Scale | Cost |
|-----------|-------|------|
| Portable 3-tray oven (electric/gas) | Local bakery | US$2,000–3,000 |
| Stationary commercial oven (32–64 trays) | Industrial | US$7,000–12,000 |

## Technology 5: Cassava Peel Animal Feed

### The Peel Waste Opportunity

- 1 tonne fresh cassava roots generates **200–300 kg of peels**
- Sub-Saharan Africa generates **40 million metric tonnes of peels per year** — mostly discarded
- Peels can substitute maize/wheat in animal feed; energy-rich but low protein → supplement with soybean/maize protein

### Processing Flow

1. Remove all soil from peels (protects grater; avoids spoilage)
2. Grate peels (up to 3 times) to reduce particle size
3. Pack into sacks; press using hydraulic press to dewater (**500 litres removed per tonne in 30 minutes** vs 2–3 days traditional)
4. Leave wet cake in sacks overnight to ferment (breaks down cyanides)
5. Grate again; sieve to separate fine and coarse fractions
6. Sun-dry (spread thinly on clean plastic/metal/cement; turn hourly) or oven/flash dry
7. Dry mash: target **10–12% moisture** for safe storage; store 4–6 months
8. Wet cake (after one grate + press cycle): shelf life **1 week**; feed directly to ruminants

### Feed Quality and Livestock Suitability

| Parameter | Wet meal | Dry coarse mash | Dry fine mash |
|-----------|---------|----------------|---------------|
| Crude protein | 4–6% | 3.1% | 2.6% |
| Starch content | — | 78% | 78% |
| Crude fat | — | 1.7% | 1.7% |
| HCN (when properly processed) | < 10 ppm | < 10 ppm | < 10 ppm (safe limit: 100 ppm) |
| Aflatoxins | < 18–20 ppb | < 18–20 ppb | < 18–20 ppb |

**Livestock substitution limits:**
- Pigs: up to **40% of dietary needs**
- Brood hens: up to **27%** of dietary needs
- Broiler chickens: up to **15%** of dietary needs
- Cattle, sheep, goats: wet cake fed in pure form

| Item | Cost (US$) |
|------|-----------|
| Motorised grater | $1,000 |
| Hydraulic/screw press | $600 |
| Motorised pulveriser | $850 |
| Mechanical sieve | $400 |
| Supplemental dryer | $550 |
| **Total equipment** | **~$3,400** |
| Production cost per tonne dry mash | ~$114 |

**Additional benefit:** Feeding poultry 50% cassava peel diet (vs 100% maize-based) reduces feed costs by over **20%**. Cassava peel utilisation could release millions of tonnes of maize for human consumption.

**Licensing:** Phytosanitary certificates required in many African countries; regular aflatoxin testing mandatory.

## Technology 6: Quality Assurance System

### Three Main Safety Threats

1. **Hydrogen cyanide (HCN)** — from bitter varieties; addressed by using sweet/low-cyanide varieties and proper processing (grating, dewatering, drying)
2. **Microbial contamination** — addressed through personal hygiene, safe handling, HACCP, sanitation
3. **Aflatoxin** — from mould growth during drying/storage; addressed by rapid drying, proper moisture control (<12%), clean storage

### HACCP Implementation Steps

1. Develop hazard and quality analysis plans; identify critical control points along processing steps
2. Educate personnel, suppliers, and buyers on the system rationale and procedures
3. Build infrastructure for health hazard testing and quality norms

**Start-up cost for quality plan:** From ~US$3,000 for small enterprise; training on food safety standards and quality testing: ~US$1,200/employee.

### Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP)

- Personal hygiene: clean protective clothing, handwashing, health checks
- Safe handling: no cross-contamination between fresh roots and processed product
- Preventing contamination: designated wet and dry processing areas; equipment cleaned after each use
- Safe storage: pallets, FIFO rotation, off-ground storage, adequate ventilation
- Sanitation: vermin control, proper waste management, wastewater via seepage tanks
