# Cassava Industry Guide — Uganda

## Industry Overview

Cassava (*Manihot esculenta*) is Uganda's second most important food crop after bananas:

- National output: approximately **3.5 million tonnes** from **0.4 million hectares**
- Ranks 2nd among food crops nationally; 1st in some northern and eastern districts
- **71% of growers** classify themselves as subsistence producers; however **60% consider cassava a new cash crop** (NAARI case study)
- Uganda's total cassava output is roughly **60% of China's**, despite having only one-third the land area under cassava — yield per hectare is approximately **one-fifth of China's**, indicating massive productivity headroom
- Leading production districts: Mbale, Iganga, Apac, Kamuli, Lira, Tororo, Kumi
- Western Uganda (Masindi/Buliisa/Kibaale corridor) significant for mechanised production pilots
- Estimated **40+ million metric tonnes of cassava peels** generated annually across Sub-Saharan Africa — a largely untapped resource

**Historical context:** Cassava was introduced to Uganda between 1862 and 1875. The 1988 African Cassava Mosaic Disease (ACMD) outbreak devastated production across Uganda; subsequent introduction of disease-tolerant varieties (NASE series) reversed the decline. Cassava has progressively shifted from famine reserve to commercial crop — a transformation that is still underway in many districts.

**Market context:** In 2020 the global dried cassava chip trade was valued at **US$2.7 billion annually**, dominated by Southeast Asian exporters. Africa's share is only US$40 million — primarily due to low productivity and poor post-harvest infrastructure, not lack of supply. Africa imports approximately **US$11 billion** of wheat per year; substituting 25% with HQCF would create a **US$2.75 billion domestic cassava flour industry** (TAAT Clearinghouse, 2022).

## Production Systems

### 1. Monocrop vs Intercrop vs Rotation

| System | Description | Uganda prevalence | Yield potential | Key consideration |
|--------|-------------|------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Monocrop** | Cassava only; wider spacing | Less common; commercial plots | Highest, with ISFM | Requires full weed management; disease monitoring easier |
| **Intercrop** | Cassava + maize, legumes, vegetables | Very common (subsistence) | Moderate | Reduces risk; legumes fix nitrogen; maize income in short season |
| **Rotation** | Cassava followed by legume/grain | Recommended for soil restoration | Sustained | Breaks disease and pest cycles; improves soil organic matter |

**ISFM (Integrated Soil Fertility Management)** is the recommended framework for smallholder cassava agronomy. The ASHC/IITA/CABI cropping guide sets a target of improving average yields from **10 t/ha to 16 t/ha** through ISFM adoption.

### 2. Conventional vs Mechanised Production

Data from the AgriTT Uganda Pilot Development Project (4 districts, western Uganda):

|-----------|------------------------|-----------------------|
| Yield (fresh roots) | **18,224 kg/ha** | **40,508 kg/ha** |
| Land preparation cost (USh) | 1,113,728 | 472,847 |
| Seed preparation & planting (USh) | 379,254 | 666,900 |
| Weed control (USh) | 641,145 | 370,500 |
| Harvest (USh) | 803,298 | 605,150 |
| Machinery wear & tear (USh) | 0 | 118,560 |
| Operators' wages (USh) | 0 | 123,500 |
| **Total cost (USh/ha)** | **2,957,425** | **2,357,457** |
| Gross returns (USh/ha) @ 269 USh/kg | 4,902,165 | 10,896,652 |
| Annualised capital cost of machines (USh) | 0 | 298,213 |
| **Net returns (USh/ha)** | **1,944,740** | **8,240,982** |
| **Net returns (US$/ha)** | **$583** | **$2,472** |

*Source: AgriTT/DFID/CATAS Uganda Pilot, 4 districts; conversion at USh 3,334/$1. Preliminary trial data — confirm over multiple seasons before large capital commitment.*

**Key mechanisation finding:** One tractor with two operators can plough and complete all planting operations on up to **10 acres per day**. The equivalent manual labour is **410 person-days** (310 land preparation + 100 planting). Mechanisation saves labour, ensures optimal plant population, and enables timely operations — particularly catching rains.

**Machinery investment:** Full Chinese equipment set (130 hp tractor + plough, rotary tiller, ridge-maker, planter, harvester) costs approximately **US$100,000** including tax and freight. A 90 hp set (more widely available for hire) reduces this cost. Shared ownership or cooperative hiring reduces entry barrier.

**Preliminary yields (without fertiliser):** Without mechanisation, good agronomy trials reached approximately **22 t/ha in Buliisa and Kigumba**. Mechanised trials averaged **41 t/ha in Kigumba and Matunda** — without fertiliser. Long-term soil fertility inputs will likely be required to sustain these levels.

## Agronomy

### Site and Climate Requirements

- **Rainfall:** Minimum 1,000 mm/year; well-distributed; drought-tolerant but performs best with reliable moisture
- **Soils:** Well-drained, light to medium loams; pH 5.5–6.5; poor on waterlogged, stony, or very heavy clay soils
- **Altitude:** 0–1,800 m; grows across Uganda's varied altitude range
- **Temperature:** 20–30°C optimal

### Planting

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Variety | NASE 14 (Uganda standard; CMD and CBSD tolerant); TMS series in Nigeria context (30–48 t/ha) |
| Planting material | Stem cuttings, 20–30 cm, from healthy disease-free plants; avoid cuttings from mosaic-infected stools |
| Spacing (monocrop) | 1 m × 1 m (10,000 plants/ha); some large-scale trials use wider spacing |
| Spacing (intercrop) | 1.5 m × 1.5 m or 2 m × 1 m to allow companion crop growth |
| Planting time | Beginning of rains; mechanised planting critical for timely establishment |
| Planting depth | 5–10 cm; horizontal or slanted cutting placement |
| Seed rate | 10,000 cuttings/ha at 1 m × 1 m |

### Nutrient Management

- **Baseline fertility:** Cassava performs on low-fertility soils but yield is severely limited without nutrients
- **Recommended rate (commercial):** Up to **400 kg/ha NPK 15:15:15** split across growth stages (Nigeria IITA data)
- **ISFM approach:** Combine mineral fertiliser with organic matter (manure, compost, crop residues); legume intercropping (beans, soybean, groundnuts) adds nitrogen and improves soil structure
- **Deficiency symptoms:** Yellowing oldest leaves = nitrogen; poor root development = phosphorus; poor water-use efficiency = potassium
- **Caution:** Sustained high yields without soil fertility inputs is not realistic over the long term — a key lesson from the Uganda mechanisation trials

### Weed Management

- Cassava is sensitive to weed competition in the first 3 months; mechanised ridge-making and proper spacing enable effective early weed control
- Manual weeding (2–3 rounds): most common method; labour-intensive; ISFM trials show timely weeding alone can increase yield by 30–40%
- Herbicide use possible but less common in Uganda smallholder context

### Disease Management

| Disease | Cause | Signs | Management |
|---------|-------|-------|------------|
| **African Cassava Mosaic Disease (ACMD/CMD)** | Whitefly-transmitted begomovirus | Mosaic yellow-green patterning on leaves; plant stunting | Plant CMD-tolerant varieties (NASE 14); rogue infected plants early; use clean cuttings |
| **Cassava Brown Streak Disease (CBSD)** | CBSV (whitefly-transmitted) | Brown streaking on stems; necrotic root lesions (tuber destruction) | Use CBSD-tolerant varieties; clean planting material; rogue infected plants |
| **Bacterial blight** | *Xanthomonas axonopodis* | Water-soaked lesions; stem die-back | Avoid infected cuttings; crop rotation; destroy infected material |

**Key principle:** Over 80% of disease risk in Uganda comes from **infected planting material**. The single highest-return disease management action is sourcing cuttings from certified, disease-free nurseries (see `cassava-seed-business.md`).

### Harvesting

- **Maturity:** 9–24 months depending on variety and end use (fresh market 9–12 months; industrial 12–18 months preferred for higher dry matter)
- **Indicators:** Leaf drop, yellowing of upper leaves, bark cracking
- **Conventional harvest:** Manual digging; 803,298 USh/ha labour cost (AgriTT data)
- **Mechanised harvest:** Mechanical lifter + manual collection; 605,150 USh/ha
- **Post-harvest urgency:** Fresh roots deteriorate within 24–48 hours of harvest; process or chip within 24 hours for best quality

## Value Chain Structure

```
[Seed/Cuttings] → [Production] → [Primary Processing] → [Secondary Processing] → [End Markets]
  NARO/CBSMs        Smallholders    Fresh root trade         HQCF mills              Households
  CSEs              (0.4–2 ha)      Chipping (sun-dried)     Starch plants           Institutional buyers
  Farmer groups     Out-growers     Gari/fufu processors     Bakery ingredient        Supermarkets
                    Mechanised      Fermented products        Animal feed mills       Export (chips)
                    commercial                               Biofuel substrate        Food manufacturers
```

### Key Value Chain Actors

| Actor | Role | Business opportunity |
|-------|------|---------------------|
| **Seed entrepreneurs (CSEs/CBSMs)** | Multiply and sell clean planting cuttings | UGX 200–400/cutting; 10,000+ cutting lots |
| **Smallholder producers** | Grow fresh roots; sell to traders or processors | Fresh root price: ~UGX 200–400/kg |
| **Aggregators/traders** | Collect, transport, sell to processors | Margin on volume; transport efficiency key |
| **Primary processors (chips/gari)** | Convert fresh roots to dried chips, gari, or fermented products | Chips: UGX 400,000–500,000/ton traditional; 3× premium for high-quality |
| **HQCF millers** | Grind dried chips into high-quality flour | HQCF: US$550–650/ton; net margin US$79/ton from chips (Uganda TAAT data) |
| **Starch plants** | Wet-milling cassava into industrial starch | Capital-intensive; industrial buyers (textiles, pharma, food industry) |
| **Bakeries** | Substitute HQCF for 10–30% of wheat flour | Cost savings of 25% on raw material if cassava flour priced at parity with wheat |
| **Animal feed processors** | Convert peel waste into livestock feed | Peel meal: US$210/ton Nigeria market; production cost ~US$114/ton |

## Startup Planning Framework

### Entry Points by Capital Level

| Capital tier | Recommended entry point | Key resources needed |
|---|---|---|
| **Low (UGX 5–20M)** | Seed cutting multiplication (CSE model); fresh root trading; sun-dried chips | Clean cuttings from NARO; drying space; simple tools; transport access |
| **Medium (UGX 20–100M)** | Small HQCF mill; chipping + drying operation; gari processing | Chipper, dryer, mill; reliable root supply contract; UNBS registration |
| **Medium-high (UGX 100–500M)** | Mechanised production on leased/owned land; HQCF processing plant with bakery link | Land; tractor hire or ownership; processing plant; market contracts |
| **High (UGX 500M+)** | Integrated production + processing + marketing; cassava starch plant; peel feed operation | Full machinery set; storage; processing infrastructure; off-take agreements |

### Critical Success Factors

1. **Disease-free planting material** — sourcing certified NASE 14 cuttings is the single highest-return investment a new grower can make
2. **Processing within 24 hours of harvest** — fresh cassava perishes rapidly; proximity between field and processor is essential
3. **Consistent market linkage** — negotiate off-take agreements with processors or bakeries before scaling production
4. **Reliable electricity** for HQCF processing — uninterrupted power supply is required for mechanised drying and milling
5. **HACCP/quality compliance** — UNBS registration and food safety systems required to access institutional buyers and formal retailers
6. **Mechanisation viability** — appropriate for medium-scale (10+ acres) with access to tractor hire or cooperative ownership; assess soil type (stony/poor drainage = mechanisation risk)

### Regulatory Framework (Uganda)

| Requirement | Responsible body | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Seed certification | NSCS (National Seed Certification Service) | Required for commercial cutting sales |
| Food safety/HQCF quality | UNBS (Uganda National Bureau of Standards) | HQCF quality specs must be met for formal buyers |
| Processing premises licence | KCCA/District Local Government | Food premises certificate required |
| Tax registration | URA | VAT threshold UGX 150M; EFRIS compliance |
| Environmental permit | NEMA | Required for processing plants above threshold scale |
| Agricultural inputs dealer | MAAIF | For seed/cutting commercial vendors |
