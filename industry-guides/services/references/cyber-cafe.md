## Operations

- **Computer spec:** Windows 7 remains functional for most use cases (browsing, printing, eCitizen); upgrading to Windows 10 prevents compatibility issues with modern websites
- **Internet provider:** Zuku, Safaricom, Faiba — compare reliability over price; unreliable internet destroys the business instantly; budget KES 4,500/month ($54) for 20 Mbps
- **Power backup:** UPS for each workstation prevents data loss and brownie points from customers
- **Printer choice:** A quality laser printer (Konica Minolta, Ricoh, HP) handles student-level printing volume; inkjet printers fail quickly under heavy use
- **Revenue tracking:** Each session logged — cash management discipline prevents staff theft (a real risk in cyber cafés)
- **eCitizen premium:** Non-tech-savvy users (farmers, elderly, first-time users) will pay KES 50–200 for a staff member to navigate government services on their behalf — document this as a service line

## Market Outlook

The cyber café sector declined sharply with smartphone proliferation (2011–2014), then partially recovered due to e-government, college growth, and gambling. **Medium-term risk:** Further smartphone penetration and improved mobile data will continue eroding browsing revenue. The cafés best positioned for 2026+ are those that pivot to a service bureau model (printing, binding, scanning, eCitizen, photocopying) rather than pure browsing.

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Cyber cafés still common in Kampala and upcountry towns. NIRA digital registration, URSB (company registration), URA tax services (TIN), and NSSF registration are all accessible through eCitizen-equivalent portals — all generating cyber café demand. Kampala district licensing required. Bandwidth: MTN, Airtel, Liquid Telecom serve most areas.
- **Tanzania:** Cyber cafés active across Dar es Salaam, Arusha, Mwanza, and Dodoma. e-Government portal adoption is growing but slower than Kenya. RITA, TRA, and immigration services are key demand drivers. TCRA business licence required.
- **Rwanda:** Kigali's high smartphone penetration and reliable mobile data (MTN, Airtel) have reduced browsing demand more aggressively than other EA cities. However, RDB (company registration), RRA (tax), RSSB (social security), and immigration services create ongoing demand for assisted digital services in lower-income areas. Secondary towns retain stronger cyber café viability.
- **Gambling note:** Online sports betting is a significant revenue driver in Kenya and Uganda. Its inclusion in a café's service mix is commercially important but carries reputational risk in conservative communities. Assess local community context.

## Data Quality Notes

Computer cost of KES 30,000 ($360) each reflects 2017 Nairobi pricing for a basic business-spec desktop; current pricing may vary. Start with fewer, higher-quality machines rather than more, lower-quality ones — reliability is the customer's primary concern.
