# Cooking Oil Filtering Service

**Source:** Crack A Business Kenya (CAB) — A Brief Look at the Feasibility of a Cooking Oil Filtering Service, 2014 | **Sub-sector:** B2B Services — Food Safety & Cost Reduction for Restaurants
**Conversion:** KES ÷ 106 × 1.27 → March 2026 USD

> A mobile B2B service visiting fast-food restaurants and cafés to filter their used cooking oil using a VITO-brand portable filter. Oil filtering removes food particles and oxidation by-products, extending oil life by 20–40% per filtering cycle. Service provider charges per session or per litre; main cost is transport. Market is constrained by slow adoption — demonstration and trust-building are essential.

## The Business Model

### Revenue Logic

1. Restaurant uses cooking oil for deep frying; oil degrades with heat, food particles, oxidation
2. After filtering, oil is reusable for 3–5 additional cycles vs. 1–3 without filtering
3. Oil savings to restaurant: KES 24–80/litre at KES 120–200/litre oil price (20–40% saving)
4. Service provider charges less than the saving — typically KES 25–30/litre, or KES 150/session
5. Net benefit to restaurant: positive (reduced oil cost); net benefit to provider: transport + return on filter investment

### Per-Session Model (Recommended)

- Charge per restaurant visit rather than per litre
- Example: KES 150/session whether filtering 5 or 10 litres → makes pricing simple and attractive
- Multiple restaurants in a cluster reduce transport cost per visit → route efficiency is key

### Breakeven Example (VITO50 at KES 197,200 / $2,363)

- If charging KES 150/restaurant/visit × 3 visits/week × 20 restaurants = KES 9,000/week
- Less transport costs (estimate KES 1,000–2,000/day) = net KES 3,500–5,500/week
- Filter investment recovered in ~40–55 weeks (10–14 months) — acceptable for a profitable niche service

## Market Reality Check

**Barriers to adoption:**
1. **Awareness gap** — many restaurant owners do not know filtering machines exist
2. **Scepticism** — some owners doubt effectiveness without seeing evidence
3. **Reluctance to spend** — even where benefit is clear, spending on a "new" service is resisted
4. **Alternative filtering** — some restaurants already filter manually with sieves or cloth (less effective but free)

**Adoption accelerators:**
- Free or discounted first demonstration (let the restaurant see the filter in action)
- Present savings in simple terms: "This will save you KES X per month on cooking oil"
- Combine with fryer cleaning service — adds value and increases stickiness of the relationship
- Professional branding (coat, business cards, motorcycle branding) builds credibility

## Operations

- **Filter maintenance:** Clean filter thoroughly after each use; no significant recurring cost; VITO comes with 2-year warranty
- **Electricity:** Filter uses restaurant's power supply — no operating electricity cost to provider
- **Employee management:** If hiring someone to operate the route — set a daily minimum revenue target; employee keeps anything above it (incentivises client acquisition). Consider commission model vs. retainer.
- **Route design:** Cluster restaurants geographically to minimise transport time and cost per client

## Feasibility Assessment

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| Market exists | Yes — fast-food sector is large and growing |
| Problem is real | Yes — oil cost is 15–25% of production costs for deep-frying restaurants |
| Solution works | Yes — VITO is an established global brand |
| Adoption speed | Slow — requires demonstration and persistent sales |
| Competition | Very low — pioneer opportunity in most EA markets |
| Exit risk | Restaurants may eventually buy own filters; plan for this |
| Capital requirement | Moderate ($2,000–$3,000) — accessible for a small entrepreneur |

**Verdict:** Viable, but success depends on: (1) finding a dense enough cluster of suitable restaurants, (2) persistent sales and demonstration effort, and (3) honest assessment of the market before committing capital.

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Rolex (egg and chapati roll) culture and deep-fried street food are ubiquitous in Kampala. Fryer-intensive quick-service restaurants in Kikuubo, Nakawa, and downtown Kampala are a primary target. KCCA health certificate required. Awareness of oil filters is even lower than Kenya — genuine pioneer advantage available.
- **Tanzania:** Mandazi, samosa, and chipsi (chips) shops line the streets of Dar es Salaam and Mwanza. TBS food safety standards apply. Similar adoption barrier — most operators have never seen a commercial oil filter. Demonstration-led sales are essential.
- **Rwanda:** Kigali's growing fast-food sector (Chicken Palace, KFC, local equivalents) is a target. Rwanda's culture of formal business compliance means operators are more receptive to documented cost-saving services than in other EA markets.
- **Pricing in UGX/TZS/RWF:** Convert KES pricing at prevailing exchange rates; key principle is that charge must be less than oil savings generated for the restaurant.

## Data Quality Notes

VITO filter prices are 2014 Kenya data (from ASL / Ramco Group); current prices will differ — verify with local distributors before purchasing. The per-litre and per-session pricing models are illustrative; actual viable rates depend on local oil prices and restaurant oil usage. Oil price of KES 120/litre ($1.44) used in examples is low — current prices may be higher, making the service more compelling to restaurant owners.
