# SACCOs and CHAMAs: Cooperative Finance for Business

**Source:** Mirieri, T. — SACCO & Chama Business Explained (self-published, undated, ~2015–2016) | **Sub-sector:** Services — Cooperative Finance / Member Financing
**Note:** This is not a CAB Kenya guide. It is an educational reference on cooperative financing structures relevant to business capital planning, financial projections, and funding strategy in the East African context.

> SACCOs (Savings and Credit Cooperative Organisations) and CHAMAs (informal investment groups) are the dominant alternative financing vehicles for businesses and individuals in East Africa who cannot access commercial bank credit. SACCOs offer loans at 12% per annum flat rate (1%/month) — significantly below commercial bank rates of 18–30%+. This reference summarises SACCO/CHAMA mechanics relevant to business plan funding strategy.

## What Is a SACCO?

A Savings and Credit Cooperative Organisation (SACCO) is a member-owned, member-governed financial institution registered under the Ministry of Cooperative Development & Marketing (Kenya) or equivalent national authority.

**Core mechanics:**
- Members contribute a fixed monthly amount (shares)
- SACCO aggregates member savings and lends to members at below-market rates
- Surplus income is returned to members as dividends (typical 10–15% per annum)
- Loan limit: typically 3–4× the member's accumulated savings (some SACCOs up to 8×)
- Loan interest: standard 12% per annum flat (1%/month) — does not vary with central bank rate
- Collateral: guarantors from other members, or member's own shares

**Kenya regulatory context:**
- Non-deposit taking SACCOs: governed by Cooperative Services Act, CAP 490
- Deposit-taking SACCOs (DTS): licensed by SACCO Societies Regulatory Authority (SASRA) under SACCO Societies Act 2008
- By 2016: 215 deposit-taking SACCOs; 6,000+ registered non-deposit-taking SACCOs
- 215 DTS account for 78% of total SACCO sector assets and 82% of membership

## SACCO vs Commercial Bank Comparison

| Feature | SACCO | Commercial Bank |
|---------|-------|----------------|
| Interest rate (loans) | 12% per annum (flat) | 18–30%+ per annum |
| Rate variability | Fixed; rarely changes | Changes with CBK/central bank rate |
| Ownership | Members (equal voting rights) | Private shareholders |
| Governance | Elected board from members | Board elected by shareholders (vote by share count) |
| Maximum single-member ownership | 25% of shares | Unlimited |
| Collateral | Member guarantors + shares | Property, securities |
| Dividends | 10–15% on savings | None (savings account rate ≠ dividend) |
| Profit distribution | All members pro-rata | Shareholders only |
| Approval process | Faster; member-knows-member | Stricter; credit scoring |

## FOSA (Front Office Services Activities)

The banking-like arm of a deposit-taking SACCO:
- Over-the-counter cash withdrawals
- ATM access
- Mobile money integration
- Salary processing
- School fees loans, emergency loans (processed same day)

FOSA services make deposit-taking SACCOs nearly equivalent to community banks for members' daily banking needs.

## SACCO Loan Products

| Product | Key Feature |
|---------|-------------|
| Development loan | Long-term; for capital investment; 3–4× savings guarantee |
| School fees loan | Short-term; processed quickly |
| Emergency loan | Same-day processing; small amounts |
| Normal/regular loan | Standard product; requires guarantors |

> A member saving KES 50,000 over 10 months qualifies for a KES 200,000 loan at 1%/month flat rate. To purchase land or expand a business, a SACCO loan at 12% flat is substantially cheaper than a bank at 24%+ on reducing balance.

## Business Plan Application: Using SACCOs for Capital

| Scenario | Application |
|----------|-------------|
| Working capital | SACCO emergency/short-term loan covers 1–3 month working capital needs |
| Equipment purchase | Development loan (3–4× savings) for machinery or vehicle |
| Business startup | Accumulated SACCO savings used as equity contribution; supplement with SACCO loan |
| Property/premises | Some SACCOs participate in land purchase schemes for members |
| Reduce bank dependency | Use SACCO loan to refinance expensive commercial bank debt |

**Planning guideline:** If a business plan includes a SACCO loan as part of the funding mix, show: (1) the member's current accumulated savings amount; (2) the loan multiple; (3) the repayment schedule at 1%/month flat; and (4) how the loan repayment fits within projected cash flows. Compare explicitly to commercial bank alternative cost.

## What Is a CHAMA?

A CHAMA is an informal investment/savings group — typically 5–30 members who contribute a fixed amount monthly and collectively invest or rotate the lump sum to each member in turn.

**Common CHAMA models:**
1. **Merry-go-round (rotating credit):** Monthly contribution e.g. KES 5,000 × 12 members = KES 60,000 pool rotated to one member each month
2. **Investment CHAMA:** Pool contributions; invest collectively (real estate, shares, business equity)
3. **Table banking:** Lend pooled funds to members at interest; interest income grows the pool

**CHAMA vs SACCO:**
- CHAMA is informal (no formal registration required; can register voluntarily under Societies Act)
- SACCO is formally registered and regulated
- CHAMAs transition to formal SACCOs when they grow in size and asset base

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** The Uganda Cooperative Savings and Credit Union (UCSCU) represents the SACCO sector. Uganda has over 2,000 registered SACCOs. SACCO supervision: Ministry of Trade, Industry and Cooperatives (for non-regulated) and Uganda Microfinance Regulatory Authority (UMRA) for regulated microfinance institutions. Standard loan rate: ~18–24% per annum (higher than Kenya). Uganda's Mobile SACCO products (MTN Mobile Money integrated) are growing. CHAMAs (called "groups" or "rounds" locally) are pervasive especially for women's groups and rotating credit circles (merry-go-round). Uganda Development Bank (UDB) offers SME development loans at subsidised rates — relevant for business plans targeting formal SME funding.
- **Tanzania:** Tanzania's SACCO sector is regulated by the Tanzania Cooperative Development Commission (TCDC). Village Community Banks (VICOBAs) are the informal equivalent of CHAMAs and are widespread in rural Tanzania. Urban SACCOs are growing in Dar es Salaam, Arusha, and Mwanza. Standard SACCO loan rate in Tanzania: approximately 12–18% per annum. Tanzania's SME lending is also available through CRDB Bank's Saccos programme and NMB Bank. Vodacom M-Pesa and Airtel Money integrate with SACCO savings products. The informal group lending model (VICOBA, SILC) is the dominant financing mechanism for rural and semi-urban businesses.
- **Uganda (specific to business plans):** The Uganda Revenue Authority (URA) requires tax compliance for any business taking loans > certain thresholds. Savings in a Uganda SACCO are exempt from withholding tax. SACCOs can issue members' statements that serve as evidence of savings track record — useful for supplementary collateral when approaching formal lenders.
- **Rwanda:** Rwanda's Umurenge SACCOs (village-level SACCOs) are a government initiative creating at least one SACCO per administrative sector (there are 416 Umurenge sectors). These are deposit-taking institutions with affordable loan products. Rwanda Cooperative Agency (RCA) regulates the sector. Standard SACCO loan rate: approximately 18–24% per annum. Rwanda's financial inclusion is high (>90% of adults have a financial account — primarily MTN Mobile Money and bank). The Umurenge SACCO network means virtually every Rwandan can access cooperative finance. Business plans in Rwanda should reference Umurenge SACCO as a potential equity partner or working capital source.
- **EA general:** SACCOs and CHAMAs are structurally important across all EA markets as the primary source of affordable credit for SMEs and individuals outside the formal banking system. In a business plan, showing that the owner is a SACCO member with accumulated savings demonstrates financial discipline and provides a credible alternative funding source. Loan costs: Kenya SACCO (~12% p.a. flat) < Uganda SACCO (~18–24%) < Tanzania SACCO (~12–18%) < Rwanda SACCO (~18–24%) < Commercial bank (18–30%+ in all markets). For EA business plans, always model at least one scenario where SACCO/cooperative financing supplements commercial bank debt to reduce overall cost of capital.

## Data Quality Notes

Membership statistics (215 DTS SACCOs, 6,000+ non-deposit taking SACCOs) are from 2015–2016 Kenya data. The SACCO sector has grown since — verify current SASRA-published figures for Kenya. For Uganda, Tanzania, and Rwanda, regulatory details should be verified with respective national cooperative authorities. The 12% flat rate for Kenya SACCOs is the established benchmark but individual SACCOs may vary. This guide is educational rather than operational — it does not include financial data for running a SACCO as a business; consult SASRA directly for SACCO licensing and capital requirements.
