# Beauty Salon & Barber Shop

**Sources:** UNDP Uganda Compendium Vol. 2 (adjusted ×1.27 to March 2026 USD); Crack A Business Kenya (CAB) — Hair Salon Plan 2013; Executive Barber Shop Guide 2018 | **Sub-sector:** Personal Care Services

> Covers two distinct but related personal care businesses: (1) women's hair salons offering styling, chemical treatments, and beauty extras; and (2) executive barber shops offering premium men's grooming. Both serve urban and peri-urban markets; both depend on service quality and stylist loyalty above all else.

### Revenue Dynamics

- Career (salaried) women visit twice/month — double the frequency of informally employed clients; their patronage drives above-average revenue
- Salons offering manicure, pedicure, facials earn ~14% extra revenue from beauty services
- Salons selling weaves, wigs, and hair pieces earn higher revenue — only viable if variety, quality, and price are competitive with specialist shops
- Differential pricing (+/– KES 50–200 from standard) based on service complexity is common and accepted

| Metric | Figure |

| Average salons per estate | 14 |

- Loyalty is very high — customers follow stylists, not salons; poaching a popular stylist with a loyal following is the fastest market-entry shortcut
- Breakeven is extended where loyalty to existing stylists is strong

### Manpower

- Average employees: 3
- Salary: KES 8,000/month ($96) + 30% commission on services
- Formal training (hair schools) vs. apprenticeship — best salons blend both

### Revenue Case Studies

**Case Study 1 (integrated shop with gym and spa):**
- Equipment: zero-gravity massage chair, barber chairs, sink seats, TV, music, CCTV, Wi-Fi
- Customers: ~40/day
- Revenue: KES 7,000–12,000/day ($84–$144)
- Barbers: basic salary KES 9,000 + commission

**Case Study 2 (managed barber shop + salon):**
- Equipment: clippers, sterilizer, barber chairs, TV, music system
- Revenue: KES 15,000/day average ($180)
- Staff: 3 barbers, salary KES 12,000 + commission

### Consumer Behaviour

- Customers visit on average once/month; twice/month for appearance-conscious professionals
- Primary choice drivers: service quality → barber skill → cleanliness → location → price
- Sterilizer visible to customers is a strong hygiene and trust signal

## Shared Notes (Salons and Barber Shops)

- **The critical success factor is service** — location, facilities, and price are secondary; poor service guarantees failure regardless of the setup
- **Loyalty is the core business asset** — customers follow individual stylists/barbers; turnover of key staff is the biggest operational risk
- **Breakeven capital is the most common failure cause** — shops close not from lack of customers but from running out of cash before reaching sustainable enrolment

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** KCCA trade licence required in Kampala; health certificate from district health office. Movit and Hair Gain are leading local hair product brands carried in most salons. Nkumba, Arua Park, and Kikuubo are key wholesale sources in Kampala. Men's barbershop culture is strong across Kampala and district towns.
- **Tanzania:** TBS and TFDA regulate cosmetics used in salons. Dar es Salaam has a large Indian-influenced salon market with threading, henna services, and braiding as distinct service lines. BRELA business registration required.
- **Rwanda:** RFDA regulates cosmetics and salon products. Kigali's Kimihurura and Remera have active premium salon markets serving expat and NGO communities alongside Rwandan middle class. RDB business registration required.
- **Hair product sourcing:** SACCOs and rotating credit groups commonly used by salon owners to purchase initial stock of weaves, relaxers, and hair foods — a key financing mechanism where bank loans are inaccessible.

## Data Quality Notes

Hair salon revenue data is from CAB Kenya 2013 survey (Nairobi-centric). Executive barber shop data is from CAB Kenya 2018 survey. UNDP capital figure ($16,050) reflects a more structured setup. Actual revenue varies sharply by estate income level — adjust pricing benchmarks to local purchasing power before use.
