# FMCG Small-Scale Distribution

**Source:** Crack A Business Kenya (CAB) — FMCG Small Scale Distribution Quick Guide | **Sub-sector:** Wholesale & Distribution — Fast-Moving Consumer Goods
**Conversion:** KES ÷ 106 × 1.27 → March 2026 USD

> A distributor/small wholesaler purchasing FMCG products from manufacturers, large distributors, or importers and selling to retail shops (kiosks, dukas, supermarkets) in a defined geographic area. Two operating models: informal (mobile, low overhead) and formal (premises-based). Margins are thin — volumes must be high. Success depends on product selection, location density, and reliable supply.

## Financial Summary

| Metric | Amount |
|--------|--------|
| Minimum entry capital (informal, single product) | From KES 1,000 ($12) |
| Standard informal setup (small wholesale) | KES 10,000–50,000 ($120–$599) |
| Formal setup (premises, licences, stock) | KES 50,000–200,000+ ($599–$2,396+) |
| Margins on FMCG products | KES 5–50/unit (5–20% depending on product) |
| Transport requirement | Bicycle/foot (dense areas), motorcycle, or vehicle |

## Operating Models

### Model 1 — Informal (Mobile Distributor)

- No fixed premises
- Purchases from large wholesalers or distributors, loads on a bicycle/motorcycle/vehicle
- Goes door-to-door to retail shops
- **Advantages:** Low overhead; flexible; can cover wide area; cash terms more common (retailers treat informals with less credit pressure)
- **Disadvantages:** Limited inventory size; harder to build corporate supplier relationships; retailers sometimes treat informals less professionally
- **Best for:** Starting up; testing a new product; markets where premises are expensive; areas with dense shop concentration accessible on foot

### Model 2 — Formal (Premises-Based Wholesaler)

- Fixed location (shop, warehouse, or room with access)
- Retailers come to you; you also do delivery
- **Advantages:** Immediate visibility and credibility; retailers pay cash more readily; easier to manage variety of products; builds permanent presence in the market
- **Disadvantages:** Rent + renovation costs; licences; may become "lazy" (waiting for customers instead of going out)
- **Best for:** Growth stage after informal model proves product-market fit; locations with no established formal wholesaler in radius

## Choosing a Product to Distribute

### Product Gap Analysis

| Gap Type | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Demographic gap | New population segment not yet served | Congolese community using tomato paste in an area without it → one entrepreneur spotted this and built a niche |
| Knowledge gap | Product doing well elsewhere, unknown locally | KDF cakes spreading from Nairobi estates to other towns via distributors |
| Supply gap | Product needed but supplier disappeared | Manufacturer cancels contract; existing stock runs out; retailer is desperate for supply |
| New product introduction | Early-mover advantage before market floods | Introducing a new bread brand or new packaged snack before competitors arrive |

### Product Selection Checklist

- Is there a gap (demand exceeding supply) in the target location?
- What capital is needed? Some products require KES 1,000 to start; others KES 10,000+
- How perishable is the product? (Bread and milk require daily delivery discipline; dry goods are more forgiving)
- What is the margin? (KES 5/unit means you need 200 units to make KES 1,000 — volume is essential)
- Are there already many distributors of this product in the area? (Saturated = margin squeeze)
- Can you break bulk? (Supplier sells you half a carton vs. full carton — important for low-capital entry)

## Location & Market Selection

### Key Principles

- **Population density is paramount** — FMCG margins are thin; you need many shops and high volume to profit
- **10 shops is not enough** — target areas with dozens to hundreds of retail shops accessible in your route
- **Low-income areas → price-over-brand** — in Huruma or Mukuru, a cheaper brand will outsell Brookside milk even with less brand recognition; opposite is true in Kilimani or Lavington
- **Income match to product** — match the product's price point to the purchasing power of the area
- **Competition assessment** — presence of successful distributors signals a working market; too many distributors = squeezed margins

### Location Decision Framework

1. Discover market → find product that fits
2. Or: find product → find market where it sells

Both work. Do not commit capital before visiting the target area and talking to 5–10 shop owners.

## Supplier Relationships

- **Price matters most** — even small differences per unit matter at volume
- **Reliability matters equally** — if a supplier runs dry, your retailer clients lose faith in you
- **Break-bulk flexibility** — suppliers who sell partial cartons/cases are better for low-capital distributors
- **Terms** — most FMCG suppliers require cash upfront; bread and milk companies offer short credit (1–3 days) due to perishability

### Supplier Source Options

- Large wholesale distributors (e.g., Nakumatt, Metro, independent Nairobi wholesalers)
- Directly from manufacturer or importer (possible at higher volumes; better prices)
- Secondary distributors (easier to access; slightly higher prices but more flexibility)

## Revenue & Margins

| Product Category | Typical Margin | Notes |
|----------------|---------------|-------|
| Bread | KES 3–8/loaf | High volume; perishable; daily cycle; on-credit from baker |
| Milk | KES 2–5/litre | Daily cycle; 1-day credit; perishable |
| Mandazi/KDF cakes | KES 5–15/unit | Cash; high turnover in low-income areas |
| Packaged snacks/crisps | KES 5–20/pack | Good margins; non-perishable; flexible delivery |
| Tomato paste, cooking oil | KES 10–40/unit | Niche but loyal market if you source correctly |
| Soap, detergent | KES 5–30/unit | Steady demand; non-perishable |

**Volume required to earn KES 5,000/day net** (example at KES 10 margin/unit): 500 units → requires ~50 shops purchasing 10 units each.

## Critical Success Factors

1. **Know your margins before starting** — calculate break-even units per day
2. **Build before you scale** — start with one product in one area; expand when it works
3. **Reliability wins** — retailers switch distributors for 2 things: better price or more reliable supply; be both
4. **Transport efficiency** — group deliveries geographically; minimize backtracking
5. **Credit discipline** — never let retailer credit exceed what you can absorb; establish limits early

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Ugandan FMCG distribution follows a similar informal-to-formal progression. Key wholesale hubs: Industrial Area Kampala, Kikuubo Trading Centre (largest informal wholesale market in EA). MTN Mobile Money and Airtel Money widely used for cash payment — reduces credit risk. KCCA trade licence required for formal setup.
- **Tanzania:** Kariakoo Market, Dar es Salaam is the equivalent of Nairobi's Industrial Area — the main FMCG wholesale hub. Strong Swahili-language branding preference in product selection. BRELA registration and TRA tax PIN required for formal distributors.
- **Rwanda:** Rwanda's formal retail sector is growing faster than Kenya or Uganda — supermarkets (Nakumatt, Simba) and convenience stores have a larger share. Small-scale FMCG distribution is more viable in secondary towns (Huye, Musanze, Rubavu) where formal retail is limited. RRA tax compliance is enforced more strictly than in other EA markets.
- **EA general:** Boda bodas (motorcycle taxis/couriers) are increasingly used by FMCG distributors for last-mile delivery in dense urban areas — reduces vehicle capital investment while maintaining delivery frequency.
