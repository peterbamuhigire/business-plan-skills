# Informal Money Lending

**Source:** Crack A Business Kenya (CAB) — Informal Money Lending Guide 2016 | **Sub-sector:** Financial Services — Informal Credit Provision
**Conversion:** KES ÷ 106 × 1.27 → March 2026 USD

> Informal money lending operates outside the formal banking system — legal as long as the lender does not take deposits (which would require licensing under the Banking Act and Microfinance Act 2006). Regulated only by the Law of Contract (Chapter 23, Laws of Kenya). Multiple models exist from shylock lending (electronics collateral) to salary advance, working capital loans, and emerging mobile/platform lending. Interest rates typically 10–30%/month.

## Legal Framework (Kenya)

| Law | Application |
|-----|-------------|
| Banking Act | Applies only if taking deposits — does not apply to pure lenders |
| Microfinance Act 2006 | Applies only to deposit-taking microfinance institutions |
| Law of Contract (Chapter 23, Laws of Kenya) | Governs lender–borrower relationship — a written, signed contract is legally enforceable |
| Interest-free zone | No legal maximum interest rate for informal lenders in Kenya |

> **Key point:** A lender who does not take deposits operates legally using standard contract law. The contract between borrower and lender is enforceable in court if written and witnessed.

## Business Registration

| Item | KES | 2026 USD | Notes |

## Loan Types

| Loan Type | Description | Typical Security |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Salary advance | Short-term loan to salaried employees; repaid from next salary | Employer letter; payslip |
| Working capital | Loan to small businesses for restocking or operations | Business assets; post-dated cheques |
| General personal | Short-term personal loan | Varies |
| Shylock (electronics) | Loan secured against deposited electronics | Electronics (laptop, phone, TV) held by lender |
| Mobile loans | App-based (Branch, Tala/Saida) | Phone credit score; no physical collateral |
| Invoice discounting | Short advance on unpaid invoices | Invoice and client relationship |
| Logbook | Loan secured against vehicle logbook | Vehicle logbook |
| Corporate | Larger loans to businesses | Business assets; directors' guarantees |

## The Five Cs of Credit Assessment

| C | What It Means |
|---|---------------|
| **Character** | History of meeting obligations; personal reputation; references |
| **Capacity** | Ability to repay: income, cash flow, existing debts |
| **Collateral** | Assets available to secure the loan |
| **Credit Score** | Track record with previous lenders (formal or informal) |
| **Capital** | The borrower's own stake/investment in the situation |

## Loan Structuring

### Secured Loans (Collateral-Based)

- Collateral value sets the maximum loan; typical advance: 30–50% of collateral value
- **Shylock example:** Laptop worth KES 25,000 → loan KES 8,000 at 30%/month
  - If borrower repays in month 1: borrower pays KES 10,400 ($125); lender earns KES 2,400 ($29) in 1 month
  - If borrower defaults: lender sells laptop at KES 25,000 and recovers more than advanced
- Collateral physically held by lender during loan period (electronics, logbooks)

### Unsecured Loans

- Higher risk; mitigated by strong character/capacity assessment
- Salary advance model: deduction from employer payroll reduces default risk
- Mobile app model: proprietary credit scoring from phone behavioural data

## Documented Business Models

### Kopokopo Grow (Lipa na M-Pesa backed)
- Loan based on mobile money (M-Pesa / MTN Mobile Money / Airtel Money) transaction history
- Repayments deducted automatically from mobile money transactions
- Borrower's M-Pesa / mobile money account acts as the cash flow indicator

### Rongai Street Vendor Loans
- Loans of KES 1,000–3,000 to informal traders
- Interest rate: 15%/month
- High volume, small ticket, relationship-based
- Repayment collected daily or weekly at trader's stall

### Tala (App-Based Platform)
- $20M loaned at time of study; 90% repayment rate
- 11% interest rate per loan cycle
- Credit scoring based on phone data (calls, SMS, app usage patterns)
- No physical collateral; no human relationship required

### Okoa Mia (USSD-Based)
- KES 100 fixed advance via USSD
- 5-day repayment window
- Ultra-micro segment; airtime/USSD channel

### Pesapata (Peer-to-Peer Platform)
- Connects individual lenders with verified borrowers
- Platform takes a fee; lender sets terms

## Interest Rates Reference

| Model | Rate |
|-------|------|
| Shylock (electronics collateral) | 30%/month |
| Rongai street vendors | 15%/month |
| Tala app | 11% per cycle |
| Salary advance (typical informal) | 10–20%/month |
| Working capital (business loan) | 10–30%/month |

> There is no legal maximum for informal lenders. Rates are set by market convention and borrower negotiating power. Competition is the primary rate moderator.

## Risk Management

| Risk | Mitigation |
|------|------------|
| Default — unsecured | Strong character assessment; start with small amounts; build up |
| Default — secured | Hold physical collateral; value conservatively; ensure title/ownership verified |
| Fraud | Verify identity documents; physical collateral inspection; references |
| Legal dispute | Written, signed contract; witnesses; clear repayment schedule in writing |
| Borrower insolvency | Diversify portfolio; avoid concentration in one borrower or sector |

> **Written contract is essential.** The Law of Contract makes verbal agreements harder to enforce; a signed agreement with a witness makes recovery through court or pressure viable.

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Informal lending is widespread and poorly regulated at micro level. The Tier 4 Microfinance Institutions and Money Lenders Act 2016 requires registration with the Uganda Microfinance Regulatory Authority (UMRA) for any person or entity lending money as a business. Unlike Kenya, Uganda requires a money lender's licence even for informal non-deposit-takers — verify current UMRA requirements before operating. Interest rates: 10–30%/month informal. MTN Mobile Money and Airtel Money data are increasingly used by Ugandan fintechs for credit scoring (Jumo, Numida, Asaak). SACCOs (Savings and Credit Cooperatives) are a major credit provider in Uganda and are worth understanding as competitive reference.
- **Tanzania:** Bank of Tanzania (BOT) and the National Microfinance Bank (NMB) regulate the formal end. Tanzania's Microfinance Act 2018 distinguishes between Tier 1 (banks), Tier 2 (deposit-taking MFIs), and Tier 3/4 (non-deposit-taking lenders and SACCOs). Tier 3 non-deposit lenders require registration with the BOT. Vodacom M-Pesa, Tigo Pesa (now Miitel), and Airtel Money are the main mobile money platforms in Tanzania — mobile money transaction histories are used by Tanzanian fintechs for credit scoring. BRELA registration plus BOT Tier 3 compliance required.
- **Rwanda:** National Bank of Rwanda (BNR) regulates credit institutions. Rwanda's Microfinance Institutions Law applies to deposit-takers; non-deposit informal lenders operate under contract law but RDB registration is standard. Rwanda's SACCO network (Umurenge SACCOs, covering every administrative sector) is a major credit provider to rural populations. Mobile money: MTN Mobile Money (dominant), Airtel Money, and Bank of Kigali's BK TecHouse app are the primary mobile financial platforms. Loan interest rates are lower in Rwanda due to stronger regulation and market competition.
- **EA mobile money credit note (per user instruction):** Where the Kenya guide references M-Pesa for loan repayment or cash flow tracking, the EA equivalents are: Uganda — MTN Mobile Money (primary), Airtel Money (secondary); Tanzania — Vodacom M-Pesa, Miitel (formerly Tigo Pesa), Airtel Money; Rwanda — MTN Mobile Money (dominant), Airtel Money. All platforms offer merchant and agent interfaces that can be used for collecting loan repayments and verifying borrower transaction history.

## Data Quality Notes

This guide covers Kenya's regulatory environment as of 2016. The legal framework (Banking Act, Microfinance Act, Law of Contract) remains structurally accurate for Kenya, but verify current positions with a local lawyer before operating at scale. Interest rates (10–30%/month) are market observations, not prescriptive — actual rates depend on competition, borrower risk, and collateral quality. Platform data (Tala, Kopokopo) reflects 2015–2016 conditions; the mobile lending landscape has changed significantly since.
