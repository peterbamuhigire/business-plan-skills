## Licensing Framework (Kenya)

| Requirement | Detail | 2026 USD |
|------------|--------|----------|
| PPB Licence to Operate | Must be held by a registered pharmacist or enrolled pharmaceutical technologist | $180/year |
| PPB Premises Registration | PPB inspects and registers the physical premises | Included above |
| County Business Permit | Standard permit issued by county government | $120/year |
| Public Health Licence | Environmental inspection of premises | $36/year |
| "Licence rental" — monthly | Non-pharmacist pays a qualified professional to hold the licence | $180–$360/month |
| "Licence rental" — annual lump sum | Alternative arrangement; risk: renewal motivation | $1,199–$2,398/year |

> The Pharmacy and Poisons Act (Cap 244) requires all retail chemists to be superintended by a registered pharmacist or enrolled pharmaceutical technologist. Approximately 50%+ of retail chemists in Kenya operate without proper PPB registration. The PPB has ~80 inspectors nationwide — enforcement is inconsistent but raids do occur and can result in stock confiscation and prosecution.

## Operations

- **Competitive pricing:** Price differences of KES 5–300 for the same medicine between neighbouring shops are common — consumers eventually learn which shop has the best prices and concentrate purchases there
- **Stock strategy:** A stock of KES 200,000 ($2,398) is the baseline for a standard estate chemist; CBD or mall locations need KES 800,000+ ($9,585+) for sufficient variety
- **Pharmacist partnership:** Most partnerships are non-equity; negotiate annual renewal terms carefully — a one-off payment creates no renewal incentive. Equity partnership (pharmacist as co-owner) aligns interests long term
- **PPB inspections:** Inspectors are regional, not county-by-county. Months can pass between visits, but the risk is not zero — especially as business grows in visibility

## Market

Kenya had approximately 4,000 registered chemists and an estimated 5,000 unregistered in the CAB survey period. Kenya's healthcare retail market is growing with urbanisation, rising chronic disease burden (diabetes, hypertension), and growing health consciousness in the middle class. Estate chemists face intense price competition; institutional locations (hospital compounds, college campuses) command premium margins but require relationships to secure.

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Regulation under the National Drug Authority (NDA). A Responsible Pharmacist must hold the licence for any retail drug shop. "Drug shop" (limited OTC licence) and "pharmacy" (full prescription dispensing) are distinct licence categories. Kampala's lower-income estates (Bwaise, Nansana, Makindye) have dense, often unregistered pharmacy populations. NDA enforcement raids occur periodically.
- **Tanzania:** Tanzania Food and Drugs Authority (TFDA) regulates pharmaceutical retail. Retail drug stores classified as "pharmacy" (pharmacist required) or "duka la dawa baridi" (OTC only, lower qualification threshold). High informal sector in Dar es Salaam.
- **Rwanda:** Rwanda Food and Drugs Authority (RFDA) regulates. Pharmacist licence required; Rwanda has stricter enforcement than Kenya or Uganda. Kigali has a strong formal pharmacy sector; secondary towns have unmet demand. Pharmaceutical supply chain is better organised than Kenya — fewer fakes.
- **Licence rental model:** Exists across all EA countries with varying degrees of formality. In Uganda, it is explicitly illegal (NDA can prosecute both parties) but widely practised. In Rwanda, enforcement is stricter.
