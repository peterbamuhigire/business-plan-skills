Market is growing rapidly since 2013 driven by health consciousness and social pressure, but remains a small percentage (~4%) of Kenya's urban population. Capital is high; patience to breakeven is essential.

## Equipment Notes

- **Commercial vs. home equipment:** Always buy commercial-grade. Home gym equipment breaks down within weeks under daily multi-user load and becomes more expensive in the long run.
- **New vs. second-hand:** Second-hand from the US/Europe (via specialist importers) is widely considered good value — often better quality than cheap new Chinese imports at similar or lower price.
- **Flooring:** Rubber is the standard (half-inch or one-inch thickness). Budget KES 3,000–6,000/m² for branded commercial rubber; KES 1,000–1,500/m² for imported general-purpose rubber (less consistent supply). Flooring is non-negotiable — protects equipment, absorbs impact, prevents slip injuries.
- **Equipment prices are wide-ranging:** Treadmill: KES 80,000–400,000+; spin bike: KES 50,000–120,000; cable machine: KES 300,000. Compare multiple suppliers; ask for warranties.

## Operations

- **Opening hours:** 5am–9pm minimum; morning (5–8am) and evening (5–9pm) are peak times; earlier opening attracts pre-work exercisers — a significant competitive advantage
- **Membership model:** Monthly subscriptions dominate; daily access for casual customers
- **Sign-in book:** Essential for revenue tracking and membership management, especially for absentee owners
- **Changing rooms:** Mandatory — customers need to change before and after exercise
- **Aerobics classes:** Requires a qualified instructor; quality of instructor is the primary reason customers choose aerobics gyms

## Market Dynamics

- Only ~4% of Kenya's urban population currently uses gyms — growth potential is large
- Competition is intensifying rapidly in urban estates since 2013
- Gyms in higher-income areas or near corporate offices command higher membership fees
- Peri-urban towns near county headquarters are a growing opportunity (less competition, urbanising consumer base)

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** KCCA trade licence required in Kampala. Gym culture growing in Kampala estates (Ntinda, Kisaasi, Najjanankumbi) and Entebbe. Many gyms combine with juice bars or sports shops. Staff need NSSF registration. Affordable Chinese equipment widely available in Kampala's industrial area.
- **Tanzania:** Fitness culture growing in Dar es Salaam (Masaki, Mikocheni, Mbezi Beach) and Arusha. Some high-end gyms (Gymkhana, hotel gyms) operate in the formal sector; estate gyms are increasingly informal but growing. BRELA business registration required.
- **Rwanda:** Kigali's Kimihurura, Remera, and Kacyiru estates have active gym markets. Rwanda's strong regulatory environment means employment contracts and RSSB (social security) registration are more consistently enforced than in Kenya. Compact city geography means gyms draw from wider catchment areas than Nairobi equivalents.

## Data Quality Notes

Capital breakdown is from 2016 CAB Kenya survey using commercial-grade equipment. Actual costs vary widely — second-hand imports can reduce equipment cost by 30–50%. A smaller aerobics-only or strength-only gym can be started for KES 700,000–1,000,000 ($8,390–$11,981) by selectively purchasing only the required equipment category.
