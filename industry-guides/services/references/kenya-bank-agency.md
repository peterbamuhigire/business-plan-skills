# Bank Agency (Agent Banking)

**Source:** Crack A Business Kenya (CAB), 2017 | **Sub-sector:** Financial Services — Agency Banking
**Conversion:** Original KES figures at KES 106/USD (2020 base), adjusted ×1.27 for USD inflation → March 2026 USD

> A neighbourhood business licensed to offer banking services (deposits, withdrawals, account opening, balance enquiries) on behalf of a commercial bank, earning per-transaction commissions — a low-capital, cash-flow intensive model requiring an existing business as the anchor.

## Financial Summary

| Metric | Amount |
|--------|--------|
| Equity float deposit required | $1,200 |
| Agency application fee (to CBK) | $12 |
| Estimated daily commission income (100 transactions) | $14–$28 |
| Estimated monthly commission income | $430–$850 |
| Estimated annual commission income | $5,200–$10,200 |
| Additional capital required (cash float for withdrawals) | $500–$2,000 |

> Revenue is entirely commission-based. A busy agent near a market, estate, or institution conducting 100+ transactions/day achieves the upper range. Rural agents average 30–60 transactions/day.

> Commission rates vary by bank and are updated periodically. Family Bank, Cooperative, and KCB offer comparable structures. Equity leads in agent count and customer volume.

## Requirements to Qualify (Equity Bank, typical)

- Existing business operating for at least 12 months
- Strategic physical location with adequate foot traffic
- Valid business permit for the existing business
- 6 months' current account statements
- National ID and certificate of good conduct
- Credit Reference Bureau report (clean record)
- Companies/partnerships: articles of association + board resolution + audited financials

## Operations

- **Equipment provided by bank:** POS terminal, transaction registers, branding materials, agent sign
- **Bank provides training** on transaction processing, fraud prevention, and customer service
- **Float management:** Agent must maintain sufficient cash for withdrawals; shortfalls mean turning customers away — reputational damage
- **Transaction types:** Deposits, withdrawals, account opening, balance enquiries, airtime, utility payments, loan repayments
- **Working hours:** Typically same as host business (6 AM–9 PM for kiosks; 8 AM–5 PM for shops)

## Market

Agency banking was pioneered in Kenya from 2010 under CBK guidelines. It solved last-mile access for the unbanked and under-banked — particularly relevant across East Africa where formal bank branch penetration remains low outside cities. Preferred bank agencies serve markets, trading centres, residential estates, and areas with mobile money overlap. Mobile money (M-Pesa, MTN Mobile Money, Airtel Money) is complementary, not directly competitive — many customers use both based on specific needs.

## Critical Success Factors

1. **Location** — high foot traffic near market, bus stage, or residential gate
2. **Anchor business** — a well-run existing business (shop, pharmacy, M-Pesa outlet) legitimises the agent and drives walk-in traffic
3. **Float discipline** — agent who consistently runs out of cash loses customers permanently
4. **Preferred bank choice** — Equity and Cooperative command the largest customer bases in Kenya; M-Pesa integration is a major pull factor

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Bank of Uganda agent banking guidelines (2017) enabled similar model. Stanbic, Centenary, DFCU, and PostBank have active agent networks. Mobile money (MTN Mobile Money, Airtel Money) dominates rural areas but bank agency fills the formal banking gap. Capital City Authority (KCCA) trading licence required.
- **Tanzania:** Bank of Tanzania agent banking framework operational since 2013. CRDB, NMB, and Equity Tanzania have large agent networks. Heavily overlaps with M-Pesa and Tigo Pesa — successful agents offer both.
- **Rwanda:** BNR (National Bank of Rwanda) agent banking regulations active. Bank of Kigali, BPR, and Equity Rwanda have agent programmes. MoMo Pay (MTN) dominates informal payments.
- **All EA:** The agent model works best as a revenue add-on to an existing business (M-Pesa outlet, pharmacy, hardware shop) rather than as a standalone operation.
