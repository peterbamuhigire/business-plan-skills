# Mobile Money Sub-Agency

**Source:** Crack A Business Kenya (CAB) — Money & Mobile: A Report on the M-Pesa Sub Agent Business (2013) | **Sub-sector:** Services — Mobile Money Agency Operations
**Conversion:** KES ÷ 106 × 1.27 → March 2026 USD

> A business operating as a registered sub-agent for mobile money services (M-Pesa/MTN Mobile Money/Airtel Money), earning commissions on customer deposits and withdrawals. Entry requires float capital (minimum KES 30,000), goodwill to obtain a till (KES 20,000–100,000), and a working capital buffer. Average monthly commissions ~KES 11,000 per agent. The market is heavily saturated in urban Kenya (3 outlets per 50m radius). Profitability depends on float size and transaction volume; best operated as a complementary service to an existing retail business.

## Agency Structure (Kenya — M-Pesa)

### Three Tiers

| Level | Description | Float Requirement |
|-------|-------------|------------------|
| Super Agent | Banks and large retailers | KES 500,000+ per store |
| Agent / Aggregator | Recruits sub-agents; operates multiple tills | KES 100,000/store min |
| Sub-Agent | Retail outlet operating under an Agent; the business described in this guide | KES 30,000 official minimum |

### How to Obtain a Till

**Route 1 — Via Agent:** Approach a registered Agent; pay goodwill (KES 20,000–100,000, average KES 50,000). Sub-agent is either formally aggregated (registered directly with Safaricom) or left as a dependent of the Agent.

**Route 2 — Purchase from exiting sub-agent:** Buy a till from someone quitting; ensure transfer of registration to your name and sign a new contract with the Agent before proceeding.

> **Critical:** If you are NOT formally aggregated (i.e., not directly registered with Safaricom), all commissions pass through the Agent who takes 20–40% before paying you. The recommended official split is 80% to sub-agent / 20% to Agent, but in practice it is often 70/30 or 60/40. Push for formal aggregation where possible.

## Operations

### Float Management

- Sub-agent holds two types of float: **electronic cash** (M-Pesa/mobile money balance) and **hard cash**
- After a withdrawal: electronic cash increases, hard cash decreases
- After a deposit: hard cash increases, electronic cash decreases
- If electronic cash runs out: cannot accept further deposits — must top up by depositing hard cash to Agent/store management account
- If hard cash runs out: cannot pay out withdrawals — must rebalance by withdrawing from Agent account
- Banks (KCB, Equity, Cooperative) offer float management credit products to help maintain balance

### Daily Operations
- Serve walk-in customers; prioritise reliability of float above everything else
- Maintain float records; reconcile daily
- Never disclose PIN or agent ID to any caller claiming to be Safaricom/MTN/Airtel staff (primary fraud vector)
- Operate from a fixed, visible location; convenience/proximity is the primary customer acquisition factor

## Fraud Risks

| Fraud Type | Method | Prevention |
|------------|--------|------------|
| Fake Safaricom/MTN call | Caller claims to be staff; extracts PIN and agent ID; drains float | Never share PIN or ID by phone; all legitimate contact comes via official channels |
| Erroneous transfer scam | Fraudster sends money to customer; calls claiming it was an error; directs customer to agent for "reversal" — agent is manipulated into releasing float | Any reversal must go through official customer service number only |
| Fake currency | Large cash transactions include counterfeit notes | Use UV light/counterfeit detector for all notes above KES 500 |

## Competition and Survival

- Market is saturated in urban areas: 3+ outlets per 50m radius in most Nairobi estates and CBDs
- Differentiation is minimal — all outlets perform the same function
- **Float reliability** is the primary loyalty driver: customers return to agents known for always having float
- **Location** is the secondary driver: walk-in convenience determines daily transaction volume
- Running mobile money as a **standalone business** is challenging at average volumes; best operated alongside a shop, airtime sales, or bank agency (Equity, KCB) to share rent and fixed costs

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda — MTN Mobile Money and Airtel Money:** MTN Mobile Money is the dominant platform in Uganda (used by approximately 70%+ of mobile money users); Airtel Money is the main alternative. The agency model mirrors Kenya's M-Pesa structure: agents/aggregators recruit sub-agents; commission splits follow similar 80/20 principles. Float management is slightly more complex as Uganda's economy is more cash-intensive outside Kampala. MTN MoMo agents registered via the MTN Business portal; Airtel Money agents via Airtel's agent application process. KCCA trade licence and URA TIN required. Sub-agent goodwill typically UGX 200,000–500,000 ($54–$135). Commission rates change frequently — verify current MTN MoMo and Airtel rate cards before committing capital.
- **Tanzania — Vodacom M-Pesa, Miitel (formerly Tigo Pesa), Airtel Money:** Vodacom M-Pesa is the dominant platform in Tanzania (separate from Safaricom Kenya despite the M-Pesa brand); Miitel (Tigo) and Airtel Money compete. Tanzania has a more distributed mobile money market than Kenya. Agent model is broadly the same. BRELA registration and TRA TIN required. TCRA regulates mobile financial services. Commission structures differ by platform — Vodacom M-Pesa Tanzania commission rates are set separately from Kenyan M-Pesa rates. Sub-agent goodwill in Dar es Salaam ranges TZS 200,000–800,000 ($77–$308) depending on location.
- **Rwanda — MTN Mobile Money:** MTN Mobile Money is the dominant platform in Rwanda, with a market share exceeding 90% of mobile money transactions. Airtel Money operates but has a much smaller share. Rwanda's mobile money ecosystem is well-developed and serves as the primary payment infrastructure for both urban and rural Rwanda. RDB registration and RRA TIN required; RURA regulates mobile financial services. Commission rates are set and published by MTN Rwanda — verify current rates before committing. Sub-agent goodwill in Kigali: RWF 50,000–200,000 ($38–$153). Rwanda's compact geography means float restocking logistics are simpler than in Kenya or Uganda.
- **EA mobile money note (per user instruction):** Wherever M-Pesa is referenced in Kenyan context, the operational equivalents are: **Uganda** — MTN Mobile Money (primary), Airtel Money (secondary); **Tanzania** — Vodacom M-Pesa (primary), Miitel (secondary), Airtel Money; **Rwanda** — MTN Mobile Money (dominant). All platforms offer agent apps and USSD interfaces for transaction recording and float management.

## Data Quality Notes

Market statistics (65,547 agents, 3 outlets per 50m) are from January 2013 Kenya data. The mobile money sector has grown substantially since. Commission rates shown are 2013 figures — the current M-Pesa commission structure has been updated multiple times since; always verify with the operator's current rate card. The core economics (float-dependent revenue, saturation in urban areas, importance of location) remain structurally valid. Monthly commissions of KES 11,000 average reflect a competitive market — well-located agents with large floats earn significantly more; poorly located agents with small floats earn much less.
