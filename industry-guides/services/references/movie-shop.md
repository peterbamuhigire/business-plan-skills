# Movie Shop (DVD Duplication)

**Source:** Crack A Business Kenya (CAB/Insightful Ventures Ltd.) — The Movie Shop Quick Business Guide | **Sub-sector:** Services — Media Retail / DVD Duplication
**Conversion:** KES ÷ 106 × 1.27 → March 2026 USD

> A small-scale business duplicating and selling movies on DVD. Sells movies at KES 50/disc; direct cost of a blank DVD KES 13–20 (average KES 16); gross margin KES 34/movie. Average daily revenue KES 1,350; mode range KES 1,800–4,400. Breakeven ~4 months. The business operates in a legal grey area — movie duplication without rights-holder permission infringes intellectual property law. Market is heavily saturated in Nairobi CBD (4 shops per 50m); somewhat less so in estates and upcountry towns. Long-term viability is uncertain given streaming trends.

> **Legal note:** The guide explicitly notes that duplicating movies without permission from rights holders is illegal under Kenyan and international law. The guide covers the business as it exists in practice; licences are issued by local authorities but do not authorise copyright infringement.

> The KES 50 price has been stable for 3+ years at time of writing (2013–2014). Shops at KES 40 record the same average daily revenue as KES 50 shops — competing on price provides no advantage. A "Buy 5 get 1 free" discount is the most effective promotional mechanism.

## Key Success Factors

1. **Customer loyalty** — built through knowledgeable service, variety, and short waiting time
2. **Location** — high-traffic, visible, convenient to target customers
3. **Speed** — burn time is a satisfaction driver; multiple drives or pre-packaged popular titles reduce wait
4. **Variety** — having 1,500–2,000+ titles, including current releases and deep back-catalogue
5. **Niche positioning** — specialising by genre (horror, family, art films, classics, DJ movies) reduces direct competition and builds a loyal repeat customer base

## Long-Term Risks

| Risk | Assessment |
|------|------------|
| Copyright enforcement | Low probability of enforcement historically; but growing global IP pressure |
| Broadband internet access | As affordable broadband grows, downloads/streaming reduce demand for physical DVDs |
| Streaming services (Netflix, etc.) | Long-term competitive threat as penetration grows |
| Digital broadcasting expansion | More TV content available reduces demand for purchased movies |
| Market saturation | Already at saturation in urban areas; revenue per shop declining |

> Guide assessment (2013–2014): short and medium term, the business remains sustainable. Long term, survival depends on distribution innovation and niche positioning.

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** DVD movie shops exist across Kampala (Owino Market area, Kikuubo, and estate kiosks) and upcountry towns. The business model is identical. KCCA trade licence required; UPRS music licence if playing music in-store. Uganda Film Classification Board (requires film screening certification for commercial exhibition but rarely enforced at street kiosk level). The DJ Afro/DJ Smith Swahili-narrated movie format is popular in Uganda as well — sourced from Kampala's Nakivubo trading area.
- **Tanzania:** Movie shops (known locally as "video stalls" or "DVD shops") are common in Kariakoo (Dar es Salaam), Arusha, and Mwanza markets. Tanzania Film Board licences apply for commercial exhibition. BRELA registration and TRA TIN required for formal operation. The streaming transition is slower in Tanzania due to less broadband penetration outside Dar — DVD market has a longer viable runway than in Kenya.
- **Rwanda:** Rwanda's more strictly enforced intellectual property and business regulations make the DVD duplication model higher-risk than in Kenya or Uganda. Rwanda Utilities Regulatory Authority (RURA) and Rwanda Film Commission have more active enforcement. The market is smaller given Rwanda's smaller population and Kigali's higher-income urban base (more likely to have smartphones and streaming access). Niche family or Christian-content video retail may be more viable than general duplication.
- **EA general:** Across all EA markets, the physical DVD model is in structural decline. The more durable variation is transitioning to USB/HDD movie libraries (pre-loaded hard disks sold outright) or becoming an informal streaming/hotspot point for downloading content — both of which reduce the copyright infringement risk profile slightly while extending the product lifecycle.

## Data Quality Notes

All revenue data from 2013–2014 Kenya surveys. The DVD movie market has changed substantially since — physical DVD sales have declined as smartphone penetration and mobile data affordability have increased. The KES 50 price point, blank DVD cost (KES 16), and daily revenue averages should be treated as historical benchmarks, not current market data. Before investing, conduct a current field survey of movie shop revenues in your target area.
