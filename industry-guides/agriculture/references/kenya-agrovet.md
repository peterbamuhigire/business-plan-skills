## Operations

- **Location:** Select for high farmer density and low competition radius (5 km+ from dominant competitor)
- **Display:** Products grouped by use (fertilisers, seeds, pesticides, acaricides, veterinary supplies); visible from road
- **Stocking logic:** Match stock to dominant crops in the region and the farming calendar; do not tie capital in slow-moving items
- **Supplier relationships:** Purchase from county/town-level distributors; build credit terms after 3+ months of consistent cash purchases
- **Credit policy:** Avoid extending credit to farmers — this is the primary cause of agrovet cash flow collapse
- **Seasonal intelligence:** Know planting calendars; pre-position fertilisers and seeds 4–6 weeks before rains

## Market

Demand scales with commercial farming intensity — zones with intensive horticulture (tomatoes, capsicums, flowers), tea, coffee, or NGO-supported outgrower schemes generate the highest per-farmer input spend. Market entry becomes harder as the sector matures: nearly every 5 km radius in agricultural Kenya has at least one agrovet. Differentiation via technical advice, genuine product authenticity, and deep stock of locally preferred brands is critical.

## Wholesale / Distributor Pathway

With sustained volumes and capital above $12,000, becoming a sub-distributor or wholesaler is achievable. Farm input companies actively recruit high-performing agrovets. Some distribute exclusively within a defined zone. Security deposit requirements for distributorship: KES 1M–5M ($12,000–$60,000).

## East Africa Notes

- **Uganda:** Equivalent licences: URA business licence (district), NDA licence for veterinary chemicals, MAAIF seed certification. Agrovets common in Mbale, Soroti, Mbarara, Masindi corridors. Monthly rent for rural Uganda agrovet: UGX 100,000–400,000 (~$27–$108).
- **Tanzania:** TPRI (Tropical Pesticides Research Institute) registration for pesticide dealers; TOSCI for seed dealers. Active market in Arusha, Moshi, Iringa, Morogoro, and Mbeya crop zones.
- **Rwanda:** RAB (Rwanda Agriculture Board) regulates agri-inputs. Growing commercial horticulture in Musanze, Bugesera, and Eastern Province drives strong demand. Government subsidy voucher programmes (e-vouchers) affect buying patterns significantly.

## Data Quality Notes

Fertiliser prices are highly volatile — import costs, government subsidy programmes, and seasonal demand cause significant swings. Prices shown are indicative from Kenya ~2014–2018. Verify current buy prices with local distributors before incorporating into any business plan.
