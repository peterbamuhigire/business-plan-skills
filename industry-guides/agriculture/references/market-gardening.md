# Market Gardening: The Profitable Small-Scale Farm Model

**Source:** Fortier, J-M. — The Market Gardener: A Successful Grower's Handbook for Small-Scale Organic Farming (New Society Publishers, 2014) | **Application:** Micro-farm model, intensive bed systems, tool selection, crop planning, season extension, soil fertility, and farm business design

> Growing market-ready vegetables on a small plot of land is not a compromise — it is a business model. At 1.5 acres, a well-managed intensive market garden can outperform a 20-acre conventional farm in revenue per acre and net profit per hour of labour. The key is intensive cultivation, appropriate tools, direct marketing, and treating the farm as a business from the first season.

---

## 1. The Micro-Farm Business Model

### 1.1 Core Premise

Fortier's farm (Les Jardins de la Grelinette, Quebec) demonstrates that a 1.5-acre intensively managed market garden can generate over $100,000 USD in annual revenue with two full-time workers plus seasonal help. This model challenges the conventional assumption that farming requires large land areas to be economically viable.

**Key economic drivers of the micro-farm model:**
1. **Intensive land use:** Multiple crop successions per bed per year; no land left idle
2. **Direct marketing:** All produce sold direct-to-consumer (CSA + farmers market); no middlemen
3. **Appropriate-scale tools:** Tools matched to bed scale reduce labour without heavy mechanisation capital
4. **Crop selection:** Focus on high-value, high-yield crops that command premium prices
5. **Biological soil fertility:** Compost-based fertility eliminates ongoing synthetic fertiliser costs

**EA application:** Peri-urban plots of 0.5–3 acres around Kampala, Jinja, Entebbe, and Mbarara represent an underutilised resource. The market garden model, adapted for EA crops and climate, is directly applicable to smallholder producers willing to invest in soil management and develop direct market relationships.

### 1.2 Revenue per Acre Benchmark

| Farm System | Typical Revenue per Acre | Labour Input |
|-------------|------------------------|--------------|
| Conventional grain (maize) | UGX 2,000,000–4,000,000/year | Seasonal |
| Conventional vegetable (Owino supply) | UGX 5,000,000–10,000,000/year | Year-round |
| Intensive market garden (direct sale) | UGX 25,000,000–60,000,000/year | Year-round, intensive |

The micro-farm model achieves 5–10× the revenue per acre of conventional vegetable production by combining intensive cultivation with direct pricing power.

## 2. Site Selection

### 2.1 Critical Site Factors (Fortier)

Site selection is the most consequential early decision. Poor sites can rarely be corrected later without prohibitive cost.

**Essential criteria:**
1. **Soil quality:** Deep (30+ cm), well-drained, loamy soil with organic matter > 3%. Sandy or clay-heavy soils require years of amendment. Start with the best soil available.
2. **Water access:** Reliable irrigation source within reach. Market gardening requires consistent irrigation — rain-fed production alone is too risky for cash crop commitment.
3. **Slope:** Flat to gently sloping (< 3%) prevents erosion in intensive bed systems. Steep slopes require terracing (high cost).
4. **Sun exposure:** Full sun (6+ hours direct sunlight) for most vegetables. Avoid north-facing slopes or areas shaded by buildings or large trees.
5. **Access:** Vehicle access to the field (transport of inputs, harvest, equipment). Poor road access increases operating costs significantly.
6. **Proximity to market:** Shorter distance to market = lower transport cost + fresher produce at market. Proximity to urban demand centres is a competitive advantage.
7. **Wind protection:** Exposed sites dry out rapidly and damage fragile transplants. Natural windbreaks (hedgerows, tree lines) on the prevailing wind side are valuable.

**EA site evaluation checklist:**
- Is there a year-round water source (borehole, stream, tank, municipal connection)?
- What is the current soil organic matter? (Request laboratory test from NARL/Makerere before purchase or lease)
- Is the land free of flooding risk in the rainy season?
- What is the distance to Kampala/the primary market in kilometres and travel time?
- What is the current land use history? (Avoid land previously used for industrial purposes or heavy pesticide application)

## 3. The Permanent Raised Bed System

### 3.1 Bed Dimensions

The permanent raised bed is the foundation of intensive market gardening. Beds are established once and maintained for the farm's lifetime — never tilled by vehicle, never compacted by foot traffic.

**Standard bed specifications (Fortier):**
- **Bed width:** 30 inches (75 cm) — reachable from both sides without stepping on the bed
- **Path width:** 18 inches (45 cm) — wide enough for a wheelbarrow; narrow enough to minimise unproductive land
- **Bed length:** Variable; 100 feet (30 m) is a common standard for tool and record-keeping purposes
- **Bed surface:** Slightly raised (5–10 cm above path level) for drainage and warmer soil temperature

**Land use efficiency calculation:**
- In a 30" bed / 18" path system: 62.5% of land is productive bed; 37.5% is path
- This compares favourably with row cultivation systems where 50%+ is often unproductive row space
- Over a 100-foot bed: 250 sq ft productive; 150 sq ft path

### 3.2 Why Permanent Beds Work

1. **No compaction:** Soil structure is never damaged by foot or vehicle traffic on bed surfaces. This preserves biological activity, drainage, and root penetration.
2. **Focused fertility:** Compost and amendments are applied only to the productive area, not wasted on paths.
3. **Efficient tool use:** All hand and wheel tools are designed for 30-inch bed width — tools pass from one end to the other without adjustment.
4. **Record keeping:** Each numbered bed can be tracked individually for crop history, fertility, and yield data.

### 3.3 Bed Preparation

**New bed establishment from sod or compacted soil:**
1. Mow or slash existing vegetation to ground level
2. Apply 5–8 cm of compost to the bed surface
3. Broadfork to a depth of 30–45 cm (loosens subsoil without inverting topsoil)
4. Rake level; install path boards or mark path edges clearly
5. Plant immediately — no need to wait

**Minimal tillage principle:** After establishment, avoid deep tillage. Use shallow (5 cm) surface cultivation only. Deep tillage destroys fungal networks, brings weed seeds to the surface, and disrupts soil biology. The broadfork is the primary soil preparation tool.

## 4. Tools and Equipment

### 4.1 The Appropriate Tool Philosophy

Fortier's philosophy: tools should be matched to the scale and bed system of the operation. Large tractors are designed for row cultivation at scale — they are inefficient and damaging in a 30-inch bed system. The right tools for a market garden are human-powered or small-engine tools designed for bed-width operation.

### 4.2 Essential Tools

**Broadfork:**
- A U-shaped frame with 10–12 tines, operated by foot pressure
- Loosens soil to 30–45 cm without inverting or mixing soil layers
- Preserves earthworm populations and fungal networks
- Replaces the tractor for soil loosening in a permanent bed system
- Cost (imported): UGX 150,000–300,000; can be fabricated locally

**Wheel hoe:**
- Walk-behind tool with oscillating stirrup blade for cultivation and weed control
- Passes precisely between rows without disturbing crop plants
- Replaces hand hoeing for inter-row cultivation — reduces labour by 80%
- Models: Valley Oak, Glaser, Hoss (all can be imported or sourced through agricultural tool suppliers)
- Cost: UGX 200,000–400,000

**Seeders:**
| Seeder | Best For | Row Spacing | Cost (approx.) |
|--------|---------|-------------|----------------|
| EarthWay precision seeder | Larger seeds (beans, beets, radish, turnip) | Adjustable | UGX 200,000–350,000 |
| Glaser seeder | Fine seeds (carrots, lettuce, onion) | Fixed | UGX 100,000–200,000 |
| Six-Row seeder | High-density small seeds (salad greens, spinach) | Fixed | UGX 150,000–250,000 |

**Other essential tools:**
- Collinear hoe: precision weeding without bending; highly efficient between close-spaced crops
- Transplanting dibble board: marks transplanting holes at precise spacings for 30-inch beds
- Garden fork: general soil loosening, compost incorporation
- Harvest knife and harvest knives: Victorinox or similar for speed and ergonomics
- Harvest crates (reusable): standard 30-litre harvest crates stack, are washable, and extend produce shelf life

### 4.3 Capital Equipment Priority

For a new market garden, invest in this order:
1. Broadfork and wheel hoe first — these replace the most labour
2. Seeder matched to primary crops — direct seeding efficiency is critical for succession planting
3. Drip irrigation system — labour cost saving and water efficiency
4. Greenhouse or low tunnels — season extension for highest-value crops
5. Refrigerated storage — extends harvest window and improves market flexibility (long-term investment)

## 5. Crop Planning and Succession Planting

### 5.1 Crop Selection Criteria (Fortier)

Not all crops are equal in a market garden context. Select crops on:
1. **Market demand:** Does your buyer want this? Do not grow what you cannot sell.
2. **Revenue per bed-foot:** High-value crops justify the intensive labour; low-value bulk crops do not.
3. **Production ease:** Match crop difficulty to team skill level — especially in early seasons.
4. **Bed turn rate:** How many successions can you fit in a single bed per season?

**Revenue per 100-foot bed benchmarks (Fortier, adapted):**

| Crop | Yield per 100-ft bed | Revenue per bed | Beds per Year |
|------|---------------------|-----------------|---------------|
| Salad mix / lettuce | 30–50 lbs | High | 4–6 successions |
| Radish | 100–150 bunches | Moderate | 6–8 successions |
| Carrots | 100–150 lbs | Moderate | 2–3 successions |
| Tomatoes | 400–600 lbs | Very High | 1 crop/season |
| Cucumber | 200–300 lbs | High | 1–2 crops/season |
| Beans (bush) | 80–120 lbs | Moderate | 2–3 successions |
| Spinach | 30–50 lbs | High | 4–6 successions |
| Kale / leafy greens | 60–100 lbs | High | 3–5 cuts per planting |

### 5.2 Succession Planting

Succession planting ensures continuous harvest rather than a single glut followed by a gap. It requires planning backwards from market day:

**Succession planting logic (Fortier):**
1. Determine: how many bunches/kg of this crop do I sell per market day?
2. Determine: what is the harvest window for this crop (days from transplant/direct seed to first harvest)?
3. Calculate: how many beds (or row-feet) do I need to plant every X days to have a continuous supply?

**Example — salad mix for Kampala market:**
- Target: 10 kg salad mix per market day (twice weekly = 20 kg/week)
- Harvest window: 28–35 days from direct seeding to cut
- Yield per 30m bed: ~8–12 kg per cut
- Therefore: plant 2 beds every 10 days → continuous supply

Key direct seeding parameters for common crops (Fortier, EA-adapted):

| Crop | Seeder Type | Seed Spacing in Row | Row Spacing | Depth |
|------|------------|---------------------|-------------|-------|
| Carrots | Glaser | 1–2 cm | 15 cm | 0.5 cm |

## 6. Season Extension

### 6.1 Low Tunnels

Low tunnels are temporary hoops (wire, conduit, or fibreglass) covered with row cover fabric or plastic film. They are placed directly over crops in the bed and removed for cultivation and harvest.

**Low tunnel benefits:**
- Extend the productive season at the beginning and end (cooler months)
- Provide protection from heavy rain damage on delicate seedlings
- Reduce insect pressure (physical exclusion)
- Increase temperature by 2–5°C under cover

**Cost:** Low tunnels are the most affordable season extension structure. Wire hoops + row cover = UGX 50,000–100,000 per 30-meter bed.

### 6.2 Caterpillar Tunnels

Caterpillar tunnels are larger low tunnels (walk-under height, 1–1.5m tall) made with galvanised wire hoops and covered with greenhouse plastic or row cover. They allow internal access without removing the cover.

**Best for:** Extending tomato, pepper, and cucumber seasons in cooler months; starting crops earlier in the wet season.

### 6.3 Hoophouses (Simple Greenhouses)

A hoophouse is a permanent or semi-permanent tunnel greenhouse with no heating system — it relies solely on passive solar gain. It provides year-round growing capability for high-value crops.

**EA hoophouse application:**
- Primary use in Uganda: protection from heavy rains (which damage leaves, compact soil, and spread disease)
- Secondary use: year-round tomato, pepper, and cucumber production where outdoor crops have disease issues
- Cost: UGX 5,000,000–15,000,000 for a 100m² structure depending on materials and construction quality

**Priority crops for hoophouse:** Tomatoes, sweet peppers, cucumbers, and high-value herbs — crops that justify the infrastructure cost through premium pricing.

## 7. Soil Fertility Management

### 7.1 The Compost-Based System

Fortier's fertility system is built on compost as the primary input. This reduces dependence on purchased synthetic fertilisers, builds long-term soil health, and supports organic certification if desired.

**Annual compost application:**
- Target: 5 cm (2 inches) of finished compost applied to all beds at the start of each season
- A 30-inch × 100-foot bed requires approximately 1.2 cubic metres of compost
- On-farm compost production: kitchen scraps + crop residues + manure + dry carbon material

**Compost sourcing in EA:**
- Poultry manure (decomposed): widely available near poultry farms; high nitrogen
- Cattle manure (aged): widely available near dairy operations; lower nitrogen, good carbon
- Market waste (fruit/vegetable trimmings): available from Owino and Nakawa markets
- Municipal compost: increasingly available in Kampala through KCCA waste programmes

### 7.2 Vermicompost

Vermicomposting (worm-processed compost) produces a superior soil amendment — higher in plant-available nutrients and beneficial microorganisms than regular compost.

**Vermicompost production system:**
- Worm species: red wigglers (Eisenia fetida) — available from agricultural suppliers
- Bedding: shredded cardboard or newspaper, pre-moistened
- Feed: kitchen scraps, vegetable trimmings, aged manure (never fresh)
- Harvest: every 60–90 days
- Application rate: 1–2 cm per bed as a soil drench or top-dressing

**EA vermicompost note:** Vermicompost production is a complementary enterprise for market gardens. Surplus vermicompost can be sold to urban gardeners at UGX 3,000–6,000/kg — a meaningful secondary income stream.

### 7.3 Green Manures (Cover Crops)

Green manures are crops grown specifically to be incorporated into the soil to add organic matter and fix nitrogen:

| Green Manure Crop | Benefit | EA Suitability |
|------------------|---------|----------------|
| Sunn hemp (Crotalaria) | Fast-growing; high nitrogen; nematode suppression | Excellent — widely available EA |
| Mucuna (velvet bean) | Very high nitrogen (120+ kg N/ha); weed suppression | Excellent — widely adapted |
| Cowpeas | Nitrogen fixing; edible if harvested before incorporation | Excellent — dual-use crop |
| Oats or rye | Organic matter; erosion protection | Moderate — requires cooler conditions |

Use green manures during bed rest periods (fallow between crop successions) to maintain and build fertility without purchased inputs.

## 8. Pest and Disease Management

### 8.1 Biological Control First

Fortier's pest management hierarchy:
1. **Prevention:** Healthy soil → healthy plants → naturally more resistant to pests
2. **Cultural controls:** Crop rotation, intercropping, sanitation (remove diseased plant material immediately)
3. **Physical controls:** Row cover exclusion, sticky traps, hand removal
4. **Biological controls:** Predatory insects (lacewings, parasitic wasps), predatory mites
5. **Organic sprays:** Neem oil, pyrethrin, soap sprays — last resort before inorganic

### 8.2 Predatory Mites for Spider Mite Control

Spider mites are a major pest of market garden crops in hot, dry conditions. Predatory mites (Phytoseiulus persimilis, Amblyseius californicus) consume spider mites at high rates without harming plants.

**Predatory mite introduction:**
- Available from biological pest control suppliers (check NARO/Makerere for EA sources)
- Release at first sign of spider mite presence — not after infestation is established
- Cost per treatment is lower than repeat chemical applications over a season

### 8.3 Crop Rotation

Never grow the same crop family in the same bed in consecutive seasons. A minimum 3-year rotation breaks pest and disease cycles:

| Bed Year 1 | Bed Year 2 | Bed Year 3 |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Solanaceae (tomato, pepper, eggplant) | Brassica (kale, cabbage, broccoli) | Allium (onion, garlic, leek) |
| Cucurbit (cucumber, pumpkin, melon) | Legume (beans, peas) | Roots (carrot, beet, radish) |

## 9. Farm Record Keeping

Fortier's minimum record system for a market garden:

1. **Bed map:** Physical drawing of all beds numbered, with current crop recorded
2. **Planting log:** Date planted, variety, quantity, bed number for every seeding
3. **Harvest log:** Date harvested, crop, quantity per bed — used to calculate revenue per bed
4. **Sales log:** Market sales by crop per market day; CSA deliveries by week
5. **Input log:** Seed, compost, tools, irrigation — monthly cost summary

**Minimum viable tool:** A ruled exercise book, updated weekly, captures all of the above. Graduate to a simple spreadsheet as complexity grows.
