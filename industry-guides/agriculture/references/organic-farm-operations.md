## 1. Crop Planning and Production

### The Planning-for-Profit Process

Four sequential planning tools connect desired profit to field-level execution:

1. **Marketing Chart** — one-page spreadsheet: accounts across the top, products down the left, amounts and quantities in each cell. Grand total = projected gross sales.
2. **Production Plan** — how to grow what you hope to sell: crop, yield per bed, number of beds required, variety, spacing.
3. **Farm Map** — visual layout of fields showing where each crop goes and when. Updated as the season progresses. Past years' maps kept layered on the office wall for rotation reference.
4. **Seedling Calendar** — backtrack from transplant dates by 4-6 weeks to schedule greenhouse/nursery sowing.

### Production Plan Parameters

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Standard bed | 350 ft long, 42 in wide (growing area), 6 ft centre-to-centre |
| Beds per acre | 20 (at 350 ft x 6 ft = 2,100 sq ft per bed; 43,560 / 2,100 ≈ 20) |
| Bed calculation | Bed length (ft) x bed width (ft) = sq ft per bed; 43,560 / bed area = beds per acre |
| Row spacing | 3 rows per bed, 14 in apart |
| Tractor wheel spacing | 60 in tread centre to centre |

### Crop Spacing System

| Spacing | Crops |
|---------|-------|
| 3 rows (14 in apart) | Carrots, beets, lettuce, spinach |
| 2 outer rows (28 in apart) | Peppers, aubergine |
| 1 middle row (6 ft overall) | Melons, squash, cucumbers, artichokes |

## 2. Weed Control Systems

### Weed Control Economics

| Method | Time/Acre | Cost/Acre |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Hand weeding (no mechanical cultivation) | 200+ hours | UGX 1,200,000 |
| Hand weeding (with tractor cultivation and flame weeding) | 80 hours or less | UGX 480,000 |
| Flame weeding (tractor-mounted) | 2 hours | UGX 50,000 propane + tractor costs |
| **Savings from flame weeding** | **120 hours** | **UGX 700,000+ per acre** |

### Tractor Cultivation Tools

**1. Tine Weeders**
- Scratch the soil surface over the **entire** growing area, including directly on top of the crop
- Kill small weeds **in the row** — unique advantage over other cultivators
- Work when the crop is better rooted than surrounding weed seedlings
- "Blind" cultivation: no need to steer precisely around rows
- Can be used on transplants after ~1 week, or direct-seeded crops at 3 in tall

**2. Basket Weeders**
- Used from bed formation until plants reach ~12 in high
- Preferred size: 6 in wide, 2 in tall baskets with 8 in spacing between sets
- **Double-pass offset method:** first pass with left edge close to crop row, second pass (opposite direction) same. Eliminates **90% of the area** to be hand-weeded
- Total time: ~2 hours per acre (both passes)
- Add sweeps on blank rows for single- or two-row crops

**3. Sweeps (V-shaped)**
- Used when crop is too large for basket weeder (6+ in tall)
- Move soil side-to-side, burying small weeds within the row
- Can reach under foliage without disturbing the crop
- Available belly-mounted or rear-mounted on three-point hitch
- Set front points of all sweeps 14 in apart using a tape measure
- Use 4 in or 6 in wide sweeps; vary depth to control soil movement

### Stale Bed Seeding and Flame Weeding

**Process:**
1. Form bed and wait 1-3 weeks for weed germination (irrigate to hasten)
2. Seed the crop, following marked lines
3. Cover small sections with row cover, Plexiglas, or glass panes
4. Check under covers daily — the microclimate gives a 1-2 day preview of uncovered rows
5. When crop emerges under covers, **immediately** flame the entire bed
6. Window for flaming is small — do not delay

**Best suited for:** slow-germinating crops (parsnips, carrots) where weeds germinate first.

### Other Weed Strategies

- Time plantings to avoid peak weed pressure (e.g. galinsoga germinates only in warm soils, typically June)
- Overwintered field spinach: seeded in autumn, dormant over winter, harvestable May 1 with **zero hand-weeding expense**
- Row covers or irrigation to hasten weed seed germination before planting
- Straw or plastic mulch

## 3. Bed Preparation System

### Step-by-Step Bed Making

1. **Soil test and amend:** spread compost (manure spreader) and rock powders/fertilisers (cone spinner)
2. **Incorporate:** disk harrow for heavy plant residue; S-tine or chain harrow for lighter soils
3. **Chisel:** mini chisel plow with 3 shanks spaced 14 in apart, plus hilling disks. Aerates soil 10+ in deep without creating a plow pan or burning organic matter
4. **Bedform:** Larchmont-style bedformer with hilling disks, shaping boards, levelling board, meeker harrows, and 3 coil springs (14 in apart) bolted to the rear to mark rows

**Time requirements:**
- Chiselling: ~1.5 hours per acre
- Bedforming: ~1.5 hours per acre
- Speed: 4th or 5th gear (~3 mph)

**Finished bed quality:**
- 4-6+ in raised height from wheel track
- Flat top surface, no dips or divots
- Firm enough to walk on without sinking more than 1-2 in
- Three parallel lines clearly visible even after heavy rain

### Rototiller Caution

Chronic rototilling causes:
- Plow pan at ~6 in depth
- Rapid breakdown of organic matter (air whipped into soil)
- Beds settle back to grade quickly

**Better approach:** precede rototiller with mini chisel pass; run rototiller at slow RPM or faster ground speed.

## 4. Seeding and Transplanting

### Push Seeder Options

| Seeder | Features | Best For |
|--------|----------|----------|
| Earthway Precision | Lightweight, plate-based, multiple plates for different seeds, can bolt 2-3 together | General seeding; affordable |
| Planet Jr. | Heavy steel, seed hopper with adjustable hole (39 sizes), scatter or single-row shoe | Precise plant density; calibration |

### Seeder Calibration (Planet Jr.)

1. Bring a blanket/tarp to the field
2. Set hole size based on germination %, past records
3. Push seeder over blanket with rear wheel raised
4. Count seeds per foot on blanket
5. Adjust hole size; repeat until correct density achieved
6. Alternative: hold hand under seed shoe, turn front wheel exactly half revolution (= 2 row-feet); count seeds

**Record on seed bag:** seed plate name or hole number, date, field location.

### Transplanting Rates

| Method | Rate | People Required |
|--------|------|-----------------|
| Hand transplanting | 900 plants/hour/person | 2 (one dropping, one planting) |
| Tractor-mounted transplanter | 3,000 plants/hour total | 3 (1 driver + 2 planters) + 1 hour setup/disconnect |
| **Break-even point** | ~6,000 plants to justify mechanical transplanter | |

**Hand transplanting technique:** One person drops plants on marked rows; the other pokes 2 fingers in the ground, pushes root ball into hole, moves immediately. Approximately 1 plant every 2 seconds = 30 plants/minute.

**Best conditions:** Afternoon, cloudy, or rainy days. Avoid sunny mornings. Water in if no rain.

## 5. Marketing Channels

### Channel Comparison

| Channel | Characteristics | Price Level |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| Growers' co-op to large wholesale | Pallet quantities | ~UGX 30,000/case (24-count lettuce) |
| Local wholesale (stores, restaurants, co-ops) | Invoiced deliveries | ~UGX 50,000/case |
| Retail (farm stand, CSA, farmers' market) | Direct to consumer | ~UGX 85,000/case (UGX 3,000-4,500/head) |

### The Marketing Circle (Four Questions)

1. **How will the market know I have what it needs?** — Phone calls, advertising, samples, website, blog, flyers
2. **To whom will I market?** — Retail, wholesale, stores, restaurants, CSA, farm stand, pick-your-own, growers' co-op
3. **Why will the market want my product?** — Assess competition, quality, price, service, friendliness
4. **What will I market?** — Focus on products that are both in demand **and** profitable

### CSA Management

**Pricing principle:** Average weekly share value must match average weekly payment received.

**Example:** UGX 750,000 season / 20 weeks = **UGX 37,500/week average**

**Potholes to avoid:**
- Giving away too much product in peak season (a UGX 75,000 share on a UGX 37,500 payment halves the value of every item)
- Members respond by purchasing half shares the following year, or not re-signing
- Monitor total share value each week against the average payment
- Sell surplus through other channels rather than overloading CSA shares
- CSA works best in concert with at least one other marketing channel

### Professional Conduct Benchmarks

Five rules for business relationships:
1. Deliver in full, and on time
2. Call in orders at the same time each week
3. Respond to all communications promptly
4. Ask buyers what works best for them
5. Maintain consistent quality and packaging

### Brand Identity

Unify the "look" across all touchpoints:
- Invoices, cheques, business cards
- Signage at markets and farm stand
- Produce bags, case labels
- Farm vehicle lettering/artwork
- T-shirts, hats, stationery
- Choose a consistent colour, lettering style, and logo

## 6. Staff Management

### Employee Types and Requirements

| Type | Pay Structure | Obligations |
|------|--------------|-------------|
| Temporary/casual | UGX 6,000/hour, weekly pay | Timesheet tracking |
| Regular employee | Semimonthly pay | Full URA and NSSF compliance |

### Employer Obligations Calendar

| When | Task |
|------|------|
| Start of each year | Confirm employee tax details and NSSF registration |
| By end of February | File annual PAYE returns (previous year) |
| Monthly | Remit PAYE, NSSF, and LST deductions |

### Employer Cost Additions

| Cost | Rate |
|------|------|
| Employer NSSF contribution | 10% of payroll |
| Workers' compensation / insurance | ~2-5% of payroll |
| **Total addition to base wage** | **~12-15%** |

### Production Standards

Keep a **STANDARDS** page in the Crop Journal with benchmark rates:

| Task | Rate |
|------|------|
| Bunching cilantro | 150-200 bunches/hour |
| Picking spinach | 3-4 cases/hour |
| Filling 3-inch pots | 60 trays/hour |
| Hoeing one bed | ~45 minutes |
| Trellising one row of tomatoes | ~30 minutes |
| Picking 60 bunches of beets | ~1 hour |

**Key principle:** Farming is production work — the farm earns money **by the piece, not by the hour**.

### Employee Management Practices

- Written job descriptions for every position
- Morning planning before crew arrives: tasks, order, supplies, target times, contingency plans
- Weekly or monthly staff meetings for bigger picture and feedback
- Mix drudgery with more engaging jobs to avoid burnout
- Hire temporary extra hands to prevent falling behind
- Use "I" statements for addressing issues (not accusatory language)
- Cite target completion rates so employees understand production expectations

## 7. Record-Keeping Systems

### The Crop Journal

A pocket folder with loose-leaf pages, one per crop, arranged alphabetically. Records **contemporaneously** (as it happens, usually end of each day):

| Information Recorded | Purpose |
|---------------------|---------|
| Soil preparation (date, time, method) | Budget input |
| Seeding (date, variety, amount, time) | Budget input, future reference |
| Cultivation passes (date, method, time) | Budget input |
| Harvesting (date, quantity, time) | Budget input, yield tracking |
| **Rates** (quantity/area, quantity/time, time/area) | Planning, budgeting, scaling |

### Office Paper Flow System

**Purchases (money going out):**
- Unpaid bills holder (plain sight)
- Account slips holder (monthly statement purchases)
- Credit card slips holder
- Paid bills folder (chronological order in desk drawer)

**Sales (money coming in):**
- Payments holder (upright paper holder with matching invoices)
- Unpaid invoices (alphabetical accordion folder)
- Deposit bundles (title page + matched invoices, chronological)

**Process bills weekly** on a set schedule (e.g. Wednesday 9am-noon). Bundle tasks for efficiency.

### Checkbook Register Method

Use **multiple lines** per transaction to itemise expense types directly in the register. This eliminates the need to sort through paid bills at year end and ensures no expenses are missed.

### Annual Storage

After all previous year's bills are paid (mid-to-late January):
- Gather paid bills folder, bank statements, and deposit bundles
- Place in a large labelled paper bag
- Store for minimum 3 years (4 years for employer records) per tax requirements

## 8. Harvesting and Pack-Out Efficiency

### Harvest Aids

| Tool | Function | Crops |
|------|----------|-------|
| Bedlifter | Loosens soil under root crops; no shovel needed | Carrots, parsnips, also removes plastic mulch |
| Chain digger (modified potato digger) | Lifts entire bed; soil falls through chain; crop lands on surface | Root crops, perennials, potatoes |
| Barrel washer | Quick, thorough cleaning | Root crops |

### Grading System (Carrots Example)

Grades: A, A-minus, B, juicers, horse feed, compost. Pull one grade at a time rather than making a judgement call on every individual piece — reduces errors and increases speed.

### Materials Handling Principles

- Use bucket brigade for unloading (crew members pass produce hand-to-hand)
- Use hand trucks for stackable containers — stack once, never lift again
- "Never walk across a job site empty-handed"
- Bundle tasks: staple all boxes first, then pick all of one crop, then the next
- Count harvested bunches in piles of 10 for easy tracking against order quantities

## 9. Greenhouse Operations

### Greenhouse Configuration (Example)

- Two 96-ft plastic-covered greenhouses
- Greenhouse 1: seedling/bedding plant production (benches)
- Greenhouse 2: in-ground tomato production

### Efficiency Improvements

| System | Benefit |
|--------|---------|
| Overhead trolley rail | Move 25 flats at once (vs. 2 by hand); used within greenhouse, between houses, and for van loading |
| Trapdoor in roof | Load potting soil directly onto potting bench from outside |
| Overhead sprinklers with timer | Handle 75% of watering needs automatically |
| Bottom-watering benches | Capillary action through pot bottoms; excess water recycled to stock tank |
| Ebb-and-flow tables | Flood and drain system; more expensive but effective |

**Caution with recirculating water systems:** Can spread disease if infection is present. Plants on each bench should be similar size.

### Watering Guidelines

- 75% of watering can be automated with overhead sprinklers and timers
- Remaining 25% requires precise hand-watering
- Watering is a skilled task — not suitable for any untrained worker
- Over- and underwatering both damage plant health

## 10. Business Plan Structure

### Components

1. **Cover page** — farm name, contact details, date, authors
2. **Table of contents**
3. **Executive summary** — written last; includes mission statement, key financial data (net worth, gross sales, net income)
4. **Descriptions** — basic farm description (1-3 paragraphs); farm products (Marketing Chart or Sales Spreadsheet)
5. **Analyses:**
   - SWOT (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats)
   - Management analysis (who does what)
   - Market analysis (status of each sales venue, trends, competition)
   - Enterprise analysis (crop budgets for top sales items)
6. **Planning:**
   - Proposed ideas and strategies
   - Implementation timeline (who, when, how, why, budget)
7. **Financial forms:**
   - Profit and Loss Statement
   - Balance Sheet
   - Cash Flow Projection (typically 3-5 years forward)

**Total length:** 5-10 pages including financial forms.

### SWOT Examples

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| Strengths | Location near good market, fertile soils, energetic management |
| Weaknesses | Lack of irrigation, lack of labour, rocky ground |
| Opportunities | New organic retailer opening, land lease available, localvore movement |
| Threats | Market competition, higher energy costs, new regulations |

## 11. Deer Fencing (Cost-Effective Method)

- Two-strand poly and metal electric fence wire
- 6-ft fibreglass posts every 25 ft, angled outward at 30 degrees
- Top wire at 4 ft, bottom wire at 2 ft
- Strong fence charger with excellent ground
- Hang a worn T-shirt on a wooden cross when fence is first placed in new location
- Slanted fence exploits deer's poor depth perception — they cannot jump a 3D obstacle
- Portable and quick to move around the farm
