## Land Use and Soil Management

### Soil Capability Classes

| Class | Description | Agricultural Suitability |
|-------|-------------|------------------------|
| I | Prime farmland, few limitations | All crops, highest yields |
| II | Good farmland, moderate limitations | Most crops with simple conservation |
| III | Moderate limitations | Crops with intensive conservation practices |
| IV | Severe limitations | Limited crop choice, careful management |
| V | Unsuitable for cultivation (wet/stony) | Pasture, range, woodland |
| VI | Severe limitations | Pasture, range, woodland with restrictions |
| VII | Very severe limitations | Grazing, forestry only |
| VIII | Unsuitable for commercial plants | Wildlife, recreation, watershed |

> **Key principle**: Farming Class I-III land for crops is most economically viable. Class IV+ land is better suited for pasture, perennial crops, or conservation.

### Soil Assessment

**Soil texture triangle** classifies soil by percentage of sand, silt, and clay:
- **Sandy soils**: Drain fast, warm quickly, low nutrient retention
- **Clay soils**: Drain slowly, compact easily, high nutrient retention
- **Loam (ideal)**: Balanced mixture, good drainage and nutrient holding

**Key soil tests and target ranges:**

| Parameter | Target Range | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| pH | 6.0 - 7.0 | Most crops; blueberries prefer 4.5-5.5 |
| Organic matter | 3-6% | Higher = better water/nutrient retention |
| Nitrogen (N) | Varies by crop | Test annually, adjust with amendments |
| Phosphorus (P) | 25-50 ppm | Excess causes runoff pollution |
| Potassium (K) | 150-250 ppm | Critical for fruit quality |
| Cation Exchange Capacity | 10-25 meq/100g | Indicates nutrient-holding ability |

### Sustainable Soil Management Practices

| Practice | Benefit | Implementation Cost |
|----------|---------|-------------------|
| Cover crops | Erosion control, nitrogen fixation, organic matter | UGX 75,000-200,000/acre seed cost |
| Crop rotation | Pest/disease break, soil fertility | Minimal additional cost |
| Conservation tillage | Reduces erosion, builds soil biology | Equipment modification |
| Compost application | Builds organic matter, slow-release nutrients | UGX 50,000-150,000/cubic yard |
| Rotational grazing | Improves pasture, distributes manure evenly | Fencing infrastructure |
| Farmscaping | Beneficial insect habitat, windbreaks | One-time planting cost |

## Water and Irrigation Management

### Irrigation System Comparison

| System | Cost per Acre | Water Efficiency | Labour Required | Best For |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|----------------|----------|
| Hand line | UGX 400,000-500,000 | 60-70% | High (daily moves) | Small farms, low budget |
| K-line (pod) | UGX 950,000-1.1M | 65-75% | Moderate | Pasture, hay fields |
| Wheel line | UGX 1.4M-1.6M | 70-80% | Low-moderate | Larger field crops |
| Solid set | UGX 2.8M-3.2M | 75-85% | Very low | Orchards, permanent crops |
| Drip/micro | UGX 1.5M-4M | 85-95% | Very low | High-value row crops, berries |

### Water Budget Calculation

```
Crop Water Need (gallons/day) = ET Rate (inches/day) x Area (sq ft) x 0.623
```

**Worked example — 1 acre of tomatoes in July:**
- Evapotranspiration rate: 0.25 inches/day
- Area: 43,560 sq ft
- Daily need: 0.25 x 43,560 x 0.623 = **6,784 gallons/day**
- At 90% drip efficiency: 6,784 / 0.90 = **7,538 gallons/day applied**
- Monthly: ~227,000 gallons (0.70 acre-feet)

### Water Rights

- **Permit-based system** (Uganda): Water abstraction permit required from Directorate of Water Resources Management for irrigation above domestic use
- **Riparian/customary rights**: Traditional access rights exist but formal permits increasingly required
- **Groundwater permits**: Borehole drilling requires permits; metering becoming standard
- Water rights can be the **most valuable asset** on a farm property

## Machinery and Equipment Management

### Equipment Ownership Costs

```
Total Annual Cost = Fixed Costs + Variable Costs

Fixed Costs:
  Depreciation = (Purchase Price - Salvage Value) / Useful Life
  Interest = Average Investment x Interest Rate
  Insurance = 0.5-1.0% of average value
  Housing = 1-2% of purchase price

Variable Costs:
  Fuel & lubrication
  Repairs & maintenance (increases with age)
  Labour for operation
```

**Worked example — Tractor ownership cost:**
- Purchase price: UGX 130M
- Salvage value: UGX 30M
- Useful life: 15 years
- Annual use: 500 hours

| Cost Component | Annual Cost | Cost per Hour |
|----------------|-------------|---------------|
| Depreciation | UGX 6.7M | UGX 13,400 |
| Interest (15% on avg. value) | UGX 12M | UGX 24,000 |
| Insurance (0.75%) | UGX 600,000 | UGX 1,200 |
| Housing | UGX 1.3M | UGX 2,600 |
| **Total Fixed** | **UGX 20.6M** | **UGX 41,200** |
| Fuel (3 gal/hr @ UGX 14,000) | UGX 21M | UGX 42,000 |
| Repairs & maintenance | UGX 4.5M | UGX 9,000 |
| **Total Variable** | **UGX 25.5M** | **UGX 51,000** |
| **Total Annual Cost** | **UGX 46.1M** | **UGX 92,200/hr** |

### Lease vs Buy Decision Framework

| Factor | Buy | Lease |
|--------|-----|-------|
| Annual use | > 200-300 hours | < 200 hours |
| Cash position | Strong; or financing available | Limited capital |
| Maintenance capability | In-house mechanic or skills | Prefer no maintenance burden |
| Tax situation | Capital allowances beneficial | Lease payments fully deductible |
| Technology change | Slow (tractors, implements) | Rapid (GPS, precision tools) |

**Decision rule**: If custom hire or rental rate x hours of use < annual ownership cost, hire rather than own.

## Labour Management

### Labour Cost Calculations

```
True Labour Cost = Wages + Payroll Taxes + Benefits + Workers' Comp + Training
```

**Typical cost multiplier**: 1.12 to 1.20 times base wage

**Worked example:**
- Base wage: UGX 7,000/hour
- NSSF (employer 10%): ~UGX 700/hour
- Workers' compensation: ~UGX 200/hour (3%)
- Benefits (if offered): ~UGX 300/hour
- Training/supervision: ~UGX 300/hour
- **True cost: UGX 8,500/hour (1.21x multiplier)**

### Labour Efficiency Benchmarks

| Task | Efficient Rate | Unit |
|------|---------------|------|
| Transplanting (crew) | 800-1,200 plants/hour | Per worker |
| Hand weeding | 0.05-0.15 acres/hour | Per worker |
| Mechanical cultivation | 2-5 acres/hour | Per tractor |
| Harvest — tomatoes | 150-300 lbs/hour | Per worker |
| Harvest — salad greens | 30-60 lbs/hour | Per worker |
| Harvest — root crops | 100-200 lbs/hour | Per worker |
| Washing & packing | 200-400 lbs/hour | Per worker |
| Farmers' market sales | UGX 200,000-600,000/hour | Per stand |

### Hiring and Management Best Practices

1. **Written job descriptions** with clear expectations and performance measures
2. **Documented training** for all tasks, especially food safety and equipment operation
3. **Regular check-ins** — weekly during season, monthly off-season
4. **Fair compensation** — research regional rates; pay above minimum to reduce turnover
5. **Legal compliance** — employment contracts, NSSF registration, PAYE withholding
6. **Seasonal labour** — casual workers common in Ugandan agriculture; ensure proper records

## Crop and Livestock Production Economics

### Crop Enterprise Profitability Factors

```
Net Return per Acre = (Yield x Marketable % x Price) - (Variable Costs + Fixed Costs)
```

**Key drivers of profitability (ranked by impact):**
1. **Price received** — direct markets typically 2-4x wholesale price
2. **Yield achieved** — soil health, variety selection, pest management
3. **Marketable percentage** — post-harvest handling, grading, storage
4. **Labour efficiency** — largest controllable cost on most farms
5. **Input costs** — seed, fertiliser, pest management

### Farm Ecosystem Management

**Three key biogeochemical cycles:**
- **Carbon cycle**: Photosynthesis captures CO2; soil organic matter stores carbon; decomposition releases it
- **Nitrogen cycle**: Fixation (legumes), mineralisation, nitrification, plant uptake, denitrification
- **Water cycle**: Precipitation, infiltration, evapotranspiration, runoff, groundwater recharge

**Integrated Pest Management (IPM) hierarchy:**
1. Prevention (crop rotation, resistant varieties, sanitation)
2. Cultural controls (timing, spacing, trap crops)
3. Biological controls (beneficial insects, microbial agents)
4. Mechanical/physical controls (cultivation, barriers, traps)
5. Chemical controls (least toxic options first, targeted application)

### CSA Model Economics

```
CSA Share Price = (Total Farm Costs + Desired Profit) / Number of Shares
```

**Worked example:**
- Total production costs: UGX 65M
- Desired owner compensation: UGX 35M
- Target profit: UGX 10M
- Total needed: UGX 110M
- Shares offered: 175
- **Share price: UGX 110M / 175 = UGX 629,000/share (round to UGX 625,000)**
- Season: 22 weeks
- Weekly value per share: UGX 625,000 / 22 = **UGX 28,400/week**

## Environmental Compliance and Sustainability

### Key Regulatory Areas

| Area | Regulation | Applies To |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| Water quality | Water Act (Cap 152), NEMA guidelines | Farms near waterways, large operations |
| Pesticide use | Agricultural Chemicals (Control) Act | All farms using pesticides |
| Worker safety | Occupational Safety and Health Act | Farms with employees |
| Food safety | UNBS standards, Public Health Act | Farms with processed/packaged products |
| Organic certification | Uganda Organic Standard (NOGAMU) | Farms marketing as "organic" |
| Animal welfare | Animal Diseases Act | Livestock operations |
| Wetlands | National Environment (Wetlands) Regulations | Farms with wetlands or streams |
| Environmental impact | NEMA EIA requirements | Large-scale land clearing or processing |

### Sustainable Agriculture Practices Summary

| Practice | Environmental Benefit | Economic Benefit |
|----------|---------------------|-----------------|
| Cover cropping | Erosion control, N fixation, carbon sequestration | Reduced fertiliser costs, improved yields |
| Crop rotation (3-4 year) | Breaks pest/disease cycles | Reduced pesticide costs |
| Conservation tillage | Soil structure, water retention, carbon storage | Reduced fuel and labour costs |
| Composting | Nutrient recycling, waste reduction | Reduced input costs |
| Rotational grazing | Improved pasture health, carbon sequestration | Higher stocking rates, less hay needed |
| Buffer strips | Water quality, beneficial insect habitat | Conservation programme payments available |
| Integrated pest management | Reduced chemical use, ecosystem health | Lower pest management costs |

> **Triple bottom line**: Sustainable practices often improve **profitability** (reduced inputs), **environmental health** (soil, water, biodiversity), and **community well-being** (food access, rural employment) simultaneously.

### Farm Energy Management

Key energy considerations:
- **Fuel**: Typically UGX 1.8M-6M/year for small farms; consolidate field operations to reduce passes
- **Electricity**: UGX 2M-10M/year; cold rooms are largest draw on most vegetable farms
- **Renewable options**: Solar panels (3-5 year payback in Uganda), biogas digesters, biomass heating for greenhouses
- **Energy grants**: Various development partner programmes cover partial costs of renewable energy projects
