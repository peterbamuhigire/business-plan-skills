## 2. Agricultural Household Characteristics

*Source: UHIS-AAS 2021/22*

**Scope:** Survey covers all agricultural households (Ag HHs) across Uganda — crop farming, livestock, fisheries.

**Key characteristics of agricultural households:**
- Agriculture is the dominant livelihood for rural Uganda (71% of population rural per NLFS 2021)
- Average household size: 5 persons (rural), 4 persons (urban)
- Most Ag HH heads: subsistence-oriented, limited access to certified inputs
- Low formal credit access: 77% of household enterprises funded by own savings (UNHS 2023/24)
- SAACOs and microfinance (MDIs) are primary formal credit sources in rural areas

## 3. Land Use & Holdings

*Source: UHIS-AAS 2021/22*

| Characteristic | Notes |
|---|---|
| Land tenure | Mix of customary, freehold, leasehold, mailo |
| Average smallholder holding size | Typically 0.5–2.0 acres for subsistence; 2–20 acres for semi-commercial |
| Main land-use system | Mixed farming (crops + livestock) dominates in most regions |

**Regional farming systems:**
- **Central (Buganda):** Banana (matooke), coffee, maize, vegetables
- **Eastern:** Maize, cassava, cotton, tobacco, rice (Mt Elgon)
- **Northern (Acholi, Lango):** Maize, sorghum, millet, sunflower, sesame
- **Western:** Coffee (arabica highlands), tea, Irish potatoes, dairy cattle, sorghum
- **West Nile:** Millet, sorghum, simsim, groundnuts

## 5. Key Crop Production Uses

*Source: UHIS-AAS 2021/22*

**Maize:** Major staple and cash crop. Production split between household consumption and market sales. Maize flour prices rose **+10.8%** annually (CPI Feb 2026) — significant cost pressure for downstream processors.

**Beans:** Dual purpose — household protein + cash crop. Prices stable (−1.1% Feb 2026). High demand in urban markets.

**Millet:** Traditional staple in Northern and Eastern Uganda. Used for local brewing (enguli, local beer).

**Sorghum:** Drought-tolerant. Key in northern and semi-arid zones.

**Rice:** Premium cash crop. Mainly grown in Eastern (Bugiri, Tororo, Iganga), Masaka lowlands, Kibimba.

**Simsim (sesame):** High-value export crop. West Nile and Northern Uganda.

**Groundnuts:** Both consumption and cash. Paste/oil processing opportunity.

## 6. Livestock Sector

*Source: Uganda National Livestock Census 2021 (UBOS/MAAIF), World Bank 2023*

**Agriculture GDP contribution from livestock:** Livestock and fishing drove agricultural growth to +4.8% in FY2023 (World Bank).

**Livestock census categories (2021):**
- Cattle: indigenous + exotic/cross (dairy and beef)
- Goats: indigenous (meat) + exotic/cross (dairy)
- Sheep: indigenous + exotic/cross
- Pigs
- Chickens: indigenous + exotic (broilers/layers)
- Rabbits, horses, donkeys, camels
- Honey production (kg)

**Key livestock zones:**
- **Cattle:** South-western (Ankole, Mbarara — Ankole long-horn cattle), Eastern (zebu), Karamoja (traditional pastoral)
- **Dairy:** South-western highlands (Mbale district, Bushenyi, Mbarara) — primary milk production
- **Pigs:** Central (Kampala peri-urban areas), Busoga, Buganda — rapidly commercialising
- **Poultry:** All regions — village poultry (indigenous) + commercial layers/broilers in peri-urban Kampala, Jinja, Mbarara
- **Fish:** Victoria (Nile perch, tilapia), Lake Albert, Lake Kyoga, Lake Edward/George

**Milk production:**
- South-western Uganda dominates commercial dairy
- Raw milk prices volatile — seasonal fluctuations
- Dairy processing capacity growing (Pearl Dairy, Amos Dairies, Sameer Agriculture)

## 7. Agricultural Finance

*Source: UBOS UNHS 2023/24, World Bank 2023*

| Financing Source | Relevance |
|---|---|
| Own savings | Primary source (77% of rural enterprises) |
| SACCOs | Main rural credit institution |
| Uganda Development Bank (UDB) | Agricultural credit facility |
| Agricultural Credit Facility (ACF) | Government-subsidised agric loans |
| Microfinance (MDIs) | FINCA, Pride Microfinance, UGAFODE |
| Commercial banks | Less accessible for smallholders |

**Commercial bank credit to agriculture:** A share of UGX 72.1 trillion in private-sector credit (Q1 2025/26), but agriculture's share remains small relative to its economic contribution.

**Agric lending challenges:** No collateral (customary land), seasonality of income, weather risk, price risk.

## 9. Uganda Agricultural Policy Context

**NDP III Agro-Industrialisation Programme:**
- Goal: Transform Uganda from peasant to market-oriented agriculture
- Key strategy: Value addition at the farm gate and in processing
- Priority value chains: Coffee, tea, dairy, maize, fish, horticulture, oil palm

**Key government programmes:**
- NAADS (National Agricultural Advisory Services)
- Operation Wealth Creation (OWC) — input distribution
- Agriculture Cluster Development Project (ACDP) — World Bank funded
- Parish Development Model (PDM) — grassroots enterprise finance

**Regulatory framework for agriculture:**
- Ministry of Agriculture, Animal Industry and Fisheries (MAAIF)
- UNBS: Uganda National Bureau of Standards (food quality standards)
- NEMA: Environmental impact for large agricultural projects
- URA: Tax obligations on commercial agricultural income

## 10. Climate & Rainfall Context

*Source: World Bank 2023, UBOS KEI*

- Uganda has two main rainy seasons: March–May and October–November
- **Rainfall irregularity** is the primary risk to crop production (explicitly noted in World Bank 2023 Uganda Economic Update)
- Adverse weather reduced FY2023 crop output despite overall agricultural GDP growth of +4.8%
- Climate change increasing flood risk in coastal/lacustrine areas; drought risk in Karamoja and dry corridor
- El Niño years bring flooding; La Niña years bring drought — East African pattern

**Implication for business plans:** Agricultural businesses must model weather risk explicitly. Two-season planning (Season A: March–May; Season B: August–November) is standard.
