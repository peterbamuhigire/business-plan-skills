# Poultry Management: Breeds, Production, and Farm Economics

**Source:** Damerow, G. — Storey's Guide to Raising Chickens, 3rd Edition (Storey Publishing, 2010) | **Application:** Poultry breed selection, layer management, meat bird production, housing, biosecurity, egg sales, and farm economics

> The most important decision a poultry farmer makes is matching breed to purpose. A layer breed will not grow fast enough for meat; a meat breed will not lay enough eggs to justify feed costs. Start with clarity on your enterprise type, then select the breed that optimises for that enterprise.

---

## 1. Breed Selection by Enterprise Type

### 1.1 Layer Breeds (Egg Production)

Layer breeds are bred for high egg output and small body size (low feed consumption). They are not suitable for meat production — the feed conversion ratio makes them uneconomical as table birds.

**Performance benchmarks (Damerow):**
- Annual egg production: 250–280 eggs per hen in first laying year
- Peak production age: 5–7 months after hatch
- Flock average (accounting for non-layers): 20 dozen eggs per hen per year
- Feed efficiency: 4 lbs of feed per dozen eggs produced (industry benchmark)
- Commercial layer lifespan: 72 weeks before replacement

**Common layer breeds and characteristics:**

| Breed | Eggs/Year | Egg Colour | Temperament | EA Suitability |
|-------|-----------|------------|-------------|----------------|
| White Leghorn | 280–300 | White | Nervous, flighty | Good for commercial; needs confinement |
| Rhode Island Red | 250–270 | Brown | Calm, hardy | Excellent; heat-tolerant, dual-purpose option |
| Plymouth Rock | 200–250 | Brown | Docile | Good; adapts to semi-intensive systems |
| Sussex | 220–260 | Tinted | Very calm | Good; forages well |
| Australorp | 250–300 | Brown | Calm | Excellent; heat-tolerant, record-holder breed |

**EA note:** Australorps and Rhode Island Reds perform well in warm climates and semi-intensive management. Avoid White Leghorns for smallholder systems — they stress easily under irregular management.

### 1.2 Meat Breeds (Broiler Production)

Meat breeds (broilers) are bred for rapid weight gain and feed conversion. They reach market weight in 6–8 weeks.

**Performance benchmarks:**
- Market weight: 4–5 lbs live weight at 6–8 weeks
- Feed conversion ratio (FCR): 2 lbs feed per 1 lb weight gain (broiler breeds)
- Dressing percentage: 70–75% of live weight
- Mortality target: Below 5% for a well-managed flock

**Key meat breeds:**
- **Cornish Cross (Cornish Rock):** The dominant commercial broiler worldwide. Reaches 5 lbs in 6–7 weeks. Requires high-protein starter feed (22–24% protein). Poor foragers — suited to confinement only.
- **Freedom Rangers / Coloured Rangers:** Slower-growing (9–11 weeks to market), better suited to pastured or semi-free-range systems. Better flavour, less breast meat, better suited to EA consumer preference for flavoured table birds.
- **Heritage meat breeds (Cornish, Jersey Giant):** Slow-growing, small market. Not recommended for commercial broiler production.

**EA broiler note:** The fast-growing Cornish Cross dominates commercial production but requires imported feeds and controlled environment. For village-level or semi-commercial production, coloured slow-growing breeds offer lower input costs, better disease resistance, and better market prices from quality-conscious buyers.

### 1.3 Dual-Purpose Breeds

Dual-purpose breeds lay reasonable numbers of eggs and produce acceptable carcass weight. Neither performance is optimal, but both are adequate for mixed-income smallholder systems.

| Breed | Eggs/Year | Cockerel Market Weight | Comment |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|---------|
| Red Sex Link | ~250 | 4–5 lbs at 12 weeks | Excellent dual-purpose; sex-linked (easy sorting) |
| Black Sex Link | 220–240 | 4 lbs at 12 weeks | Similar to Red Sex Link |
| Rhode Island Red | 250–270 | 4.5 lbs at 16 weeks | Slightly slower than purpose-bred broilers |
| Barred Plymouth Rock | 200–230 | 4 lbs at 18 weeks | Forages well; good for free-range systems |

**EA application:** Dual-purpose breeds are the most appropriate for small-to-medium poultry enterprises selling eggs to local markets and culled cockerels as table birds. The Red Sex Link is particularly recommended — males can be identified at hatch (different colours) and sold or raised for meat while females are retained for laying.

## 2. Housing and Space Requirements

### 2.1 Space Standards

| System | Floor Space per Bird | Roost Space | Nest Box Ratio |
|--------|---------------------|-------------|----------------|
| Deep litter (intensive) | 2–3 sq ft (0.2–0.3 m²) | 6–8 inches per bird | 1 box per 4–5 hens |
| Semi-intensive (indoor + run) | 2 sq ft indoor + 4 sq ft run | 6 inches | 1 box per 4–5 hens |
| Free-range | 1.5 sq ft indoor + outdoor range | 6 inches | 1 box per 4–5 hens |
| Broilers (confinement) | 0.75–1 sq ft per bird | N/A (broilers do not roost) | N/A |

**Critical principle:** Overcrowding is the single most common cause of disease, feather pecking, and poor performance in smallholder flocks. When in doubt, stock less densely.

### 2.2 Ventilation

Ammonia buildup from manure is the primary health risk in enclosed poultry housing. Design principles:
- Open eaves (covered with hardware cloth) on both sides of the house
- Ridge vent along the roof peak for hot air escape
- Wind breaks on the prevailing wind side to prevent cold drafts
- Target: fresh air movement without direct wind on birds

### 2.3 Litter Management

Deep litter systems (6–8 inches of wood shavings, rice husks, or dry grass) absorb moisture and, when managed correctly, generate heat through microbial activity:
- Add fresh litter on top when moisture exceeds 25–30%
- Turn litter weekly to aerate and break up crusting
- Replace completely at the end of each flock cycle
- Composted spent litter is high-value fertiliser (2–3% nitrogen)

## 3. Feeding and Water Management

### 3.1 Feed by Stage

| Stage | Age | Protein % | Feed Form | Consumption |
|-------|-----|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Chick starter | 0–6 weeks | 20–22% | Crumbles | 1–2 oz/bird/day |
| Grower | 6–14 weeks | 16–18% | Pellets or mash | 2–3 oz/bird/day |
| Layer developer | 14–18 weeks | 14–16% | Pellets | 3–3.5 oz/bird/day |
| Layer | 18+ weeks | 15–17% | Pellets or mash | 3.5–4.5 oz/bird/day |
| Broiler starter | 0–3 weeks | 22–24% | Crumbles | 1–2 oz/bird/day |
| Broiler finisher | 3 weeks to market | 18–20% | Pellets | 4–6 oz/bird/day |

**Calcium for layers:** Laying hens require 3.5–4g of calcium per day for eggshell formation. Standard layer feed provides this. Supplement with crushed oyster shell or limestone grit in a separate feeder for free-choice consumption.

**Water:** Hens drink 2× their feed weight in water. A hen producing an egg per day needs 500ml water. Restrict water access for even one hour in hot weather and egg production drops immediately.

### 3.2 EA Feed Options

- **Commercial pelleted layer feed:** Most reliable (NBS or imported brands). Cost: approximately UGX 2,500–3,200 per kg (2026).
- **On-farm mixed rations:** Maize grain (55–60%) + soybean meal (25–30%) + premix (5%) + limestone (5%). Reduces cost by 15–25% but requires consistent ingredient sourcing and careful mixing.
- **Foraging supplement:** Free-range birds reduce feed costs by 20–30% through insects, worms, and vegetation. Quality of eggs (yolk colour, taste) improves significantly.

## 4. Laying Hen Management

### 4.1 Light and Egg Production

Hens require 14–16 hours of light per day to sustain peak lay. As day length decreases (dry season or equatorial regions), production drops without supplemental lighting.

**Lighting management:**
- Add supplemental light in the morning (before dawn) to maintain 15 hours total
- Use LED bulbs: one 9-watt LED bulb per 40 sq ft of floor space is sufficient
- Gradual light increase at point of lay: increase by 30 minutes per week from 14 to 16 hours
- Never reduce light hours during the laying cycle — this triggers premature moult

### 4.2 Flock Replacement Economics

The economic case for flock replacement at 72 weeks (Damerow):

| Metric | Pullets (Year 1) | Hens (Year 2) |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| Lay rate | 80–90% | 65–75% |
| Egg size | Smaller initially | Larger |
| Feed consumption | Same | Same |
| Income per 100 birds/week | UGX X | ~20% less |

At 72 weeks, the cost of feed begins to exceed the value of eggs produced at declining lay rates. A well-managed layer enterprise replaces the flock every 12–14 months for maximum profitability.

**Moulting option:** Some operations force-moult the flock at 72 weeks (restrict feed and water temporarily) to trigger a second laying cycle. This extends productive life by 4–6 months but at reduced efficiency. Not recommended unless replacement pullet costs are prohibitively high.

## 5. Egg Sales and Marketing

### 5.1 Egg Grading

Standard commercial egg grading (Damerow):
- **Grade AA:** Clean shell, firm albumen, small air cell. Premium market.
- **Grade A:** Clean shell, slightly less firm. Standard retail.
- **Grade B:** Slightly stained, thinner albumen. Processed food or reduced price.
- **Grade C (crack/dirty):** Not for retail. Household use or discard.

**EA grading note:** The formal grading system is rarely used in smallholder markets. Buyers sort visually by size and shell condition. Maintain clean nest boxes (collect eggs 2–3× daily) to minimise dirty eggs — the single most effective quality control action.

### 5.2 Egg Collection and Storage

- Collect eggs at least twice daily (morning and afternoon)
- Store at room temperature in EA conditions: eggs remain good for 2–3 weeks if unwashed
- Washing removes the natural bloom (cuticle) that seals the pores — wash only eggs for immediate use
- Do not refrigerate eggs that have been laid at ambient temperature (condensation damages the bloom on retrieval)
- Pack in reused egg trays (30-count) — available from distributors and markets

### 5.3 Market Research Before Starting

Damerow's pre-investment checklist for egg producers:
1. Who are the local buyers? (households, restaurants, institutions, traders?)
2. What is the current farm-gate price per egg or per tray?
3. What is the quantity demanded per week from each buyer type?
4. What is the price differential between tray eggs (30-count) vs. retail individual eggs?
5. What is the premium (if any) for free-range, organic, or "village" eggs?
6. What is the competition — commercial farms, imports, local smallholders?

**EA pricing benchmark (2026):** Farm-gate tray (30 eggs) prices in Uganda range from UGX 9,000–12,000 (commercial intensive) to UGX 14,000–18,000 (free-range / organic / improved local breeds).

## 6. Biosecurity and Disease Prevention

### 6.1 Core Biosecurity Principles

1. **All-in, all-out:** Raise one batch at a time per house. Do not mix ages. Clean and disinfect completely between batches.
2. **Controlled entry:** Visitors change footwear or use disinfectant footbaths at the house entrance.
3. **Quarantine new birds:** Isolate newly purchased birds for 14–30 days before introducing to the existing flock.
4. **Rodent and wild bird control:** Rodents carry Salmonella; wild birds carry Newcastle disease and Avian influenza. Secure feed storage and block entry points.
5. **Dead bird disposal:** Burn or deep-bury dead birds immediately. Do not leave carcasses where predators or other birds can access.

### 6.2 Vaccination Programme

Core vaccines for EA commercial poultry (Damerow framework, adapted for EA):

| Disease | Vaccine Type | Age at First Dose | Booster |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|---------|
| Newcastle Disease (NCD) | Live (Lasota/F strain) | Day 7–10 via eye drop or water | Every 6–8 weeks |
| Infectious Bronchitis | Live (H120 strain) | Day 1–14 | Every 6–8 weeks |
| Gumboro (IBD) | Live intermediate | Day 14–21 | Day 28–35 |
| Fowl Pox | Live | 6–8 weeks via wing web | Once |
| Marek's Disease | At hatchery | Day 1 (hatchery-applied) | Not required |

**EA vaccination note:** Newcastle Disease is the most critical vaccination for EA poultry. NCD outbreaks can kill an unvaccinated flock within 3–5 days. Vaccinate on schedule — do not delay based on cost. The cost of one vaccination cycle (UGX 500–800/bird) is far below the cost of a flock loss.

## 7. Economic Efficiency Benchmarks

### 7.1 Key Production Metrics

| Metric | Target | Calculation |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| Feed conversion (layers) | 4 lbs feed per dozen eggs | Monthly feed used ÷ dozens produced |
| Annual production per hen | 20 dozen (240 eggs) | Total eggs ÷ average hens in flock |
| Mortality rate (layers) | Below 10%/year | Deaths ÷ starting flock |
| Culling rate | 15–20%/year | Culled birds ÷ starting flock |
| Break-even price per egg | Calculate monthly | Total monthly costs ÷ monthly eggs sold |
| Feed cost as % of revenue | 60–70% | Monthly feed cost ÷ monthly egg revenue |

### 7.2 Flock Size for Financial Viability

Minimum viable flock sizes (Damerow, adapted for EA):
- **Household + small income:** 50–100 hens (sustainable on one person's part-time attention)
- **Primary income source:** 500+ hens minimum; 1,000–2,000 hens for full-time enterprise
- **Commercial scale:** 5,000+ hens, requiring professional management and capital investment

**EA scale note:** A 500-hen layer unit producing 400 eggs/day (80% lay rate) generates:
- Daily revenue: 400 eggs = 13.3 trays × UGX 10,000 = UGX 133,000/day
- Monthly revenue: ~UGX 4,000,000
- Monthly feed cost: ~UGX 2,400,000 (60%)
- Net margin before overheads: ~UGX 1,600,000 (~$430)
- This supports one owner-operator with modest profit after labour and overheads.

## 8. Organic and Free-Range Certification

### 8.1 Free-Range Requirements (Damerow)

True free-range requires:
- Outdoor access during daylight hours
- Minimum 4 sq ft outdoor range per bird
- Range must have vegetation (not bare soil)
- No routine antibiotic use

**Marketing premium:** Free-range eggs command 30–60% price premium over commercial cage/intensive eggs in developed markets. In EA, the premium is smaller (10–25%) but growing, particularly in Kampala's middle-class market.

### 8.2 Organic Certification

Organic poultry standards require:
- 100% certified organic feed (no GMO ingredients)
- No synthetic medications (vaccines are permitted)
- Outdoor access at all times
- No routine preventive antibiotics

Certification body in Uganda: Uganda Organic Certification (UgoCert). Cost and timeline: approximately UGX 800,000–1,500,000 for initial certification, 6–12 month process.

**EA business plan note:** For most EA smallholder poultry enterprises, "organic" certification is not worth the investment unless selling to export markets or premium urban retailers (Nakumatt, Carrefour, specialty stores). Free-range management practices without certification still command a meaningful price premium from informed consumers.
