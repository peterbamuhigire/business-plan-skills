# Marketing and Sales for Agricultural Businesses

> Reference: Barnard, F., Akridge, J., Dooley, F. and Foltz, J. (2012) *Agribusiness Management*. 4th edn. Routledge.

> Figures adapted to Ugandan context (UGX). Percentages and ratios from the source are universal.

## The Marketing Concept

Marketing is the process of anticipating the needs of targeted customers and finding ways to meet those needs profitably. The full marketing process involves:

1. Identifying customer needs
2. Developing products and services to meet those needs
3. Establishing promotional programmes and pricing policies
4. Designing distribution systems for products and services

### Evolution of Marketing Orientation

| Orientation | Focus | Characteristic |
|-------------|-------|---------------|
| **Product-driven** | "Build it and they will come" | Internal focus on production efficiency; assumes customers will buy whatever is produced |
| **Sales-driven** | "Sell what we make" | Emphasis on aggressive sales tactics; push products through distribution channels |
| **Market-driven** | "Find a need and fill it" | External focus on customer needs; products developed based on market research |

**Key distinction:** Market-driven organisations start with the customer, then work backward to design products, services, and programmes. Product-driven organisations start with what they can produce and then try to find customers.

## Strategic Market Planning

### The Market Planning Process

A systematic framework with six steps:

1. **Evaluate current situation** — Where are we now? (marketing audit)
2. **Identify opportunities and threats** — SWOT analysis
3. **Set marketing objectives** — Specific, measurable goals
4. **Develop marketing strategy** — Segmentation, targeting, positioning
5. **Design the marketing mix** — Product, price, promotion, place
6. **Implement and evaluate** — Execute and measure results

### Market Segmentation

Market segmentation divides a broad market into subsets of consumers with common needs or characteristics.

**Segmentation bases for agricultural markets:**

| Basis | Consumer Markets | Business-to-Business (Farm) Markets |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------------------------|
| Geographic | Region, urban/rural, climate zone | Farm location, soil type, growing region |
| Demographic | Age, income, family size | Farm size, enterprise type, sales volume |
| Psychographic | Lifestyle, values, attitudes | Management style, technology adoption |
| Behavioural | Usage rate, brand loyalty, benefits sought | Purchase frequency, loyalty, service needs |

**Technology adopter categories** (Rogers' Diffusion of Innovations):

| Category | % of Market | Characteristics |
|----------|-------------|----------------|
| Innovators | 2.5% | Risk-takers, first to adopt, often well-funded |
| Early adopters | 13.5% | Opinion leaders, respected by peers |
| Early majority | 34% | Deliberate, adopt before average |
| Late majority | 34% | Sceptical, adopt only after most others |
| Laggards | 16% | Traditional, last to adopt, often price-sensitive |

### Positioning

Positioning is the act of designing the organisation's offering and image to occupy a distinctive place in the mind of the target market. Effective positioning communicates:

- What the product/service **is**
- Who it is **for**
- Why it is **better** than alternatives

Example: A Ugandan agro-input company might position itself as "the most reliable source of certified seed in the region" — emphasising quality assurance and local availability.

## The Marketing Mix (Four Ps)

### 1. Product Strategy

#### The Total Product Concept

Products have four layers of value:

1. **Generic product** — The basic commodity or product (e.g., nitrogen fertiliser)
2. **Expected product** — Features customers expect as standard (e.g., consistent analysis, timely delivery)
3. **Value-added product** — Benefits beyond expectations (e.g., soil testing, agronomic advice, custom application)
4. **Potential product** — Future possibilities (e.g., precision application using GPS, variable-rate technology)

#### The Value Equation

```
Customer Value = Benefits Received / Price Paid

Where benefits include:
- Product performance and quality
- Services provided (delivery, technical support, credit)
- Information and expertise
- Relationship and trust
- Brand image and reputation
```

To increase value, a firm can either increase the numerator (add benefits) or decrease the denominator (lower price). The strongest competitive positions are built by increasing benefits rather than cutting price.

#### Product Life Cycle

| Stage | Characteristics | Marketing Strategy |
|-------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Introduction** | Low sales, high costs, limited competition | Build awareness, target innovators, skimming or penetration pricing |
| **Growth** | Rapidly rising sales, competitors enter | Expand distribution, differentiate, build brand preference |
| **Maturity** | Sales plateau, intense competition | Defend market share, reduce costs, find new segments |
| **Decline** | Falling sales, shrinking margins | Harvest profits, reduce spending, phase out or revitalise |

### 2. Pricing Strategy

#### Price Boundaries

```
Upper limit: Price at which customer perceives NO value (switches to substitute)
    |
    |  <-- Pricing range -->
    |
Lower limit: Price that does NOT cover the firm's costs
```

#### Pricing Approaches

| Strategy | Description | When to Use |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Cost-plus** | Add standard markup to cost | Commodity products, stable markets |
| **Value-based** | Price reflects perceived value to customer | Differentiated products, strong brand |
| **Competitive** | Match or undercut competitor prices | Commodity markets, price-sensitive segments |
| **Penetration** | Set low price to gain market share | New market entry, economies of scale needed |
| **Skimming** | Set high initial price, lower over time | Innovative products, limited competition |
| **Loss leader** | Price below cost on select items | Retail traffic generation |

#### Advertising Budget Guidelines

- Local farm-oriented businesses: typically 1-2% of total sales
- Larger organisations and consumer goods companies: 5-10% of total sales
- Standard marketing adage: prospects must see or hear a message an average of 7 times before taking action

### 3. Promotion Strategy

#### The Promotion Mix

| Tool | Best For | Cost Level |
|------|----------|-----------|
| **Advertising** | Mass awareness, brand building | High (media) but low per contact |
| **Personal selling** | Complex products, relationship building | Very high per contact |
| **Sales promotion** | Short-term sales boost, trial generation | Variable |
| **Public relations** | Credibility, community goodwill | Low direct cost |
| **Digital/e-commerce** | Reach, information delivery, convenience | Moderate, highly trackable |

#### Personal Selling in Agribusiness

Personal selling is often the most important promotional tool in agribusiness because:
- Products are technically complex (seed genetics, crop protection, animal nutrition)
- Purchase decisions involve significant financial commitment
- Customers value trusted advisory relationships
- After-sale service and support are critical

**Key sales activities:** prospecting, pre-approach research, approach, presentation, handling objections, closing, and follow-up service.

### 4. Place (Distribution) Strategy

#### Major Distribution Channels

**For agricultural inputs:**

1. **Direct system** — Manufacturer sells directly to producer
   - Examples: seed companies selling direct, online input purchases
   - Advantage: higher margin, direct customer relationship
   - Disadvantage: requires large sales force, extensive logistics

2. **Dealer-distribution system** — Manufacturer to dealer/retailer to producer
   - Most common channel for farm inputs in East Africa
   - Examples: agro-dealer shops, farm supply stores, retail co-ops
   - Advantage: local presence, customer service, inventory access

3. **Distributor system** — Manufacturer to wholesaler/distributor to dealer to producer
   - Used when manufacturers lack distribution infrastructure
   - Advantage: leverages existing distribution networks
   - Types: merchant-distributors (take title), agent-distributors/brokers (earn commission)

**For food products:**

- Manufacturer to retailer (supermarket chains)
- Manufacturer to wholesaler to independent retailer
- Manufacturer to food service distributor to restaurants/institutions
- Direct-to-consumer (farm shops, farmers' markets, e-commerce)

#### Logistics Considerations

Key factors in distribution channel decisions:
- **Cost and efficiency** — Transport, warehousing, inventory carrying costs
- **Customer access** — Geographic coverage, convenience
- **Timeliness** — Especially critical for perishable products
- **Control** — Over pricing, brand presentation, customer relationship
- **Freshness** — Cold chain management for food products

## Tools for Marketing Decisions

### The Marketing Audit

A five-step objective examination of a firm's entire marketing programme:

1. **Analyse market success** of each product/service — market potential, market share, factors driving position
2. **Review market conditions** — customer trends, competitive landscape, regulatory changes
3. **Identify distinctive competencies** — what the firm does better than competitors
4. **Evaluate marketing plan and objectives** — are goals realistic and aligned with resources?
5. **Study human resources and organisation** — does the marketing team have the right skills and structure?

### Sales Forecasting

**Methods from simple to sophisticated:**

| Method | Approach | Best For |
|--------|----------|---------|
| **Sales force composite** | Aggregate individual sales rep estimates | Firms with experienced sales teams |
| **Customer survey** | Ask customers about purchase intentions | B2B markets with identifiable buyers |
| **Trend analysis** | Extrapolate historical sales data | Stable markets with consistent patterns |
| **Moving average** | Average of recent periods | Smoothing seasonal fluctuations |
| **Expert opinion** | Panel of industry experts (Delphi method) | New products, uncertain markets |
| **Regression analysis** | Statistical model of demand drivers | Data-rich environments |

### Competitor Analysis

#### Competitor Strength and Weakness Grid

| Factor | Our Firm | Competitor A | Competitor B |
|--------|----------|-------------|-------------|
| Product quality | Strong | Strong | Moderate |
| Price competitiveness | Moderate | Strong | Strong |
| Distribution coverage | Strong | Moderate | Weak |
| Technical support | Strong | Moderate | Moderate |
| Brand recognition | Moderate | Strong | Weak |
| Financial resources | Moderate | Strong | Moderate |

Rate each factor on a scale (e.g., weak/moderate/strong or 1-5) and identify where competitive advantages and vulnerabilities exist.

### Customer Analysis Methods

| Method | Description | Sample Size | Cost |
|--------|-------------|-------------|------|
| **Focus groups** | Moderated group discussion (6-12 participants) | Small (1-5 groups) | Moderate |
| **Customer surveys** | Structured questionnaires (mail, phone, online) | Large (100-1,000+) | Variable |
| **In-depth interviews** | One-on-one detailed conversations | Small (10-30) | High |
| **Observation** | Watch customer behaviour in natural settings | Variable | Low-Moderate |
| **Market mapping** | Visual plotting of market positions and segments | N/A | Low |

#### Gap Analysis

Gap analysis compares customer expectations with actual performance to identify improvement priorities:

```
Performance Gap = Customer Expectation Score - Actual Performance Score

Positive gap = Exceeding expectations (potential over-investment)
Zero gap     = Meeting expectations
Negative gap = Falling short (priority for improvement)
```

Large negative gaps on factors that are highly important to customers represent the most critical areas for action.

## Agricultural Commodity Marketing

### Unique Characteristics of Agricultural Markets

1. **Seasonality** — Production concentrated in specific seasons; demand year-round
2. **Perishability** — Products deteriorate; require cold chain, preservation, or timely sale
3. **Biological variability** — Weather, disease, and pests create supply uncertainty
4. **Derived demand** — Input demand depends on commodity prices and farm profitability
5. **Price volatility** — Commodity prices fluctuate based on global supply/demand
6. **Government intervention** — Subsidies, price supports, trade policies affect markets

### Supply and Demand Determinants

**Six supply shifters:**
1. Change in technology (e.g., new seed varieties increase yield)
2. Change in input prices (e.g., rising diesel costs)
3. Weather conditions (drought, floods)
4. Prices of alternative products that can be produced
5. Government subsidies or taxes
6. Number of suppliers in the market

**Five demand shifters:**
1. Consumer income levels
2. Tastes and preferences (e.g., organic, local, low-carb trends)
3. Consumer expectations about future prices
4. Number of buyers (population growth, new export markets)
5. Prices of substitute or complementary products

### Cross-Price Elasticity Applications

```
Cross-price elasticity < 0  -->  Complement goods (hamburgers and buns)
Cross-price elasticity = 0  -->  Independent goods (coffee and ketchup)
Cross-price elasticity > 0  -->  Substitute goods (Coke and Pepsi)
```

Grocers study these relationships to optimise weekly promotions — putting hamburger buns on sale increases hamburger meat sales (complement effect).
