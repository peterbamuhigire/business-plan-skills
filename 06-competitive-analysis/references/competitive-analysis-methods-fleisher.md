## 1. FAROUT Evaluation Framework

Before selecting any analysis method, evaluate it against six quality criteria. Fleisher & Bensoussan's FAROUT framework rates every technique on a Low/Medium/High scale:

| Criterion | What It Tests |
|---|---|
| **F**uture-orientation | Does the output look forward, or is it trapped in historical data? |
| **A**ccuracy | How closely do outputs match actual conditions? |
| **R**esource efficiency | Does the insight generated justify the cost of collection? |
| **O**bjectivity | Is the analysis free from organisational or cognitive bias? |
| **U**sefulness | Is the output actionable for the decision maker? |
| **T**imeliness | Can the method produce results before the decision window closes? |

**Application:** Use FAROUT to justify method selection to investors or clients. When combining methods (triangulation), show how each method's weaknesses are covered by another's strengths.

## 2. Strategic and Industry Analysis Methods

### 2.1 Industry Analysis — The Nine Forces Model
**What it is:** An extension of Porter's Five Forces that adds four macro-level forces: government, social, technology, and international. Assesses structural attractiveness of an industry across nine dimensions.

**Trigger:** Use at the start of any business plan to establish whether the industry structure supports profitable entry and growth.

**Key outputs:** Force-by-force assessment (High/Medium/Low), weighted industry attractiveness score, list of forces requiring monitoring.

**Limitations:** Nine force weighting requires judgement; government and social forces are harder to quantify; static snapshot unless periodically updated.

**FAROUT:** Future-orientation Medium; Accuracy Medium; Resource efficiency Medium; Objectivity Medium; Usefulness High; Timeliness Medium.

---

### 2.2 Competitive Positioning Analysis
**What it is:** Maps a firm's current market position relative to competitors on two key dimensions (e.g. price vs. quality; breadth vs. specialisation) and identifies strategic options — build, maintain, defend, or withdraw.

**Trigger:** Use when defining go-to-market positioning or when a competitor has shifted position.

**Key outputs:** Positioning map (perceptual map), four strategic options with implications, recommended repositioning moves.

**Limitations:** Dimension selection is subjective; customer perception data may be unavailable in thin-data markets; map can oversimplify multi-dimensional rivalry.

---

### 2.3 Business Model Analysis
**What it is:** Eight-step deconstruction of a firm's or competitor's business model: value proposition → target segment → competitors → value chain and cost model → value network → revenue model → critical success factors → analysis grid. Identifies how value is created and captured.

**Trigger:** Use when assessing a new venture's viability, auditing a competitor's model, or stress-testing a pivot.

**Key outputs:** Completed analysis grid for each competitor, revenue model comparison, value chain architecture map, critical success factor list.

**Limitations:** Requires significant secondary research on competitors; data on private firms' cost structures is rarely public; disruptive threats from outside the industry can be missed.

---

### 2.4 SERVO Analysis
**What it is:** Integrative internal assessment across five dimensions — Strategy, Environment, Resources, Values, and Organisation. Tests alignment and identifies gaps that prevent strategy execution.

**Trigger:** Use when diagnosing why a firm is underperforming despite a sound strategy, or before a major strategic shift.

**Key outputs:** SERVO alignment map, identified misalignments by dimension, strategic options for resolving gaps.

**Limitations:** Qualitative; requires insider knowledge of the firm; can become a "laundry list" without disciplined facilitation.

---

### 2.5 McKinsey 7S Analysis
**What it is:** Seven interconnected organisational elements — Strategy, Structure, Systems, Shared Values, Style, Staff, and Skills — assessed for internal fit and alignment. Shared Values sit at the centre as the integrating force.

**Trigger:** Use when diagnosing organisational capability gaps that threaten strategic execution, or when assessing an acquisition target.

**Key outputs:** 7S alignment matrix, identification of the most misaligned element, change management priority list.

**Limitations:** No explicit competitive dimension; strong on diagnosis, weak on prescription; can miss external threats.

**Case study in book:** Kenya Airways privatisation in the new millennium.

---

### 2.6 Critical Success Factors (CSF) Analysis
**What it is:** Identifies the small number of performance areas where excellent results are necessary to outcompete in an industry. Uses Leidecker and Bruno's eight identification techniques (industry analysis, competitive intelligence, industry experts, industry leaders, temporal factors, company-specific factors, environmental scanning, intuition/judgement).

**Trigger:** Use when defining what a business must do exceptionally well to win — feeds directly into the competitive advantage and operations sections.

**Key outputs:** Ranked list of 3–7 CSFs with justification, CSF importance weighting, CSF gap assessment (firm vs. best-in-class).

**Limitations:** CSFs change over time; expert-derived CSFs may reflect hindsight bias; firms may confuse inputs with outcomes.

---

### 2.7 Supply Chain Management (SCM) Analysis
**What it is:** Analyses the flow of materials, information, and finances through the supply chain from raw material to end customer. Uses the SCOR (Supply Chain Operations Reference) model as a planning framework. Identifies vulnerabilities, redundancies, and cost optimisation opportunities.

**Trigger:** Use when operations planning requires supply chain risk assessment, when sourcing strategy is a competitive differentiator, or when assessing a competitor's cost advantage.

**Key outputs:** Supply chain map, SCOR-aligned process assessment, vulnerability register, cost-per-link analysis.

**Limitations:** Requires data on tier-2 and tier-3 suppliers that is rarely available; SCOR model is complex to implement for SMEs.

---

### 2.8 Strategic Relationship Analysis (SRA)
**What it is:** Maps and analyses a firm's strategic alliances, joint ventures, partnerships, and supplier/customer relationships. Also used to assess a competitor's relationship network as an intelligence source. Supported by network visualisation software.

**Trigger:** Use when assessing a competitor's hidden strengths (relationship capital), when planning partnership strategy, or when identifying who a target firm relies upon.

**Key outputs:** Relationship network diagram, relationship formation readiness assessment, competitor relationship map, vulnerability points in rival networks.

**Limitations:** Relationship quality (not just existence) is hard to assess from outside; rapidly evolving field — best practices change quickly.

## 3. Competitor Intelligence Methods

### 3.1 Competitor Profiling
**What it is:** Structured deep-dive into a competitor's goals, strategy, capabilities, and likely future moves using Porter's four-component framework (future goals, current strategy, assumptions, capabilities). Produces an actionable intelligence brief.

**Trigger:** Use for each significant competitor during competitive analysis section development.

**Key outputs:** Completed competitor profile (strengths, weaknesses, likely responses, threat level), prediction of competitor's next move.

**Limitations:** Requires significant secondary research; private firm data is scarce; analyst assumptions about competitor motives may be wrong.

---

### 3.2 Shadowing
**What it is:** Creates internal "shadow teams" that think and act as specific competitors. Each team deeply adopts a competitor's strategy, incentives, and culture, then plays out competitive scenarios. Produces insights unavailable from conventional desk research.

**Trigger:** Use before a major product launch, market entry, or pricing decision where competitor reaction is critical.

**Key outputs:** Competitor shadow market plan, predicted competitive responses by scenario, blind-spot identification for each competitor.

**Limitations:** Expensive and time-intensive; requires team members who can genuinely inhabit competitor mindset; findings can be too optimistic about own position.

---

### 3.3 Win/Loss Analysis
**What it is:** Systematic post-sales review of both won and lost deals through structured interviews with customers and prospects. Identifies what drove the decision and what the competitor did better or worse.

**Trigger:** Use when sales win rate is declining, when entering a new segment, or when a new competitor is winning deals.

**Key outputs:** Win/loss ratio by segment, ranked decision criteria from buyer perspective, specific competitor strengths that drove losses, actionable improvements.

**Limitations:** Customers may not reveal true reasons for purchase; survivor bias (only contactable customers respond); requires disciplined interview process with neutral interviewer.

---

### 3.4 Corporate Reputation Analysis
**What it is:** Measures and monitors a firm's or competitor's reputation across multiple stakeholder groups using instruments like the Reputation Quotient (RQ). Tracks media coverage, stakeholder surveys, and reputation management activity.

**Trigger:** Use when brand/reputation is a key competitive differentiator, when a reputational crisis has occurred, or when evaluating an acquisition target.

**Key outputs:** Reputation score vs. competitors, stakeholder perception map, reputation risk register, media monitoring baseline.

**Limitations:** Reputation measurement instruments have varying reliability; correlation with financial performance is context-dependent; requires longitudinal data for trend analysis.

---

### 3.5 Product Line Analysis
**What it is:** Audits the firm's or a competitor's product portfolio against the product life cycle (PLC) — introduction, growth, maturity, decline. Assesses portfolio balance, cannibalisation risk, and strategic product investment priorities.

**Trigger:** Use when assessing product strategy, portfolio rationalisation, or competitive product differentiation.

**Key outputs:** Product-by-PLC-stage map, portfolio balance assessment, investment/harvest/exit recommendations, gap map vs. competitor portfolio.

**Limitations:** PLC stage determination is often contested; ignores market segment variation within a product category; can oversimplify complex multi-product portfolios.

---

### 3.6 Benchmarking Analysis
**What it is:** Systematic comparison of the firm's processes, costs, or performance against best-in-class organisations (internal, competitive, functional, or generic benchmarking). Produces objective stretch goals and identifies the improvement gap.

**Trigger:** Use when setting performance targets, identifying operational improvement priorities, or demonstrating to investors that management knows industry standards.

**Key outputs:** Current performance baseline, best-practice benchmark, improvement gap table, action plan.

**Limitations:** "Copycat syndrome" risk — copying what worked elsewhere without understanding context; benchmarking targets may be achieved but context may differ; best performers may not share data; resource-intensive if done rigorously.

---

### 3.7 Event and Timeline Analysis
**What it is:** Plots key events in a competitor's history on a timeline to identify patterns — strategic pivots, product launches, management changes, funding rounds, crises. Constructs a chronological event matrix and conducts causal factor analysis.

**Trigger:** Use when profiling a new competitor, assessing a merger/acquisition target, or identifying the pattern behind a competitor's strategic moves.

**Key outputs:** Annotated timeline, event pattern analysis, identified causal factors, predicted next events.

**Limitations:** Relies on publicly available records; private firm histories are sparse in East Africa; causation is inferred, not proven.

## 4. Financial and Quantitative Methods

### 4.1 Competitor Cash Flow Analysis
**What it is:** Analyses a competitor's historical and projected cash flows from public financial statements or estimated from market data. Assesses their capacity to fund competitive responses, sustain price wars, or invest in new capabilities.

**Trigger:** Use when assessing whether a competitor can realistically respond to your market entry or pricing move.

**Key outputs:** Competitor operating cash flow estimate, free cash flow capacity, debt service burden, investment capacity assessment.

**Limitations:** Requires published accounts (unavailable for most African SMEs and private firms); estimation from proxies introduces significant uncertainty; cash flow analysis is a lagging indicator.

---

### 4.2 Interpretation of Statistical Analysis
**What it is:** Framework for applying and correctly interpreting quantitative methods in competitive analysis: percentage changes, percentiles, normal distribution, central tendency, standard deviation, regression analysis, correlation, and p-values.

**Trigger:** Use when analysing market data, sales trends, survey results, or any claim involving numbers.

**Key outputs:** Correctly interpreted statistics with confidence ranges, regression-derived relationships, correlation-causation distinctions, statistically valid conclusions.

**Limitations:** Assumes access to sufficient data; misapplication of statistical tests is common; correlation findings are frequently (incorrectly) reported as causation.

## 5. Environmental and Futures Methods

### 5.1 Country Risk Analysis
**What it is:** Systematic assessment of six risk dimensions for an international market entry or cross-border investment: economic risk, transfer risk, exchange risk, location/neighbourhood risk, sovereign risk, and political risk.

**Trigger:** Use for any export, regional expansion, or investment beyond the home market.

**Key outputs:** Six-dimension risk rating per target country, risk-ranked country shortlist, mitigation strategies by risk type.

**Limitations:** Country risk is inherently difficult to predict with accuracy; ratings from commercial providers (PRS, EIU) may lag reality; political risk models often fail to predict sudden events.

---

### 5.2 Driving Forces Analysis
**What it is:** Identifies the external forces (technological, economic, social, regulatory, competitive, demographic) that are fundamentally changing an industry's structure and rules of competition. Assesses direction and magnitude of each force's impact.

**Trigger:** Use at the start of any business plan to establish the macro-level tailwinds and headwinds that will shape the market over the plan horizon (3–5 years).

**Key outputs:** Ranked driving forces list, impact direction (accelerating/decelerating), strategic implications, monitoring triggers.

**Limitations:** Distinguishing true driving forces from trends requires experience; forces interact non-linearly; relative weighting is subjective.

---

### 5.3 Technology Forecasting
**What it is:** Structured methods for anticipating direction and rate of technology change. Covers eight techniques: Delphi (expert consensus), trend extrapolation, growth curves (S-curves), historical analogy, scenario analysis, morphological analysis, relevance trees, and technology monitoring.

**Trigger:** Use when technology adoption or disruption risk is a material factor in the business plan's assumptions.

**Key outputs:** Technology trajectory forecast, S-curve stage assessment, adoption timeline, strategic options for each scenario.

**Limitations:** Technology forecasts are notoriously difficult to calibrate; Delphi requires access to credible experts; long-range forecasts (>5 years) have poor track records.

---

### 5.4 War Gaming
**What it is:** A structured simulation in which teams role-play as competitors, customers, and the firm itself to explore how a strategy plays out under competitive pressure. A 6-step process: assess fit → launch → assemble teams → prepare → play → debrief.

**Trigger:** Use before a major strategic decision — market entry, product launch, pricing change, acquisition — where competitive reaction will determine success.

**Key outputs:** Competitor response playbooks, identified vulnerabilities in own strategy, strategy refinements, early warning indicators.

**Limitations:** Quality depends heavily on facilitator skill and team's willingness to play competitor roles honestly; resource-intensive; may produce false confidence if teams play too defensively.

---

### 5.5 Indications and Warning (I&W) Analysis
**What it is:** Establishes a system of observable indicators that signal an impending competitive or strategic threat. Four steps: establish indicator set → assign meaning → validate and rank against thresholds → recommend responses.

**Trigger:** Use to create the early warning dashboard in a risk analysis or monitoring and evaluation plan.

**Key outputs:** Indicator library with threshold levels, early warning dashboard, response protocols by trigger.

**Limitations:** Indicator lists can grow unwieldy; threshold levels require calibration; false positives erode trust in the system; requires ongoing data collection to be useful.

---

### 5.6 Historiographical Analysis
**What it is:** Uses rigorous historical research methods — primary and secondary source collection, source validation, and critical narrative — to reconstruct a competitor's or industry's past in order to draw forward-looking conclusions.

**Trigger:** Use when understanding the historical evolution of an industry or competitor is essential to predicting its future trajectory.

**Key outputs:** Validated historical narrative, identified strategic patterns, lessons from industry history, propositions for future testing.

**Limitations:** Requires significant archival research; primary sources may not exist in East African markets; risk of interpreting past patterns as deterministic of the future.

---

## 6. Analytical Process Methods

### 6.1 Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH)
**What it is:** Structured method for evaluating multiple competing explanations of a competitive situation. An 8-step process: identify hypotheses → list evidence → build hypothesis × evidence matrix → refine → draw tentative conclusions → sensitivity analysis → report → identify future milestones.

**Trigger:** Use when multiple interpretations of a market situation are plausible and the cost of being wrong is high — e.g. "Is this competitor preparing to enter our segment, or are they distracted internally?"

**Key outputs:** Ranked hypotheses by likelihood, evidence-hypothesis matrix, sensitivity to critical assumptions, monitoring milestones.

**Limitations:** Requires disciplined facilitation to avoid confirmation bias; only as good as the hypotheses initially identified; evidence quality determines output quality.

---

### 6.2 Linchpin Analysis
**What it is:** Identifies the single most critical assumption (the "linchpin") that the entire analysis rests on. If that assumption is wrong, the conclusions collapse. Includes linchpin assumption sensitivity analysis and a process for assessing linchpin-related risk.

**Trigger:** Use as a final check on any major analysis before presenting to decision makers. Ask: "What single assumption, if wrong, would invalidate this conclusion?"

**Key outputs:** Identified linchpin assumption(s), sensitivity analysis, risk assessment if linchpin fails, monitoring protocol.

**Limitations:** Analysts often resist identifying their linchpin (it makes vulnerability explicit); identifying the right linchpin requires experience; multiple interacting linchpins may exist.

## 9. Uganda / East Africa Application Notes

### Data Scarcity
Most East African markets lack the public company filings, industry association reports, and market research data that these methods assume in their original Western context. Practical adaptations:

- **Competitor profiling:** Build profiles from physical observation (visiting competitor premises), job listings (reveal capabilities and growth plans), social media activity, supplier conversations, and customer interviews. OSINT replaces desk research.
- **Win/loss analysis:** Adapt for relationship-based sales. Use casual debrief conversations rather than formal structured interviews. Uganda buyers rarely explain decisions candidly in formal settings; trust-based follow-up yields better data.
- **Benchmarking:** Use regional peers (Kenya, Rwanda, Tanzania) rather than global comparators. East African industry associations (e.g. USSIA, UMA, UNCCI) and development institution reports (IFC CPSD, UBOS) provide baseline data.

### Informal Competition
A significant share of competition in Uganda and East Africa comes from the informal sector — operators who do not appear in any database, do not file accounts, and have no digital presence. Methods must be adapted:

- **Nine Forces:** Include informal sector as a competitive force. Informals typically compete on price; their cost advantage is low overhead, not efficiency. Assess whether formalisation is a mobility barrier (can informals easily register and comply?).
- **Driving Forces:** Formalisation, mobile money adoption (MTN Mobile Money / Airtel Money), and NIN/BRN requirements are Uganda-specific driving forces that reshape competition.
- **Country Risk:** Uganda-specific dimensions include EFRIS compliance penalties (UGX 8M/month), currency depreciation risk (UGX 3,550/$1 current; plan at UGX 3,700), and political economy risk around import levies and sector-specific regulation.

### Relationship Intelligence
In EA markets, the most valuable competitive intelligence comes from the "community knowledge layer" — suppliers, distributors, former employees, and customers who share information through relationship networks. Strategic Relationship Analysis (Section 2.8) and Corporate Reputation Analysis (Section 3.4) are particularly effective when adapted to social network mapping rather than formal database searches.

### Mobile-First Research
Use WhatsApp broadcast lists and Facebook groups for informal win/loss surveys. Mobile data collection tools (KoboToolbox, ODK) make primary data collection feasible even in rural Uganda. Google My Business reviews are an underused competitive intelligence source for Kampala businesses.

### Regional Expansion
For EAC expansion (Kenya, Tanzania, Rwanda, Burundi, DRC), Country Risk Analysis (Section 5.1) must be combined with `country-context/{country}/SKILL.md` for regulatory and financial context. No work permit is required for EAC nationals working in any EAC member state (EAC Common Market Protocol, Art. 10).
