> Fahey, L. & Randall, R.M. (eds.) (2000). *The Portable MBA in Strategy* (2nd ed.). Wiley.
> Chapters 5 (Dahame) and 8 (Collis & Ghemawat)

## 1. Layered Competitive Advantage

Competitive advantage must be thought of as existing in layers, like the skins of an onion. A company's offerings can be better than competitors' on many different dimensions — price, delivery time, quality, technology, aesthetics. The more layers that contribute to competitive advantage, the more sustainable the business's market dominance (Dahame, in Fahey & Randall, 2000, Ch.5).

**Five principles of layered competitive advantage (Dahame):**

1. **Always think in relative terms** — What matters is not how good the product is in absolute terms, but whether it is perceived as superior to competitors' offerings. Example: General Motors reduced defects from 110 per 100 new cars (1980) to 40 per 100 cars (1990) — a remarkable improvement. But Japanese manufacturers improved from 43 to 17 defects per 100 cars over the same period. In relative terms, GM was still at a competitive disadvantage in 1990 despite significant absolute improvement.

2. **Be explicit about both advantages and disadvantages** — No business can be superior to all competitors on every criterion; such a player would need 100% market share. Every business has some dimensions of relative advantage, some of relative disadvantage, and some of competitive parity. Ignoring disadvantages leads to overestimating the impact of advantages and missing improvement priorities.

3. **Strive for multiple sources** — The obsolete strategy canon said: choose either cost leadership OR differentiation. The reality: even the most premium businesses (e.g., Mercedes-Benz) must practise passionate cost control — not to lower prices, but to reinvest savings in product improvement. Both cost control and differentiation apply simultaneously.

4. **Align onstage and backstage** — see Section 2 below.

5. **Expect the terms of competition to shift** — The quartz watch example: before quartz, timekeeping accuracy was a primary differentiator. After quartz became universal, accuracy was a commodity and the differentiating factor shifted to design and features. Honda's reliability advantage was copied by all major competitors by the late 1990s, and styling became the new battleground.

## 2. The Onstage / Backstage Model

This is the most actionable framework from Chapter 5. Competitive advantage exists simultaneously at two levels:

**Onstage advantage** — competitive advantage in terms of customer buying criteria. What customers evaluate and compare: price, quality, delivery speed, aesthetics, brand, service. The "theatre" the customer sees.

**Backstage advantage** — competitive advantage at the level of the underlying resources and competencies that drive onstage performance. Cost structure, process efficiency, supply chain management, organisational capabilities, talent. The "backstage" the customer never sees but which determines what is possible onstage.

**The alignment rule:** Onstage advantages cannot be sustained without matching backstage advantages. Launching a competitive attack without first verifying backstage alignment is almost always disastrous.

**Pepsi vs. Coke case (verbatim logic from text):** Suppose it is 1975 and Pepsi wants to steal supermarket share from Coke. Options: cut prices, increase dealer margins, increase advertising spend. All of these invite retaliation — and the backstage driver of all three moves is cost structure. If Coke's cost structure is lower, Coke will win every onstage price/margin/advertising war because it can sustain those moves longer. Pepsi's correct move: find an onstage battlefield where Coke is backstage-disadvantaged. Answer: blind taste tests — consumer taste preference is behaviourally sticky, and Coke cannot reformulate without alienating loyal customers. Pepsi found an onstage attack that Coke was backstage-frozen to match.

**Application to business plans:**
- In the competitive analysis, list both onstage criteria (what customers compare) and backstage capabilities (what drives each criterion)
- Identify where the business has backstage advantage that competitors cannot easily replicate
- Identify where the business has onstage claims without backstage support — these are the vulnerabilities

**Onstage / Backstage Mapping Template:**

| Onstage Criterion | Our Position | Competitor Position | Backstage Driver | Backstage Support? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Price | Lower / Same / Higher | — | Cost structure, sourcing | Yes / Partial / No |
| Quality | Superior / Parity / Inferior | — | Process capability, QC system | Yes / Partial / No |
| Delivery speed | Faster / Same / Slower | — | Logistics, inventory model | Yes / Partial / No |
| Brand/trust | Stronger / Parity / Weaker | — | Marketing investment, track record | Yes / Partial / No |
| After-sale service | Better / Same / Worse | — | Service infrastructure, staff training | Yes / Partial / No |

Any row where Backstage Support = No or Partial is a strategic vulnerability that the business plan must address.

## 3. The Purchase and Consumption Cycle

The purchase cycle is a 13-step analytical tool for identifying non-obvious competitive advantage points. Each step is a potential differentiation point that most competitors ignore because they focus only on the core product (Dahame, Ch.5).

| Step | Question | Differentiation Opportunity |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Need recognition | How does the customer realise they need the product? | Trigger marketing, reminders, seasonal prompts |
| 2. Needs definition | How does the customer define their requirements? | Consultative selling, needs assessment tools |
| 3. Alternatives identification | How does the customer identify options? | SEO, referral networks, directory listings |
| 4. Vendor choice | How does the customer choose between suppliers? | Social proof, trust signals, demonstration |
| 5. Order placement | How does the customer place the order? | Ease of ordering, payment flexibility |
| 6. Payment | How does the customer pay? | Credit terms, mobile money, instalment options |
| 7. Enquiry follow-up | How does the customer track their order? | Status updates, proactive communication |
| 8. Delivery | How are goods or services delivered? | Speed, reliability, delivery experience |
| 9. Storage | How are delivered goods stored? | Packaging design, storage instructions |
| 10. Use | How are the products used? | User support, documentation, training |
| 11. Maintenance/upgrade | How are products maintained? | After-sale service, upgrade paths |
| 12. Replacement decision | How does the customer decide when to replace? | Proactive refresh suggestions, loyalty signals |
| 13. Disposal | How does the customer dispose of old product? | Take-back programmes, recycling |

**Strategic insight:** Most businesses compete only on steps 3–5 (identification through order). Companies that differentiate on steps 1–2 (triggering the need) or steps 7–13 (after the sale) typically face far less competition and build deeper customer relationships.

**Audit question for business plans:** Which steps in the purchase and consumption cycle does this business address that direct competitors do not? If the answer is only steps 3–5, the competitive analysis needs to explore differentiation opportunities further along the cycle.

## 4. Value Chain Architecture — Design and Disruption

The value chain architecture is the basic design of the value-creation system: how inputs are converted into outputs, and which activities are performed where (Dahame, Ch.5).

**Architecture disruption examples:**

**Nucor Steel:** US Steel used traditional integrated mills — iron ore → ingots → reheat → roll. Nucor used scrap steel + minimill technology → continuous sheet steel in one process. For most of Nucor's 30-year history as a steel maker, its total costs were less than US Steel's variable costs alone.

**IKEA furniture:** Traditional furniture → assembled in factory → shipped to store → customer waits weeks. IKEA → designed for flat-pack → manufactured in low-cost countries (Poland, Thailand) → shipped globally → customer takes it home immediately and self-assembles. Value chain innovation: low shipping costs + customer assembly (outsourced to buyer) + global sourcing.

**Dell Computers:** Competitors → buy components → assemble in factory → ship to retailer → sell to customer. Dell → take order by phone/internet → custom-assemble → ship directly to customer. Result: 7–13 days' inventory vs. 60–100 days for indirect models; 6% lower component costs (fresher stock); zero retailer margin cost; converts sales to cash in under 24 hours vs. 35 days for Compaq.

**Three criteria for value chain architecture choice:**
1. Will this architecture allow serving target customers most effectively and efficiently?
2. Will this architecture allow flexibility to scale, expand/contract, or switch if needed?
3. Will this architecture be difficult for competitors to imitate?

**Value chain design questions for business plans:**
- At which activities does the business add distinctive value vs. activities that are generic?
- Which activities should be performed in-house vs. outsourced (see Section 5)?
- Is the proposed architecture genuinely different from incumbents, or a replication?
- What would a disruptive entrant's architecture look like, and how exposed is the current model?

## 5. Outsourcing Risk Taxonomy

Outsourcing creates competitive risk on three dimensions (Dahame, Ch.5):

**Risk 1 — Non-performance**
The supplier fails to deliver to the required quality, quantity, or timeline. Mitigation: supplier qualification, dual-sourcing, contractual service levels.

**Risk 2 — Disproportionate value expropriation (the IBM/Microsoft/Intel case)**
This is the most dangerous and least discussed risk. IBM outsourced its PC operating system to Microsoft and its microprocessors to Intel — both decisions seemed rational at the time. Result: Microsoft and Intel captured the majority of value in the PC industry while IBM became a hardware assembler in a commodity market. The supplier captured more value than the integrator.

**The expropriation risk rises when:**
- The outsourced activity is in the critical path of value creation for customers
- The supplier develops exclusive relationships with downstream customers
- The supplier's capability becomes harder to replicate over time (learning curves)
- The integrator loses internal capability to evaluate or switch suppliers

**Risk 3 — Elimination**
The supplier integrates forward and becomes a direct competitor. The supplier's knowledge of the integrator's cost structure, customer base, and operations becomes a strategic weapon.

**Outsourcing Risk Assessment Matrix:**

| Outsourced Activity | Risk Type | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Manufacturing | Non-performance | Medium | High | Dual suppliers, SLA |
| Software development | Value expropriation | High | High | IP ownership clauses |
| Distribution/logistics | Non-performance | High | Medium | Multiple carriers |
| Customer service | Non-performance | Medium | Medium | In-house escalation tier |
| Core technology platform | Elimination | Low | Critical | In-house capability retention |

**Application:** When a business plan includes outsourcing of any significant function, the plan must address which risk type applies and what the mitigation is. For Ugandan agri-processors outsourcing logistics, the key risk is usually non-performance. For tech companies outsourcing software development, the key risk is disproportionate value expropriation if the outsourced code becomes the core of the product.

## 6. The Value Net — Adding Complementors to Competitive Analysis

Porter's Five Forces framework has a well-documented gap: it focuses on competitive forces but misses cooperative relationships that also shape industry profitability (Collis & Ghemawat, Ch.8, drawing on Brandenburger & Nalebuff's Value Net framework).

**The Value Net adds a sixth player: Complementors**

Complementors are firms from which buyers obtain complementary products or services, or to whom suppliers sell complementary inputs. Their role mirrors competitors — but in the opposite direction:

- On the demand side: complementors **increase** buyers' willingness to pay for the focal company's products
- On the supply side: complementors **decrease** the price that suppliers require

**Classic examples:**
- Microsoft Windows and Intel processors: each makes the other more valuable. Running Windows on a Pentium processor is faster than on a 486 chip. Microsoft and Intel are complementors — each firm's success depends on the other's continued presence, yet they also compete for value share.
- Pharmaceutical manufacturers and physicians: physicians (who prescribe drugs) are complementors — they increase willingness-to-pay but do not pay directly.
- Game console manufacturers and game developers: Nintendo deliberately fragments its developer base (many small studios) to prevent any developer from gaining enough power to expropriate value.

**Five determinants of complementor power:**

1. **Relative concentration** — More concentrated complementors (fewer, larger) can pursue their own agenda more effectively
2. **Switching costs** — When buyers/suppliers face higher switching costs for complementors than for competitors, complementors have more leverage
3. **Ease of unbundling** — Complements carry less leverage if customers can buy and use the focal product independently
4. **Pull-through capacity** — Complementors who drive significant demand for the core product gain power proportional to that pull
5. **Integration threat asymmetry** — Complementors with credible forward-integration threats (invading the core business) hold stronger positions than those without

**Strategic implication (Brandenburger & Nalebuff):** "Thinking complements is a different way of thinking about business. It's about finding ways to make the pie bigger rather than fighting with competitors over a fixed pie." Cooperation with complementors to expand total market size often creates more value than zero-sum competition.

**Value Net Mapping Template:**

```
                        CUSTOMERS
                            |
SUPPLIERS ——— [FOCAL COMPANY] ——— COMPETITORS
                            |
                      COMPLEMENTORS
```

For each player type, the business plan should answer:

| Player | Who are they? | How do they affect us? | Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Competitors | Direct + indirect substitutes | Reduce our market share / margins | Differentiate, defend, or coexist |
| Suppliers | Input providers | Raise costs, limit supply | Qualify alternatives, dual-source |
| Customers | Target segments | Bargain on price, switch | Build switching costs, loyalty |
| Complementors | Firms whose products increase our value | Expand the market, raise WTP | Partner, co-market, or invest |

**For business plan competitive analysis:**
- Identify complementors alongside competitors — who benefits from the business's success?
- Map the cooperative relationship: what does each party contribute to expanding the market?
- Assess complementor power: could any complementor become a competitor?
- Identify whether the business should proactively develop or invest in complementary offerings

## 7. Integrating the Frameworks — A Competitive Analysis Workflow

The frameworks in this reference file work together in a logical sequence:

**Step 1 — Identify the competitive arena** (Value Net)
Map all five players: customers, suppliers, competitors, complementors, and the focal firm. Understand the full system before focusing on any one relationship.

**Step 2 — Assess relative advantage** (Layered Competitive Advantage, Principle 1)
For each dimension customers care about, score the business relative to the top two or three competitors. Be explicit about where the business is disadvantaged.

**Step 3 — Verify backstage alignment** (Onstage/Backstage Model)
For each claimed onstage advantage, confirm the backstage capability exists. Flag gaps as strategic vulnerabilities.

**Step 4 — Scan the full purchase cycle** (Purchase and Consumption Cycle)
Identify which of the 13 steps competitors have abandoned and where the business can differentiate with lower competitive intensity.

**Step 5 — Stress-test the architecture** (Value Chain Architecture)
Confirm the value chain design meets the three criteria: effective/efficient service, flexibility, and imitability. Consider what a disruptive entrant might do differently.

**Step 6 — Audit outsourcing decisions** (Outsourcing Risk Taxonomy)
For every outsourced activity, assign a risk type and confirm a mitigation is documented in the operations plan (cross-reference: `08-operations-plan`).

**Step 7 — Map complementor relationships** (Value Net)
Confirm no complementor has been misclassified as neutral, and identify whether any complementors could be converted into active partners.

## 8. Uganda / East Africa Application

| Framework | Uganda / EA Application |
|---|---|
| **Relative advantage** | Ugandan businesses frequently benchmark against absolute standards rather than relative competitors. A supermarket with 30-minute checkout may seem slow in absolute terms but be the fastest in the market. Relative positioning is what matters. |
| **Onstage/backstage** | In Ugandan agri-processing, onstage claims (high-quality, timely delivery) often have no backstage support (no certified processing, no logistics system). The business plan must close this gap before investors do. |
| **Purchase cycle** | In Uganda, differentiation on step 6 (payment — mobile money, credit terms) and step 13 (disposal/take-back, e.g., plastic packaging) often creates more loyalty than competing on product quality alone. |
| **Value chain architecture** | IKEA-style flat-pack disruption has a direct East Africa parallel: informal furniture makers in Kampala who deliver custom furniture within 24 hours of a WhatsApp order, at 40–60% of the price of formal retailers. The architecture (local materials + direct WhatsApp commerce + home delivery) is genuinely disruptive. |
| **Outsourcing risk** | In Uganda, the most common outsourcing failure is non-performance (supplier capacity or reliability). Disproportionate value expropriation is rising in tech (offshore software developers who retain code ownership). The business plan must specify IP ownership in any tech outsourcing arrangement — cross-reference `10-financial-projections/references/uganda-ip-framework.md`. |
| **Value Net / Complementors** | Common complementors in Uganda: mobile money providers (MNOs are complementors to any e-commerce business), transport companies (complementors to any goods-delivery business), electricity providers (complementors to cold-chain businesses). Map these explicitly — the business's success may depend on the complementor's reliability more than on direct competition. |
| **Terms of competition shifting** | In Uganda's banking sector, proximity (number of branches) was once the dominant onstage differentiator. Mobile money shifted the battleground to digital access. Any business plan in a sector undergoing digital transition must explicitly address the shifting terms of competition. |

**Uganda-specific outsourcing note:** The Uganda Registration Services Bureau (URSB) requires that IP created by contractors be explicitly assigned to the commissioning company in a written agreement. Without such assignment, the contractor retains ownership by default under the Copyright and Neighbouring Rights Act (Cap.215). Any business plan involving outsourced software, creative content, or product design must include this assignment requirement in the legal/operations section.

## 9. Common Errors in Competitive Analysis — Diagnostic Checklist

These are the most frequent weaknesses in business plan competitive sections, mapped to the frameworks above:

| Error | Framework | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Competitor table lists only direct competitors | Value Net | Add complementors and indirect substitutes |
| All competitive advantages listed, no disadvantages | Principle 2 | Add a "relative disadvantages" column |
| Onstage claims without backstage verification | Onstage/Backstage | Add a backstage capability audit |
| Competitive analysis stops at point-of-sale | Purchase Cycle | Map all 13 steps; flag post-sale differentiation |
| Outsourcing mentioned without risk discussion | Outsourcing Risk | Classify risk type; add mitigation to operations plan |
| Value chain described but not compared to competitors | Architecture | Add an architecture comparison (ours vs. incumbent) |
| Complementors treated as neutral background | Value Net | Assess complementor power and integration threat |
| Advantages described in absolute terms | Principle 1 | Restate every claim as relative to named competitors |
