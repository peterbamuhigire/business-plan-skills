# Strategy Choice Cascade Framework

The Strategy Choice Cascade is a framework for making integrated strategic choices. Strategy is defined as a coordinated and integrated set of five choices — not a vision, plan, or set of best practices — that together produce sustainable competitive advantage.

> Reference: *Playing to Win: How Strategy Really Works* by A.G. Lafley & Roger L. Martin (Harvard Business Review Press, 2013)

## 1. The Strategy Choice Cascade

Strategy is the answer to five interrelated questions, arranged as a reinforcing cascade. Choices at the top set the context for choices below, and choices at the bottom influence and refine the choices above.

### The Five Questions

| # | Question | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **What is our winning aspiration?** | The purpose and motivating aspiration of the enterprise |
| 2 | **Where will we play?** | The playing field — geography, customer segments, channels, product categories, vertical stages |
| 3 | **How will we win?** | The way the business will win on the chosen playing field — cost leadership or differentiation |
| 4 | **What capabilities must be in place?** | The set and configuration of capabilities required to win in the chosen way |
| 5 | **What management systems are required?** | The systems, structures, and measures that enable the capabilities and support the choices |

### How the Cascade Works

- The cascade is **not linear**. It is iterative — insights at one level trigger revisions at other levels.
- Choices reinforce one another. Where-to-play and how-to-win are tightly coupled and form the heart of strategy.
- In larger organisations, cascades are **nested** — company-level choices set constraints for division-level choices, which set constraints for product-level choices, and so on downward.
- A complete cascade can be captured on a single page.

### Worked Example — Ugandan Food Processing Company

**Scenario:** A Kampala-based company processing and packaging dried fruit, vegetable crisps, and grain-based snacks for the East African market.

| Cascade Question | Choice |
|---|---|
| **Winning aspiration** | Become the leading Ugandan-owned healthy snack brand in East Africa within five years, achieving UGX 8 billion in annual revenue and category leadership in dried fruit snacks |
| **Where to play** | *Geography:* Uganda first (Kampala, then upcountry towns), then Kenya and Rwanda. *Segments:* Health-conscious urban consumers aged 20–45, institutional buyers (schools, airlines, hotels). *Channels:* Modern trade (supermarkets), convenience stores, e-commerce. *Products:* Dried fruit, vegetable crisps, grain bars. *Vertical stage:* Sourcing from contract farmers, processing, packaging, and distribution (not retail) |
| **How to win** | Differentiation — superior product quality (no artificial preservatives, locally sourced ingredients), distinctive Ugandan branding, and premium-but-accessible pricing (UGX 3,000–8,000 per pack vs. imported alternatives at UGX 10,000+). Build retail partnerships for dedicated shelf space in the healthy-snacks aisle |
| **Capabilities** | (1) Contract-farmer sourcing network for consistent supply, (2) food-safety-certified processing (UNBS, KEBS), (3) consumer insight and brand building, (4) cold-chain and distribution logistics, (5) product innovation pipeline |
| **Management systems** | Quality management system (HACCP), weekly sales dashboards by channel and SKU, quarterly farmer-relationship reviews, annual brand-health tracking, new-product stage-gate process |

**Key insight:** Each choice constrains and enables the others. The where-to-play choice (health-conscious urban consumers) demands a how-to-win built on quality differentiation, which requires specific capabilities (food-safety certification, sourcing network), which require specific management systems (HACCP, dashboards).

## 2. Five Strategy Traps

Lafley and Martin identify five common ways leaders misdefine strategy. Each produces an incomplete or ineffective approach.

### The Traps and How to Avoid Them

| # | Trap | Description | Why It Fails | How to Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Strategy as vision** | Defining strategy as a mission or vision statement alone | Offers no guide to productive action, no explicit road map, no choices about what businesses to be in or not be in | Translate the vision into specific where-to-play and how-to-win choices. A vision without choices is a wish |
| 2 | **Strategy as plan** | Defining strategy as a detailed operational plan — what the firm will do and when | A plan that specifies activities does not ensure those activities add up to sustainable competitive advantage | Ensure the plan is built on integrated strategic choices, not just a list of initiatives. Ask: does this plan create a unique position? |
| 3 | **Strategy as emergent** | Denying that medium- or long-term strategy is possible and simply responding to threats and opportunities as they arise | Places the company in a reactive mode, making it easy prey for more-strategic rivals. Even in fast-changing markets, Apple, Google, and Microsoft think strategically | Make deliberate choices even amid uncertainty. Revisit and revise them as conditions change, but always have a current set of choices |
| 4 | **Strategy as optimisation** | Defining strategy as optimising current practices and the status quo | Risks exhausting assets by optimising the wrong activities while more-strategic competitors pass you by (e.g. legacy airlines optimising hub-and-spoke while Southwest built point-to-point) | Step back and ask whether the current activities are the right ones before making them more efficient. Optimisation has a place, but it is not strategy |
| 5 | **Strategy as best practices** | Defining strategy as benchmarking against competition and doing the same activities but more effectively | Sameness is not strategy — it is a recipe for mediocrity. If every competitor adopts the same practices, no one gains advantage | Seek distinctive choices. The more your choices look like those of your competitors, the less likely you will ever win |

### Self-Test for Ugandan Businesses

- If your "strategy" is a vision statement on the wall with no specific choices beneath it — you are in **Trap 1**.
- If your "strategy" is an annual budget with a list of projects but no clear competitive logic — you are in **Trap 2**.
- If your response to market changes is always "let's wait and see" — you are in **Trap 3**.
- If your strategic planning meeting is entirely about cutting costs in existing operations — you are in **Trap 4**.
- If your plan is to copy what the market leader does but "do it better" — you are in **Trap 5**.

## 3. Where to Play Choices

Where to play narrows the competitive field. It defines the boundaries within which the business will compete. No company can be all things to all people and still win.

### The Five Dimensions

| Dimension | Key Questions | Ugandan Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Geography** | Which regions, countries, cities, or districts? | Kampala metro only? All of Uganda? East Africa? |
| **Product type** | Which product lines, categories, or services? | Full-service restaurant vs. takeaway only? All beverages vs. juice only? |
| **Consumer segment** | Which demographic, psychographic, or needs-based segments? | Mass market vs. middle-class urban professionals vs. high-net-worth individuals? |
| **Distribution channel** | Which routes to market? | Direct-to-consumer, modern trade supermarkets, traditional market stalls, e-commerce, wholesale? |
| **Vertical stage** | Which stages of the value chain? | Raw material sourcing, manufacturing, distribution, retail, after-sales service? |

### Choosing Where NOT to Play

Equally important is defining where you will **not** play. Explicitly listing exclusions prevents strategic drift and resource dilution.

**Principles:**

- If your answer to "where do we play?" is "everywhere," you have not truly come to grips with the need to choose.
- Large companies that appear to play everywhere (Apple, Toyota) actually make very deliberate where-to-play choices within their seemingly broad scope.
- Refusing to choose, and attempting to play in every field at once, is one of the most common strategic mistakes.

**Template for documenting where-to-play choices:**

```
WHERE WE WILL PLAY:
- Geography: [specific regions/markets]
- Products: [specific categories/lines]
- Segments: [specific customer groups]
- Channels: [specific routes to market]
- Vertical stages: [specific value chain positions]

WHERE WE WILL NOT PLAY (and why):
- Geography: [excluded regions — reason]
- Products: [excluded categories — reason]
- Segments: [excluded groups — reason]
- Channels: [excluded routes — reason]
- Vertical stages: [excluded positions — reason]
```

**Ugandan example — retail chain:** "We will play in Kampala and the five largest upcountry towns (Gulu, Mbarara, Jinja, Mbale, Fort Portal) through company-owned stores targeting middle-income households. We will NOT play in rural trading centres (insufficient purchasing power), we will NOT sell through wholesale (margin erosion), and we will NOT offer financial services (outside core competence)."

## 4. How to Win Choices

Where to play selects the playing field; how to win defines the way the business will win on that field. There are only two generic ways to win: **cost leadership** and **differentiation**.

### Cost Leadership

Profit is driven by having a lower cost structure than competitors, producing comparable products or services at lower cost.

| Characteristic | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Source of advantage** | Lower cost per unit than any competitor |
| **Price flexibility** | Can underprice competitors or reinvest the margin differential |
| **Critical constraint** | There is only one true low-cost player in any industry — the competitor with the very lowest costs. Having lower costs than some but not all competitors is not a winning cost strategy |
| **Ugandan example** | A maize flour processor using high-capacity milling equipment and direct farmer sourcing to produce at UGX 2,800/kg while competitors produce at UGX 3,500/kg. The UGX 700/kg advantage funds either lower retail prices or superior distribution |

### Differentiation

Profit is driven by offering products or services perceived as distinctively more valuable to customers, at a price premium that exceeds any additional cost.

| Characteristic | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Source of advantage** | Higher willingness to pay — customers choose you over competitors at a higher price |
| **Drivers** | Product design, performance, quality, branding, customer experience, distribution |
| **Critical constraint** | Differentiation must be along a dimension consumers actually care about, and the cost of delivering differentiation must not exceed the price premium it commands |
| **Ugandan example** | A Ugandan coffee roaster selling single-origin, traceable Ugandan coffee at UGX 45,000/kg vs. generic blends at UGX 25,000/kg. The premium is sustained by verified farm-to-cup traceability, distinctive packaging, and brand storytelling |

### Joint Consideration

How-to-win must always be considered jointly with where-to-play. A how-to-win strategy that works brilliantly in one market segment may fail entirely in another.

- A differentiation strategy built on premium branding requires a where-to-play choice that targets customers with sufficient willingness to pay.
- A cost-leadership strategy requires a where-to-play choice in markets where price is the primary purchase driver and the business can achieve the lowest cost structure.

### Changing Industry Structure

An important insight from the framework: industry structure is not fixed. A business can change the competitive context to fit its strategic choices. Lafley and Martin cite Red Hat, which gave its software away free to gain dominant market share, then earned revenue from services — reshaping the competitive dynamics of the Linux market entirely.

**Ugandan implication:** A Ugandan fintech start-up might offer zero-fee person-to-person transfers to build scale rapidly, then monetise through merchant payments — changing the competitive dynamics of the mobile money market rather than accepting them as given.

## 5. Activity System Mapping

Core capabilities are best understood not as a list but as a **system of reinforcing activities** — an activity system. The concept, first articulated by Michael Porter, holds that sustainable competitive advantage arises not from any single capability but from a set of capabilities that fit with and reinforce one another.

### Building an Activity System

An activity system is a visual representation of the firm's competitive advantage, capturing on a single page the core capabilities and how they interconnect.

**Steps to create an activity system:**

1. List the 3–6 core capabilities that underpin your where-to-play and how-to-win choices
2. Place each capability in a node on a diagram
3. Draw connections between capabilities that reinforce one another
4. Identify the most critical reinforcing links — these are the hardest for competitors to replicate

### The Three Tests

| Test | Question | Pass Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| **Feasibility** | Can we actually build and operate this activity system? | The organisation has (or can acquire) the resources, talent, and infrastructure needed. If not feasible, reconsider where to play and how to win |
| **Distinctiveness** | Is our activity system different from competitors' systems? | The system delivers value in a way no competitor currently replicates. If it looks like everyone else's, it will not produce competitive advantage |
| **Defensibility** | Can competitors easily replicate our activity system? | Individual capabilities may be copied, but the system as a whole — the way capabilities reinforce one another — is difficult to replicate. If any single capability can be readily replicated or overcome, the overall strategy is not defensible |

### Reverse-Engineering Competitor Systems

A powerful exercise is to reverse-engineer the activity systems of your key competitors:

1. Identify the competitor's apparent where-to-play and how-to-win choices
2. Map out the capabilities that seem to support those choices
3. Identify the reinforcing links between their capabilities
4. Look for gaps — capabilities they lack or links that are weak
5. Design your own activity system to exploit those gaps

**Ugandan example:** A new entrant in packaged drinking water might map a dominant competitor's activity system (large-scale purification plant, nationwide distribution fleet, brand advertising, supermarket shelf agreements). The gaps might be: no direct-to-office delivery, no subscription model, weak digital presence. The new entrant's activity system could be built around these gaps — a smaller, flexible purification unit, a subscription-based delivery service to offices and residences, and a digital ordering platform.

### Indivisible Activity Systems

Start building activity systems at the lowest indivisible level — the level at which the business has a single, coherent set of where-to-play and how-to-win choices. For a multi-product company, this might be at the product or brand level. Aggregate upward from there.

## 6. Strategy Logic Flow

The Strategy Logic Flow is a four-dimension analytical framework with seven questions. It structures the thinking that informs where-to-play and how-to-win choices.

### The Four Dimensions

| Dimension | Focus | Key Questions |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Industry analysis** | The competitive landscape | (a) What are the distinct segments of the industry? (b) What is the structural attractiveness of each segment (using Porter's five forces — supplier power, buyer power, rivalry, threat of entry, threat of substitutes)? |
| **2. Customer analysis** | Channel customers and end consumers | (c) What do our channel customers value? (d) What do our end consumers value? What are their unmet needs? Why do they choose what they choose? |
| **3. Relative position** | The firm's current competitive standing | (e) What are our current capabilities relative to competitors? (f) What is our current cost position relative to competitors? |
| **4. Competitor analysis** | How competitors will react | (g) What will competitors do in response to our strategic choices? |

### Applying the Logic Flow

The flow runs from left to right — industry, then customers, then relative position, then competitive reaction — but requires iterative back-and-forth. Different dimensions will be more or less important in different contexts.

**Industry analysis — segmentation:** Map the industry into distinct segments along geography, product type, channel, and consumer needs. Challenge conventional industry maps — the accepted segmentation may be imperfect or outdated.

**Industry analysis — attractiveness:** Use Porter's five forces to assess the structural attractiveness of each segment. Favour segments with: low supplier power, low buyer power, moderate rivalry, high barriers to entry, and few substitutes.

**Customer analysis:** Understand both channel customers (retailers, distributors) and end consumers. Why do consumers choose what they do? What job are they trying to get done? What are their unmet needs?

**Relative position:** Honestly assess current capabilities and cost position relative to competitors. Where does the business have genuine advantage? Where is it disadvantaged?

**Competitor analysis:** Predict how competitors will respond. Will they retaliate aggressively? Will they ignore you? Can you choose a playing field where competitors are unlikely to follow?

### Ugandan Example — Logic Flow for a Retail Pharmacy Chain

| Dimension | Analysis |
|---|---|
| **Industry segments** | Hospital pharmacies, standalone pharmacies, pharmacy chains, online pharmacies. Urban vs. rural. Prescription-led vs. OTC-led |
| **Segment attractiveness** | Pharmacy chains in urban areas: moderate rivalry (few chains exist), low threat of new entrants (regulatory barriers — NDA licensing), moderate buyer power. Most attractive segment |
| **Customer value** | Channel: doctors value reliable drug availability and information. Consumers value: trustworthy medicines (not counterfeit), convenience, pharmacist advice, affordable pricing |
| **Relative position** | Advantages: multiple locations, trained pharmacists, bulk purchasing power (UGX cost advantage of 15–20% over standalone pharmacies). Disadvantage: higher fixed costs (rent, staffing) |
| **Competitor reaction** | Standalone pharmacies unlikely to consolidate quickly. Hospital pharmacies locked into institutional supply chains. Online pharmacies face delivery and trust barriers |

## 7. Reverse Engineering Strategic Choices

Reverse engineering is a structured process for making strategic choices collaboratively. It prevents the typical dynamic in which advocates argue for their preferred option and critics attack alternatives, and instead focuses on specifying the conditions under which each option would be a great choice.

### The Seven Steps

| Step | Action | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Frame the choice** | Articulate the problem as a choice between two or more mutually exclusive options | Until there are at least two mutually exclusive options, the choice is not truly framed. Framing makes the stakes clear and motivates action |
| **2. Generate possibilities** | Broaden the list of strategic possibilities | Express each possibility as a positive narrative — a happy story describing a positive outcome. Include creative and unexpected options. Never dismiss suggestions at this stage |
| **3. Specify conditions** | For each possibility, ask "what would have to be true?" for this to be a great choice | This is NOT arguing about what IS true. It is laying out the logic of what WOULD HAVE TO BE true. Sceptics must specify the exact source of their scepticism as a condition, not as a blanket denunciation |
| **4. Identify barriers** | Among the conditions, identify which seem least likely to hold | These least-likely-to-be-true conditions are the barriers. Until the team has confidence about whether these conditions hold, it cannot move ahead with a possibility |
| **5. Design tests** | Design tests to assess whether the barrier conditions actually hold | Focus testing effort on the barriers — the conditions that are genuinely uncertain. Do not waste time testing conditions everyone already agrees are true |
| **6. Conduct tests** | Run the tests — market research, financial modelling, pilot programmes, expert interviews | Keep tests focused and time-bound. The goal is to resolve the key uncertainties, not to achieve perfect information |
| **7. Choose** | Based on test results, select the possibility whose conditions most robustly hold | The best strategic choice gradually becomes clear as each possibility is assessed against its barrier conditions |

### The "What Would Have to Be True?" Technique

This is the core innovation of the reverse-engineering process. Instead of arguing "this will work" vs. "this won't work," team members ask: "For me to be confident in this possibility, what would have to be true about our industry, our customers, our capabilities, and our competitors?"

**Benefits:**
- Converts critics into contributors — sceptics specify conditions rather than blocking ideas
- Separates logic from opinion — the focus is on conditions, not preferences
- Creates a clear standard of proof — if the conditions hold, the team commits
- Produces actionable tests — barrier conditions become research questions

### Ugandan Example — Retail Chain Expansion

**Choice to frame:** Should we expand by opening five new stores in secondary towns, or by launching an e-commerce platform serving existing urban customers?

**Possibility A — Physical expansion:**
- What would have to be true? Secondary towns have sufficient middle-class population (>50,000 with UGX 500,000+ monthly discretionary spend). Rental costs allow break-even within 18 months. Supply chain can reliably serve upcountry locations. Competitor presence is low.

**Possibility B — E-commerce launch:**
- What would have to be true? Target customers are comfortable ordering online. Last-mile delivery infrastructure exists or can be built affordably. Digital payment adoption is high enough. Customer acquisition cost online is lower than the cost of physical store foot traffic.

**Barrier conditions:** For Possibility A, the biggest uncertainty might be supply-chain reliability upcountry. For Possibility B, it might be last-mile delivery cost. Design and conduct tests specifically on these barriers before choosing.

## 8. Applying the Cascade to a Business Plan

Each question in the Strategy Choice Cascade maps directly to specific sections of a business plan. Use the cascade as the strategic backbone that holds the plan together.

### Cascade-to-Business-Plan Mapping

| Cascade Question | Business Plan Section(s) | What to Write |
|---|---|---|
| **What is our winning aspiration?** | Executive Summary, Vision & Mission, Company Overview | Define what winning looks like — market position, revenue targets, impact goals. Be specific: "Category leader in X segment with UGX Y revenue" not "be the best" |
| **Where to play?** | Market Analysis, Target Market, Competitive Analysis | Define the playing field across all five dimensions (geography, product, segment, channel, vertical stage). Explicitly state where you will NOT play and why. Size the addressable market within your where-to-play boundaries |
| **How to win?** | Competitive Analysis, Value Proposition, Marketing Strategy, Pricing Strategy | Articulate whether you are pursuing cost leadership or differentiation. Explain how your where-to-play and how-to-win choices reinforce each other. Show how your approach creates a margin advantage (lower cost or higher willingness to pay) |
| **What capabilities must be in place?** | Operations Plan, Team & Management, Technology, Partnerships | Map the 3–6 core capabilities required. Present them as an activity system showing reinforcing links. Identify which capabilities exist today and which must be built. Include external partnerships that extend the capability set |
| **What management systems are required?** | Operations Plan, Financial Plan, Milestones, KPIs | Define the systems, processes, and measures that enable the capabilities and support the choices: quality systems, dashboards, review cadences, hiring processes, financial controls |

### Integration Checklist

Use this checklist to verify that your business plan reflects an integrated cascade:

- [ ] The winning aspiration is specific and measurable, not a generic platitude
- [ ] Where-to-play choices are explicit across all five dimensions
- [ ] Where-NOT-to-play choices are documented with rationale
- [ ] How-to-win is clearly cost leadership OR differentiation (not both, unless you can justify a rare dual strategy)
- [ ] How-to-win makes sense given the where-to-play choices
- [ ] Core capabilities are presented as a reinforcing system, not a laundry list
- [ ] The activity system passes all three tests (feasibility, distinctiveness, defensibility)
- [ ] Management systems directly enable the stated capabilities
- [ ] Competitor analysis includes reverse-engineering of competitor activity systems
- [ ] The strategy logic flow has been applied — industry, customer, relative position, and competitor dimensions are all addressed
- [ ] Financial projections are consistent with the strategic choices (e.g. a differentiation strategy should show premium pricing, not rock-bottom prices)
- [ ] The plan identifies the key barrier conditions and describes how they have been (or will be) tested

### Common Mistakes in Business Plans (Cascade Perspective)

| Mistake | Cascade Diagnosis | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "We will serve everyone in Uganda" | No where-to-play choice has been made | Narrow to specific segments, geographies, and channels |
| "Our strategy is to provide excellent customer service" | Generic aspiration with no how-to-win specificity | Define what "excellent" means in measurable terms, and explain how it creates a margin advantage competitors cannot match |
| "We have no real competitors" | Failure to analyse the competitive field | Every business has competitors — at minimum, the customer's alternative of doing nothing. Apply the strategy logic flow |
| Lists 15 capabilities as "core" | No distinction between generic strengths and critical, reinforcing capabilities | Reduce to 3–6 truly core capabilities and show how they reinforce one another |
| Strategy section disconnected from financial projections | Cascade is incomplete — management systems and measures are missing | Ensure financial model reflects the strategic choices (pricing, cost structure, investment priorities) |

## Six Warning Signs (Strategy Traps in Action)

Beyond the five traps above, Lafley and Martin identify six specific strategic patterns that signal trouble:

| # | Pattern | Warning |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **The do-it-all strategy** | Failing to make choices, making everything a priority. Strategy is choice |
| 2 | **The Don Quixote strategy** | Attacking the strongest competitor head-on. Pick somewhere you can win |
| 3 | **The Waterloo strategy** | Fighting on multiple fronts simultaneously. No company can do everything well |
| 4 | **The something-for-everyone strategy** | Attempting to capture all segments at once. Serve some constituents really well |
| 5 | **The dreams-that-never-come-true strategy** | High-level aspirations that never become concrete choices. Aspirations are not strategy |
| 6 | **The programme-of-the-month strategy** | Adopting generic industry strategies. If your choices look like competitors' choices, you will not win |

## Six Signs of a Winning Strategy

| # | Sign | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | An activity system that looks different from any competitor's | You are delivering value in a distinctive way |
| 2 | Customers who adore you, and non-customers who cannot see why anyone would buy from you | You have been choiceful |
| 3 | Competitors who make good profit doing what they do | Your strategy has left room for competitors, so they do not need to attack your core |
| 4 | More resources to spend than competitors have | You are winning the value equation |
| 5 | Competitors attack one another, not you | You look like the hardest target to attack |
| 6 | Customers look to you first for innovation | You are seen as uniquely positioned to create value |
