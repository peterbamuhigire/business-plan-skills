# Competitive Strategy — Porter (1980)

## Overview

Porter's *Competitive Strategy* established the foundational analytical framework for understanding industries and positioning businesses within them. The central argument: **a firm's profitability is determined both by industry structure and by its chosen competitive position within that industry**. Strategy is about deliberately choosing a position that is defensible against competitive forces — not just reacting to them.

This reference covers five extraction targets: Five Forces, Generic Strategies, Competitor Analysis Framework, Market Signals, Strategic Groups, and Competitive Moves. All frameworks are from Porter (1980) unless otherwise noted.

---

## 1. Porter's Five Forces Framework
*(Chapter 1: The Structural Analysis of Industries)*

The five forces determine the **long-run average profitability of an industry**. The collective strength of the five forces defines the profit potential of an industry — strong forces constrain profitability; weak forces allow above-average returns. The goal of competitive strategy is to find a position where the firm can best defend against these forces or influence them in its favour.

### Force 1: Threat of New Entrants

New entrants bring new capacity and desire to gain market share, which drives down prices and inflates costs. The threat depends on **barriers to entry** and the **expected retaliation** from incumbents.

**Barriers to entry:**

| Barrier | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Economies of scale** | Entrant must enter at large scale (risky) or accept cost disadvantage |
| **Product differentiation** | Established brands force entrants to spend heavily to overcome loyalty |
| **Capital requirements** | Large investment requirements filter out underfunded entrants |
| **Switching costs** | One-time costs buyer faces when switching supplier; locks buyers in |
| **Access to distribution channels** | Entrant must secure shelf space, agents, distributors — incumbents may block |
| **Cost advantages independent of scale** | Proprietary technology, favourable locations, learning curve, government subsidies |
| **Government policy** | Licensing requirements, environmental standards, regulations limiting entry |

**Expected retaliation** also deters entry: industries with history of vigorous retaliation, excess capacity to wage price war, or slow growth (so incumbents lose significant share) will see less entry.

**Assessment questions:**
- How much capital would a new entrant need to reach minimum efficient scale?
- Do incumbents have meaningful brand loyalty or switching cost advantages?
- Are distribution channels open to new players or locked up?
- What government licences or standards would a new entrant need to obtain?
- Have incumbents retaliated against previous entrants?

**Force rating: HIGH** when entry barriers are low and retaliation is expected to be mild. **LOW** when barriers are high or retaliation is fierce.

---

### Force 2: Bargaining Power of Suppliers

Powerful suppliers can squeeze profitability by raising prices or reducing the quality of goods and services. A supplier group is powerful when:

- It is **dominated by a few companies** and more concentrated than the industry it sells to
- It does **not depend heavily** on the industry for its revenues (can afford to lose a customer)
- The **product is differentiated** or has **switching costs** — buyers cannot easily switch
- It poses a credible **threat of forward integration** (can enter the buyer's business)
- The industry is **not an important customer** to the supplier group

**Assessment questions:**
- How many suppliers exist for each key input? Is the market concentrated?
- How critical is this input to the final product — can it be substituted?
- How significant is this industry to each supplier's total revenue?
- What would it cost to switch to an alternative supplier?
- Do suppliers have the capability and incentive to sell direct (forward integrate)?

**Force rating: HIGH** when few powerful suppliers dominate an undifferentiated-buyer market. **LOW** when many suppliers compete for the firm's business.

---

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers

Buyers compete by forcing down prices, demanding better quality, and playing competitors against each other. A buyer group is powerful when:

- It is **concentrated** or purchases in **large volumes** relative to seller sales
- The products it purchases are **standard or undifferentiated** — buyers can easily switch
- The product represents a **significant fraction of the buyer's costs** — buyers are motivated to shop
- The buyer earns **low profits** — creating pressure to reduce input costs
- Buyers pose a credible threat of **backward integration** (producing the product themselves)
- The **buyer has full information** about demand, market prices, and supplier costs

**Consumer vs. industrial buyers:** Individual consumers are less price-sensitive when the product is high involvement, emotionally meaningful, or performance-critical. Institutional buyers behave more like rational economic actors.

**Assessment questions:**
- How concentrated are customers relative to sellers?
- How large is the typical purchase relative to the buyer's total spending?
- How easy is it for buyers to switch between competing suppliers?
- Do buyers have realistic ability to produce the product themselves?
- Are buyers well-informed about alternatives and prices?

**Force rating: HIGH** when buyers are few, large, well-informed, and price-sensitive. **LOW** when buyers are fragmented, uninformed, and making high-involvement decisions.

---

### Force 4: Threat of Substitute Products or Services

Substitutes limit the industry's potential return by capping the price ceiling. They are products from other industries that perform the same function for the buyer.

**High-threat substitutes:**
- Those subject to trends **improving price-performance** relative to the industry's product
- Those produced by industries earning **high profits** — can reduce prices aggressively

**Assessment questions:**
- What other products or technologies could meet the same underlying buyer need?
- Are substitute products on an improving price/performance trend?
- What is the switching cost to the substitute?
- Are substitutes produced by well-resourced industries that could expand?

**Force rating: HIGH** when substitutes offer a better or equal price-performance alternative with low switching costs. **LOW** when no close substitutes exist or switching costs are prohibitive.

---

### Force 5: Rivalry Among Existing Competitors

Rivalry takes the form of price competition, advertising, product introductions, and customer service battles. Intense rivalry drives industry profitability to zero.

**Factors that intensify rivalry:**

| Factor | Why It Intensifies Rivalry |
|--------|--------------------------|
| **Numerous or equally balanced competitors** | Instability — many players try to make moves, one may not be noticed |
| **Slow industry growth** | Zero-sum — growth requires taking share from rivals |
| **High fixed or storage costs** | Firms must fill capacity, driving prices down in downturns |
| **Lack of differentiation / low switching costs** | Competition shifts to price; no loyalty buffer |
| **Capacity additions in large increments** | Causes recurring periods of overcapacity and price cutting |
| **Diverse competitors** | Different goals, strategies, cultures — unpredictable, harder to coordinate |
| **High strategic stakes** | Firms fighting for survival or critical strategic objectives will be irrational |
| **High exit barriers** | Specialised assets, emotional commitment, government restrictions keep firms competing even at low returns |

**Assessment questions:**
- How many comparable-sized competitors exist?
- Is the industry growing, stagnant, or declining?
- Are products differentiated or essentially commodities?
- Can competitors exit the industry easily if margins deteriorate?
- Is there recent evidence of price wars, advertising wars, or product flooding?

**Force rating: HIGH** when many evenly matched rivals compete in a slow-growth industry with undifferentiated products and high exit barriers. **LOW** when a few differentiated players dominate a growing market.

```
-------------------------|------------|------------------------------------------

```

## 2. Three Generic Competitive Strategies
*(Chapter 2: Generic Competitive Strategies)*

Porter argues that competitive advantage comes from **one of two sources**: lower cost than rivals, or differentiation that commands a premium price. Combined with **competitive scope** (broad vs. narrow target), this produces three internally consistent generic strategies. A firm that fails to choose — that is "stuck in the middle" — achieves no competitive advantage.

### Strategy 1: Overall Cost Leadership

The firm sets out to be the **lowest-cost producer** in its industry. Cost leadership does not mean selling at the lowest price — it means having the lowest cost structure, which can be deployed either as price leadership (capturing volume) or as superior margins (at market price).

**Cost leadership requirements:**
- Aggressive construction of efficient-scale facilities
- Vigorous pursuit of cost reductions from experience curve effects
- Tight cost and overhead control
- Avoidance of marginal customer accounts
- Cost minimisation in areas like R&D, service, sales force, advertising

**Competitive advantage conferred:**
- Defends against powerful buyers (buyers can only drive price to next-most-efficient competitor's level)
- Defends against powerful suppliers (more flexibility to absorb input cost increases)
- Defends against new entrants (economies of scale and experience curve are barriers to entry)
- Defends against substitutes (price advantage reduces incentive to switch)

**Risks of cost leadership:**
- Technology change can nullify past capital investment and learning
- Competitors may learn to imitate
- Inability to see required product or marketing changes due to focus on cost
- Cost inflation may erode price advantage without commensurate differentiation

**When to choose cost leadership:** Large market share; commodity-like products; price-sensitive buyers; when competing on price is credible and sustainable.

---

### Strategy 2: Differentiation

The firm seeks to be **unique along dimensions that buyers value widely**. It selects one or more attributes that buyers perceive as important and positions itself to meet those needs exclusively. Differentiation is rewarded with a price premium.

**Differentiation methods:**
- Product design and features
- Customer service and support
- Dealer network and availability
- Technology and performance attributes
- Brand image and prestige
- Geographic coverage and delivery reliability

**Competitive advantage conferred:**
- Creates customer loyalty — buyers are willing to pay a premium rather than substitute
- Insulates against rivalry — the differentiated firm is not directly compared on price
- Creates entry barriers — competitors must match the differentiation premium to enter
- Limits buyer power — buyers have fewer alternatives that match the firm's unique attributes
- Provides margins to withstand supplier price increases

**Risks of differentiation:**
- Cost differential between differentiated firm and low-cost rivals becomes too large for buyers to justify the premium
- Buyers' need for the differentiating factor diminishes (e.g., technology changes the value of features)
- Imitation by rivals narrows perceived differentiation

**When to choose differentiation:** When buyers have diverse and well-defined preferences; when the differentiating attributes are genuinely valued; when the firm can sustain the uniqueness through proprietary capabilities.

---

### Strategy 3: Focus (Cost Focus or Differentiation Focus)

The firm **selects a narrow competitive scope** — a particular buyer group, segment of the product line, or geographic market — and tailors strategy to serve it to the exclusion of others. The premise: by focusing, the firm can serve a specific target more effectively than broadly competing rivals.

- **Cost focus:** Exploiting cost behaviour differences in specific segments (some segments are structurally lower-cost to serve)
- **Differentiation focus:** Exploiting special needs of buyers in specific segments that broad-target competitors overlook or underserve

**Risks of focus:**
- The target segment becomes attractive enough to draw broadly focused competitors
- The differences between the target segment and other segments narrow
- A new focuser identifies a sub-segment within the focused firm's target

**When to choose focus:** When the target segment has genuinely different needs from the rest of the industry; when broad competitors are not optimising for the segment; when the segment is large enough to be profitable but small enough to be unattractive to large rivals.

---

### The "Stuck in the Middle" Trap

A firm that does not achieve any generic strategy is "stuck in the middle." It has no competitive advantage and competes at a disadvantage:
- It lacks the cost structure of cost leaders (who win on price)
- It lacks the perceived uniqueness of differentiators (who win on value)
- It lacks the depth of market focus of niche focusers

**Stuck in the middle firms are almost guaranteed below-average returns** unless the industry is particularly attractive or competitors are equally stuck.

**Generic strategy selection summary:**

| Competitive Advantage | Broad Target | Narrow Target |
|----------------------|--------------|---------------|
| **Lower Cost** | Cost Leadership | Cost Focus |
| **Differentiation** | Differentiation | Differentiation Focus |

## 3. Competitor Analysis Framework
*(Chapter 3: A Framework for Competitor Analysis)*

Before a firm can formulate a competitive strategy, it must understand its competitors thoroughly. Porter's framework analyses each significant competitor along **four diagnostic components** that together predict **how each competitor will behave**.

### The Four Diagnostic Components

**Component 1: Future Goals**
What does the competitor want to achieve over the long run?
- Financial goals: revenue growth targets, ROI objectives, EPS targets
- Market goals: market share targets, geographic aspirations
- Strategic goals: technology leadership, brand positioning, diversification
- Management goals: prestige, security, social mission

*Why this matters:* A competitor pursuing market share will behave differently from one maximising current profits. A competitor with financial distress will make desperate moves; a well-capitalised one can absorb losses.

**Component 2: Current Strategy**
How is the competitor currently competing?
- The competitive strategy the competitor is employing (cost, differentiation, or focus)
- Operating policies: pricing policies, R&D investment, advertising spend, service levels
- Key activities: where the competitor is investing resources
- Alliance strategy: partnerships, joint ventures, licensing arrangements

*Why this matters:* A competitor cannot easily change strategy in the short run. Current strategy reveals where they are committed and therefore where they are vulnerable.

**Component 3: Assumptions**
What does the competitor believe about itself and the industry?
- How does the competitor think the industry will evolve?
- What assumptions does it hold about its own strengths and weaknesses?
- Where does it believe it has advantages?
- Are any of these assumptions demonstrably wrong — and exploitable?

*Why this matters:* Strategy is built on assumptions. If a competitor assumes it dominates a customer segment and actually customer loyalty is eroding, that blind spot is an opportunity.

**Component 4: Capabilities**
What can the competitor actually do?
- Core strengths: technology, brand, cost structure, customer relationships, distribution
- Core weaknesses: underinvested areas, organisational constraints, financial limitations
- Ability to grow: capacity to add production, hire, fund expansion
- Ability to respond: how quickly and effectively can the competitor react to moves?

*Why this matters:* Goals and strategy are intentions; capabilities determine execution. A competitor with ambitious goals but weak capabilities is less threatening than one with modest goals but exceptional execution ability.

---

### The Competitor Response Profile

Combining the four components produces a **response profile** for each competitor — a prediction of likely behaviour:

| Question | Derived From |
|----------|-------------|
| Is the competitor satisfied with its current position? | Future Goals |
| What moves is the competitor likely to make? | Goals + Strategy |
| Where is the competitor vulnerable to attack? | Assumptions + Capabilities |
| What competitive moves will provoke the most vigorous retaliation? | Goals + Capabilities |
| How can we avoid triggering a damaging response? | Assumptions + Goals |

---

### Good Competitors vs. Bad Competitors

Not all competitors are equally damaging. **Good competitors** help stabilise the industry:
- Absorb demand fluctuations
- Credibly deter entry by occupying market niches
- Improve the industry's structure (average cost, innovation rate)
- Avoid destructive price cutting

**Bad competitors** destabilise the industry through irrational pricing, capacity over-expansion, or kamikaze tactics driven by survival desperation.

**Strategic implication:** It may be in a firm's interest to preserve certain competitors (the good ones) while attacking others. Eliminating all competition is rarely optimal — it attracts new, potentially worse entrants.

---

### Building a Competitor Intelligence System

Porter recommends a systematic intelligence function:
1. **Sources:** Published materials (filings, annual reports, trade press, job postings), salesforce reports, suppliers/distributors, industry observers, reverse engineering
2. **Compilation:** Designated competitor files; assigned responsibility per competitor
3. **Analysis:** Regular updates to the four-component profiles
4. **Dissemination:** Accessible to strategy, sales, and product teams

For Uganda/EA SMEs, intelligence sources include: URSB company searches, trade association data, supplier conversations, physical observation, LinkedIn, and regulatory filings.

## 4. Market Signals
*(Chapter 4: Market Signals)*

Market signals are **actions or communications by competitors that provide indication of their intentions, motives, goals, or internal situation**. Reading signals allows a firm to anticipate competitive moves without waiting for them to materialise.

### Types of Market Signals

| Signal Type | Example | What It May Indicate |
|------------|---------|---------------------|
| **Prior announcements** | Competitor announces price increase in advance | Testing market reaction; signalling intent to coordinate |
| **Post-hoc explanations** | Competitor explains a price cut after making it | Justifying behaviour; asserting that it was forced, not aggressive |
| **Discussion of industry conditions** | Executive interviews about demand or capacity | Signalling concerns or intentions to investors or rivals |
| **Capacity announcements** | Pre-announcing a plant expansion | Deterring rivals from investing (commitment signal) |
| **Private communication** | Industry association meetings, trade press statements | Signalling willingness or unwillingness to compete aggressively |

### Using Signals Strategically

**Reading signals:**
- Compare what a competitor says with what it does — divergence reveals real intentions
- Look for signals that are **costly to make falsely** (irreversible commitments carry more weight than statements)
- Watch for **retaliation signals** after you make a move — early warning of escalation

**Making signals:**
- Signal your own commitments credibly: capacity investments, long-term supplier contracts, public announcements
- Use signals to deter unwanted competitive moves without incurring the cost of actual retaliation

## 5. Strategic Groups
*(Chapter 7: Strategic Groups and the Strategic Group Map)*

A **strategic group** is a cluster of firms within an industry that are following the same or similar strategy. Firms within a group resemble each other more than they resemble firms in other groups.

### Identifying Strategic Groups

Strategic groups are defined by **strategic dimensions**:
- Degree of product specialisation
- Brand identification vs. commodity positioning
- Technology leadership or followership
- Cost position
- Service level
- Distribution channel choice
- Geographic scope
- Vertical integration level

**Mapping strategic groups:** Plot the industry on two key strategic dimensions (axes) and position each competitor. Firms that cluster together form a strategic group. Circle size can represent firm revenues.

### Mobility Barriers

**Mobility barriers** are factors that prevent firms from moving between strategic groups. They are similar to entry barriers but apply between groups within an industry:
- Brand investment required to move to a premium group
- Scale required to compete in a cost leadership group
- Access to distribution channels controlled by incumbents in a group
- Proprietary technology required to move to a technology-leadership group

**Strategic implication:** High mobility barriers between groups mean that each group has protected territory. A focuser in a small premium segment may be well-protected from cost leaders who cannot easily replicate the brand and service requirements.

### Strategic Group Implications

- **Within-group rivalry** is typically most intense — firms in the same group compete most directly
- **Between-group competition** is moderated by mobility barriers
- **Profitability varies by group** — some strategic positions are structurally superior
- **An empty space on the strategic group map** may represent an opportunity (no incumbents serving that position) or a trap (no incumbent tried because the position is unviable)

## 6. Competitive Moves
*(Chapter 5: Competitive Moves)*

### Cooperative vs. Threatening Moves

Not all competitive moves provoke retaliation. **Cooperative moves** — those that improve both the firm's position and the overall industry structure — are less likely to trigger destructive responses.

**Moves less likely to provoke retaliation:**
- Moves that improve quality without cutting price
- Moves that expand the overall market rather than take share
- Moves signalled in advance that give competitors time to adjust
- Moves that allow competitors to "save face" in retreat

**Moves likely to provoke retaliation:**
- Direct price cuts in core segments of powerful competitors
- Moves that threaten a competitor's strategic objectives (not just profits)
- Moves that are perceived as unprovoked aggression

### Commitment as a Competitive Tool

**Credible commitments** deter competitive responses. A firm that has made an irreversible investment — a long-term lease, a dedicated manufacturing facility, a public proclamation of intent — signals that it will not retreat. This can deter entry or discourage rivals from making the move the commitment pre-empts.

### Focal Points

When multiple equilibria exist, **focal points** are outcomes that competitors naturally converge on without communication. Classic focal points:
- Geographic division of markets along natural boundaries
- Price points that represent psychologically significant thresholds
- Historical market share levels (competitors expect to return to pre-conflict shares)

Focal points matter when coordinating industry behaviour implicitly — identifying them helps predict where competitive battles will settle.

## Uganda/East Africa Application

### Five Forces in Uganda/EA Industries

**Example: Uganda Poultry Industry**

| Force | Assessment | Key Factors |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Threat of Entry | **High** | Low capital requirement for backyard producers; no significant barriers to small-scale entry |
| Supplier Power | **Medium** | Day-old chick suppliers are concentrated (2–3 major hatcheries); feed cost is 60–70% of production cost; some dependence |
| Buyer Power | **Low–Medium** | Fragmented retail buyers; supermarkets (Carrefour, Nakumatt/similar) have more power; informal buyers have little |
| Threat of Substitutes | **Medium** | Fish (tilapia, mukene), beef, pork — price-sensitive consumers substitute freely at premium prices |
| Competitive Rivalry | **High** | Many small-scale producers; difficult to differentiate commodity broilers; price-driven market |

**Overall: Low industry attractiveness** — strategy must focus on cost discipline, vertical integration (own feed mill or hatchery), or differentiation (certified organic, brand, restaurant-direct).

---

**Example: Uganda Private Education (Secondary Schools)**

| Force | Assessment | Key Factors |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Threat of Entry | **Medium** | Capital requirement for facilities; UNEB registration requirements; but low barriers for low-end operators |
| Supplier Power | **Low** | Many available teachers; curriculum set by UNEB (no supplier dependency for core product) |
| Buyer Power | **Low** | Parents choose once per term; switching cost is high (child disruption, re-registration fees) |
| Threat of Substitutes | **Low** | Government schools exist but quality gap creates premium private demand |
| Competitive Rivalry | **Medium** | Location-based competition limits rivalry; reputation creates stickiness |

**Overall: Moderate-high industry attractiveness** — strategy should build reputation, focus on UACE results, and lock in parent relationships early.

### Generic Strategies in the Uganda/EA Context

**Cost Leadership** is viable for:
- Agricultural commodity processing (maize milling, rice hulling, cassava processing) — volume-driven, thin-margin businesses where efficiency wins
- Mass-market consumer goods (soap, cooking oil) — competing with imported products requires local cost advantage
- Transport/logistics — fuel efficiency, fleet utilisation, and route density determine cost position

**Differentiation** is viable for:
- Tourism/hospitality — Uganda's wildlife, culture, and landscape are genuinely differentiating; high willingness to pay from international visitors
- Professional services (law, accounting, consulting) — reputation, expertise, and relationships are the differentiators
- Premium food and beverage — coffee, specialty grains, certified organic produce command export premiums

**Focus strategies** are highly appropriate for Uganda SMEs:
- Serving a specific geographic district (the only reliable supplier within 50 km)
- Serving a specific buyer type (government institutions, NGOs, hotel sector)
- Serving a premium segment within a commodity market (branded, packaged, certified)

**"Stuck in the middle" is the default failure mode** for Uganda SMEs that compete on neither price (informal sector undercuts them) nor differentiation (no brand, no quality standard, no service commitment). Choosing a clear generic strategy is the single most important strategic decision.

### Competitor Analysis in Low-Data Environments

In Uganda and East Africa, formal competitor data is scarce. Adapt the four-component framework:

| Component | Uganda/EA Intelligence Sources |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| **Future Goals** | Director interviews (LinkedIn, events), investment announcements, URSB new branch registrations |
| **Current Strategy** | Physical price surveys, product range audits, mystery shopping, supplier conversations |
| **Assumptions** | Trade association meetings, local press, what competitors say publicly vs. what they do |
| **Capabilities** | Job postings (indicate investment areas), trade credit terms offered to shared suppliers, visible capacity utilisation |
