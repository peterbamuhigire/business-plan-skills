# The Mind of the Strategist — Ohmae (1982)

Kenichi Ohmae spent his career as a senior partner at McKinsey & Company's Tokyo office, working with Japan's most competitive corporations during the period when Japanese industry was reshaping global markets. This book distils the analytical habits that distinguished Japan's best strategists from their Western counterparts — habits built not on superior data but on superior ways of framing problems and reassembling their parts.

The book is not a step-by-step planning manual. It is a guide to the quality of thinking that precedes formal planning — the capacity to dissect a complex competitive situation and reconstruct it in ways that create lasting advantage.

---

## 1. Core Strategic Philosophy

The strategist's job is to dissect complex situations into their constituent parts, identify what actually matters, then reassemble the pieces in ways that maximise competitive advantage. This stands apart from two inadequate alternatives:

- **Mechanical systems thinking** — linear, step-by-step analysis that produces solutions only marginally better than the status quo, because it never questions the underlying structure of the problem
- **Pure intuition** — rapid, experience-based judgement that works in stable environments but fails when conditions shift or when the situation is genuinely novel

True strategy combines rational analysis with imaginative reintegration — what Ohmae calls "nonlinear brainpower." The analyst breaks things apart; the strategist puts them back together differently.

**Key principle:** "Analysis is the critical starting point of strategic thinking."

Without rigorous analysis, strategic thinking degenerates into wishful thinking. But analysis alone, without the imaginative leap to a new configuration, produces only incremental improvement. Both elements are necessary.

### Determining the Critical Issue

One of Ohmae's most practically useful contributions is his insistence on framing questions in solution-oriented rather than symptom-oriented terms.

Consider the difference:

| Symptom-oriented (poor framing) | Solution-oriented (correct framing) |
|---|---|
| "How do we reduce overtime?" | "Is our workforce large enough for current demand?" |
| "How do we cut costs?" | "Which cost elements are structurally above industry benchmark?" |
| "How do we improve sales?" | "Do we have the right product for the customers we are targeting?" |

The symptom-oriented question invites incremental tinkering. The solution-oriented question yields a binary — yes or no — that immediately points toward a category of action. This reframing is not semantic; it changes what data you collect, who you involve, and how fast you can act.

### Issue Diagram Method

The Issue Diagram is Ohmae's tool for structuring an analysis once the critical question has been correctly framed. It works as a decision tree in which the overall problem is broken into mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive sub-issues — analogous to the MECE principle (Rasiel, 1999) that McKinsey formalists later codified.

The process:

1. State the problem as a correctly framed question
2. Identify the major branches — the highest-level possible causes or dimensions
3. At each branch, ask again: what are the possible explanations at the next level of detail?
4. Continue until each terminal item is individually manageable and has a measurable outcome
5. Assign ownership and a testing method to each terminal item

**Worked example — Product A has unacceptably high costs:**

```
Product A: high costs
├── Is product design too expensive?
│   ├── Are materials specified above functional requirement? → VA/VE review
│   └── Are there more parts than necessary? → Design simplification
├── Are fixed costs too high?
│   ├── Is plant utilisation below target? → Volume strategy
│   └── Is overhead allocation misaligned? → Overhead review
└── Are variable costs too high?
    ├── Is purchasing non-competitive? → Supplier renegotiation / rebidding
    ├── Is yield below standard? → QC and process improvement
    └── Is scrap above benchmark? → Root cause analysis on defects
```

Each terminal node now has a clear owner, a testable hypothesis, and a defined remediation path. Without the Issue Diagram, a manager presented with "Product A has high costs" will instinctively attack the most visible element — often labour — while missing the structural driver.

## 2. The 3C Strategic Triangle

"In the construction of any business strategy, three main players must be taken into account: the corporation itself, the customer, and the competition. Each of these 'strategic three C's' is a living entity with its own interests and objectives." (Ohmae, 1982, Ch. 8)

The triangle is not a static model. Each vertex is dynamic: customers evolve their needs, competitors change their strategies, and the corporation itself changes its capabilities. Sustainable strategy requires continuous monitoring of all three vertices simultaneously.

**Strategy definition:** "The way in which a corporation endeavours to differentiate itself positively from its competitors, using its relative corporate strengths to better satisfy customer needs."

### The Three Vertices

**Corporation**
The corporation vertex encompasses relative strengths, functional capabilities, cost structure, technology, brand, distribution networks, and financial resources — but always relative to the competitor, not in absolute terms. A corporation that is strong in absolute terms may still be weak relative to the specific competitor it is facing in a specific market segment.

**Customer**
The customer vertex represents needs, objectives, purchasing criteria, decision-making processes, price sensitivity, and segments. Ohmae's insight is that customers do not want products — they want outcomes. The strategist must understand the underlying objective function the customer is trying to satisfy, not merely the stated preference for a product category.

**Competition**
The competition vertex includes the competitor's strategy, assumptions about the market, resource base, cost structure, and — critically — the corporate context in which the competitor operates. A division of a large conglomerate competes differently from a standalone company, because it can absorb losses in one area and cross-subsidise from another.

### Why Differentiation Is Non-Negotiable

If a corporation's approach is identical to that of its competitors, customers cannot distinguish between offerings. When offerings are indistinguishable, price becomes the only competitive variable. Price competition benefits neither the corporation nor (in the long run) the customer, because it destroys margins that would otherwise fund innovation and service quality.

Ohmae's triangle makes this structural: without a gap between how the corporation satisfies customer needs and how the competitor does so, strategy collapses into tactics.

## 3. Strategic Planning Unit (SPU)

The SPU is the correct organisational unit at which strategy should be formulated. Defining the SPU incorrectly — either too narrowly or too broadly — produces strategies that optimise the wrong level of competition.

Three tests for correctly defining an SPU:

**Test 1 — Customer Test**
Can the unit address the total relevant market, not just a narrow segment? A unit defined too narrowly will design strategy against a subset of competitors while ignoring those who serve the broader customer need. The strategist must start from the customer's perspective and define the competitive space accordingly.

**Test 2 — Functional Test**
Does the unit encompass all critical functions required to compete? This includes procurement, product design, manufacturing, distribution, sales, after-sales service, and — where relevant — financing. A unit that controls only manufacturing but not distribution cannot formulate a complete competitive strategy, because distribution is often where the battle is actually won or lost.

**Test 3 — Competitor Test**
Can the strategist view the competition in its totality, including shared resources across the competitor's full business? If the competitor is a diversified conglomerate, the SPU must be defined broadly enough to understand how the competitor's cross-business resources affect their competitive behaviour in this segment.

### Case Illustrations

**Air-conditioning (SPU too narrow):** An engineering-oriented manufacturer designed a product that performed reliably and met all technical specifications. The product failed commercially because it was too heavy for installers to manoeuvre on-site. The company had defined its SPU around engineering and manufacturing, excluding the distribution and installation function. Had the SPU test been applied correctly, the installation channel would have been included in the design process from the outset.

**Copier (SPU correctly applied):** A company with an underdeveloped servicing network correctly identified that it could not compete on the conventional basis — where service quality was a KFS. Rather than attempting to build a service network (which would have taken years and consumed capital), the company redirected its engineering resources to develop a copier that minimised servicing requirements. The SPU was defined to encompass the full competition: not just manufacturing, but the service dimension that competitors had turned into a moat.

## 4. Key Factors for Success (KFS)

KFS are the small number of factors that, in any given industry at any given time, essentially determine competitive success. Every industry has them; most managers mistake secondary factors for primary ones. Strategic resource allocation that targets KFS consistently outperforms dispersed investment across all competitive dimensions.

**Ohmae's KFS identification method — hypothesis-first:**

1. Ask experienced operators in the industry: "What is the secret of success here?"
2. Form a set of candidate hypotheses based on interviews, observation, and your own analysis
3. Test each hypothesis: can you identify firms that succeeded without this factor? Can you identify firms that had this factor but still failed?
4. For validated KFS, ask: which sub-variables determine performance on this factor? Which of those sub-variables can be influenced by management action?
5. Concentrate resources on the controllable sub-variables that most affect the validated KFS

**KFS vary significantly by industry and should never be assumed:**

| Industry | Key Factors for Success | Strategic Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Banking | Cost of capital minimised; return on lending maximised | Optimise deposit base cost structure and credit risk selection simultaneously |
| Shipbuilding / Steel | Economies of scale in manufacturing | Volume and capacity utilisation are primary; product mix is secondary |
| Beer | Economies of scale in distribution (not manufacturing) | Own or control the distribution network; manufacturing efficiency matters less |
| Ice cream | Manage seasonal demand variations; ensure economical refrigeration throughout the supply chain | Supply chain investment is the primary strategic lever, not product quality |
| Lumber | Own large forest estates; maximise yield per hectare | Growth rate is the key sub-variable; growth rate depends on climate inputs (sunshine and water) |

**Lumber case — extended (Ohmae verbatim logic):** An industry director explained the two KFS as forest ownership and yield maximisation. Yield depends on tree growth rate, which in turn depends on sunshine and water availability. In Arizona and Utah (sunshine in abundance, water in deficit), trees take 30 years to mature instead of 15. The KFS-derived strategic project: find irrigation solutions that cut the maturity cycle by half. In the Columbia River area (water abundant, sunshine limited), the KFS flip: the correct investment is fertilisers and shade-tolerant tree varieties. The same KFS framework — yield — produces entirely different strategic actions depending on the input constraint.

**Focus principle:** "It is perfectionist pursuit of these key factors — nothing more or less — that brings in the profits." Resources spread across all competitive dimensions produce mediocrity on all of them. Concentrated resources on two or three KFS produce genuine superiority where it counts.

## 5. Four Routes to Strategic Advantage

When a business cannot match a competitor across all dimensions — which is the normal condition — four strategic routes provide legitimate paths to competitive advantage.

### Route 1 — Focus on Key Factors for Success

Identify the one or two variables that most determine competitive success in the industry. Concentrate resources obsessively on mastering them while accepting deliberate weakness on non-KFS dimensions. This route requires the discipline to resist dispersing resources to "fix" weaknesses that do not affect the KFS.

This is a reinforcing strategy: the more resources concentrated on the KFS, the stronger the position; the stronger the position, the greater the returns available for further investment in the KFS.

### Route 2 — Build on Relative Superiority

Exploit any dimension where the competitor is comparatively weak — even if the company is not absolutely strong on that dimension. The strategist must look for asymmetries: the competitor may be strong overall but weak in one region, one customer segment, one product feature, or one time period.

This requires detailed understanding of the competitor's cost structure, organisational priorities, and the internal constraints they operate under. A competitor's weakness on a dimension is often invisible unless you study their organisation, not just their products.

Attack with concentrated resources where the competitor is comparatively weakest, not where you feel strongest. The distinction matters: what matters is the gap, not the absolute level.

### Route 3 — Pursue Aggressive Initiatives

Challenge the conventional wisdom about how the industry works. Most strategic thinking is constrained by shared industry assumptions — what everyone "knows" about how business must be done. The strategist who identifies and challenges a false assumption can restructure the competitive landscape.

**The "ideal state" method:**
1. Ask: "What would we do if all current constraints were removed?"
2. The answer reveals the ideal competitive position
3. Now ask: "What is preventing us from reaching that ideal state?"
4. Identify each constraint and classify it: legally required, economically required, or merely assumed
5. Redirect strategic energy from accepting assumed constraints to removing them

**Zero-based production** is the aggressive initiative applied to product design. Rather than improving an existing product incrementally, design both the product and the production system from zero around a defined target customer need. Ask: what is the customer willing to accept? What does the production system need to look like to deliver that at the required cost?

Examples: Honda Civic and Ricoh copiers — designed from scratch around what the user needed and what they were willing to pay, not from existing engineering assumptions. Both produced significant competitive disruption because they escaped inherited design constraints.

**Value Analysis (VA) and Value Engineering (VE):** Systematically dismantle the most successful competing product. Compare: construction methods, number of components, material specifications and grades, assembly sequence, and supply chain structure. Identify the specific mechanism by which the competitor's cost is lower. Re-engineer the home product to match or beat that element without introducing new competitive handicaps elsewhere. VA/VE is Route 3 applied to cost structure.

### Route 4 — Exploit Strategic Degrees of Freedom (SDF)

Identify the dimensions along which the company can innovate to satisfy the customer's core objective more fully. The customer's "objective function" — the underlying need they are trying to satisfy — is usually broader than the product category suggests. Customers are not buying a product; they are satisfying a need. The SDF is the set of dimensions along which that need can be more completely satisfied.

**How SDFs work over time:** In any product category, early competition focuses on the most obvious SDFs (functionality, reliability, price). As these dimensions saturate — as competitors converge on similar performance levels — new SDFs emerge: aesthetics, service, customisation, software integration, community, environmental attributes. The strategist who identifies the next SDF before competitors invest in it captures the new competitive ground.

**The pioneer advantage:** When users' objective function evolves (e.g., from basic functionality to an experiential or social dimension), early movers who identify the new SDF become the definers of the new competitive space. Later entrants must match both the functional specification and the experiential dimension simultaneously — a significantly harder task.

## 6. Customer Strategy — Segmentation

Ohmae distinguishes two fundamentally different bases for customer segmentation, each appropriate to different strategic situations.

| Segmentation Approach | Basis | Best Applied When |
|---|---|---|
| **Segmentation by objectives** | How customers use the product; what outcome they are seeking; what job they are hiring the product to do | Different customers have fundamentally different functional needs from the same product category, even if they look similar demographically |
| **Segmentation by coverage** | Geographic, demographic, channel, or account-size boundaries | Distribution economics, service accessibility, or regulatory context differ substantially by location or channel |

**Why objectives-first matters:** Segmenting first by geography or demographics frequently obscures the real competitive structure. Two customers who look demographically identical may have entirely different objective functions — and therefore require different products, different pricing, different service models, and face different competitive alternatives.

Segmenting by objective reveals the true competitive alternatives the customer is considering. A customer whose objective is "rapid transit across the city" is choosing between a boda-boda, a taxi, public transport, and walking — not between different boda-boda operators. The competitive set only becomes clear once the objective is stated.

Once the objective-based segments are correctly identified, coverage-based segmentation provides the operational filter: how do we reach the right objective-segments through the most efficient channels?

## 7. Competitor Strategy

The strategist must be able to inhabit the mindset of the strategic planner at the rival organisation — to understand not just what the competitor is doing, but why they believe it is the right thing to do, what assumptions underpin their strategy, and what constraints limit their freedom to respond.

"Put himself mentally in the place of a strategic planner in the rival company and thus ferret out the key perceptions and assumptions on which the competitor's strategy is based." (Ch. 8)

### Understanding the Competitor's Corporate Context

For competitors that are part of larger organisations, the parent structure fundamentally shapes competitive behaviour:

- **Shared resources:** A competitor that shares procurement, manufacturing, or distribution infrastructure across multiple business units has a cost structure that cannot be understood by looking at the business unit alone
- **Cross-subsidy capacity:** A competitor that can absorb losses in one business because other units are profitable is structurally more resilient than a standalone competitor — they can sustain a price war longer, invest in distribution more aggressively, or absorb the cost of regulatory compliance without threatening their overall survival
- **R&D and technology inheritance:** Technologies developed for one business frequently transfer to adjacent ones; understanding the competitor's R&D investment across their full portfolio reveals future capabilities not yet visible in their current products

**Mitsubishi example:** Mitsubishi Heavy Industries could absorb shipbuilding losses by redeploying skilled labour to automobile and chemical plant manufacturing. A standalone shipbuilder facing the same downturn has no such option. Competing against Mitsubishi in shipbuilding without understanding this corporate context would lead to systematically underestimating their ability to sustain losses and maintain market share.

### The Relative Superiority Principle in Practice

Finding relative superiority requires mapping the competitor's capabilities across multiple dimensions simultaneously:

1. List every dimension on which competition occurs: price, quality, reliability, delivery speed, service, customisation, brand, financing terms, geographic coverage, regulatory compliance, after-sales support
2. Rate both the company and the competitor on each dimension (a simple 1–5 scale is sufficient)
3. Calculate the gap on each dimension — positive where the company leads, negative where it trails
4. Identify dimensions where the gap is positive and where those dimensions are valued by a meaningful customer segment
5. Build strategy around concentrating resources to widen positive gaps, not to close negative ones on dimensions that are not KFS

## 8. Challenging Constraints

When managers face a difficult competitive situation, the instinctive response is to list everything that "cannot be done" — regulatory limits, budget limits, time limits, capability gaps, market resistance — and then work from whatever remains. This produces strategies that are, by construction, constrained to the status quo.

### The Ohmae Method for Constraint Reframing

1. Ask: "What precisely are the limiting factors that have convinced you nothing more can be done?"
2. Write down each constraint explicitly
3. Ask: "What alternatives would be open to us if every one of these constraints were removed?"
4. The answer describes the ideal state — which becomes the strategic goal
5. Return to each constraint and classify it: is it legally mandated, economically necessary, or merely assumed?
6. Redirect strategic energy from accepting assumed constraints to systematically removing them

"By directly challenging the constraints, the strategic thinker usually finds that in reality they are far less formidable than they had appeared."

### Common Managerial Rationalisations and Their Challenges

| Constraint as Stated | Ohmae's Challenge |
|---|---|
| "Top management doesn't understand what's going on at the front line" | Clarify the information gap: what specific data would change their understanding? Then provide it. |
| "The quality of our people isn't what it used to be" | Diagnose root cause: is this a training deficit, a hiring problem, or a role design problem? Each has a different solution. |
| "We're hamstrung by government regulations" | Distinguish legally required constraints from assumed ones. Many "regulatory" constraints are industry conventions, not legal requirements. |
| "The competition has too much clout with distributors" | Identify the specific mechanism: is it exclusivity contracts, volume rebates, relationships, or service? Then calculate the cost of overcoming the specific mechanism — rarely as high as assumed. |
| "We can't afford to invest in that area" | Ask whether this is a capital constraint or a prioritisation constraint. Capital constraints that prevent KFS investment are strategy problems, not finance problems. |

## 9. Profit Diagrams

Ohmae presents profitability as a three-variable structure:

**Profit = (Price − Cost) × Volume**, i.e., (P − C) × V

This simple identity has significant analytical power because it prevents collapsing all profit problems into a single diagnosis. A business with declining profits may be suffering from price pressure, cost inflation, volume decline, or some combination — and each has a different strategic response.

### The Three Issue Trees

Ohmae draws separate issue diagrams for each variable:

**Volume (V) tree — can profit be raised by increasing volume?**
- Are we losing existing customers? → What is the churn rate and its cause?
- Are we failing to acquire new customers? → Is this an awareness, consideration, or conversion problem?
- Are we in the right channels? → Where is the unreached addressable market?
- Could pricing stimulate volume enough to more than offset the margin reduction?

**Price (P) tree — can profit be raised by raising ex-factory price?**
- Is the product genuinely differentiated in the customer's perception?
- Are we in a segment where customers are price-sensitive or value-sensitive?
- Is there a premium segment we are not currently serving?
- Do our terms of trade (payment periods, minimum orders) effectively discount our price?

**Cost (C) tree — can profit be raised by reducing product costs?**
- Are material specifications above functional requirement? → VA/VE
- Is fixed cost per unit too high? → Volume or overhead reduction
- Is variable cost above industry benchmark? → Purchasing, yield, scrap
- Is working capital cost (inventory, receivables) embedded in apparent operating cost?

Each branch then has its own sub-tree. The Profit Diagram prevents the common error of jumping from "profits are low" to a single-cause remedy — which almost always targets the most politically convenient variable rather than the structural driver.

## 10. Avoiding Strategic Tunnel Vision

Under competitive pressure, executives narrow their field of attention. They focus on the immediate threat, the immediate metric, the immediate action. This narrowing — Ohmae's "tunnel vision" — prevents them from perceiving strategic alternatives that are available.

The deer-on-the-highway metaphor: a deer, caught in headlights, freezes and then charges directly into the oncoming vehicle rather than stepping to the side. The action that would save it (lateral movement) is available but invisible, because the deer's attention has locked onto the threat.

The corporate equivalent: a company under competitive attack narrows its strategy to defending the specific position being attacked — reducing price, matching features, accelerating production — rather than stepping aside to compete on a different dimension where the attacker has no advantage.

### The Antidote

**Change the objective function:**
- When the instinctive objective is "win at all costs," the visible options are only those that address the current competitive axis
- Changing the objective to "avoid the worst outcome" immediately opens a wider range of options
- For companies in decline: first define the worst-case outcome with precision, then systematically identify every route by which it can be avoided
- For companies on track: define the ideal end state first, then map the obstacles between the current position and that state

**Managing perfectionism:**
Strategic paralysis frequently masquerades as thoroughness. The executive who cannot act because "we don't yet have all the data" is exhibiting perfectionism, not rigour. Ohmae's remedy:

1. Write down every uncertainty that is preventing action
2. Estimate the probability that each uncertainty resolves negatively
3. Estimate the impact on the overall outcome if it does
4. If no individual uncertainty would significantly alter the overall result even in its worst-case resolution — act boldly and immediately

The test is not whether uncertainty exists; uncertainty always exists. The test is whether any specific uncertainty is material to the outcome. If none are material individually, perfectionism is costing more than the combined downside of all the uncertainties.

## 11. Uganda / East Africa Application

The frameworks in Ohmae (1982) were developed in the context of large Japanese industrial corporations, but the underlying logic translates directly to East African competitive environments — often with greater clarity, because the analytical rigour is less common among local competitors and the differentiation opportunities are consequently larger.

| Ohmae Framework | Uganda / EA Application |
|---|---|
| **3C Strategic Triangle** | In many Ugandan markets the "competitor" vertex is poorly understood. Operators typically enumerate only formal registered competitors. A complete competitor analysis must include: informal sector players (often the largest volume competitors), imported substitutes (which compete at the wholesale level, invisible at retail), and the "do nothing" option — the customer retaining their money. A 3C analysis that excludes any of these will systematically underestimate competitive intensity. |
| **KFS identification** | The most reliable KFS identification method in Uganda is interviewing the most experienced trader in the sector — the wholesaler who has survived three economic cycles. In Ugandan retail markets, KFS are typically: supplier credit terms (ability to stock on credit), physical proximity and foot-traffic management, and willingness to extend credit to customers. Product quality frequently ranks lower than expected. |
| **Issue Diagram** | Particularly valuable for diagnosing why a Ugandan SME is losing margin. The diagram prevents the most common diagnostic error: jumping from "costs are too high" to "hire fewer staff." The correct first branch: are costs in materials, labour, premises, or working capital (float extended to customers)? Each branch has a different intervention. Float cost (the effective interest cost of extending credit to customers who pay in 30–90 days) is frequently the largest hidden cost item in Ugandan trading businesses and almost never appears in informal accounts. |
| **Challenging constraints** | Ugandan SMEs frequently treat assumed constraints as permanent: "We cannot afford a website," "The market will not pay more than UGX X," "Banks do not lend to businesses like ours." Each of these should be subjected to the Ohmae challenge: what evidence exists that this constraint is structural rather than assumed? What would the ideal state look like if the constraint were removed? Work backward from the ideal to identify the minimum action required to remove the constraint. |
| **SDF / Objective function** | In boda-boda transport: the user's objective function is not "a boda-boda ride" — it is "arrive at the destination on time and safely." SDFs that remain largely unexploited in Uganda: guaranteed delivery windows (advance booking), package and parcel delivery (same infrastructure, different use), female-rider services (safety-sensitive customer segment), and subscription models for regular commuters. Each is a new SDF on the same objective function. |
| **Route 2 — Relative Superiority** | A small Ugandan manufacturer competing against imported goods rarely achieves cost superiority. The correct route is relative superiority on dimensions where imported goods are structurally weak: speed of delivery and restocking, after-sale support and warranty administration (imports require sending goods abroad), trade credit terms (local manufacturer can extend credit; importer typically cannot), customisation (product specification adapted to local conditions), and knowledge of local regulatory compliance requirements. |
| **Route 3 — Zero-based design** | In Ugandan agri-processing, most products are designed by reverse-engineering imported equivalents. Zero-based design — starting from what the Ugandan consumer actually needs, at the price point they can afford, using inputs that are locally available at consistent quality — consistently outperforms import-copy strategies. The design constraint is not "match the imported product" but "satisfy the objective function better than any available alternative." |
| **Customer segmentation by objectives** | Urban and rural Ugandan consumers may appear entirely different geographically but often share the same underlying objective (affordable, reliable protein; safe, accessible financial services; durable affordable clothing). The strategic question is not "are these different customers?" but "does the same objective function apply?" If yes, the product may be the same; the distribution model and packaging format will differ. Segment by objective first; then choose channel and format by geography and economics. |
| **Profit Diagram** | Ugandan SMEs in margin difficulty almost universally diagnose the problem as "sales are too low" (volume) or "costs are too high" (cost) — rarely "our price is below what the market would support" (price). In practice, many Ugandan businesses are priced below their differentiation value because they have never tested price elasticity. The Profit Diagram forces consideration of all three variables before choosing a remedy. |
| **Tunnel vision** | The competitive equivalent in Uganda is the business owner who responds to a new competitor entering the market by immediately dropping prices — the deer in the headlights response. The Ohmae alternative: step to the side. What dimensions of the 3C triangle does the new competitor not address? Where is their relative weakness? Attack there rather than engaging on the dimension they have chosen. |

## 12. Quick Reference — Ohmae Frameworks in Practice

**For the 06-competitive-analysis skill, the three most actionable Ohmae tools are:**

**1. The 3C Audit** — At the opening of any competitive analysis, explicitly populate all three vertices: What are the corporation's relative strengths (not absolute)? What is the customer's objective function (not stated product preference)? Who are the real competitors (including informal and substitutes)?

**2. KFS Identification** — Before recommending competitive strategy, identify the two or three KFS through industry-specific analysis. Test each against the criteria: does success correlate with this factor? Does failure in its absence correlate? Recommend strategy that concentrates resources on validated KFS.

**3. Four Routes Assessment** — For each competitive gap identified, determine which route is most applicable: concentrate on KFS, exploit relative superiority, challenge constraints aggressively, or identify unexploited SDF. Avoid the common error of defaulting to Route 1 (KFS focus) when Routes 2, 3, or 4 are more available and less contested.
