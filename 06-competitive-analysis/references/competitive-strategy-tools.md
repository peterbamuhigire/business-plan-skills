## 1. Competitor Analysis Framework

*Source: Harris & Lenox, Ch 2*

Competitor analysis is a framework for understanding and evaluating current or potential competitors. It assesses the durability of any competitive advantage and predicts how rivals will respond to strategic actions.

**Core principle:** If everyone can do it, it is difficult to create value from it. Barriers to entry and barriers to imitation are what allow a firm to earn sustained economic profits.

### Step 1. Identify Competitors

| Approach | Method | Best When |
|---|---|---|
| **Customer viewpoint** | Group all firms providing a similar product/service; measure cross-price elasticity where possible | Understanding substitution from the buyer's perspective |
| **Strategic groups** | Cluster industry players with similar strategies (geography, price tier, technology) | Large industry with distinct sub-groups |

**Ugandan example — mobile money:** Telco-led (MTN MoMo, Airtel Money), Bank-led (Stanbic FlexiPay), Fintech entrants (Wave, Chipper Cash) each form a strategic group.

### Step 2. Gather Intelligence

For each competitor, collect: revenue and market share, R&D investment, strategic partnerships, hiring patterns, and capital investments. Organise into a spreadsheet grouped by strategic group.

### Step 3. Analyse Rivals — The Competitor Response Profile

Answer four questions per competitor (adapted from Porter):

| Question | Focus |
|---|---|
| **What drives them?** | Objectives — growth targets, profit goals, share ambitions |
| **What do they assume?** | Operating assumptions about market, themselves, and you |
| **What are they doing?** | Current strategy — pricing, product focus, distribution |
| **What can they do?** | Resources and capabilities — financial strength, talent, technology |

These combine into a **competitor response profile** predicting how each rival will react to your moves.

### Predicting Rival Responses

| Your Move | Strong Rival Response | Weak Rival Response |
|---|---|---|
| Price cut in core segment | Aggressive match or undercut | Reluctant match; may exit |
| Launch differentiated product | Imitate within 6-12 months if no IP barrier | Ignore or partner |
| Enter their territory | Defend with promotions and loyalty incentives | Slow or no response |

## 2. Competitive Position Rating

*Source: Evans, Ch 2*

A quantitative method for rating your firm and each competitor against weighted success factors on a 0–5 scale.

### Rating Scale

| Score | Label | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Dominant | Clear market leader on this factor |
| 4 | Strong | Better than most competitors |
| 3 | Favourable | On par with the competition |
| 2 | Tenable | Slightly behind; survivable but needs work |
| 1 | Weak | Significantly behind competitors |
| 0 | Non-viable | Cannot compete on this factor at all |

### Process

1. **Identify success factors** — Convert customer buying criteria into factors for success (typically 2–3 cost-related, 5–6 differentiation-related, plus management and market share — about 10 total).
2. **Weight each factor** — Assign percentage weights totalling 100%. Higher weight = greater importance.
3. **Identify must-haves** — Any factor so critical that failing it means you should not be in the market (e.g. regulatory licence, ISO certification).
4. **Rate your firm** — Score 0–5 against each factor.
5. **Rate each competitor** — Score 0–5 against each factor.
6. **Calculate weighted position** — Multiply each rating by its weight, sum for overall position.

### Worked Example — Kampala Coffee Roastery

A specialty coffee roaster competing against two main rivals in the Ugandan premium coffee segment.

| Factor for Success | Weight | Our Roastery | Competitor A (Large Exporter) | Competitor B (Artisan Rival) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Market share | 15% | 2 | 5 | 2 |
| Cost competitiveness | 20% | 3 | 4.5 | 2 |
| Bean sourcing quality | 20% | 4.5 | 3 | 5 |
| Roasting consistency | 15% | 4 | 4 | 3 |
| Brand / packaging | 15% | 4 | 3 | 4.5 |
| Distribution reach | 15% | 2.5 | 5 | 1.5 |
| **Weighted total** | **100%** | **3.33** | **4.08** | **2.93** |

**Interpretation:** Competitor A leads (4.08) on market share and distribution. Our Roastery (3.33) is favourably positioned with strengths in bean quality and brand. Competitor B (2.93) is tenable but weak on cost and distribution. **Strategic priority:** Invest in distribution (weakest high-weight factor) and protect bean-sourcing advantage.

**Competing over time:** Add columns for ratings in three years. Track what competitors are planning and how success factor weightings may shift. Update annually.

## 3. Strategy Maps / Perceptual Maps

*Source: Harris & Lenox, Ch 8*

A strategy map (or perceptual map) is a visual tool for plotting competitors on two or three key dimensions that define competition in the industry.

### Purpose

- Identify the generic strategy each competitor is pursuing
- Spot **positioning gaps** — unoccupied spaces where demand may exist
- Communicate competitive landscape quickly to investors or the team
- Track shifts in positioning over time

### Generic Strategy Positions on the Map

| Position | Competitive Scope | Source of Advantage | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cost leader** | Broad | Low cost | Supermarket chain offering UGX 3,000 basic meals |
| **Differentiator** | Broad | Uniqueness | Full-service restaurant with craft cocktails at UGX 35,000+ |
| **Focused low cost** | Narrow | Low cost in one segment | Street-food vendor specialising in rolex at UGX 2,000 |
| **Niche** | Narrow | Uniqueness in one segment | Vegan café targeting Kampala expatriates at UGX 25,000 |

### How to Build a Strategy Map

1. **Select dimensions** — two factors capturing the key axes of competition (e.g. price vs. quality, breadth vs. reach, convenience vs. experience).
2. **Assess each competitor** on both dimensions — quantitatively where possible, or via expert judgement.
3. **Plot the map** — place each competitor as a circle; circle size can represent a third dimension (e.g. revenue).

### Worked Example — Kampala Fitness Market

Dimensions: Price (monthly membership) vs. Service breadth (number of services offered).

```
High service
breadth
  |
  |     [Kabira Club]        [Sheraton Health Club]
  |         (UGX 350K/mo)       (UGX 500K/mo)
  |
  |              *** GAP: mid-price, full-service ***
  |
  |    [Body by Barre]
  |        (UGX 150K/mo)
  |
  |  [Iron Gym]                         [CrossFit KLA]
  |    (UGX 60K/mo)                      (UGX 200K/mo)
  |
  +---------------------------------------------------> Price
Low                                              High
```

**Gap identified:** No competitor occupies the mid-price (UGX 180K–300K), full-service position. This could be a viable entry point.

**Tips:** Keep to two or three dimensions. Use multiple maps with different dimension pairs. Validate with customer perception data. Redraw annually to track movement.

## 4. Strategic Gap Analysis

*Source: Evans, Ch 2*

The strategic gap is the distance between your firm's current capabilities and where the ideal player stands. It has two components:

### 4a. Portfolio Gap — The Attractiveness/Advantage Matrix

**Question answered:** In which segments should you compete?

**Step 1.** Rate each segment for market attractiveness (1–5 scale):

| Criterion | 1 = Unattractive | 5 = Highly Attractive |
|---|---|---|
| Market size | Tiny | Very large |
| Market growth | Declining | Rapidly growing |
| Competitive intensity* | Intense competition | Limited competition |
| Industry profitability | Low margins | High margins |
| Market risk* | High volatility | Stable and predictable |

*Note: For competitive intensity and market risk, lower = more attractive, so invert the scoring.

**Step 2.** Pull in the competitive position rating (from Section 2 above) for each segment.

**Step 3.** Plot the matrix — competitive position on the x-axis, market attractiveness on the y-axis, circle size proportional to profit contribution.

### Worked Example — Ugandan Agribusiness

| Segment | Mkt Size | Mkt Growth | Comp. Intensity | Profitability | Mkt Risk | Attractiveness (avg) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| A: Domestic maize | 3 | 2 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 2.8 |
| B: Export beans | 2 | 3 | 2 | 2 | 2 | 2.2 |
| C: Processed flour | 3 | 4 | 3 | 4 | 4 | 3.6 |
| D: Animal feed | 4 | 5 | 4 | 3 | 3 | 3.8 |
| E: Organic herbs (new) | 2 | 5 | 5 | 4 | 2 | 3.6 |

Combined with competitive position ratings:

| Segment | Attractiveness | Competitive Position | Matrix Zone | Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A: Domestic maize | 2.8 | 4.0 | Lower right | **Hold** — strong position, moderate market |
| B: Export beans | 2.2 | 2.5 | Lower left | **Harvest or exit** — weak in unattractive market |
| C: Processed flour | 3.6 | 3.5 | Upper right | **Invest** — attractive market, good position |
| D: Animal feed | 3.8 | 2.0 | Upper left | **Build or partner** — attractive but weak position |
| E: Organic herbs | 3.6 | 1.5 (new) | Upper left | **Enter selectively** — attractive, but must build capability |

### 4b. Capability Gap — Ideal Player Profiling

**Question answered:** How well do you compete and what must improve?

**Process:**

1. **Envision future scenarios** — How will customer needs and competitive dynamics change in 3–5 years? Which success factors will become more or less important?

2. **Profile the ideal player** — The hypothetical firm that would score 5 on every success factor. What capabilities would it need?

3. **Stretch your sights** — Set a target competitive position that is ambitious but achievable. Aim to close the gap with the ideal player of *tomorrow*, not just today.

4. **Specify the capability gap** — For each success factor where your rating falls short of your target, name the gap.

### Worked Example — Kampala Coffee Roastery (continued)

| Success Factor | Current Rating | Target (3 years) | Gap | Action to Bridge |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Market share | 2 | 3 | 1.0 | Expand retail partnerships |
| Cost competitiveness | 3 | 3.5 | 0.5 | Invest in roasting automation (UGX 120M) |
| Bean sourcing quality | 4.5 | 5 | 0.5 | Lock in direct-trade contracts with Mt Elgon farmers |
| Roasting consistency | 4 | 4.5 | 0.5 | Hire experienced roast master |
| Brand / packaging | 4 | 4.5 | 0.5 | Redesign packaging for supermarket shelf appeal |
| Distribution reach | 2.5 | 4 | 1.5 | Partner with national distributor; launch e-commerce (UGX 45M) |

**Priority:** Distribution reach has the largest gap (1.5) and a high weight (15%). This is the single biggest lever for improving overall competitive position.

## 5. Generic Strategies

*Source: Evans, Ch 2; Harris & Lenox, Ch 8 (Porter framework)*

Three generic strategies can yield sustainable competitive advantage. Pursuing two simultaneously in the same business risks being "stuck in the middle."

### The Three Strategies

| Strategy | Source of Advantage | How It Works | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cost leadership** | Lowest cost in the industry | Economies of scale, operational efficiency, process innovation, tight overhead control | Customer needs shift towards quality; low-cost entrant undercuts you |
| **Differentiation** | Unique product/service customers pay a premium for | Superior features, quality, brand, service, or innovation; premium price covers incremental cost | Customers stop valuing the differentiation; cheaper "good enough" rival emerges |
| **Focus** | Dominance of a narrow segment | Concentrate all resources on one slice of the market; achieve either cost or differentiation leadership within it | The segment shrinks; a broad player decides to target your niche |

### Decision Framework

1. Review success factor weights — cost factors weighted higher? Consider cost leadership. Differentiation factors higher? Consider differentiation.
2. Review your ratings — do you rate well on cost or differentiation factors? Choose accordingly.
3. Consider scope — can you serve the full market (broad) or better to dominate one segment (focus)?

### "Stuck in the Middle" Warning

Trying to be both cheapest *and* most differentiated leads to confused positioning, conflicting priorities, and long-term underperformance.

**Rule:** Choose one generic strategy per business unit. If pursuing a different strategy in another segment, operate it as a separate unit with its own brand.

### Ugandan Examples

| Strategy | Example | How It Works |
|---|---|---|
| Cost leadership | SafeBoda (early phase) | Lowest-fare boda rides via app efficiency; high volume, thin margin |
| Differentiation | Café Javas | Premium dining experience at UGX 25,000–50,000; distinctive brand, consistent quality |
| Focus (differentiated) | Rolex vendor at Owino Market | Narrow product (one item), hyper-local, perfected through repetition, loyal following |
| Focus (low cost) | Solar Now (rural solar) | Focused on off-grid rural segment; pay-as-you-go model keeps entry cost under UGX 150,000 |

## 6. Value Chain Analysis

*Source: Portable MBA, Ch 3 (Gupta)*

The value chain is the set of interrelated activities from upstream to downstream that a business carries out — by itself and with suppliers, customers, and partners — to create and deliver value.

Without clarity on value chain design, other statements about strategy are "little more than hopes, wishes, and dreams." The design directly determines customer value, competitive advantage, and shareholder value.

### Two Design Decisions

#### Decision 1: Value Chain Architecture

**Question:** What is the design of the basic value-creation system through which inputs are converted into outputs? There is always more than one way to design it, and the choice has profound competitive implications.

**Three criteria for choosing architecture:**

| Criterion | Question |
|---|---|
| **Effectiveness** | Does it let us deliver to target customers in the most effective and efficient manner? |
| **Flexibility** | Can we scale up/down, expand the product bundle, or switch to a superior architecture? |
| **Defensibility** | Is it difficult for competitors to imitate and neutralise? |

**Ugandan example — poultry:** A traditional model (buy feed from middlemen at UGX 2,800/kg, outsource rearing, sell live birds at farm gate) vs. an integrated direct model (own maize mill at UGX 1,900/kg feed, own climate-controlled farm, slaughter/pack/brand, deliver direct to supermarkets via own cold chain). The integrated model costs more to establish (est. UGX 800M) but yields a competitive advantage through lower unit cost, quality control, and customer relationships that are difficult to replicate.

#### Decision 2: Internalisation vs. Outsourcing

**Guiding principle:** Do not do internally any activity that other firms can do more effectively and efficiently. But balance the payoff size against your share of payoffs.

**Decision matrix:**

| Your Capability | Partner's Capability | Recommended Action |
|---|---|---|
| Strong product technology, strong process technology | Weaker on both | **Internalise** — do it yourself |
| Strong product technology, weak process technology | Strong process technology | **Hybrid** — design in-house, outsource production |
| Weak product technology, strong process technology | Strong product technology | **Licence in** — acquire design, manufacture yourself |
| Weak on both | Strong on both | **Outsource fully** — buy from the best supplier |

### Step-by-Step Value Chain Audit

1. **Map every activity** — from raw material to after-sale service.
2. **Assess value added** — Does each activity add value the customer will pay for?
3. **Benchmark costs** — What would an external provider charge?
4. **Identify core competencies** — Activities giving competitive advantage stay in-house.
5. **Decide internalise or outsource** — for each non-core activity, using the matrix above.
6. **Optimise linkages** — Connections between activities (e.g. linking ordering to production scheduling) often matter more than individual activities.

## 7. Strategy Evaluation Tests

*Source: Portable MBA, Ch 13 (Day)*

Before committing resources to a strategy, subject it to four rigorous tests. Poor strategic choices dissipate scarce resources, consume time, and cause management to neglect other opportunities.

### The Four Tests

#### Test 1: Market Attractiveness — Is the opportunity worth pursuing?

Assess: market size and growth, demand cyclicality, competitive intensity, market fragmentation (protected niches?), channel access cost, and regulatory constraints/opportunities.

**Key insight:** Even high growth is not always good — it may draw an overabundance of competitors and erode profitability for all.

#### Test 2: Competitive Advantage Sustainability — Can the advantage last?

Five conditions make an advantage sustainable:

| Condition | Meaning | Ugandan Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Valuable** | Significant contribution to customer value | Proprietary API reducing transaction time from 3 min to 10 sec |
| **Durable** | Not quickly made obsolete | 20-year brand reputation of Mukwano in household goods |
| **Causally ambiguous** | Competitors cannot work out how it works | Tacit knowledge in a team of 50 agronomists trained over a decade |
| **Non-duplicable** | Even if understood, cannot be copied | Exclusive 10-year sourcing agreement with Kapchorwa vanilla cooperative |
| **Defensible** | Credible retaliation deters imitators | Dominant player able to match any price cut within 48 hours |

**The Law of Nemesis:** Nothing good lasts indefinitely. The question is how long the advantage can be sustained and at what cost to attackers.

#### Test 3: Implementation Feasibility — Can you actually do it?

| Sub-test | Key Question |
|---|---|
| **Feasibility** | Do you have the financial resources, skills, technology, and distribution? If not, can you acquire them before the window closes? |
| **Supportability** | Do implementers understand and believe in the strategy? Can it be explained in 2–3 pages? |
| **Consistency** | Do functional strategies (marketing, operations, finance, HR) all point the same way? Any contradictions (e.g. claiming premium quality but cutting the packaging budget)? |

#### Test 4: Risk-Reward Acceptability — Is the return worth the risk?

Forecast cash flows (not just accounting profit), discount at cost of capital plus risk premium, and calculate NPV. Identify major uncertainties, rate by probability and impact. Test robustness: a robust strategy succeeds under multiple scenarios; a fragile one only works if everything goes right.

### Strategy Evaluation Scorecard — Worked Example

**Scenario:** A Kampala-based logistics startup evaluating two growth strategies.

| Test | Strategy A: Expand to Jinja/Mbale | Strategy B: Launch last-mile cold chain in Kampala |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Market attractiveness** | Moderate — smaller market (UGX 15B), growing 8%/yr, 3 established competitors | High — large unmet need (UGX 40B), growing 15%/yr, no dedicated cold-chain provider |
| **2. Competitive advantage** | Tenable — brand awareness advantage only; easily replicated | Strong — proprietary temperature-tracking technology; first mover in cold chain |
| **3. Implementation** | Feasible — existing model replicable; team has upcountry experience | Challenging — requires UGX 400M in refrigerated vehicles; new technical skills needed |
| **4. Risk-reward** | Low risk, moderate reward; NPV est. UGX 180M over 5 years | Higher risk, high reward; NPV est. UGX 650M over 5 years |
| **Verdict** | Acceptable fallback | **Preferred** — pursue with phased investment to manage risk |

### Guarding Against Bias

Four biases to watch for: **anchoring** (fixating on one outcome — counter with best/worst/base scenarios), **selective perception** (seeing only confirming evidence — assign a devil's advocate), **illusion of control** (believing planning ensures outcomes — prepare contingency plans), and **availability** (over-weighting past successes — deliberately seek disconfirming evidence).

## 8. Competitive Life Cycle

*Source: Harris & Lenox, Ch 5*

Technologies and product markets progress along S-curves through three phases, each with distinct competitive dynamics and strategic imperatives.

### The Three Phases and Transition Points

| Phase | Characteristics | Transition Into Next Phase |
|---|---|---|
| **Emergent** | New concept; multiple designs competing; slow adoption; high uncertainty; widespread entry | **Annealing** — designs converge on a dominant design; shift from R&D to scaling |
| **Growth** | Dominant design established; adoption accelerates; head-to-head competition; prices fall | **Shakeout** — growth slows; marginal firms exit; cost efficiency becomes critical |
| **Mature** | Market saturated; survivors compete on cost and incremental innovation; growth from stealing share | **Disruption** — new technology starts a new S-curve; decide to adopt, adapt, or defend |

### Strategic Responses by Phase

| Phase | Strategy | Ugandan Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Emergent** | Experiment; keep costs flexible; be prepared to pivot | Fintech testing three USSD savings designs at UGX 5M pilot each |
| **Growth** | Scale rapidly; invest in operations; build brand; secure distribution | Jumia Uganda investing UGX 2B in warehouse and delivery fleet |
| **Mature** | Cut costs; incremental innovation; defend share; explore niches | Mukwano optimising production; launching economy packs for rural |
| **New disruption** | Invest in next S-curve while harvesting current one | Solar company investing in battery storage while selling home systems |

### Timing of Entry

| Entry Timing | Advantage | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Pioneer** | Shape dominant design; lock in early customers | Technology may fail; heavy cash burn |
| **Fast follower** | Learn from pioneer's mistakes; lower R&D cost | Pioneer may have locked in distribution |
| **Late entrant** | Proven demand; can focus on cost or niche | High barriers; thin margins |

### Nested Life Cycles

Life cycles can be nested: the Ugandan telecoms industry is mature, but mobile money is in the growth phase, and mobile lending is still emergent.

## 9. Blue Ocean Strategy

*Source: Evans, Ch 2 (summarising Kim & Mauborgne)*

Blue ocean strategy challenges the conventional approach of competing within existing industry boundaries ("red oceans") and instead advocates creating uncontested market space.

### Red Ocean vs. Blue Ocean

| Dimension | Red Ocean | Blue Ocean |
|---|---|---|
| **Market space** | Existing, well-defined | New, uncontested |
| **Competition** | Head-to-head; "bloody" rivalry | Competitors become irrelevant |
| **Demand** | Fight over existing demand | Create new demand |
| **Value-cost trade-off** | Choose differentiation *or* low cost | Break the trade-off — pursue *both* |
| **Strategic focus** | Beat the competition | Make the competition irrelevant |

### The Value Innovation Formula

```
Differentiated product + Low cost = Value innovation
```

Value innovation occurs when a firm simultaneously reduces costs (by eliminating and reducing factors the industry competes on) and increases buyer value (by raising and creating factors the industry has never offered).

### Six Principles

1. **Reconstruct market boundaries** — look across alternative industries, strategic groups, or buyer groups.
2. **Focus on the big picture** — fundamental rethink, not incremental improvements.
3. **Reach beyond existing demand** — who are the non-customers and why?
4. **Get the strategic sequence right** — buyer utility, accessible price, healthy-margin cost.
5. **Overcome organisational hurdles** — cognitive, resource, motivational, political.
6. **Build execution into strategy** — engage implementers from the start.

### The Four Actions Framework

| Action | Question | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Eliminate** | Which taken-for-granted factors should be removed? | Cuts cost without reducing value |
| **Reduce** | Which factors should drop well below industry standard? | Cuts cost further |
| **Raise** | Which factors should rise well above industry standard? | Increases buyer value |
| **Create** | What has the industry never offered? | Unlocks new value |

### Worked Example — Ugandan Funeral Services

Traditional funerals cost UGX 5M–15M with multiple intermediaries and no price transparency.

| Action | Blue Ocean Approach |
|---|---|
| **Eliminate** | Multiple separate negotiations — replace with one-stop platform |
| **Reduce** | Multi-day vigil expenses — streamlined 2-day package, dignity preserved |
| **Raise** | Pricing transparency — published fixed-price packages (UGX 3M, 5M, 8M) |
| **Create** | Pre-need savings plan at UGX 30,000/month; payout covers full funeral |

**Result:** Serves the large "non-customer" population who cannot afford or plan for funeral costs — creating demand rather than fighting over existing customers.

### Caution for SMEs

Evans notes: "Nine times out of ten, strategy development will be about improving strategic position in red ocean markets." For every iTunes or Cirque du Soleil, scores of blue ocean attempts have foundered. Use blue ocean thinking to generate creative options, then evaluate with the four tests (Section 7) before committing resources.
