# Pitch Anything — Klaff Framework

Oren Klaff raises capital at approximately $2 million per week using a method grounded in neuroscience. His core argument: pitching fails not because of poor content, but because of an evolutionary mismatch between how messages are sent (from the neocortex) and how they are received (by the crocodile brain). This reference distils his complete framework for any high-stakes presentation.

---

## 1. The Neurofinance Foundation: Three-Brain Architecture

### The Three Brains

The human brain evolved in three distinct stages, and all three operate simultaneously but with different priorities:

**Crocodile (croc) brain** — the most ancient structure. Handles survival instincts, fight/flight responses, and initial filtering of ALL incoming information. Every pitch passes through this gate first. It is a cognitive miser: it prefers simple, concrete, non-threatening, and novel information. It has almost no capacity for abstract reasoning.

**Midbrain** — determines the meaning of things in social contexts. Assesses: friend or threat? Familiar or unknown?

**Neocortex** — the modern problem-solving brain. Handles language, logic, mathematics, abstraction, creativity.

### The Fatal Disconnect

"Messages that are composed and sent by your young neocortex are received and processed by the other person's old crocodile brain."

When you pitch a business plan, your neocortex crafts the message (full of abstract concepts, projections, synergies, ROI). The listener's croc brain receives it and applies its primitive filter:

1. If it's not dangerous, ignore it.
2. If it's not new and exciting, ignore it.
3. If it is new, summarise it quickly and forget the details.
4. Do not send anything to the neocortex for problem-solving unless it is truly unexpected and extraordinary.

Result: up to 90 percent of your message is discarded before it reaches the neocortex. Complex, abstract pitches can be routed to the amygdala (fear circuitry), producing the physical sensation of wanting to escape.

### Croc Brain Operating Characteristics

The croc brain:
- Goes to sleep during PowerPoint presentations.
- Wants to choose between two clearly explained options, not analyse ten.
- Needs concrete, visual, novel information.
- Is emotional — but its default emotion is fear, not excitement.
- Has a very short attention span and craves novelty.
- Cannot hold hot cognitions (desire, excitement) and cold cognitions (analysis, calculation) simultaneously.

### Rules of Engagement for Pitching

To survive the croc brain's filter, every message must be:
- **Safe** — does not trigger threat alarms.
- **Novel** — perceived as new, exciting, out of the ordinary.
- **Simple** — concrete, not abstract; big picture, not granular.
- **Visual** — the brain is wired to detect movement and images, not dense text.

## 2. The STRONG Method — The Six-Step Pitch Process

Klaff's master framework:

| Letter | Step | Purpose |
|--------|------|---------|
| **S** | Setting the frame | Establish the context and perspective that governs the entire interaction |
| **T** | Telling the story | Create the "Why now?" narrative using three market forces |
| **R** | Revealing the intrigue | Deploy the intrigue frame to maintain engagement and break analytical thinking |
| **O** | Offering the prize | Position yourself as the prize, not the supplicant |
| **N** | Nailing the hookpoint | Reach the moment where the target actively wants what you have |
| **G** | Getting the decision | Close from a position of strength, not neediness |

This method is not sequential in the rigid sense — frames are set, stacked, and reinforced throughout the pitch. But the sequence provides the architecture.

## 3. Frame Control — The Central Mechanism

### What Is a Frame?

"Frames are mental structures that shape the way we see the world and put relationships in context. The frame you put around a situation completely and totally controls its meaning."

**The person who owns the frame owns the conversation.**

When two frames meet, the stronger frame absorbs the weaker frame. Rational arguments and facts bounce off a winning frame — they cannot overcome it through logic alone.

### Types of Frames

#### Power Frame
The most common frame in business. Used by targets (buyers, investors, decision-makers) to assert dominance — they control the meeting, the agenda, the timing. Classic power frame moves: keeping you waiting in the lobby, being late, taking phone calls during your pitch, sending subordinates instead of the decision-maker.

**How to break it:** Small acts of denial and defiance combined with humour. When told you only have 15 minutes: "That's okay — I only have 12." Do it with a smile. You have transferred the power frame.

#### Time Frame
Used by targets to rush or control the pace of the meeting. "I only have 10 minutes." "We're done here."

**How to break it:** Set your own time constraint first — "I've got about 20 minutes to give you the big idea." This puts the target at ease AND takes control of the clock. If a target uses time pressure on you after you have begun: "Looks like time's up. I've got to get to my next meeting." When they are interested, they will extend.

#### Analyst Frame
The most dangerous mid-pitch frame. Emerges when audience members drill down into technical details, numbers, and statistics — switching into cold cognition mode. "What are your exact unit costs?" "What discount rate did you use?" Once the audience goes analytical, hot cognitions freeze.

**How to break it:** The intrigue frame (see below). Answer the technical question with only high-level summary data, then redirect: "The revenue is $X, expenses $Y, net $Z. You can verify those figures later — right now, the question is: are we a good fit to work together?"

#### Intrigue Frame
A personal narrative — ideally about you, involving risk, tension, time pressure, and unresolved outcome — that hijacks analytical thinking and forces the listener into narrative mode. The brain cannot be coldly analytical and emotionally engaged in a story at the same time.

**Elements of an effective intrigue story:**
1. Brief and relevant to your pitch.
2. You must be at the centre of the story.
3. Risk, danger, and uncertainty.
4. Time pressure — a clock is ticking with ominous consequences.
5. Tension — you are trying to do something but are being blocked.
6. Serious consequences — failure will not be pretty.
7. **No resolution** — stop the story at the most intriguing moment ("Anyway...") and return to the pitch. Complete the arc only at the end of the presentation.

The Jaws principle: "If you fit a GPS tracker to the shark, there's no mystery, no tension, no blockbuster."

#### Prize Frame
Repositions the dynamic so that you are the prize being evaluated, not the supplicant seeking approval. "The money has to earn you — not the other way around."

Prizing rests on three fundamental human behaviours:
1. We chase that which moves away from us.
2. We want what we cannot have.
3. We only place value on things that are difficult to obtain.

**Prizing in practice:** Make the buyer qualify themselves to you. "Can you tell me more about your organisation? I'm selective about who I work with." Issue challenges with light humour: "So many buyers, yet only one of me. How are you going to compete for my attention?"

Never chase the deal. Embrace ABL — Always Be Leaving — over the old ABC (Always Be Closing).

#### Moral Authority Frame
A nearly unshakeable frame grounded in ethical high ground. Used when you are clearly in the right, when you are protecting the interests of a community or a principle larger than money. Mother Teresa's frame — "help the downtrodden, life and death is not the critical thing" — overrode the powerful medical establishment's doctor frame because her values sat above theirs.

In business pitches: frame your work around legacy, community benefit, protection of the vulnerable, historical significance. These frames are very difficult for opponents to attack without appearing mercenary.

### Frame Collision and Plowing

When two frames collide, one absorbs the other. At the moment of collision you will feel anxiety in the pit of your stomach. This is the critical moment. "You must stay composed and stick to your frame. No matter what happens, no matter how much social pressure and discomfort you suffer, you must stay composed and stick to your frame. This is called plowing. Always moving forward. Never stopping. Never any self-doubt."

### Beta Traps — What They Are and How to Avoid Them

Beta traps are social rituals designed to place you in a low-status, subordinate position and keep you there. They exist in virtually every business setting:

- **The lobby**: Sign the guest book, take a badge, sit in the waiting area, read old magazines. All signals that you are a supplicant.
- **The conference room**: Arrive first, sit alone, wait for the targets who will arrive late and chat amongst themselves as if you are not there.
- **Sending subordinates**: The decision-maker does not appear because "something came up."
- **The coffee shop meeting**: No privacy, constant interruptions, zero status control.
- **The trade show booth**: "A person standing in a trade show booth may as well erect a neon sign above his head that reads, 'I Am Needy!'"
- **The Walmart supercollider**: The ultimate beta trap — purpose-built cells designed to strip the vendor of all frame control and compress everything to price.

**How to avoid beta traps:**
1. Politely ignore them — do not sign the visitor book with eager compliance.
2. Use denial and defiance: stride past the receptionist, open doors, assert your presence.
3. Set your own timing: "I can wait 15 minutes, but then I have to leave."
4. Change the venue to your turf when possible.
5. Never supplicate to gatekeepers. They are part of the beta trap mechanism.

## 4. Status — Alpha, Beta, and Situational Power

### Why Status Matters

"If you are pitching from a lower-level platform, or low social status, your ability to persuade others will be diminished, and your pitch will be difficult, no matter how great your idea or product."

Status is the platform from which you pitch. High status makes everything easier. Statements are trusted without challenge. The group follows. The alpha's emotional state sets the tone of the room.

### Global vs. Situational Status

**Global status** — the accumulated social rank from wealth, power, and popularity. Fixed. You cannot fake being a billionaire in front of a billionaire. Do not try.

**Situational status (local star power)** — temporarily elevated status created within a specific domain or interaction. This is what you can control, create, and seize.

The golf pro example: in a parking lot, the cardiac surgeon has higher global status. On the golf course, the golf pro is the domain expert. Status becomes fluid. The surgeon follows the pro's instructions without question.

Benoit the French waiter: seized local star power immediately upon entering, stripped the host of status using domain expertise (wine selection), co-opted guests into his frame, and controlled the entire evening — all within minutes.

### Seizing Situational Status: Six Steps

1. Politely ignore power rituals and avoid beta traps.
2. Be unaffected by the customer's global status.
3. Look for opportunities to perpetrate small denials and defiances that strengthen your frame.
4. As soon as you take power, move the discussion into an area where you are the domain expert — where your knowledge is unassailable.
5. Apply a prize frame: position yourself as the reward for choosing to work with you.
6. Confirm your alpha status by having the customer make a statement that qualifies your higher status. "Remind me again why I want to do business with you?" Then, when they answer: "Yeah, that's good. I'll keep that in mind."

### Additional Status Rules

- **Arrive on time.** Being late gives away power before the meeting starts.
- **Create momentum immediately.** Do not hesitate. Choose a frame and force a collision early. The longer you wait, the more you reinforce the target's status.
- **Avoid idle social banter.** It reduces your status, not theirs.
- **Have fun and enjoy your work.** Nothing is as attractive as someone who is genuinely enjoying what they do. It draws the group toward you.
- **Situational status evaporates.** Once you leave the interaction, it is gone. Even a five-minute break resets the clock. You must rebuild it each time.

## 5. Pitching the Big Idea — The 20-Minute Pitch Architecture

### The 20-Minute Rule

"The most important scientific discovery of the twentieth century [the double helix] can be pitched in five minutes. Yet nearly every pitch that I've seen takes at least 45 minutes — a ridiculous amount of time."

The human attention span sustains genuine engagement for approximately 20 minutes. After that, the brain does not simply stop absorbing new information — it begins forgetting what it has already learned.

**The time-constraint pattern** — always open with this: "Guys, let's get started. I've only got about 20 minutes to give you the big idea, which will leave us some time to talk it over before I have to get out of here."

This does three things: (1) sets a time frame that you control; (2) signals professional competence ("anything can be pitched in 20 minutes by a pro"); (3) communicates that you are busy and in demand.

### The Four Phases of the Pitch

| Phase | Content | Time |
|-------|---------|------|
| 1 | Introduce yourself and the big idea | 5 minutes |
| 2 | Explain the budget and secret sauce | 10 minutes |
| 3 | Offer the deal | 2 minutes |
| 4 | Stack frames for hot cognition | 3 minutes |

---

### Phase 1: Introduce Yourself and the Big Idea (5 minutes)

**Your background — 2 minutes maximum.**

Lead with your track record of successes only. "Research has shown that your impression of someone is generally based on the *average* of the available information about them, not the sum." Telling someone one great thing about yourself leaves a better impression than telling them one great thing and one mediocre thing. Stop with your single most impressive achievement.

Example: "My degree is from [University]. I was at [Firm] for four years, but my only homerun there was [specific achievement]. I left six months ago to work on this."

**The "Why Now?" Frame — using three market forces.**

Before introducing your idea, explain why it is relevant now. Targets do not like old deals. "Nobody wants to invest time or money into an idea that has been sitting around."

Describe three converging forces that have opened a brief market window:

1. **Economic forces** — what has changed financially in the market? Credit availability? Interest rates? Consumer purchasing power?
2. **Social forces** — what behavioural changes are driving demand? Environmental concern? Urbanisation? Digital adoption?
3. **Technology forces** — what new technology has enabled your idea or disrupted the existing solution?

Show the three forces converging now — this communicates movement, and the brain is wired to detect movement. This overcomes "change blindness" (showing two static states without showing the transition between them — the brain ignores it).

Example: "Economic force: the cost of making this product has just dropped below $10, making retail pricing viable. Social force: awareness of the problem is skyrocketing. Technology force: the controlling chip is now small enough and affordable enough for mass production."

**The Idea Introduction Pattern** (developed by Geoff Moore, 1999):

> "For [target customers]
> Who are dissatisfied with [current offerings in the market],
> My idea/product is a [new category]
> That provides [key problem-solution features].
> Unlike [competing product],
> My idea/product [describe key differentiating features]."

This pattern avoids triggering a threat response in the croc brain. It is concrete, structured, and non-threatening.

---

### Phase 2: Explain the Budget and Secret Sauce (10 minutes)

"Tune the message to the mind of the target" — not simply simplify. The croc brain hates statistics and probabilities. It loves human relationships and narratives.

**Two key realisations:**
1. It does not matter how much information you give (a lot or a little) — what matters is how well you tune it to the listener's mind.
2. All important content must fit within the limits of attention: approximately 20 minutes.

**Pitching numbers and projections:**

"Every experienced buyer and investor knows that you will be saying your budgets are 'conservative' while preparing absurdly aggressive plans." Do not fight this expectation — demonstrate your skill at *budgeting* (which is genuinely difficult and respected) rather than your skill at projecting revenue (which any simpleton can do).

Focus on: What are your assumptions? How have you managed costs? What happens if the plan goes wrong?

**Competition:**

Address competition directly. Answer two questions:
1. How easy is it for new competitors to enter?
2. How easy is it for customers to switch from your product to a competitor's?

**Secret sauce:**

The one unfair advantage that gives your idea staying power. This is your competitive moat — whether it is proprietary technology, a unique process, exclusive relationships, first-mover position, or a licensed asset. Be brief. Name it clearly. Do not over-explain.

---

### Phase 3: Offer the Deal (2 minutes)

In clear, concise terms, describe exactly what the target receives when they decide to do business with you:
- What you will deliver.
- When it will be delivered.
- How it will be delivered.
- What role they play.

"The most important deliverable in your deal is you." Their confidence in you as the operator is the foundation of every other commitment they make.

---

## 6. Attention — The Dopamine-Norepinephrine Cocktail

Genuine attention requires two neurotransmitters working together:

- **Dopamine** — the chemical of *desire* and *anticipation of reward*. Triggered by novelty, surprise, and the prospect of a positive outcome. "Your brain is stimulated by surprise because our world is fundamentally unpredictable." (Dr. Greg Berns, *Satisfaction*)
- **Norepinephrine** — the chemical of *tension* and *alertness*. Triggered by the presence of real consequences — something can be gained or lost.

**The formula: Attention = Desire + Tension**

To create desire: offer a reward (the dopamine kick).
To create tension: take something away, or introduce the possibility of loss (the norepinephrine kick).

This cocktail sustains attention for approximately 20 minutes in most people, after which it becomes chemically impossible to maintain regardless of your skill or content quality.

### Creating Novelty (Dopamine)

Novelty triggers dopamine release. Ways to introduce novelty in a pitch:
- A short product demonstration of something genuinely new.
- A new idea presented in a surprising way.
- A strong metaphor that reframes a complex subject.
- Moving objects and visual contrast (the croc brain is wired to detect movement).
- Unexpected information that violates the target's predictions.

"You create novelty by violating the target's expectations in a pleasing way."

Beware of satiation: once the target feels they fully understand your idea, curiosity ends and attention drops. Do not over-explain. Leave space for curiosity to operate.

### Creating Tension (Norepinephrine) — Push/Pull Patterns

Tension comes from controlled conflict. You must not be afraid of creating it.

Three push/pull patterns at increasing intensity:

**Low-intensity:**
- Push: "There's a real possibility that we might not be right for each other."
- [Pause.]
- Pull: "But then again, if this did work out, our combined forces could become something great."

**Medium-intensity:**
- Push: "A venture group I know doesn't even look at the idea — they only care about who the people are. If you and I don't share that view, it would never work between us."
- [Pause.]
- Pull: "But that's crazy to think — I can see you value people over smart ideas. You're not a robot."

**High-intensity:**
- Push: "Based on a couple of reactions I'm getting — it seems like this isn't a good fit. Let's wrap this up and agree to get together on the next one."
- [Pause. Start packing up. Be willing to leave.]

The Don Draper principle: "Looks like there's not much else to do here. Let's call it a day." The moment you are willing to walk away — and the target senses that you mean it — the frame shifts immediately.

Without tension loops, nothing is compelling the audience to stay with the pitch storyline. A pitch narrative is a series of tension loops: push, then pull; create tension, then resolve it.

## 7. Frame Stacking and Hot Cognitions

### What Is Hot Cognition?

"Deciding that you like something before you fully understand it — that's a hot cognition."

Hot cognitions are:
- **Primal** — when the croc brain is excited, the neocortex barely functions.
- **Unavoidable** — you cannot choose not to experience them.
- **Instant and enduring** — formed the moment you encounter something, not after deliberation.

Cold cognitions are analytical: evaluation, calculation, logical assessment. Cold cognitions develop in the neocortex and can take hours or days.

Most pitches are built to trigger cold cognition. This is backwards. The goal is wanting — a hot cognition that precedes and drives decision.

"By the time consciousness kicks in, most of the work has already been done." (John-Dylan Haynes, Max Planck Institute)

### The Four-Frame Hot Cognition Stack

Stack these four frames in rapid succession at the end of the pitch to ignite wanting in the target's croc brain:

#### Hot Cognition 1: The Intrigue Frame
Introduce something the target desires but cannot have immediately. Deploy an unfinished personal narrative (the "man in the jungle" pattern):

- Put a man in the jungle.
- Have beasts attack him.
- Will he get to safety? (Stop here — do not resolve the story yet.)

"The intrigue is created by the fact that there's no final resolution."

The "ticking time bomb" variant: show a time-critical situation with escalating consequences, arrive at the edge of resolution, then say "Anyway..." and move to the next frame.

#### Hot Cognition 2: The Prize Frame
Position yourself as the scarce resource, not the target. "I need to figure out if you are right for me. Can you tell me why we would enjoy working together?"

Internal monologue (what you say to yourself): "I am the prize. You are trying to impress me. You are trying to win my approval."

Elements of the prize frame in delivery:
1. I have one of the better deals in the market.
2. I am choosy about who I work with.
3. I could see working with you — but I need to know more.
4. Please qualify yourselves to me.
5. My existing partners are selective.

The Helen Woodward Animal Shelter example: Klaff entered as the hero rescuing a dog. The young adoption counsellor immediately reversed the frame — he ended up completing an application and begging to be approved as a suitable rescuer. The dog became the prize. The shelter had flipped the frame completely.

#### Hot Cognition 3: The Time Frame
Use real time constraints, not pressure tactics. "Every single person knows what you're talking about when you say the train is leaving the station at such and such date and time."

Time frame script: "Nobody likes time pressure — I don't like it, and you don't. But this deal is bigger than me or you or any one person. The deal is going ahead. There's a critical path that everyone has to work with. So we need to decide by [date]."

Key distinction: scarcity and urgency must feel genuine, not manufactured. "Extreme time pressure feels forced and cut-rate." Set a real time constraint based on actual deal dynamics.

#### Hot Cognition 4: The Moral Authority Frame
Ground the deal in values that transcend money. Show that you operate from a high moral position. "The most powerful politicians have people who will do exactly what they're told. But there is one person whose direction even the president will follow almost blindly: his doctor."

The doctor frame rules: (1) Do what I say. (2) Defer to my expertise. (3) Accept my conclusions.

The Mother Teresa example: her moral authority frame (help the downtrodden; life and death is not the critical thing) collapsed the doctor frames of the world's most powerful physicians at Scripps Clinic. They ended up signing up to volunteer in Tijuana.

In a pitch: "We want the community's football field to be restored — no matter what happens with our bid. We are going to pay for it ourselves."

## 8. Eradicating Neediness

"If you talk to investment bankers — the pros that make million-dollar decisions almost daily — they'll tell you: validation-seeking behaviour is the number one deal killer."

### What Causes Neediness?

Neediness is caused by the combination of:
1. Wanting something only the target can give (money, contract, job).
2. Sensing the target is becoming uncooperative or disengaged.
3. Believing the target's acceptance will relieve your anxiety.
4. The fear loop: anxiety → validation-seeking → needy signals → target pulls back → more anxiety.

"Neediness triggers fear and uncertainty, causing the target's croc brain to take over — but not in a good way. Its goal is to prevent further threat by effectively blocking out the higher-level brain."

Signals of neediness that kill deals:
- "Do you still think it's a good deal?"
- "So, what do you think so far?"
- "We can sign a deal right away if you want."
- Trial closes: "Are we in the ballpark?"

### The Three-Step Formula for Eradicating Neediness

1. **Want nothing.** Eliminate your desire for the specific outcome in this meeting. Reconnect with your broader purpose — the adventure of deal-making, not this single result.
2. **Focus only on things you do well.** In the final minutes, demonstrate mastery. Do one thing with excellence.
3. **Announce your intention to leave the social encounter.** "I have to get to my next meeting." Start packing up. The willingness to withdraw signals self-control, strength, and confidence that most targets will greatly admire.

### The Tao of Steve

From the film *The Tao of Steve*, Klaff adapts three rules:

1. **Eliminate your desires.** It is not necessary to want things. Sometimes you have to let them come to you.
2. **Be excellent in the presence of others.** Show people one thing that you are very good at.
3. **Withdraw.** At a crucial moment, when people are expecting you to come after them, pull away.

"People want what they can't have. So, when you finish your pitch, deny your audience. Start to pull away. In so doing, you banish insecurity and trigger a powerful prizing effect on your audience. They will come to you."

### The Final Pitch Example (Enterprise Partners)

With $468 in his bank account and the company near collapse, Klaff ran the full prize frame without neediness:

1. "This deal will be fully subscribed in the next 14 days."
2. "We don't need VC money, but we want a big name on our cap sheet to strengthen the IPO registration."
3. "I think you guys are interesting, but are you really the right investor? We need to know more about you and the relationships your firm can bring."

Then he sat in silence. They committed. $2.1 million wired within days; $4 million more within 30 days.

## 9. The Hookpoint

The hookpoint is "the place in the presentation where your listeners become emotionally engaged. Instead of you giving them information, they are asking you for more on their own. At the hookpoint, they go beyond interested to being involved and then committed."

The hookpoint is not a moment you announce — it is a moment you create through:
- Sustained frame control that has held the target's attention.
- Hot cognitions that have created genuine wanting.
- A prize frame that makes them lean forward to earn access.
- Eradicated neediness that signals you are comfortable walking away.

When the hookpoint is set, the target's energy shifts from passive reception to active engagement. They ask questions not to challenge but to understand how to participate. The deal begins to close itself.

## 10. The Case Study — The $1 Billion Airport Pitch

Klaff's most instructive case study demonstrates the full STRONG method under competitive conditions.

**Context:** Klaff and two-person team (Greenberg Capital) vs. Goldhammer (a major Wall Street firm with 12+ staff) pitching to raise $1 billion for a private airport redevelopment.

**Key tactical decisions:**

**Frame choice:** Rather than competing on size and reputation (where Goldhammer would win), Klaff chose the "best idea" frame from the opening: "This is a decision not about who is the most charming or the most skilled in finance, but about who has the right ideas." This minimised Goldhammer's core advantage before they could deploy it.

**Reframing the competition:** "I know how great those guys are. Is there anything they can't do with all that youthful energy and those amazing bespoke suits?" This painted Goldhammer as large, expensive, and corporate — a liability in a community-sensitive project.

**Three market forces pattern:** Social (transparency fatigue with banker fees), economic (a glut of investor money for transparent deals), and technology (LEED certification = 10% tax reduction).

**The big idea — legacy:** The financial case was secondary. The frame was: "You will have your names written in history. We are not building a shopping centre. This has to be done right."

**Hot cognitions — aviation porn:** Large poster boards of jets (knowing the committee were pilots) created immediate dopamine responses.

**The human element:** Klaff brought Joe Ramirez — a local mechanic — to speak about his childhood football field that had been paved over. This triggered genuine emotion in the room, creating memory encoding through heightened emotional state.

**Moral authority frame:** "We are deciding what will be done with 1,000 acres in the middle of a community of 115,000 people." The frame shifted from financial transaction to stewardship.

**The withdrawal:** "When you feel that the time is right, I encourage you to come to our office and talk over how we can make that happen." He did not ask for the deal — he invited them to come to him.

**Result:** Greenberg Capital won. Goldhammer's 42-slide, 60-minute cold-cognition presentation produced a "coma-inducing" result despite its polish.

## 11. Getting Started — The Seven Steps to Frame Mastery

1. **Learn to recognise beta traps** without engaging them. Notice them everywhere. See the lobbies, conference rooms, and social rituals for what they are.
2. **Gradually step around beta traps.** Start small. "I can wait 15 minutes, but then I have to leave." Practice with a partner.
3. **Identify and label social frames.** Power frames, time frames, analyst frames are present in daily life. Develop the vocabulary to see them.
4. **Initiate frame collisions with safe targets** — not the CEO on day one. Practice in low-risk settings. Always keep humour in it.
5. **Small acts of defiance and denial** — perpetrate them with a soft touch. If you are triggering defensive responses, you are coming on too strong. Pause and reset.
6. **Do not force frame control.** It is not theatre. "When you say something that causes a frame collision, do it with a twinkle in your eye and a smile in your heart."
7. **Work with other frame masters.** Apprenticeship accelerates mastery. Teach others once you have developed basic competence.

### The Lingua Franca of Frame Control

Key terms to internalise:
- Frame control / frame collision / deframing / reframing
- Power frame / power-busting frame
- Beta traps / seizing status / local star power
- Prize frame / prizing
- Intrigue frame / intrigue ping
- Time frame / analyst frame / moral authority frame
- Push/pull / tension loop
- Hot cognition / cold cognition
- Crocodile brain / neocortex
- Hookpoint / wanting
- Alpha / beta

| Framework | Application |

## 13. Business Plan Integration — Applying Klaff to Written Documents

Klaff's framework is primarily for live pitches, but its principles apply directly to written business plans.

### Executive Summary

The executive summary is a croc-brain document. It must:
- Open with the **Why Now?** frame — three forces creating the market window (not "we have identified an opportunity").
- Use the **idea introduction pattern** as the first description of the business.
- Lead with a compelling **narrative** about the founder's insight or a customer problem — not with company registration details.
- Include only **one impressive background fact** about the founders — the single most credible achievement.
- Avoid dense tables, charts, and statistics in the first two pages — these trigger cold cognition and the reader checks out before they reach the substance.

### Products and Services Section

- Describe the **secret sauce** clearly and confidently — the one thing competitors cannot easily replicate.
- Frame the business model using the **Why Now?** three-force pattern — why is this product the right solution at this moment?
- Use **analogy and metaphor** for complex technical features — the croc brain understands concrete comparison, not abstract specification.

### Market Analysis

- Show **market movement**, not market size. "The market is $X" is a static fact that triggers change blindness. Show the three forces that are moving the market in your direction.
- Human stories about target customers (Joe Ramirez) are more persuasive to decision-makers than demographic tables.

### Financial Projections

- Lead with **budgeting skill**, not revenue projections. Demonstrate that you understand costs, risks, and cash flow discipline.
- Frame projections as **conservative assumptions** backed by named sources — not hockey-stick ambition.
- The **"Why now?" time constraint** applies to financials: show why the capital is needed at this specific point in the project timeline.

### Funding Request

- Never open with the amount needed. Open with **what the capital enables** — legacy, community impact, market leadership.
- Frame the investor/lender as **earning access** to the deal — not as the provider of last resort.
- Use the **moral authority frame**: connect the funding to outcomes that transcend financial return.
- End with a **withdrawal** — "We welcome the opportunity to present in person to your investment committee at a time that suits you" — not "We urgently request your approval."

### Risk Section

- Use the **tension-loop structure**: name the risk (push), then show the mitigation (pull). Tension without resolution is anxiety; tension followed by resolution is confidence.
- Demonstrate moral authority: you have identified risks others would hide. This is a credibility move.

## 14. East Africa and Uganda Context Notes

Klaff's frameworks are based on universal croc-brain dynamics. "The crocodile brain is the same everywhere. There aren't New York croc brains and California editions and special French ones. Every croc brain responds the same." However, cultural calibration matters in application:

### Relevant adaptations for Uganda / East Africa context:

**Status and respect:** In East African business culture, respect for elders, seniority, and institutional authority is embedded. Overt defiance must be calibrated carefully. The Klaff "small acts of defiance" principle should be expressed through **confident expertise** rather than confrontational acts. Demonstrating domain knowledge (the golf pro principle) is culturally compatible and powerful.

**The prize frame:** Works strongly across all cultures. Ugandan lenders and investors — like all investors — respond to scarcity and the signal that others want what you have. "UDB (Uganda Development Bank) is reviewing our application alongside this bank" is a legitimate and powerful prize frame.

**Moral authority frame:** Extremely powerful in the Ugandan context. Framing a business around community employment, supporting smallholder farmers, reducing youth unemployment, or contributing to Vision 2040 goals resonates deeply with local DFIs, government-linked investors, and impact lenders. This is not manipulation — it is accurate for most Ugandan SMEs.

**Why Now? / three market forces:** The Ugandan economic environment provides genuine material for this frame: GDP growth at 6.3% (FY 2024/25); AfCFTA implementation creating new export opportunities; EAC Common Market freedoms opening cross-border trade; NIN/BRN digitisation creating formal sector growth; and the demographic dividend (1.1–1.2 million new workers entering annually) driving consumer demand.

**Beta traps:** Government and bank offices in Uganda can be deeply beta-trapped — long waiting periods, multiple gatekeepers, subordinate officers conducting initial reviews. The Klaff principle of polite but confident self-presentation applies, though always with deference to cultural norms. Arrive prepared, arrive early, and treat every interaction with quiet confidence.

**Neediness:** Urgently relevant. Ugandan SMEs seeking bank loans often appear desperate — the financials are thin, the collateral marginal, and the entrepreneur visibly anxious. The single most powerful improvement available to most Ugandan loan applicants is to reduce visible neediness by: preparing one alternative funding option before the meeting; practising the prize frame; and using the Tao of Steve withdrawal principle.

**Intrigue frame:** Storytelling is deeply culturally embedded in East African communication. Personal narratives of overcoming challenge — the origin story of the founder, a community problem solved, a near-failure averted — are not only acceptable but expected. The intrigue story formula (brief, personal, uncertain outcome withheld) fits naturally into Ugandan business communication styles.

## 15. Quick Reference — Klaff's Core Principles

1. **Pitches are sent from the neocortex but received by the croc brain.** Design for the receiver, not the sender.
2. **The person who owns the frame owns the conversation.** Set it early; defend it always.
3. **Hot cognition precedes and drives decision.** Create wanting before you explain everything.
4. **Status is the platform.** You cannot pitch well from a low-status position. Create local star power through domain expertise.
5. **Attention = Desire + Tension.** Both are required. You have approximately 20 minutes.
6. **Neediness is the number one deal killer.** Want nothing. Be excellent. Withdraw.
7. **You are the prize, not the money.** Money is a commodity. You and your deal are unique.
8. **Always Be Leaving, not Always Be Closing.** The willingness to walk away is your most powerful signal.
9. **Novelty captures attention; tension holds it.** Violate expectations pleasantly. Create conflict with push/pull.
10. **Humour is not optional.** It signals confidence, defuses tension, and invites the target into the frame game as a participant, not a subject.
