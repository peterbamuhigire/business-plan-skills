# Theory of Change — Construction Guide with Uganda Examples

*The Theory of Change (ToC) is the logical argument that links a project's inputs and activities to long-term social impact. Required by USAID, EU, DFID/FCDO, UN agencies, and most impact funds. This guide covers ToC construction principles, Uganda sector examples, and common mistakes.*

---

## 1. What a Theory of Change Is

A Theory of Change answers one question: **Why do we believe that doing X will lead to Y?**

It is more than a LogFrame. Where a LogFrame is a planning and accountability tool (what will happen, how will we measure it), a ToC is an explanation of **the mechanism of change** — the causal logic that connects your activities to outcomes.

### The Standard ToC Hierarchy

```
INPUTS → ACTIVITIES → OUTPUTS → OUTCOMES → IMPACT
                                    ↑
                            KEY ASSUMPTIONS
                            (the conditions that must hold
                            for each causal step to work)
```

| Level | Definition | Timeframe | Who controls it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Inputs** | Resources invested | Before project | Funder + implementer |
| **Activities** | What the project does | During project | Implementer |
| **Outputs** | Direct, countable products of activities | During project | Implementer |
| **Outcomes** | Changes in knowledge, behaviour, or conditions | 1–3 years | Influenced by project |
| **Impact** | Long-term change in lives or systems | 3–10 years | Partly beyond project control |

## 2. IF–THEN–BECAUSE Structure

The most rigorous way to write a ToC is the IF–THEN–BECAUSE sentence:

```
IF we [activities]
THEN we will produce [outputs]
WHICH WILL LEAD TO [outcome changes] among [target group]
BECAUSE [state the causal mechanism — why this works]
WHICH WILL CONTRIBUTE TO [long-term impact]
ASSUMING THAT [key external conditions hold].
```

**Why the BECAUSE clause matters:** Funders want to know you have evidence that your approach works. The BECAUSE clause forces you to cite evidence, local precedent, or theory that justifies your causal logic.

### Example (Coffee Value Chain):

> IF we train 500 smallholder coffee farmers in Good Agricultural Practices (GAP) and GPS-register their farms for EUDR traceability THEN 500 farms will be certified and GPS-linked to the cooperative's traceability system WHICH WILL LEAD TO premium buyer contracts and higher farm-gate prices BECAUSE premium roasters (EU/UK specialty sector) are actively seeking EUDR-compliant Uganda Robusta sources and currently pay USD 1.00–1.50/kg above standard grade WHICH WILL CONTRIBUTE TO increased household incomes for 500 farming families in Mbale District ASSUMING THAT UCDA maintains its traceability database without service disruption and EU buyers maintain demand for Uganda specialty coffee.

## 3. ToC Construction Steps

### Step 1: Define the Problem Precisely

Start from evidence:
- Who is affected? (be specific — "youth aged 18–30 in Luwero District", not "unemployed youth")
- What is the scale? (use UBOS data, sector surveys, UNDP reports)
- What is the root cause? (apply 5 Whys from `12-risk-analysis/SKILL.md`)
- What has been tried before? (show you understand the landscape)

### Step 2: State Your Intended Impact

Write one sentence that describes the long-term change you want to contribute to. This should link to:
- Uganda NDP III goals (if applying to government grants)
- UN Sustainable Development Goals (if applying to UN agencies)
- Funder's country strategy (if applying to USAID, EU, GIZ, etc.)

### Step 3: Work Backwards from Impact

Ask: "What would have to be true for this impact to happen?"
Answer = outcomes. Then ask for each outcome: "What would have to be true for this outcome to occur?"
Answer = outputs. Repeat until you reach activities.

This **backwards mapping** prevents the common mistake of designing activities first and then claiming they produce impact — without a credible causal chain.

### Step 4: State Assumptions Explicitly

For every causal arrow in your ToC, ask: "What has to be true, outside of our control, for this to work?"

These are your **assumptions** — the risk register of your ToC. Each assumption is a potential point of failure.

Good assumptions are:
- Specific and testable ("UCDA maintains GPS registration database without disruption > 6 months")
- Not generic ("government is supportive" = too vague)
- Assessed for likelihood and severity

### Step 5: Identify Enabling Conditions

Some outcomes cannot be produced by activities alone. List the pre-conditions that must exist for your project to succeed:
- Infrastructure (road access, electricity)
- Policy environment (supportive regulation, functioning land titles)
- Community cooperation (willingness to participate)
- Market conditions (buyer demand, price stability)

## 4. Uganda Sector-Specific ToC Examples

### 4.1 — Agribusiness / Smallholder Farmer

**Impact:** Increased household incomes for smallholder farming families in [district]

**ToC narrative:**
> Small-scale farmers in [district] receive low prices for their produce because they sell individually to brokers at farm gate, lack quality certification, and have no direct access to formal market buyers. IF the project establishes a primary cooperative with collective marketing and quality grading infrastructure THEN farmers will sell collectively at premium prices and meet buyer quality standards WHICH WILL LEAD TO a 30–40% increase in farm-gate price received AND reduction in post-harvest losses from 35% to <10% BECAUSE collective volume enables direct negotiation with institutional buyers (supermarkets, exporters, processors) and quality infrastructure enables premium pricing WHICH WILL CONTRIBUTE TO household income increases of ≥ UGX 250,000/month per family ASSUMING buyers maintain demand and the cooperative operates with functional governance.

**Key assumptions to monitor:**
- Buyer demand stability (track quarterly commodity price)
- Cooperative governance (monthly management committee meetings)
- Weather conditions (crop yield assumption)

---

### 4.2 — Youth Employment / Vocational Skills

**Impact:** Reduced youth unemployment and increased economic participation for youth aged 18–30 in [area]

**ToC narrative:**
> Youth in [area] face unemployment because employers in [target sector] cannot find locally-trained workers with [specific skills], while youth lack the certification and networks to access formal employment. IF the project provides accredited [skill] training for 150 youth (60% women) THEN 150 youth will hold Uganda Business and Technical Examinations Board (UBTEB) certificates in [skill] WHICH WILL LEAD TO ≥ 70% of graduates securing employment or self-employment at earnings ≥ UGX 400,000/month within 6 months BECAUSE [local employers] face a documented skills gap in [skill] (evidence: survey of 20 employers, Q1 2026) and UBTEB certification is the industry-recognised standard WHICH WILL CONTRIBUTE TO reduced youth unemployment in [area] and increased tax base ASSUMING that demand for [skill] in the local economy remains stable and employers honour pre-commitment letters.

**Key assumptions to monitor:**
- Employer absorption rate (track quarterly via phone survey)
- UBTEB certification process (track registration and exam dates)
- Participant retention (track monthly — target < 10% dropout)

---

### 4.3 — Women's Economic Empowerment

**Impact:** Increased financial independence and reduced economic vulnerability for women in [district]

**ToC narrative:**
> Women in [district] have lower incomes than men because they operate in low-return sectors, lack access to credit, and face time poverty from unpaid care responsibilities. IF the project establishes a Village Savings and Loan Association (VSLA) for 200 women and provides business skills training THEN women will have access to rotating credit (average UGX 300,000 per cycle) and improved financial literacy WHICH WILL LEAD TO women starting or expanding income-generating activities, increasing average monthly earnings from UGX 150,000 to ≥ UGX 350,000 BECAUSE VSLAs are proven in East Africa (CARE Uganda evidence base: VSLAs increase savings 40–60%, reduce food insecurity 25%) and the combination of credit + skills training outperforms credit alone WHICH WILL CONTRIBUTE TO women's financial independence and reduced household vulnerability to income shocks ASSUMING that male household members do not capture VSLA proceeds and community VSLA facilitators are sustained post-project.

**Key assumptions to monitor:**
- VSLA repayment rate (monthly tracking — red flag below 95%)
- Business survival rate (quarterly check-in with members)
- Intra-household dynamics (gender-sensitive monitoring — observe for capture)

---

### 4.4 — Health Access / Rural Clinic

**Impact:** Improved health outcomes for households within 10 km of [facility]

**ToC narrative:**
> Communities in [sub-county] face preventable illness and mortality because the nearest functioning health facility is >20 km away, resulting in delayed care-seeking and high out-of-pocket transport costs. IF the project constructs and equips a Health Centre II and trains 3 clinical officers and 15 Village Health Teams (VHTs) THEN community members will have access to outpatient consultation, maternal and child health services, and basic medicines within 5 km WHICH WILL LEAD TO early treatment of malaria, diarrhoea, and maternal complications, reducing under-5 mortality and maternal mortality in the catchment area BECAUSE proximity is the primary determinant of healthcare-seeking behaviour for rural households (MoH Uganda Primary Healthcare data) and early treatment of malaria with ACTs reduces case fatality from 15% to <3% WHICH WILL CONTRIBUTE TO improved child survival rates and reduced household economic burden from illness ASSUMING that MoH provides ongoing health worker salary support and essential medicines supply chain functions.

---

### 4.5 — Climate / Environment / Renewable Energy

**Impact:** Reduced greenhouse gas emissions and increased energy access in [area]

**ToC narrative:**
> Households in [area] rely on kerosene lamps and charcoal cooking, causing indoor air pollution, high energy expenditure (UGX 15,000–30,000/month), and deforestation. IF the project installs 500 solar home systems (SHS) and 1,000 improved cookstoves THEN 500 households will have clean lighting and 1,000 households will reduce cooking fuel consumption by 50–60% WHICH WILL LEAD TO elimination of kerosene expenditure (UGX 15,000/month saved), 60% reduction in cooking fuel costs, and elimination of indoor air pollution in project households BECAUSE SHS technology is proven to replace kerosene at same or lower total cost, and improved cookstoves (ICS) reduce fuel consumption by 50–60% (evidence: SNV Uganda ICS programme 2019–2023) WHICH WILL CONTRIBUTE TO Uganda's NDC (Nationally Determined Contribution) target of 22% reduction in emissions by 2030 ASSUMING that solar equipment is maintained and spare parts remain available locally.

## 5. ToC Visualisation Formats

### Format A: Flow Diagram (for presentations)

```
[INPUTS]
Grant: USD [X]
Equipment: [list]
Staff: [list]
Partners: [names]
        ↓
[ACTIVITIES]
1. [Activity A]
2. [Activity B]
3. [Activity C]
        ↓
[OUTPUTS]                     ⚠ ASSUMPTIONS
1. [# trained]                - [Assumption A]
2. [# reached]                - [Assumption B]
3. [# produced]
        ↓
[OUTCOMES]                    ⚠ ASSUMPTIONS
Short-term (0–12 months):     - [Assumption C]
- [Behaviour change A]
Medium-term (1–3 years):
- [Condition change B]
        ↓
[IMPACT]
[Long-term societal change]
Contributing to: [NDP III / SDG X]
```

### Format B: Narrative Paragraph (for written proposals)

Use the IF–THEN–BECAUSE structure. Maximum 400 words. Include:
- One sentence on the problem
- One IF–THEN–BECAUSE paragraph for the core mechanism
- One sentence on how outcomes contribute to impact
- Three bullet points on key assumptions

### Format C: Results Chain Table (for logical frameworks)

| Level | Statement | Indicator |
|---|---|---|
| Impact | [Long-term impact] | [UBOS / national-level indicator] |
| Outcome | [Behaviour/condition change] | [% change among target group] |
| Output 1 | [Countable deliverable] | [# / quantity / quality] |
| Output 2 | [Countable deliverable] | [# / quantity / quality] |
| Activity 1 | [Action] | Budget line reference |

## 6. Common Theory of Change Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Confusing Outputs with Outcomes

❌ **Wrong:** "Outcome: 500 farmers trained in GAP"
✅ **Correct:** "Output: 500 farmers trained in GAP" / "Outcome: Trained farmers adopt GAP on ≥ 80% of farm area"

Training is an output. Behaviour change is an outcome. This distinction is critical — USAID and EU evaluators specifically test this.

### Mistake 2: Missing the BECAUSE Clause

❌ **Weak:** "Training farmers will lead to better yields."
✅ **Strong:** "Training farmers in composting techniques will lead to 25% yield increases BECAUSE composting addresses the specific soil nitrogen depletion identified in the baseline soil survey (pH 4.8, N below 0.1%), and trial plots using compost showed 28% yield increase (NARO trial, Masaka, 2023)."

The BECAUSE clause is where credibility is won or lost. It shows evidence, not hope.

### Mistake 3: Too Many Assumptions = Unbelievable

If your ToC requires 15 external conditions to all hold simultaneously, the funder will not believe it. Identify your three most critical assumptions and show how you are managing those risks.

### Mistake 4: Impact Too Ambitious, Too Fast

❌ **Wrong:** "Within 2 years, malnutrition in [district] will be eliminated."
✅ **Right:** "Within 3 years, the proportion of children under 5 classified as stunted in the project catchment will decrease from 37% to ≤ 28%."

Funders know that systemic change takes time. A modest, credible outcome commitment is stronger than an ambitious, unbelievable one.

### Mistake 5: No Link to Funder Strategy

Every funder has a country strategy, sector plan, or results framework. Your ToC impact level must explicitly connect to it:
- EU: Link to NDP III and Uganda-EU Partnership priorities
- USAID/CDCs: Link to PEPFAR, Feed the Future, or USAID Uganda CDCS
- UN agencies: Link to UNSDCF Uganda (2021–2025) outcomes
- GIZ: Link to German Development Cooperation bilateral priorities (Green Economy, Good Governance)

### Mistake 6: Ignoring Gender

A ToC that does not address gender dynamics will be marked down by most international funders. Ask:
- Are women and men affected by the problem differently?
- Are the project activities equally accessible to women (time, location, childcare)?
- Will the outcomes benefit women and men equally — or will benefits be captured by men?

Add a gender assumption: e.g., "ASSUMING that project activities are scheduled to avoid conflict with women's domestic responsibilities (market days, school drop-off times) and that women-only sessions are held for financial literacy topics."
