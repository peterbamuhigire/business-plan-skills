# Discovery Experiments

**Source:** Bland & Osterwalder, *Testing Business Ideas* (Wiley/Strategyzer, 2020)

Discovery experiments are used when you have an idea but need to learn whether you are solving a real problem for real people. They are cheap, fast, and generate **directional** evidence — not proof. Run multiple discovery experiments before concluding anything.

**Goal:** Identify the riskiest assumptions about desirability. Understand customer jobs, pains, and gains. Shape your value proposition before investing in delivery.

---

## Explore — Qualitative Interviews and Observation

### 1. Customer Interview (Exploration)

**Purpose:** Understand customer jobs, pains, and gains in depth. Learn what customers are trying to accomplish and what currently frustrates them.

**Evidence type:** Qualitative opinions → Weak-Moderate

**Process:**
1. Recruit 15–20 target customers (not friends or family)
2. Write interview script: open-ended questions about behaviour, not product
3. Conduct 30–45 minute interviews (in person or voice call)
4. One person interviews, one takes verbatim notes
5. Debrief immediately after each session
6. After 15–20 interviews, affinity sort: group responses by theme
7. Rank top 3 jobs, pains, and gains by frequency
8. Update Value Proposition Canvas

**Good interview questions:**
- "Walk me through the last time you had to deal with [problem area]."
- "What is the most frustrating part of that process?"
- "What do you currently do to solve this? How is that working?"
- "What would need to be true for you to switch to a different solution?"

**Avoid:** Pitching your idea during discovery interviews. You are learning, not selling.

**EA adaptation:** In-person visits to businesses (workshops, market stalls, offices) are expected and effective. Warm introductions from mutual contacts dramatically increase participation rates.

**Metrics:** Number of interviews; % mentioning each pain/job unprompted; strength of emotion when describing pains

---

### 2. Partner and Supplier Interviews

**Purpose:** Test feasibility assumptions — can key partners or suppliers actually deliver what the business model requires?

**Evidence type:** Qualitative opinions → Weak-Moderate

**Process:** Same structure as Customer Interview but focused on:
- Can they supply at the volume and quality assumed?
- What are their real costs and lead times?
- What conditions or minimums do they require?
- Who else are they supplying — who is the competition for their capacity?

**EA adaptation:** Identify key input suppliers, government agencies, or distribution partners early. Their constraints often define what business models are feasible.

---

### 3. Expert Stakeholder Interviews

**Purpose:** Access domain knowledge quickly. Understand regulatory environment, market structure, competitive dynamics, or technical requirements from people who already know.

**Process:** Same as Customer Interview but recruit 5–10 domain experts: industry veterans, regulatory officials, academics, successful competitors.

**Good expert questions:**
- "What do you wish you had known when you started in this space?"
- "Who succeeds in this market and why?"
- "What is the most common reason businesses in this sector fail?"

**EA adaptation:** Uganda has deep informal knowledge networks. Sector associations (e.g., UMA, UMBA, NOTU), UNBS technical officers, and KCCA licensing staff are valuable expert sources.

---

### 4. A Day in the Life (Ethnographic Observation)

**Purpose:** Observe customers in their actual environment, doing their actual work. Uncover unstated needs, workarounds, and hidden jobs that interviews miss.

**Evidence type:** Observed behaviour → Moderate

**Process:**
1. Obtain permission to shadow a customer through their workday
2. Observe without intervening — note what they do, not what they say
3. Note every workaround, inefficiency, and frustration visible in their behaviour
4. Ask clarifying "why" questions after observing, not during
5. Look for: manual workarounds that could be automated; processes done differently than described in interviews; resources customers cobble together on their own

**EA adaptation:** Particularly valuable in informal sector businesses where many processes are undocumented and owners often cannot articulate their own pain points until you observe them.

---

### 5. Discovery Survey

**Purpose:** Gather quantitative directional data from a larger sample. Useful for sizing a problem or confirming interview findings.

**Evidence type:** Self-reported opinions at scale → Weak-Moderate

**Process:**
1. Design open-ended or rank-order questions (not leading questions)
2. Ask about current behaviour, not hypothetical future behaviour
3. Recruit minimum 50 respondents from target segment
4. Analyse frequency of responses to identify top jobs/pains/gains
5. Use to prioritise — not to validate

**Good survey questions:**
- "What tools or methods do you currently use to handle [job]?"
- "Rank these pain points in order of how much they affect your work."
- "How often does [problem] occur, and how much time does it cost you?"

**Avoid:** "Would you use..." or "Would you pay for..." — people vastly over-state willingness to pay in surveys.

**EA adaptation:** WhatsApp polling is more accessible than Google Forms for many Ugandan SME owners. Keep surveys to 5–7 questions max.

## Data Analysis

### 6. Search Trend Analysis

**Purpose:** Gauge market demand and vocabulary using Google Trends and keyword planners. Understand whether people are actively searching for a solution.

**Evidence type:** Observed search behaviour at scale → Moderate

**Process:**
1. Use Google Trends (trends.google.com) — enter problem keywords; note relative volume and trajectory
2. Use Google Keyword Planner (Ads account needed) — note search volume and competition for keywords
3. Restrict to Uganda, East Africa, or Africa region
4. Look for: growing search trends; high volume unaddressed by local suppliers; seasonal patterns

**Useful for:** Market sizing estimates; confirming that the problem is actively felt; identifying vocabulary customers use (for marketing copy)

**EA adaptation:** Internet penetration in Uganda is mobile-first. Search volumes for Ugandan markets are lower than Western benchmarks — a keyword with 500 searches/month in Uganda may represent a significant addressable market. Also search in Luganda and Swahili variants.

---

### 7. Web Traffic Analysis

**Purpose:** If you have an existing website, analyse what pages, content, and keywords attract and retain visitors. Identify what value propositions resonate.

**Evidence type:** Observed behaviour → Moderate

**Process:**
1. Install Google Analytics or equivalent on your site
2. Identify top landing pages, top search queries, high exit pages
3. Compare time-on-page across different value proposition descriptions
4. Track: unique visitors, pages per session, bounce rate, conversion events

**Use:** Only applicable once you have a web presence with meaningful traffic.

---

### 8. Discussion Forums

**Purpose:** Find communities where your target customers discuss their problems candidly. Read what they actually complain about without you in the room.

**Evidence type:** Observed behaviour (authentic, unprompted) → Moderate

**Sources:** Facebook groups (primary in Uganda for informal sector), WhatsApp group admins, LinkedIn groups (professional services), Reddit (global but useful for B2B tech), sector-specific online communities, Jumia and Jiji product reviews

**Process:**
1. Identify 3–5 communities where target customers are active
2. Search for pain-related keywords; read threads about problems
3. Note: most common complaints, language they use, what solutions they are comparing, what they wish existed
4. Do not engage or pitch — observe only during discovery

---

### 9. Sales Force Feedback

**Purpose:** For businesses with an existing sales team, systematically extract customer intelligence they have already collected.

**Evidence type:** Second-hand qualitative → Weak (but efficient)

**Use:** Analyse patterns in: near-misses (almost bought, but why didn't they?), top 3 feature requests, most common objections, customer quotes on what almost prevented purchase.

**EA adaptation:** Applicable for existing businesses considering a new product line or entering a new customer segment.

---

### 10. Customer Support Analysis

**Purpose:** For businesses with existing customers, analyse support tickets, complaints, and feedback to uncover unmet needs.

**Evidence type:** Observed customer behaviour → Weak-Moderate

**Use:** Look for patterns in: repeat complaints about the same issue; feature requests that cluster around a single job; language customers use to describe what they need.

## Interest Discovery

### 11. Online Ad (Discovery)

**Purpose:** Test whether your value proposition can attract a target customer's attention at scale. Use to test messaging, not to sell.

**Evidence type:** Click-through behaviour → Moderate (interest, not intent)

**Key metric:** Click-through rate (CTR). Industry average varies — research your sector. Target at least 1–2% CTR for early-stage validation.

**Process:**
1. Define target audience on Facebook Ads or Google Ads
2. Create 2–3 ad variants with different value statements
3. Direct clicks to a simple landing page
4. Run for 7–14 days with a small budget (UGX 100,000–300,000)
5. Compare CTR across variants; kill low performers; iterate on the winner

**EA adaptation:** Facebook Ads is the dominant digital advertising platform in Uganda. Target by location (Kampala specific districts), interest, and job title. Instagram Ads (via Facebook) effective for younger urban consumers.

---

### 12. Link Tracking

**Purpose:** Track exactly how many people click on a specific link in a specific context. Use to test call-to-action copy, email subject lines, or button placement.

**Evidence type:** Click behaviour → Moderate

**Tools:** UTM parameters in Google Analytics, Bitly, WhatsApp link tracking

**EA adaptation:** Add UTM tracking to all links shared via WhatsApp broadcasts. Compare click rates across different message framings.

---

### 13. Email Campaign (or WhatsApp Broadcast)

**Purpose:** Test whether a targeted value message drives action from a warm audience.

**Evidence type:** Open + click rates → Moderate; conversions → Moderate-Strong

**Key metrics:**
- Open rate: 20–30% is healthy for a warm list
- Click rate: 2–5% of opens is acceptable
- Conversion (desired action taken): 1–5% of total sent

**Process:**
1. Build a list of target contacts (warm — not cold)
2. Write subject line that communicates value, not features
3. Single clear call-to-action per message
4. Send; measure opens, clicks, and conversions
5. Iterate on underperforming elements

**EA adaptation:** WhatsApp broadcasts are more effective than email for most Ugandan audiences. Keep messages short. Send at 9–11am on weekdays. Do not broadcast more than once per week without explicit consent.

**Case study:** Ryan Hoover built the initial Product Hunt community entirely via a Linkydink email group in 20 minutes. 200 subscribers in two weeks confirmed the hypothesis.

---

### 14. Social Media Campaign

**Purpose:** Build awareness, generate interest, and test what content resonates with a target audience.

**Evidence type:** Engagement → Weak; conversions → Moderate-Strong

**Key metrics:**
- Views and reach
- Shares and comments (qualitative signal — what do they say?)
- Click-through rate on links
- Conversion rate (sign-up, purchase, contact)

**Process:**
1. Define campaign goal (awareness vs. lead generation vs. sales)
2. Create content calendar: 2–3 posts per week minimum
3. Post consistently for 4–8 weeks before drawing conclusions
4. Analyse what content drives shares vs. clicks vs. conversions
5. Iterate; double down on best-performing format

**EA adaptation:** Facebook is the primary platform for most Ugandan consumer and SME audiences. TikTok effective for younger demographics. LinkedIn for professional services. Instagram for fashion, food, hospitality.

---

### 15. Referral Programme

**Purpose:** Test whether satisfied customers will recommend the product to peers. A proxy for product-market fit.

**Key metrics:**
- Advocate share rate: % of customers who share a referral code — target 15–20%
- Friend click-through rate: % of recipients who click — target 50–80%
- Friend conversion rate: % of clickers who sign up or buy — target 5–15%

**EA adaptation:** Word-of-mouth is the primary growth channel for most Ugandan businesses. Formalising it with trackable referral codes (shared via WhatsApp) can quantify what is already happening organically.

## Discussion Prototypes

### 16. Paper Prototype

**Purpose:** Sketch a product or service interface on paper; simulate the experience with customers to get rapid feedback before any digital build.

**Evidence type:** Task completion + qualitative feedback → Weak-Moderate (higher fidelity than conversation alone)

**Process:**
1. Sketch key screens or steps on paper
2. Test internally first — does the flow make sense?
3. Recruit 5–10 target customers
4. One person guides the interaction; another takes notes
5. Observe where customers get confused, stuck, or excited
6. Do not explain or defend during the test — observe

**Cost:** Almost zero. Can be done in a few hours.

---

### 17. Storyboard

**Purpose:** Sketch a sequence of illustrations showing how the customer experiences the value proposition. Tests whether the narrative resonates.

**Process:**
1. Define the customer segment and core value proposition
2. Each team member sketches 8–12 alternative value proposition scenarios
3. Present sketches to 15–20 target customers
4. Note which scenarios generate excitement; update Value Proposition Canvas

---

### 18. Explainer Video

**Purpose:** Communicate the value proposition through a short video; measure watch completion and share rate as proxies for resonance.

**Key metrics:** Video completion rate; shares; clicks from video to sign-up page

**EA adaptation:** Short-form video (60–90 seconds) performs best on Facebook and Instagram in Uganda. Voice-over in Luganda or local language significantly increases engagement with non-English-dominant audiences.

---

### 19. Product Box

**Purpose:** Ask customers to design an imaginary product box for the solution they want — surfaces what features and benefits they truly value.

**Process:**
1. Recruit 15–20 target customers
2. Give each group cardboard boxes and markers
3. Ask them to design a box for the product they wish existed: messaging, features, benefits
4. Each group pitches their product box to you as a sceptical customer
5. Note: which features are emphasised; what problems they claim it solves; the language they use

**Output:** Top 3 jobs, pains, and gains; value proposition messaging ideas

**EA adaptation:** Works well as a community event (church groups, market trader associations, sector cooperatives). Frame it as a business development workshop.

---

### 20. Speed Boat

**Purpose:** Identify what is slowing customers down with an existing product or service. Surfaces friction and gaps between the value proposition and reality.

**Process:**
1. Draw a speed boat on a large sheet of paper
2. Ask 15–20 existing customers to write "anchors" — things slowing them down — on cards and place them under the boat
3. Anchors placed deeper = more severe
4. Cluster similar anchors; assign severity and urgency as a team
5. Use results to prioritise experiments that address the most severe, frequent anchors

---

### 21. Card Sorting

**Purpose:** Understand how customers rank and categorise their jobs, pains, and gains. Validates the priority assumptions behind your value proposition.

**Process:**
1. Create cards for each known customer job, pain, and gain
2. Include blank cards for customers to add what is missing
3. Ask 15–20 customers to sort and rank: most to least important
4. Cluster results; identify top 3 jobs, pains, and gains across all participants

---

### 22. Buy a Feature

**Purpose:** Give customers play money to "purchase" the features they want most. Forces genuine trade-off decisions that reveal true priorities.

**Process:**
1. List 15–30 potential features with imaginary prices (higher price = higher complexity)
2. Give each customer a limited budget of play money
3. Let them buy the features they want most; they can collaborate with others for expensive features
4. Calculate which features received the most investment

**Output:** Prioritised feature list grounded in customer preferences, not internal assumptions

---

### 23. Brochure

**Purpose:** Create a printed or digital brochure describing the product/service. Tests whether the value proposition is communicable. Use physical brochures at trade shows, markets, or meetings.

**Evidence type:** Stated interest + feedback → Weak

**EA adaptation:** Print flyers (A5 or A4) are effective for testing value proposition messaging in Ugandan markets. Distribute at trade fairs, industry associations, or sector meetings. Note which version generates more inbound enquiries.
