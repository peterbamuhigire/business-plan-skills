# Test Card, Learning Card, and Decide Framework

**Source:** Bland & Osterwalder, *Testing Business Ideas* (Wiley/Strategyzer, 2020)

---

## Test Card Template

Complete one Test Card per experiment, before running it.

```
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
TEST CARD
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Business idea / hypothesis area:
Experiment #:          Date:          Owner:

WE BELIEVE:
[The hypothesis — a specific, falsifiable assumption]
Example: "Small retailers in Kampala will pay UGX 50,000/month for
an inventory tracking service via WhatsApp."

TO VERIFY THIS, WE WILL:
[The specific experiment — what exactly we will do]
Example: "Run a WhatsApp broadcast campaign to 200 market traders
in Owino and Nakasero, directing them to a simple landing page
with a pay-now option."

WE WILL MEASURE:
[The specific metrics — what data we collect]
Example: "Number of landing page visits, number who click 'Subscribe',
number who complete mobile money payment."

WE ARE RIGHT IF:
[The criteria — specific pass/fail threshold]
Example: "At least 10 traders (5%) complete a payment within 14 days."
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

**Tips for writing good hypotheses:**
- One hypothesis per Test Card
- Specific enough to be falsifiable (not "customers will love it")
- Include the customer segment, the behaviour, and the context
- Set criteria before running the experiment — not after seeing results

## Learning Card Template

Complete one Learning Card after each experiment.

```
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
LEARNING CARD
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Links to Test Card #:          Date:          Owner:

WE OBSERVED:
[Raw facts — what actually happened, without interpretation]
Example: "Landing page received 87 visits. 12 people clicked
'Subscribe'. 3 people completed a mobile money payment of UGX 50,000."

FROM THIS WE LEARNED:
[The insight — what the data means for the hypothesis]
Example: "3% payment conversion is below our 5% threshold. However,
exit interviews revealed traders want weekly billing (UGX 12,500/week),
not monthly. Price anchoring is the issue, not the value proposition."

THEREFORE WE WILL:
[The next action — specific decision]
Example: "Pivot pricing to weekly billing. Retest same landing page
with weekly price. Run 5 additional in-person interviews to confirm."

WE NEED TO VALIDATE:
[Open hypotheses that remain — what still needs testing]
Example: "Whether traders will pay weekly, whether collection via
mobile money agent is operationally feasible."
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

## Evidence Strength Framework

Not all evidence is equal. Use this guide to assess how much to trust what you have gathered.

### The Four Dimensions of Evidence Strength

| Dimension | Weaker | → | Stronger |
|-----------|--------|---|---------|
| **Type** | Opinions and self-reports | → | Observable behaviour |
| **Behaviour** | What people say they would do | → | What people actually did |
| **Volume** | One data point / one person | → | Multiple independent data points |
| **Method** | Single experiment type | → | Multiple converging experiment types |

### Confidence Levels

**Not Really Confident (weak evidence)**
- 1–3 interviews or conversations
- Unstructured opinions without criteria
- One survey with a leading question
- Your own assumption stated as fact
→ *Do not decide or build. Run more experiments.*

**Somewhat Confident (moderate evidence)**
- 15–20+ interviews with consistent themes
- Landing page clicks (people expressed interest by action)
- Prototype test with task completion data
- Survey with 50+ respondents and specific response criteria
→ *Proceed to next hypothesis or increase fidelity of same hypothesis.*

**Very Confident (strong evidence)**
- Real payments received (even small amounts)
- Signed Letters of Intent from B2B buyers
- Multiple experiment types all pointing the same direction
- Sustained customer behaviour over multiple interactions
→ *Persevere and invest further. The hypothesis is validated.*

### Evidence by Experiment Type

| Experiment | Evidence Type | Typical Strength |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|
| Customer Interview | Opinions + stated intent | Weak-Moderate |
| Discovery Survey | Opinions at scale | Weak-Moderate |
| Online Ad (clicks only) | Interest signals | Moderate |
| Email Campaign (open+click) | Interest signals | Moderate |
| Simple Landing Page (sign-up) | Interest + intent | Moderate |
| Prototype feedback | Usability + stated intent | Moderate |
| Validation Survey | Stated intent at scale | Moderate |
| Mock Sale (click to buy) | Purchase intent action | Moderate-Strong |
| Presale (payment collected) | Real behaviour | Strong |
| Concierge (delivery + satisfaction) | Real behaviour + satisfaction | Strong |
| Letter of Intent (signed) | Commitment by B2B buyer | Strong |
| Crowdfunding (funded) | Real payment | Strong |
| Single Feature MVP (payment) | Real product purchase | Very Strong |

## Decide Framework

After reviewing the Learning Card, the team makes one of four decisions:

### 1. Persevere
**When:** Evidence clearly supports the hypothesis; confidence is high
**Action:** Test the next riskiest hypothesis — move to higher fidelity or address a different assumption

### 2. Pivot
**When:** Evidence refutes the hypothesis but suggests a better direction
**Pivot types:**
- **Customer segment pivot** — same solution, different target customer
- **Value proposition pivot** — same customer, different core benefit
- **Pricing pivot** — same offer, different price point or billing model
- **Channel pivot** — same offer, different distribution channel
- **Revenue model pivot** — same product, different monetisation approach

### 3. Kill
**When:** Multiple experiments consistently refute desirability, feasibility is impossible, or no viable revenue model can be found despite thorough testing
**Rule:** Kill early and cheaply. A failed experiment that costs UGX 500,000 is far better than a failed business that costs UGX 50,000,000.

### 4. Continue Testing
**When:** Evidence is mixed, inconclusive, or sample size too small to draw conclusions
**Action:** Run same hypothesis with a different or higher-fidelity experiment

## Assumptions Mapping

Before selecting experiments, map out all the assumptions behind a business idea and prioritise by risk.

### Step 1 — List all assumptions
For each assumption, ask: "If this turns out to be false, does it kill our idea?"

**Desirability assumptions (examples):**
- Customers have this problem
- The problem is painful enough to pay to solve
- Our solution addresses the key pain
- Customers will switch from their current behaviour

**Feasibility assumptions (examples):**
- We can source the inputs at the assumed cost
- We can deliver the service at the assumed quality
- The technology/process is technically possible
- We can hire the required skills locally

**Viability assumptions (examples):**
- Customers will pay the assumed price
- Volume of customers is large enough for target revenue
- Unit economics are positive (revenue per customer > cost per customer)
- The business scales without proportional cost increases

### Step 2 — Risk scoring

For each assumption, score:
- **Importance:** 1 (low) to 3 (high) — how much does it matter if wrong?
- **Evidence:** 1 (none) to 3 (strong) — how much do we already know?
- **Priority score:** Importance × (4 – Evidence) → higher = test first

### Step 3 — Test in priority order
Start with highest-priority assumptions. Do not build until the top 5–10 assumptions have at least moderate evidence.

## Experiment Board (Visual Management)

Track experiments in a simple board visible to the whole team:

```
BACKLOG        │ RUNNING        │ ANALYZING     │ DECIDED
───────────────┼────────────────┼───────────────┼──────────────
[Test Card 1]  │ [Test Card 3]  │ [Test Card 2] │ Pivot: TC-1
[Test Card 4]  │                │               │ Persevere: TC-2
[Test Card 5]  │                │               │
```

**Limits:** Max 2–3 experiments running simultaneously. Completing experiments is more important than starting new ones.
